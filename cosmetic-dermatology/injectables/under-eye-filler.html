Under-Eye Filler: Tear Trough Treatment and Periorbital Rejuvenation

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Tear Trough Anatomy

The tear trough, or infraorbital hollow, is a depression beneath the eye extending from the medial canthus to the lateral orbit. This depression results from loss of suborbicularis oculi fat (SOOF) and decreased dermal collagen. The tear trough becomes increasingly prominent with age, contributing to a tired, aged appearance. Tear trough hollowing may be genetic or develop progressively over time. Volumizing this area creates a rejuvenated, rested appearance.

Anatomical Considerations

The under-eye region is delicate with thin, mobile skin overlying complex anatomy including the orbicularis oculi muscle, eyelid retractors, and orbital fat. The tear trough lies on the orbital rim; injecting too superficially causes visibility or irregularity, while injecting too deep is ineffective. The medial canthal area near the medial canthal ligament requires careful technique. Vascular anatomy, including angular artery and anterior superior orbital vein, must be respected to avoid occlusion.

Filler Selection

Soft, smooth HA fillers optimized for under-eye use are ideal for tear trough treatment. Juvederm Volbella and Restylane Kysse are commonly used. Their cohesive properties allow even distribution without visible particles. Thicker fillers are contraindicated in this delicate area due to visibility risk. Some practitioners prefer cannula technique using 25-27 gauge cannulas to minimize bruising and vessel trauma in this vascular area.

Injection Technique

Traditional needle injection places filler at the junction of the tear trough and cheek, beneath the orbicularis oculi, in the suborbicularis plane. Cannula technique involves careful insertion through a small puncture site (usually in the medial canthus), followed by linear threading along the tear trough. Careful visualization and control prevent excessive depth penetration or vascular injection. Meticulous hemostasis prevents postoperative bruising.

Volume and Placement

Tear trough treatment typically uses 0.25-0.5 mL per eye, distributed evenly along the depression. Over-filling creates puffy appearance or under-eye bags; conservative correction creates a natural result. Strategic placement slightly medial to the tear trough deepest point prevents visible bulging or malar mounds. Gentle massage post-injection ensures even integration.

Results and Appearance

Results are immediately visible, with dramatic improvement in tired appearance within hours. The tear trough depression fills, creating smooth transition between eyelid and cheek. Dark circles often appear less prominent due to improved light reflection from smooth, even skin. Optimal results appear at 2 weeks as swelling resolves. Duration typically lasts 9-12 months, shorter than other facial areas due to high mobility and periocular circulation.

Complementary Treatments

Under-eye filler pairs well with Botox for periocular lines, or with cheek filler for comprehensive tear trough-cheek rejuvenation. Addressing volume loss at the source prevents descent of cheek tissue and supports periocular skin. Upper lid hooding requires different treatment approaches and should not be addressed with filler alone.

Complications and Safety

Vascular occlusion is a serious potential complication; practitioners must recognize signs (pain, discoloration, skin changes) and have hyaluronidase immediately available. Tyndall effect (blue discoloration from shallow placement) can occur; careful injection depth prevents this. Bruising and temporary swelling are common but resolve within 7-10 days. Infection is rare but requires prompt treatment.
