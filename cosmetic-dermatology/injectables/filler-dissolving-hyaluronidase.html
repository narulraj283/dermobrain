Filler Dissolving with Hyaluronidase: Correcting HA Filler Complications

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Hyaluronidase Overview

Hyaluronidase is an enzyme that degrades hyaluronic acid, dissolving HA-based dermal fillers. Marketed as Vitrase, Amphadase, and other formulations, hyaluronidase provides the ability to reverse HA filler complications, overcorrection, or unsatisfactory aesthetic results. This reversibility is a major advantage of HA fillers compared to permanent alternatives. Practitioners administering HA fillers should maintain hyaluronidase availability for emergency use and elective dissolution.

Mechanism of Action

Hyaluronidase enzymatically cleaves hyaluronic acid into smaller oligosaccharide fragments, degrading HA filler structure. The enzyme works rapidly; injected hyaluronidase begins dissolving HA filler within minutes. The speed of action allows practitioners to perform controlled filler dissolution. Complete dissolution typically occurs within hours to days as injected hyaluronidase continues enzymatic action and disperses through tissues.

Indications for Hyaluronidase Use

Emergency indications include vascular occlusion signs (pain, blanching, cyanosis, skin necrosis), which require immediate hyaluronidase injection to restore blood flow. Elective indications include asymmetry, overcorrection, unnatural appearance, or patient dissatisfaction with HA filler results. The availability of reversal makes HA fillers ideal for anxious patients or those seeking reversible results.

Vascular Occlusion Management

Vascular occlusion is the most serious filler complication, requiring immediate recognition and treatment. Clinical signs include sharp pain at injection site, blanching, cyanosis, or ulceration within hours post-injection. Immediate hyaluronidase injection at the occlusion site aims to dissolve filler, restore blood flow, and prevent tissue necrosis. Early recognition and treatment significantly improve outcomes.

Injection Technique for Hyaluronidase

Hyaluronidase is injected directly into or around the area of concern using fine needles. The enzyme disperses through tissues, accessing filler throughout the treated area. Multiple injection sites may be necessary for large filler volumes. Careful technique prevents additional trauma in an already compromised area. The enzyme works quickly; observable filler dissolution occurs within hours.

Dosing Considerations

Standard hyaluronidase doses for filler reversal are typically 50-200 units depending on filler volume and area of concern. Emergency vascular occlusion cases may use higher doses for rapid complete dissolution. Subsequent doses can be injected if residual filler remains after initial treatment. Practitioners must understand product-specific dosing and concentration for accurate delivery.

Elective Filler Dissolution Timeline

Patients requesting elective filler dissolution should return within 1-2 weeks for partial reversal while filler provides structure. Complete immediate reversal removes all filler benefit; staged partial reversal allows better aesthetic results. Multiple hyaluronidase injections over days or weeks allow controlled progressive filler dissolution and aesthetic refinement.

Limitations of Hyaluronidase

Hyaluronidase only dissolves HA fillers; it does not affect other filler types (calcium hydroxylapatite, PLLA, PMMA). Hyaluronidase cannot precisely target filler; it dissolves all hyaluronic acid in treated area, potentially affecting both intentionally placed and inadvertently placed filler. Some HA filler may be encapsulated or fibrosed, limiting hyaluronidase access and dissolution.

Safety and Side Effects

Hyaluronidase has an excellent safety profile; serious allergic reactions are extremely rare. Temporary swelling, bruising, or discomfort at injection sites may occur. The enzyme has been used in ophthalmology and surgery for decades with excellent safety record. Patients with known hyaluronidase allergies should not receive treatment; allergy testing is available if needed.

Cost and Accessibility

Hyaluronidase cost varies by formulation and volume required. Insurance rarely covers hyaluronidase for cosmetic filler reversal. Cost of reversal may approach cost of original filler treatment. The expense is justified by the ability to correct serious complications or patient dissatisfaction. Practitioners should discuss hyaluronidase availability and costs during pre-treatment consultation.
