PRP and PRF Injections: Platelet-Rich Plasma and Platelet-Rich Fibrin

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

PRP and PRF Overview

Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) and platelet-rich fibrin (PRF) are autologous preparations derived from patient's own blood that contain growth factors and cytokines stimulating tissue healing and collagen remodeling. PRP and PRF have gained popularity in dermatology for facial rejuvenation, skin texture improvement, and hair loss treatment. The use of the patient's own biologic material makes these treatments autologous and theoretically safer than exogenous substances.

PRP Preparation and Mechanism

PRP is prepared by centrifuging whole blood to concentrate platelets 5-10 times baseline levels. The resulting plasma contains numerous growth factors including platelet-derived growth factor, transforming growth factor-beta, and vascular endothelial growth factor. These growth factors stimulate fibroblast proliferation, collagen synthesis, and tissue remodeling. PRP's mechanism involves growth factor delivery and inflammatory response stimulation.

PRF Preparation and Differences

PRF is similarly prepared through centrifugation but contains higher platelet concentrations and includes fibrin matrix formation, which provides sustained growth factor release over weeks. PRF's fibrin matrix structure provides a biological scaffold for collagen deposition and tissue regeneration. The fibrin framework may provide superior results compared to PRP for certain applications due to prolonged growth factor release.

Clinical Applications

PRP and PRF are used for facial rejuvenation (microneedling with PRP), skin texture improvement, fine line reduction, and scar remodeling. The treatments are increasingly used for hair loss, though evidence for hair applications is mixed. Some practitioners combine PRP with dermal fillers or use PRP as an adjunct to surgical procedures. Indications continue expanding as applications are researched.

Combination with Microneedling

PRP is frequently combined with microneedling, where small needles create controlled skin injury stimulating collagen remodeling. PRP application during microneedling enhances the treatment's collagen-stimulating effects. This combination is popular for treating fine lines, acne scars, and general skin rejuvenation. The synergistic effect of mechanical injury and growth factor stimulation maximizes collagen remodeling.

Injection Technique

PRP is injected intradermally or via microneedling channels depending on application. Multiple injection sites distributed across treatment areas ensure adequate growth factor delivery. Volumes typically range from 2-8 mL per session depending on treatment area. Some practitioners use a series of 3-4 treatments spaced 4-6 weeks apart for optimal results.

Results and Timeline

Results from PRP develop gradually over weeks and months as collagen remodeling occurs. Initial improvements may be subtle; progressive improvement continues over 3-6 months. Results typically last 6-12 months; maintenance treatments sustain benefits. Patient expectations must be set appropriately; PRP provides more subtle improvement than surgical interventions.

Safety and Considerations

PRP's autologous nature makes it exceptionally safe; serious adverse effects are extremely rare. Temporary bruising, swelling, or mild discomfort at injection sites may occur. The treatment carries no significant infection risk given autologous source. Regulatory requirements vary by jurisdiction; some areas classify PRP as biologic drug requiring specific handling.

Evidence and Efficacy

PRP demonstrates scientific support for collagen stimulation and growth factor delivery; however, clinical efficacy varies and high-quality evidence is still developing. Heterogeneous preparation protocols and variable clinical study designs create inconsistency in published results. Practitioner experience, patient selection, and appropriate expectations significantly influence outcomes.

Cost-Effectiveness Considerations

PRP treatment costs vary based on preparation technology and clinical delivery. The expense is primarily in collection and processing equipment. Compared to fillers requiring ongoing treatments, PRP's extended results may provide better long-term cost-effectiveness for some patients. Insurance rarely covers PRP, limiting accessibility for cost-conscious patients.
