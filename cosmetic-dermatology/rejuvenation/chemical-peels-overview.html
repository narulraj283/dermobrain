Chemical Peels: What Every Patient Should Know

Chemical peels represent a fundamental rejuvenation modality utilizing specific acids and chemical agents to induce controlled exfoliation and dermal remodeling. Classification by depth—superficial, medium, and deep—guides treatment selection, patient education, and outcome expectations.

Classification and Depth

Superficial peels affect epidermis and superficial dermis: minimal downtime (0-3 days), achievable results (15-30% improvement), suitable for maintenance. Medium peels penetrate mid-dermis: moderate downtime (3-7 days), significant results (50-70% improvement), require 1-3 treatments. Deep peels extend into deep dermis: substantial downtime (1-2 weeks), dramatic results (70-90% improvement), typically single treatment suffices.

Superficial Peels: Glycolic and Lactic Acid

Glycolic acid (AHA, 20-70% concentration, pH 0.6-3.9) penetrates rapidly due to small molecular size. Lactic acid (AHA, 15-50% concentration, pH 1.5-4) provides gentler exfoliation suitable for sensitive skin. Salicylic acid (BHA, 20-30% concentration) oil-soluble, ideal for acne-prone oily skin. Treatment duration 3-15 minutes depending on strength. Minimal downtime, repeated weekly or bi-weekly for cumulative benefit.

Results: fine line reduction, textural improvement, pigmentation lightening. Patient satisfaction 75-85%. No downtime enables rapid treatment succession.

Medium Peels: TCA and Jessner's Solution

Trichloroacetic acid (TCA, 15-35% concentration) induces controlled keratolysis and collagen remodeling. 15-20% TCA: superficial-medium depth. 25-35% TCA: true medium depth. Jessner's solution (14% salicylic acid + 14% lactic acid + 14% resorcinol) combined effect creates medium-depth penetration. Treatment duration 5-10 minutes. Results visible 5-7 days as frosting develops indicating adequate penetration.

Downtime: 5-7 days social downtime (crusting/peeling), 2 weeks complete healing. Results: 50-70% improvement in wrinkles, acne scars, melasma, pigmentation. Often requires 1-3 treatments for optimal results. Patient satisfaction 85-90%.

Deep Peels: Phenol

Phenol (90% solution, historically "Baker-Gordon formula") provides deepest chemical peel penetration, approaching surgical results. Contraindicated on eyelids, neck, hands due to scarring risk. Full-face phenol limited due to systemic toxicity risk (cardiac arrhythmias). Now typically reserved for selected areas (forehead, perioral) rather than full face.

Results: 70-90% improvement in severe photoaging, deep rhytides, acne scars comparable to CO2 laser. Downtime substantial: 1-2 weeks crusting, 2-3 months erythema, SPF 50 for 12 months. Patient satisfaction highest for appropriate candidates (90-95%). Risk of permanent hypopigmentation (5-15%) particularly in darker skin types limits use.

Fitzpatrick Skin Type Considerations

Types I-II: tolerate all depths well, risk of prolonged erythema. Types III-IV: medium/deep peels carry increased hyperpigmentation and hypopigmentation risk, conservative approaches preferred. Types V-VI: superficial peels only recommended due to high risk of dyspigmentation from deeper peels. Darker skin types benefit from lower-strength acids and shorter application times.

Patient Selection and Contraindications

Absolute contraindications: isotretinoin use within 6-12 months, active bacterial or viral infection, unrealistic expectations. Relative contraindications: darker skin types for medium/deep peels, history of hypertrophic scarring, photosensitivity disorders, recent sun exposure.

Recovery and Post-Operative Care

Superficial: cleanse gently, moisturize, SPF 30 daily. Medium: antiviral prophylaxis (herpes reactivation risk 5-10%), antibiotic ointment, occlusive dressing 24-48 hours, SPF 50 for 2-3 months. Deep: hospitalization historically recommended, careful cardiac monitoring if phenol full-face, strict wound care, SPF 50 for 12 months.

Outcomes and Results Timeline

Superficial: progressive improvement with repeated treatments over weeks, results plateau at 30-50% improvement. Medium: initial visible improvement 1-2 weeks, progressive 4-6 weeks, final results 3-6 months. Deep: dramatic immediate improvement, peak results 3-6 months, may continue improving to 12 months.

Complications

Superficial: minimal (temporary erythema, rare hyperpigmentation). Medium: post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (20-40%), hypopigmentation (2-5%), milia, textural changes. Deep: persistent hypopigmentation (5-15%), ectropion risk if periocular, scarring (2-5%), permanent textural changes possible.

Conclusion

Chemical peels represent versatile rejuvenation tool with multiple options for various patient concerns and skin types. Superficial peels enable convenient maintenance treatments. Medium peels provide dramatic improvement with moderate downtime. Deep peels achieve surgical-grade results but carry significant downtime and complication risks. Proper selection based on indication, skin type, and patient expectations ensures optimal outcomes.
