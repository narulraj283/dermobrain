Scar Treatment Overview: Acne Scars, Surgical, and Burn Scars

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Understanding Scar Formation

Scars result from wound healing after injury, surgery, severe acne, or burns. The healing process involves inflammation, collagen deposition, and tissue remodeling. Scars can present as atrophic (depressed/indented), hypertrophic (raised), or keloid (excessive) formations, each requiring different treatment approaches.

Acne Scars

Acne scars predominantly present as atrophic depressed scars resulting from severe cystic acne with dermis destruction. Common types include ice-pick scars (narrow, deep), box car scars (broad, angular depression), and rolling scars (undulating depressions from fibrous tethering).

Subcision: Mechanical separation of tethering fibers lifting depressed scars. Particularly effective for rolling scars. Often combined with other modalities. Results improve over 3-6 months as inflammation resolves.

Fractional Laser: Fractional CO2 or Erbium lasers ablate scar tissue triggering collagen remodeling. Particularly effective for atrophic acne scars. Typically requires 3-5 treatments. Downtime is significant (1 week erythema, 1-2 weeks peeling). Results continue improving over 6-12 months.

Radiofrequency Microneedling: RF microneedling induces collagen without ablation, effectively treating acne scars with minimal downtime. Multiple treatments (4-6) typically required. Less dramatic results than fractional laser but more tolerable. Results develop over 3-6 months.

Filler Injections: Injectable fillers provide immediate depression filling for atrophic scars. Results are temporary (6-24 months depending on filler). Best for depressed scars not amenable to resurfacing or as supplementary treatment.

Surgical Scars

Surgical scars present as linear marks that may be depressed, hypertrophic, or pigmented depending on wound care and individual healing characteristics.

Early Management: Prevention-focused strategies during healing include proper wound care, tension reduction with surgical tape, and sun protection. Early treatment with silicone products may improve scar appearance.

Fractional Laser: Fractional laser resurfacing is highly effective for surgical scar improvement, particularly for discoloration and surface texture. Multiple treatments (3-5) typically needed. Most effective when performed 6-12 months post-surgery after initial scar maturation.

Scar Revision Surgery: Severe surgical scars may benefit from surgical revision repositioning scars along natural tension lines or breaking up linear scars with Z-plasty or W-plasty techniques.

Burn Scars

Burn scars vary greatly depending on burn depth and healing process. Severe burns often result in hypertrophic or keloid scars and contractures limiting function.

Laser Treatment: Both vascular lasers (PDL for erythema) and fractional lasers (CO2/Erbium) effectively improve burn scar appearance. PDL addresses redness and vascular proliferation. Fractional lasers address scar tissue and surface irregularities. Multiple treatments often necessary.

Steroid Injections: Intralesional steroid injections reduce hypertrophic and keloid burn scars by suppressing collagen synthesis. Multiple injections spaced 4-6 weeks apart typically required.

Surgical Revision: Significant burn scars and contractures often require surgical intervention including scar excision, Z-plasty, or skin grafting for functional and aesthetic improvement.

Hypertrophic and Keloid Scars

Hypertrophic scars are raised but respect original wound boundaries, while keloids extend beyond original injury boundaries and are more aggressive. Both benefit from:

  • Intralesional steroid injections (triamcinolone acetonide)
  • Silicone pressure therapy
  • Laser treatment (PDL for vascular components, fractional for tissue remodeling)
  • Surgical excision in cases of poor medical management response

Treatment Selection Algorithm

Atrophic scars (acne, surgical) respond well to laser resurfacing, subcision, or RF microneedling. Hypertrophic/keloid scars benefit from intralesional steroids and vascular laser therapy. Burn scars require combination approaches often involving multiple modalities. Early intervention during active remodeling phase (0-12 months post-injury) produces best outcomes.

Timeline and Realistic Expectations

Scar improvement requires months to develop fully, with progressive changes over 6-12 months post-treatment. Complete scar elimination is rarely achievable; the goal is significant improvement in visibility and function. Combination treatments often produce superior results compared to single modalities.
