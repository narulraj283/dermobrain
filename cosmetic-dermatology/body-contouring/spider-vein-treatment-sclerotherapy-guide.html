Spider Vein Treatment: Sclerotherapy Guide

Clinical Overview

Spider veins (telangiectasia), characterized by small dilated superficial blood vessels appearing as fine red, purple, or blue lines typically on the legs and face, represent one of the most common cosmetic vascular concerns affecting up to 40% of the population. These benign but cosmetically objectionable telangiectatic vessels result from congenital predisposition, venous insufficiency, prolonged sitting or standing, hormonal influences, and cumulative environmental factors. Sclerotherapy, the gold-standard treatment for spider veins, involves injection of chemical irritants that damage the endothelium and cause vessel thrombosis and fibrosis, permanently eliminating affected vessels. Modern sclerotherapy techniques with improved formulations and delivery systems achieve excellent cosmetic results with minimal downtime and adverse effects. Understanding sclerotherapy mechanisms, agent selection, and injection technique enables clinicians to provide patients with superior outcomes.

Pathophysiology and Etiology

Spider veins result from vasodilation of superficial venules and capillaries in response to multiple contributing factors. Venous insufficiency, whether primary from valve dysfunction or secondary from prior deep vein thrombosis, creates backpressure promoting superficial vessel dilation. Hormonal influences including estrogen from oral contraceptives and hormone replacement therapy predispose women to telangiectasia through effects on venous tone. Pregnancy-related hormonal changes and increased blood volume promote venous dilation. Repetitive microtrauma from activities involving prolonged dependent leg positioning (standing occupations, extended sitting) promotes vessel dilation over time. Genetic predisposition accounts for approximately 60% of spider vein development, explaining familial clustering. Chronic sun exposure damages elastic fibers in vessel walls, predisposing to dilation. Inflammatory conditions and rosacea increase facial telangiectasia risk. Understanding contributing factors allows for targeted prevention strategies and identification of patients at risk for recurrence.

Sclerotherapy Mechanisms and Agents

Sclerotherapy functions through direct chemical injury to vessel endothelium, causing inflammation, thrombosis, and subsequent fibrosis with vessel obliteration. Liquid sclerosing agents including sodium tetradecyl sulfate (STS), polidocanol, and hypertonic saline directly contact endothelial cells, causing denudation and cellular injury. Foam sclerosants, created by mixing liquid agent with air or other gas, increase surface area contact with vessel wall and improve visualization. STS remains most popular in the United States, available in concentrations of 0.5% to 3%. Polidocanol, preferred in Europe, demonstrates reduced systemic absorption and excellent safety profile. Hypertonic saline, though effective, carries higher pain and post-sclerotherapy pigmentation risk. Chromated glycerol represents an alternative with improved tolerability. Selection of agent depends on vessel size, desired durability, patient tolerance, and clinician experience.

Treatment Technique and Injection Protocol

Successful sclerotherapy requires precise injection technique to deliver sclerosant accurately into target vessels while minimizing extravasation and surrounding tissue injury. Small vessels (less than 1 mm) may be treated with microinjections using 30-gauge needles and minimal volume of dilute agent. Larger vessels may require slightly higher sclerosant concentration and larger injection volumes. Most practitioners utilize hand-held magnification or operating microscopes to visualize small vessels during injection. Light-based vessels may be more challenging to visualize, requiring careful palpation and sometimes slight elevation of the leg to enhance vessel visibility. Intradermal injection rather than intravenous injection improves efficacy and reduces systemic absorption. Post-injection compression through compression garments or elastic bandages applied immediately following treatment reduces vessel recanalization through pressure-mediated thrombosis promotion. Most patients tolerate sclerotherapy well with topical anesthetic cream providing additional comfort, though the stinging sensation during injection discomfort is not completely eliminated.

Clinical Efficacy and Treatment Response

Sclerotherapy demonstrates excellent efficacy with success rates of 70-90% of treated vessels becoming permanently obliterated following single injections. Smaller vessels generally demonstrate higher response rates compared to larger vessels (2-3 mm). Most patients require 1-3 treatment sessions spaced 4-6 weeks apart for optimal results. Larger treatment areas may require more sessions due to limitations on sclerosant volume per treatment to reduce systemic absorption risks. Progressive improvement becomes apparent 4-6 weeks following injection as vessels fibrosis occurs. Vessels may appear initially darker or bruised following treatment as thrombosis occurs, then gradually lighten as fibrosis replaces thrombotic material. Some vessels demonstrate partial resolution requiring additional sclerotherapy, while others completely disappear. Complete resolution of all spider veins is not universally achievable, but cosmetically acceptable improvement (70-80% reduction) is the typical outcome with appropriate treatment.

Post-Treatment Care and Recovery

Sclerotherapy requires minimal downtime with most patients resuming normal activities immediately. Compression garments should be worn for 24-48 hours post-treatment to promote vessel thrombosis and reduce recanalization risk. Patients should remain active and avoid prolonged sitting or standing during the immediate post-treatment period, as immobility may increase thrombosis risk. Bruising commonly develops at injection sites, typically resolving within 1-2 weeks. Mild edema may persist for several days. Temporary hyperpigmentation at injection sites may occur in 5-10% of patients, typically resolving over weeks to months. Patients should avoid excessive heat, exercise, and saunas for 24-48 hours post-treatment as these may increase thrombosis risk. Sun protection is important during healing as sun exposure may worsen post-inflammatory pigmentation.

Adverse Events and Safety Considerations

Sclerotherapy is generally very safe with adverse events typically minor and self-resolving. Common mild effects include injection site bruising, edema, and tenderness. More concerning complications, though rare, include matting (development of fine telangiectatic vessels at treatment site), skin necrosis from extravasation, and thrombophlebitis. Serious complications including pulmonary embolism or stroke from paradoxical embolism are extremely rare when appropriate dosing and technique are employed. Patients with thrombophilic disorders, recent surgery, or immobility require careful patient selection and may benefit from thromboprophylaxis. Foam sclerosants carry theoretically increased embolism risk compared to liquid agents, though clinical data supporting this concern remains limited. Absolute contraindications include acute thrombosis, uncontrolled hypercoagulable states, and severe allergy to sclerosant agents. Relative contraindications include pregnancy, recent surgery, and uncontrolled hypercoagulable disorders requiring careful risk-benefit assessment.

Recurrence and Long-Term Management

Complete permanent elimination of all spider veins is difficult to achieve, with recurrence rates of 10-30% reported in long-term follow-up studies. Recurrence may result from recanalization of partially obliterated vessels or development of new telangiectatic vessels in predisposed individuals. Recurrent veins may respond to retreatment, though some demonstrate relative resistance. Ongoing preventive strategies including leg elevation, avoidance of prolonged sitting or standing, compression garments, and lifestyle modifications may reduce recurrence risk. Addressing underlying venous insufficiency through compression therapy or occasionally more advanced interventions may improve long-term outcomes in patients with significant functional venous disease.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are spider veins dangerous?

Spider veins are purely cosmetic concerns with no health implications. They differ from varicose veins, which may indicate underlying venous insufficiency. Spider veins do not cause pain or edema and require no treatment unless for cosmetic reasons.

Can sclerotherapy result in permanent vessel obliteration?

Yes, successfully treated vessels are permanently destroyed. However, recurrence rates of 10-30% occur due to either recanalization of partially treated vessels or development of new telangiectatic vessels in predisposed individuals.

How many sclerotherapy treatments are needed?

Most patients require 1-3 treatment sessions spaced 4-6 weeks apart to achieve 70-80% improvement. Larger treatment areas may require additional sessions.

What is the recovery period after sclerotherapy?

Sclerotherapy requires minimal downtime with most patients returning to normal activities immediately. Compression garments are recommended for 24-48 hours. Bruising typically resolves within 1-2 weeks.
