Spider Vein Treatment: Sclerotherapy Comprehensive Guide

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Understanding Spider Veins

Spider veins (telangiectasia) are small dilated blood vessels appearing as red, purple, or blue lines on the skin surface. These benign vascular lesions result from valvular incompetence, venous reflux, or congenital vascular abnormalities. While typically asymptomatic, patients seek treatment for cosmetic reasons.

Sclerotherapy Mechanism

Sclerotherapy involves injection of a sclerosing agent into spider veins, causing inflammation and fibrosis that destroys vessel wall integrity and leads to vein obliteration. The sclerotic vessel is then gradually reabsorbed by the body and replaced with fibrotic tissue, resulting in permanent vessel elimination.

Sclerosing Agents

Sodium Tetradecyl Sulfate (STS): FDA-approved anionic detergent that damages vessel endothelium and triggers thrombosis. Concentrations typically used are 0.5-1% for spider veins. Effective and well-established but carries higher thromboembolism risk compared to newer agents.

Polidocanol: Non-ionic detergent with similar mechanism to STS but potentially fewer side effects and better tolerability. Increasingly preferred over STS. Available concentrations 0.5-1%.

Hypertonic Saline: Non-detergent option causing osmotic damage to endothelium. Less effective than detergent agents but good option for patients with detergent sensitivity. Often requires larger injection volumes.

Foam Sclerosants: Liquid sclerosants mixed with gas creating foam that has improved vessel contact and penetration. Foam allows lower sclerosant concentrations while maintaining efficacy. Increasingly popular for larger vessels.

Treatment Technique

Spider vein sclerotherapy is performed as outpatient procedure with minimal preparation:

  • Skin cleansing and vessel visualization
  • Precise needle placement into target vessel
  • Slow sclerosant injection while monitoring for immediate adverse response
  • Multiple vessels treated in single session (typically 10-50 vessels)
  • Compression application post-injection

Sessions typically last 15-30 minutes depending on number of vessels treated. No anesthesia is usually required, though some practitioners use topical anesthesia for sensitive patients.

Results Timeline

Vessel response begins immediately with inflammation and hyperemia visible over 24-48 hours. Progressive fibrosis and obliteration occur over 4-6 weeks. Complete vessel resorption may take 8-12 weeks. Most patients require 2-3 treatment sessions spaced 4-6 weeks apart for comprehensive spider vein clearance.

Clearance rates for individual treated veins exceed 80-90% on average. However, new spider veins may develop over time, requiring periodic maintenance treatments.

Efficacy Factors

Treatment success depends on multiple factors:

  • Vessel Size: Vessels <1 mm respond better than larger ones. Very fine telangiectasia (microvasculature) may not obliterate completely.
  • Sclerosant Selection: Appropriate agent selection for vessel type and size optimizes results.
  • Injection Technique: Proper needle placement and injection rate affect outcomes.
  • Underlying Venous Reflux: Significant venous insufficiency may require treatment to prevent recurrence.

Side Effects and Complications

Sclerotherapy is generally safe with minimal serious complications:

  • Temporary bruising and induration at injection sites
  • Transient hyperpigmentation lasting weeks to months
  • Matting (fine vascular networks developing at treatment site, usually temporary)
  • Rare allergic reactions to sclerosant
  • Very rare thromboembolism or other serious vascular complications

Compression garments for 48-72 hours post-treatment reduce swelling and complications.

Contraindications and Precautions

Sclerotherapy should be avoided in pregnant patients, those with active thrombosis, and severe underlying venous insufficiency should be evaluated for correction before treatment. Hypercoagulable states may increase complication risk.

Long-Term Outcomes

Treated spider veins do not recur. However, new spider veins often develop over time, particularly in predisposed individuals or those with underlying venous insufficiency. Many patients return for maintenance treatments every 1-2 years as new vessels appear.
