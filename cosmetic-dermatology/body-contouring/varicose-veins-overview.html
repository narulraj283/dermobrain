Varicose Veins: Overview, Pathophysiology, and Treatment Options

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Understanding Varicose Veins

Varicose veins are enlarged, twisted veins resulting from chronic venous insufficiency and valve dysfunction. Unlike spider veins (asymptomatic cosmetic concerns), varicose veins often cause symptoms including pain, heaviness, swelling, and potential serious complications like thrombosis. Treatment addresses both cosmetic and medical aspects of the condition.

Pathophysiology

Varicose veins develop through progressive venous dysfunction. Venous valve incompetence (from genetics, pregnancy, prolonged standing, or weight gain) causes blood reflux and venous hypertension. Chronically elevated venous pressure damages vessel walls, causing progressive dilatation. Progressive dilatation worsens valve dysfunction, creating a self-perpetuating cycle of increasing venous insufficiency.

Risk Factors and Etiology

  • Genetic predisposition (strong family history)
  • Female gender and hormonal factors
  • Pregnancy (hormonal changes and increased venous volume)
  • Obesity and weight gain
  • Prolonged standing or immobility
  • Age (cumulative venous damage)
  • History of deep vein thrombosis

Clinical Presentation

Varicose veins may present with:

  • Visible tortuous, enlarged veins
  • Pain, heaviness, or fatigue in legs
  • Swelling and edema, particularly evening
  • Skin changes (discoloration, thickening)
  • Ulceration in severe chronic cases
  • Complications including thrombosis and bleeding

Duplex Ultrasound Evaluation

Venous duplex ultrasound is essential for varicose vein evaluation, demonstrating:

  • Reflux location and severity
  • Saphenous vein involvement (great vs. small saphenous)
  • Deep venous system integrity
  • Valve function and hemodynamic flow
  • Risk factors for complications

This anatomic information guides treatment selection and planning.

Conservative Management

Initial treatment of varicose veins emphasizes conservative measures:

  • Compression stockings (20-30 mmHg for mild disease)
  • Leg elevation and frequent position changes
  • Regular exercise and weight management
  • Avoidance of prolonged standing

Many mild varicose veins can be managed indefinitely with conservative care alone.

Sclerotherapy

Sclerotherapy is effective for small to moderate varicose veins and associated spider veins. Multiple injections and treatment sessions (typically 3-6) are required for significant venous burden. Sclerotherapy addresses cosmetic appearance and can improve symptoms but may not adequately address severely dilated vessels or high-pressure reflux.

Endovenous Ablation

Radiofrequency Ablation (RFA): Catheter-based radiofrequency delivery causes vein wall heating and collapse. The procedure is performed under local anesthesia with ultrasound guidance. Effective for saphenous vein ablation treating the primary incompetent vessel responsible for varicose development.

Endovenous Laser Ablation (EVLA): Similar catheter-based approach using laser energy instead of radiofrequency. EVLA and RFA demonstrate comparable efficacy and safety, with success rates exceeding 95% for saphenous vein closure.

Mechanochemical Ablation

Newer technique combining mechanical vein wall disruption with sclerosant injection. Devices like ClariVein allow efficient saphenous vein ablation with potentially less discomfort than thermal methods. Clinical experience is still accumulating but early data shows excellent efficacy.

Surgical Ligation and Stripping

Traditional open surgical approaches address varicose veins through vein ligation and removal. While effective, surgical methods produce more recovery time and morbidity compared to newer minimally invasive approaches. Current use is limited to cases with extensive varicosity or failed endovenous intervention.

Treatment Selection

Treatment selection depends on varicose vein severity, hemodynamic characteristics, patient preference, and symptom burden. Mild asymptomatic varicosities may not warrant intervention. Significant symptomatic varicosities with reflux typically benefit from saphenous vein ablation, with supplementary sclerotherapy for residual branch varicosities. Combined approaches addressing both primary incompetent vein and secondary branches produce optimal results.

Long-Term Outcomes

Endovenous ablation provides durable closure in 95%+ of patients at 5+ year follow-up. However, new varicose veins may develop from other venous branches or progression of pre-existing insufficiency. Periodic evaluation and treatment of emerging varicosities may be needed.
