Mohs & Skin Cancer Surgery

Mohs Surgery for Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

SCC and the Role of Mohs Surgery

Squamous cell carcinoma accounts for approximately 20% of skin cancers and has greater potential for metastasis compared to BCC. While most SCCs are curable with standard excision, Mohs micrographic surgery is recommended for high-risk lesions where the consequences of recurrence are significant.

High-Risk SCC Features

Risk factors for SCC recurrence include tumor size greater than 2 cm, depth greater than 4 mm, poorly differentiated histology, Clark level IV or V invasion, location on the ear or lip, immunosuppression, recurrent lesions, and perineural invasion. Lesions with any of these features are candidates for Mohs surgery.

Clinical Presentation of SCC

Squamous cell carcinoma may present as a scaly nodule, non-healing ulcer, or rapidly growing nodule with surrounding erythema. Some lesions are keratotic and may originate from actinic keratosis. Unlike BCC, SCC may present with rapid growth and may be tender or painful.

Mohs Surgery Cure Rates

For low-risk SCC, standard excision achieves cure rates of 95-98%. However, for high-risk SCC, Mohs surgery improves cure rates to 99% or higher. Recurrence rates after Mohs surgery are typically 1-3% for high-risk SCC, compared to 5-15% with standard excision for similar lesions.

Special Considerations for SCC

When Mohs surgery is performed for SCC, special attention is paid to assessing perineural invasion, as this finding may indicate the need for adjuvant radiation therapy. Additionally, the depth of invasion is carefully noted. Some high-risk SCCs may require sentinel lymph node biopsy in addition to Mohs surgery.

Reconstruction After SCC Removal

The defect created by SCC removal, particularly on the face or ears, requires careful reconstruction. Mohs surgery's tissue-sparing nature minimizes the reconstruction burden while ensuring complete cancer removal.
