Mohs & Skin Cancer Surgery

Skin Cancer Recurrence Rates by Surgical Procedure

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Understanding Recurrence Rates

Recurrence rate is the percentage of treated skin cancers that return at the same site (local recurrence) within a specified time period, typically 5 years. Recurrence rates vary significantly based on the surgical technique, tumor characteristics, and patient factors.

Basal Cell Carcinoma Recurrence Rates

Mohs Micrographic Surgery

Five-year recurrence rates following Mohs surgery for BCC are typically less than 1% (0.5-1%). This represents the gold standard for BCC treatment, particularly for high-risk lesions.

Standard Excision

Five-year recurrence rates following conventional excision with 5 mm margins are approximately 3-5%. Re-excision rates for incomplete removal are 5-10%.

Curettage and Electrodesiccation

Five-year recurrence rates for C&E are approximately 5-15%, depending on lesion size and operator experience. This technique is best reserved for small, low-risk BCCs.

Cryosurgery

Five-year recurrence rates for cryosurgery range from 5-15%, making it less ideal than other techniques but acceptable for very small lesions.

Squamous Cell Carcinoma Recurrence Rates

Mohs Micrographic Surgery

For low-risk SCC, Mohs surgery achieves five-year recurrence rates of less than 1%. For high-risk SCC, recurrence rates are 1-3%, which is superior to conventional excision.

Standard Excision

For low-risk SCC, conventional excision achieves five-year recurrence rates of 1-3%. For high-risk SCC, recurrence rates increase to 5-15%, making Mohs surgery preferable.

Other Destructive Techniques

C&E and cryosurgery have higher recurrence rates for SCC (10-20%) and are not recommended for invasive cancers.

Melanoma Recurrence Rates

Local recurrence rates for melanoma depend heavily on Breslow thickness and stage. Melanomas treated with appropriate wide excision have five-year local recurrence rates of less than 5% for localized disease. Patients with positive lymph nodes or systemic involvement have much higher rates of distant recurrence.

Factors Affecting Recurrence Risk

  • Surgical technique and margin adequacy
  • Tumor size and depth
  • Histological subtype and differentiation
  • Perineural invasion
  • Immunosuppression status
  • Location and previous treatment
  • Follow-up surveillance quality

Long-Term Recurrence Patterns

Most recurrences occur within 2-5 years of initial treatment, though late recurrences after 5+ years are possible. Early detection through patient self-examination and dermatologist surveillance improves outcomes even if recurrence occurs.

Recurrence at Different Sites

Lesions on the nose, ears, and lip have higher recurrence rates than lesions on other facial areas, likely due to deeper anatomical structures and tissue characteristics. Lesions in these high-risk locations are particularly suited for Mohs surgery.
