Transposition Flaps: Applications and Technique

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Transposition Flap Overview

A transposition flap is a reconstructive technique in which tissue adjacent to a defect is moved perpendicular to its length to fill the defect. Unlike advancement flaps, transposition flaps pivot around a pedicle. The most common transposition flap is the rhombic (Limberg) flap.

Rhombic (Limberg) Flap

The rhombic flap is designed using geometric principles to create a defect and donor site with rhombus (diamond) shapes. The flap is elevated with sufficient pedicle width and transposed into the defect. The technique is elegant and creates predictable scars aligned with relaxed skin tension lines.

Other Transposition Flaps

Additional transposition flaps include the Bilobed flap (particularly useful for defects on the nose), the Karapandzic flap (for lip defects), and the Abbé flap (for lip reconstruction). Each has specific applications based on defect location and characteristics.

Technical Principles

Transposition flaps require adequate tissue laxity. The defect should be designed optimally before flap elevation. The pedicle must be wide enough to maintain blood supply. The pivoting motion of the flap creates tension that must be managed carefully to prevent flap necrosis.

Advantages

  • Single-stage procedure
  • Good blood supply maintenance
  • Excellent color and texture match
  • Can be designed using geometric principles
  • Versatile for various defect locations

Complications

Flap necrosis is possible if pedicle blood supply is compromised. Standing cones may develop at flap margins. Pin-cushioning (excessive fullness) can occur if not managed carefully during closure.

Specific Applications: Rhombic Flap for Cheek Defects

The rhombic flap is particularly useful for defects on the cheek where tissue laxity is available. The flap design creates scars that align with facial expression lines, improving cosmetic outcomes. This technique is a workhorse for moderate-sized facial defects.

Post-Operative Management

After transposition flap repair, wound care focuses on protecting the flap and maintaining adequate blood supply. Pressure dressings may be used. Sutures are removed according to location-specific timelines (typically 7-14 days on the face).
