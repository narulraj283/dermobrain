Mohs & Skin Cancer Surgery

Curettage and Electrodesiccation (C&E) for Skin Lesions

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Understanding Curettage and Electrodesiccation

Curettage and electrodesiccation (C&E) is a destructive procedure combining mechanical removal (curettage) with electrical cauterization (electrodesiccation) to treat benign and selected malignant skin lesions. The technique is quick, cost-effective, and commonly used in office settings.

Technique Overview

After local anesthesia, a curette (a spoon-shaped surgical instrument) is used to scrape away the lesion along with superficial dermis. The resulting defect is then treated with electrocautery to coagulate blood vessels and destroy remaining tissue. This cycle may be repeated 2-3 times to ensure adequate treatment depth.

Appropriate Lesions for C&E

  • Small basal cell carcinomas (less than 1 cm)
  • Benign skin lesions (seborrheic keratosis, skin tags, xanthelasma)
  • Viral warts
  • Actinic keratosis
  • Selected squamous cell carcinomas in experienced hands

Advantages of C&E

The procedure is quick (5-10 minutes), requires minimal pain control, does not require sutures, and results in minimal scarring. Cost is low, and the procedure can be performed in most office settings with basic instrumentation.

Disadvantages and Limitations

The primary limitation of C&E is the inability to assess margins histopathologically. No specimen is submitted for pathological examination, so the adequacy of treatment cannot be verified. Re-treatment rates are higher than with excisional or Mohs surgery, particularly for skin cancers. The technique is not appropriate for thick or aggressive skin cancers.

Cure Rates

For small BCC, C&E achieves cure rates of approximately 90-95%, which is lower than excisional surgery or Mohs. For benign lesions, cure rates approach 95-98%, though recurrence may occur.

Healing and Wound Care

The wound from C&E heals by secondary intention over 3-4 weeks. Patients must keep the wound clean and protected. The resulting scar is typically minimal but may be slightly atrophic or hypopigmented, particularly on darker skin types.
