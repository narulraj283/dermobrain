Shave Removal Technique for Skin Lesions

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

What Is Shave Removal?

Shave removal (also called shave biopsy or shave excision) is a quick, office-based technique for removing superficial skin lesions. Using a local anesthetic and a specialized blade, the dermatologist removes the lesion flush with the surrounding skin surface.

How Shave Removal Works

After infiltration with local anesthesia, the skin is held taut. A blade or specialized shave tool is used to remove the lesion tangentially, creating a thin specimen containing the lesion and minimal underlying dermis. The technique is particularly useful for lesions that are elevated above the skin surface.

Appropriate Lesions for Shave Removal

Shave removal is appropriate for benign lesions (seborrheic keratosis, skin tags, some nevi), superficial malignancies, and suspicious lesions requiring histopathological examination. The technique is not recommended for lesions suspected to be thick melanomas or deeply invasive skin cancers.

Advantages of Shave Removal

  • Quick outpatient procedure requiring minimal time
  • Simple hemostasis with minimal scarring
  • No sutures needed in most cases
  • Excellent for facial lesions
  • Low cost and minimal patient morbidity

Disadvantages and Limitations

The main disadvantage of shave removal is the inability to assess deep margins in the submitted specimen. This is not a concern for superficial benign lesions but is a limitation for skin cancer treatment where deep margins are important. Residual lesion remains in deeper dermis in approximately 1-5% of cases, requiring re-treatment.

Hemostasis and Wound Care

After shave removal, hemostasis is achieved with electrocautery or application of topical hemostatic agents. The resulting wound heals by secondary intention, typically within 2-3 weeks. A small, often barely perceptible scar results from this technique.

Histopathological Examination

The shaved specimen is sent to pathology for examination. If a malignancy is identified, additional treatment such as excision may be needed to ensure adequate deep margin assessment and removal.
