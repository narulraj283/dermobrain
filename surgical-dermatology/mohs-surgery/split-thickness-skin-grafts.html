Split-Thickness Skin Grafts: When and Why

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Split-Thickness Skin Graft Overview

A split-thickness skin graft (STSG) contains epidermis and only a portion of dermis. The graft is thinner than full-thickness grafts, making it easier to apply and more likely to survive on marginal recipient beds. However, aesthetic outcome and long-term function are inferior to full-thickness grafts.

Graft Composition and Types

STSGs are classified as thin (0.005-0.010 inches), intermediate (0.010-0.015 inches), or thick (0.015-0.025 inches). Thicker grafts provide better function and appearance but are more difficult to apply. Thinner grafts are more likely to survive on compromised beds but provide inferior long-term outcomes.

Donor Site Considerations

Common donor sites for STSG include the anterior thigh, posterolateral arm, and buttocks. These areas have sufficient skin for grafting and can be harvested with adequate thickness. The donor site is painful and requires wound care; it heals by epithelialization from hair follicles and sweat gland ducts.

Technique

After creating the surgical defect and achieving hemostasis, a dermatome is used to harvest the appropriate thickness of skin from the donor site. The graft is placed on the recipient bed. Meshing (creating small cuts in the graft) may be performed to expand the graft and allow drainage of fluid. The graft is secured and immobilized.

Graft Expansion and Meshing

Meshing allows a smaller donor site graft to cover a larger area. A 1.5:1 meshed graft covers 1.5 times its original size. Meshing also prevents fluid accumulation under the graft but results in a grid pattern that may be cosmetically apparent.

Revascularization Timeline

STSG revascularization is faster than FTSG, typically occurring within 3-5 days. The thinner graft requires less oxygen and nutrients. Plasmatic imbibition (absorption of plasma) provides initial graft nourishment before vascular connection is established.

Appropriate Indications

  • Large defects requiring coverage
  • Defects with marginal vascularity
  • Areas where appearance is less critical (trunk, extremities)
  • Wounds with compromised blood supply

Donor Site Healing

STSG donor sites heal by re-epithelialization within 1-2 weeks. Donor site scars are typically minor, though cosmetic concerns may persist for months. Donor site pain can be significant and requires appropriate management.

Long-Term Outcomes

STSGs are prone to contracture (shrinkage) over time, which can limit function, particularly on the hands or joints. Hypertrophic scar formation is common. Despite these limitations, STSG remains an important technique for extensive wounds.
