Mohs & Skin Cancer Surgery

Mohs Surgery vs Standard Excision: Comparison and When to Choose

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Comparing the Techniques

Both Mohs micrographic surgery and standard excision are effective treatments for skin cancer. Understanding the differences helps patients and physicians choose the appropriate technique for each lesion.

Margin Assessment

Mohs Surgery

Mohs examines 100% of peripheral and deep margins in real-time. The surgeon can identify exact locations of any residual cancer and remove additional tissue only from affected areas. This results in complete margin control in nearly 100% of cases and minimizes the final defect size.

Standard Excision

Standard excision examines selected sections of the margin via conventional pathology. Approximately 5-10% of cases require re-excision for inadequate margins. The final defect size may be larger than necessary.

Cure Rates

Basal Cell Carcinoma

Mohs surgery: < 1% recurrence rate. Standard excision: 3-5% recurrence rate.

Squamous Cell Carcinoma

Mohs surgery (high-risk): 1-3% recurrence rate. Standard excision (high-risk): 5-15% recurrence rate.

Tissue Preservation

Mohs surgery's real-time margin assessment results in removal of only the necessary tissue. Larger margins are not needed because tumor extent is known precisely. This is particularly advantageous for lesions on the face and near critical structures.

Procedural Time and Cost

Standard excision is quicker (15-30 minutes) and less expensive ($500-1500). Mohs surgery typically takes 2-4 hours and costs more ($1500-3500), though the total cost is often comparable when accounting for potential re-excision.

Patient Convenience

Standard excision is a one-stage procedure completed quickly. Mohs requires multiple visits and waiting periods for tissue processing. Patients with limited time availability may prefer standard excision if appropriate.

Appropriate Indications for Mohs Surgery

  • Recurrent skin cancers
  • Skin cancers on the face, eyelids, ears, or lips
  • Skin cancers with ill-defined clinical borders
  • Large skin cancers
  • Skin cancers with aggressive histological subtypes (morpheaform BCC, high-risk SCC)
  • Skin cancers in immunosuppressed patients

Appropriate Indications for Standard Excision

  • Small, clearly demarcated skin cancers
  • Low-risk skin cancers
  • Skin cancers on the trunk or extremities
  • Lesions where Mohs is not available
  • Patients preferring a single-stage procedure
  • Cost-conscious patients

Location-Based Considerations

For facial skin cancers, particularly on cosmetically sensitive areas (nose, lips, eyelids, ears), Mohs surgery is generally preferred because tissue preservation and complete margin control are paramount. For trunk and extremity lesions, standard excision is often adequate.

Accessibility and Availability

Mohs surgery requires specialized training and equipment. If Mohs is not available in your area, standard excision performed by an experienced dermatologic surgeon is an excellent alternative.

Reconstruction Complexity

After Mohs or standard excision, reconstruction requirements depend on the final defect size and location. Smaller defects from Mohs surgery may require simpler reconstruction. Both techniques should be followed by appropriate reconstruction expertise.

Decision-Making Framework

The choice between techniques should be based on: tumor characteristics (size, location, type), patient factors (age, preferences, location), lesion risk factors, and surgeon expertise. A consultation with a dermatologist can help determine the best approach for your specific lesion.
