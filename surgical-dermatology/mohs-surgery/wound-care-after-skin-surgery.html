Wound Care After Skin Surgery: Best Practices

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Principles of Post-Surgical Wound Care

Proper wound care after skin surgery is critical for optimal healing and cosmetic outcomes. The goal is to maintain a clean, moist wound environment that promotes healing while protecting the wound from infection and trauma.

Dressing Types and Selection

Dressings serve multiple functions: protection from contamination, absorption of fluid, maintenance of moisture, and support of the healing environment. Common dressing types include gauze, antibiotic ointment, petroleum jelly, hydrocolloid dressings, and silicone-based products. Dressing selection depends on wound characteristics and location.

Daily Wound Care Regimen

Cleansing

The wound should be gently cleansed daily with mild soap and water or saline. Harsh scrubbing should be avoided. After cleansing, the wound is patted dry with clean, non-lint material.

Ointment Application

After cleansing, a thin layer of antibiotic ointment (such as bacitracin or triple antibiotic) or petroleum jelly is applied. This keeps the wound moist and prevents crust formation, which can impede healing and worsen scarring.

Dressing Application

A non-adherent dressing is placed over the ointment, followed by an outer layer of gauze if needed for protection. Dressings are changed daily or more frequently if they become saturated.

Wound Immobilization and Tension Reduction

Minimizing tension on the wound during healing improves scar appearance. Techniques to reduce tension include tape stripping across the wound, compression garments, and avoiding activities that stress the wound. Some surgeons use specialized taping (such as Steri-Strips) to support the wound after suture removal.

Activity Restrictions

Activity should be modified to prevent stress on the healing wound. Strenuous exercise, heavy lifting, and activities causing excessive sweating should be avoided. Light activity and normal daily tasks are typically permitted after a few days.

Sun Protection

Sun exposure should be avoided during the first several weeks to months after surgery. New scars are particularly susceptible to hyperpigmentation (darkening) with sun exposure. Sunscreen (SPF 30 or higher) should be applied once the wound is closed and intact.

Managing Wound Complications

Bleeding

Minor oozing is normal; apply gentle pressure with clean gauze. Contact your surgeon if bleeding persists or is excessive.

Infection Signs

Watch for increasing warmth, redness, swelling, pain, pus, or foul odor. These signs warrant immediate medical evaluation.

Dehiscence (Wound Opening)

If sutures break or the wound opens, contact your surgeon immediately. Do not attempt to close it yourself.

Suture Removal Timing

Suture removal timing depends on location: face (5-7 days), scalp (7-10 days), trunk (10-14 days), extremities (14-21 days). Premature removal risks wound reopening; delayed removal may result in suture marks.
