Mohs & Skin Cancer Surgery

Understanding Surgical Margins in Skin Cancer Treatment

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

What Are Surgical Margins?

Surgical margins refer to the edge of normal-appearing skin removed around a skin cancer during surgical excision. The goal of any cancer surgery is to remove the tumor with adequate margin of normal tissue while minimizing the size of the surgical defect.

Margin Standards for Different Skin Cancers

Basal Cell Carcinoma

Standard excision margins for BCC are typically 5 mm. This achieves complete removal in approximately 95% of cases. For recurrent BCC or morpheaform subtypes, margins of 5-10 mm may be appropriate. Mohs surgery offers superior margin control.

Squamous Cell Carcinoma

Standard margins for low-risk SCC are typically 4-5 mm. For high-risk lesions (size > 2 cm, depth > 4 mm, poorly differentiated), wider margins of 6-10 mm are recommended. Mohs surgery is preferred for many high-risk SCCs.

Melanoma

Melanoma margin recommendations depend on Breslow thickness: melanoma in situ (5 mm margin), melanoma < 1 mm (5-10 mm margin), melanoma 1-2 mm (10-20 mm margin), melanoma > 2 mm (15-20 mm margin or greater based on location). These are linear margins around the lesion.

Mohs Micrographic Surgery and Margins

Mohs surgery provides superior margin assessment by examining 100% of the peripheral and deep margins. This contrasts with conventional excision, where only selected sections of the margin are examined. Mohs surgery achieves complete margin control in the highest percentage of cases.

Margin Assessment Methods

Conventional Excision

In conventional excision, the specimen is sectioned vertically and selected sections are examined. If cancer is identified at the margin, re-excision is necessary. Approximately 5-10% of conventional excisions require re-excision for inadequate margins.

Mohs Micrographic Surgery

Mohs surgery examines 100% of peripheral margins and allows the surgeon to identify exact locations of residual cancer. This results in lower re-excision rates and smaller final defects.

Factors Influencing Margin Decisions

  • Tumor size and depth
  • Histological subtype and differentiation
  • Location (cosmetically sensitive areas may justify wider margins)
  • Patient age and immunosuppression status
  • Previous treatment or recurrence
  • Surgeon experience and technique

Re-excision for Positive Margins

If histopathological examination identifies cancer at the margin, re-excision is necessary to ensure complete removal. Re-excision rates vary by technique: approximately 5-10% for conventional excision and 1-3% for Mohs surgery (typically for areas where only one margin was positive).

Clinical Implications of Margin Status

Tumors removed with adequate margins have significantly lower recurrence rates. Patients with positive margins at diagnosis are at higher risk for recurrence and warrant closer follow-up surveillance. Some patients with positive margins may benefit from adjuvant radiation therapy.
