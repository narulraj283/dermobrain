Mohs & Skin Cancer Surgery

Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy in Dermatologic Surgery

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy Overview

Sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB) is a procedure to assess whether melanoma or other skin cancers have spread to regional lymph nodes. The procedure involves injecting tracer materials to identify the first lymph node(s) to which tumor cells are most likely to drain, then removing that node for histopathological examination.

Indications for SLNB

SLNB is most commonly indicated for melanoma with Breslow thickness greater than 0.75-1.0 mm, or thinner melanomas with other risk factors such as ulceration, high mitotic rate, or clark level IV-V invasion. The procedure is occasionally used for high-risk SCC with deep invasion or perineural invasion.

Technical Procedure

Before SLNB, the primary melanoma is marked and prepped. A radioactive tracer (technetium-99m) and blue dye are injected around the primary lesion. Lymphatic drainage is tracked to identify the sentinel lymph node(s). A gamma counter localizes the radioactive tracer, and a small incision is made over the sentinel node(s). The blue-stained, radioactive lymph node is removed and sent for histopathological examination.

Pathological Examination of Sentinel Nodes

The sentinel lymph node is sectioned and examined for the presence of metastatic melanoma. If melanoma is identified, additional lymph nodes in that basin may require removal through lymphadenectomy. If no melanoma is identified, the patient is staged as node-negative.

Prognostic Significance

The presence of melanoma in the sentinel lymph node (a positive SLNB) significantly worsens prognosis and may trigger systemic therapy recommendations. Patients with negative SLNB have improved prognosis but still require ongoing surveillance for distant metastasis.

Timing of SLNB

SLNB is typically performed as a separate procedure from the wide excision of the primary melanoma, either at the same operative session or within 1-2 months. Recent data suggests performing SLNB during or soon after primary site excision may offer advantages.

Complications

Complications of SLNB are uncommon and include lymphedema (rare after sentinel node biopsy), seroma formation, infection, and nerve injury. Allergic reactions to blue dye are possible but rare.
