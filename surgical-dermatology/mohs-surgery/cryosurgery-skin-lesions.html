Cryosurgery for Skin Lesions: Freezing Technique

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Cryosurgery Fundamentals

Cryosurgery uses extreme cold (liquid nitrogen at -196°C) to destroy tissue through freeze-thaw cycles that cause cellular injury and death. The technique is simple, painless, and widely used for benign and selected premalignant skin lesions.

Cryosurgery Technique

Liquid nitrogen is applied to the lesion using a spray applicator or cotton-tipped applicator. The tissue freezes, and the freeze cycle continues for 5-30 seconds depending on lesion type and size. A thaw period follows, during which tissue thaws naturally. A second freeze-thaw cycle is often performed to increase efficacy.

Indications for Cryosurgery

  • Actinic keratosis
  • Viral warts
  • Benign skin lesions (seborrheic keratosis, angioma)
  • Selected cases of Bowen's disease (cutaneous SCC in situ)
  • Lentigines

Mechanism of Tissue Destruction

Freezing causes intracellular ice crystal formation and cellular dehydration, leading to cell death. The freeze-thaw cycle is more destructive than freezing alone. The destroyed tissue sloughs over 1-2 weeks as new epithelium regenerates beneath.

Advantages of Cryosurgery

The procedure is quick, requires no anesthesia (though may be uncomfortable), produces no bleeding, requires no sutures, and is very cost-effective. Multiple lesions can be treated in one visit. Healing results in minimal scarring.

Disadvantages and Limitations

Cryosurgery cannot assess margins histopathologically. Efficacy depends on operator technique and lesion characteristics. Hypopigmentation is common, particularly in darker skin types. The technique is not appropriate for skin cancers where margin assessment is critical. Re-treatment rates are higher than with other techniques.

Cure Rates and Recurrence

For actinic keratosis, single-session cure rates are 75-90%, with higher rates for thicker lesions and multiple freeze-thaw cycles. For Bowen's disease, cure rates are approximately 85-90%. Higher recurrence rates occur if the lesion recurs, particularly for viral warts.

Post-Treatment Care

After cryosurgery, the area may be red, swollen, and blistered. Care includes keeping the wound clean and protected. The blister or scab sloughs in 1-2 weeks. Hypopigmentation may persist for months to years.
