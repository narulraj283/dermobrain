Mohs & Skin Cancer Surgery

Excisional Surgery for Skin Lesions: Techniques and Indications

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview of Excisional Surgery

Excisional surgery is the standard surgical technique for removing skin lesions, including skin cancers, benign nevi, and other cutaneous growths. Unlike Mohs micrographic surgery, excisional surgery removes the lesion in a single stage with a predetermined margin of normal skin.

Indications for Excisional Surgery

Excisional surgery is appropriate for benign skin lesions (nevi, lipomas, cysts), low-risk skin cancers, suspicious lesions requiring histopathological examination, and lesions in areas where Mohs surgery is not available or necessary. The technique is straightforward and cost-effective.

Technique and Margins

For skin cancer excision, standard margins are typically 5 mm for BCC and 4-5 mm for low-risk SCC, though margins may be modified based on lesion characteristics. The incision is made with a scalpel to remove the lesion and surrounding margin of normal skin in an elliptical shape. The ellipse is oriented along tension lines (Langer's lines) of the skin to optimize healing and minimize visible scarring.

Closure Methods

After excision, closure methods depend on the defect size and location. Small defects are closed with simple sutures (primary closure). Larger defects may require rotation flaps, advancement flaps, or skin grafts. Some wounds on non-facial areas may be allowed to heal by secondary intention.

Specimen Processing

After excision, the specimen is sent to a pathology laboratory for processing and histopathological examination. The pathologist examines the margins to determine if all tumor has been removed. If margins are positive (tumor at the edge), re-excision may be necessary.

Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantages of excisional surgery include simplicity, lower cost, quicker completion, and reliability for most skin cancers. Disadvantages include higher re-excision rates for incomplete removal compared to Mohs surgery, particularly for lesions with ill-defined borders or aggressive subtypes.

Comparison to Mohs Surgery

While excisional surgery achieves excellent cure rates for most skin cancers (95%+), Mohs surgery offers superior cure rates (99%+) with better tissue preservation. The choice between techniques depends on lesion characteristics, patient factors, and surgeon expertise.
