Mohs & Skin Cancer Surgery

Mohs Surgery for Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

BCC and Mohs Surgery

Basal cell carcinoma is the most common type of skin cancer, representing approximately 80% of all skin cancers. While most BCCs are curable with various treatments, Mohs micrographic surgery offers the highest cure rate and best cosmetic outcomes, particularly for high-risk lesions.

Why Mohs for BCC?

Although standard excision cures approximately 95% of BCCs, Mohs surgery achieves cure rates exceeding 99%. This superior efficacy is particularly important for recurrent BCCs, lesions in cosmetically sensitive areas, large tumors, and BCCs with aggressive histological subtypes such as morpheaform or infiltrative variants.

Clinical Characteristics of BCC

Basal cell carcinoma typically presents as a pearly nodule with telangectasia (dilated blood vessels), sometimes with central ulceration. Some BCCs appear as scaly plaques or non-healing wounds. The lesion may bleed with minor trauma and does not heal despite ointments or topical treatments.

BCC Histological Subtypes

  • Nodular BCC: most common, generally has the best prognosis
  • Superficial BCC: limited to superficial dermis, favorable prognosis
  • Morpheaform/infiltrative BCC: aggressive growth pattern, often requires Mohs surgery
  • Basosquamous: mixed features, higher recurrence rates

Mohs Outcomes for BCC

Studies consistently demonstrate that Mohs surgery provides superior outcomes for BCC, particularly for tumors larger than 2 cm, recurrent lesions, and tumors with ill-defined clinical borders. The combination of complete margin control and tissue conservation makes Mohs the gold standard for high-risk BCCs. Five-year recurrence rates following Mohs surgery are typically less than 1%, compared to 3-5% with standard excision.

Reconstruction Considerations

After Mohs removal of BCC, the size and location of the defect determine reconstruction options. Small defects may be closed with simple sutures, while larger defects may require flaps or grafts to achieve optimal functional and cosmetic results.
