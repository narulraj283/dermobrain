Full-Thickness Skin Grafts: Indications and Technique

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Understanding Full-Thickness Skin Grafts

A full-thickness skin graft (FTSG) contains epidermis and the entire thickness of dermis. Unlike split-thickness grafts, FTSGs include dermal appendages (sweat glands, hair follicles) and provide superior functional and cosmetic outcomes. However, they require excellent vascularization and are more difficult to achieve than split-thickness grafts.

Graft Composition

Full-thickness grafts include all layers necessary for complete skin function. The inclusion of dermis provides better color match, texture, and long-term aesthetic outcome. The graft contains a network of collagen fibers that integrate with the recipient bed, providing structural stability.

Donor Site Selection

Common donor sites for FTSG include the postauricular area (behind the ear), upper eyelid, supraclavicular area, and groin. These areas have adequate excess skin and skin characteristics similar to common recipient sites on the face. Donor site closure may require suturing or leave a small linear scar.

Technique

After the surgical defect is created and hemostasis achieved, the graft is harvested from the donor site with adequate dermis. The graft is placed on the recipient bed and secured with sutures or fibrin glue. The graft must have complete contact with the recipient bed to ensure revascularization.

Graft Survival and Revascularization

FTSG survival depends on adequate blood supply in the recipient bed. Revascularization begins within hours of grafting. The graft can survive ischemia for several hours, typically up to 6-12 hours depending on conditions. Revascularization is typically complete within 7-10 days.

Indications for FTSG

  • Large defects on the face where flaps are not feasible
  • Defects with poor vascularity but still adequate for grafting
  • Areas requiring pigmentation match (lower eyelid, lips)
  • Reconstruction after extensive Mohs surgery
  • Defects where reduced wound contracture is desired

Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantages include excellent cosmetic outcome, minimal wound contracture, and good long-term function. Disadvantages include the need for adequate recipient bed vascularity, higher graft loss rates compared to split-thickness grafts, and the requirement for meticulous hemostasis and immobilization.

Post-Operative Care

The grafted area must be immobilized with bulky dressings for 5-7 days to prevent shearing forces. The graft should not be disturbed during this critical period. After dressing removal, gentle care continues for several more days. Long-term sun protection is essential.
