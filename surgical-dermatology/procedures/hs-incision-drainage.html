HS Incision and Drainage: Acute Abscess Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Acute Abscess Formation in Hidradenitis Suppurativa

Acute abscess formation is a common manifestation of hidradenitis suppurativa, causing significant pain and functional impairment. Incision and drainage provides rapid symptom relief and infection control. However, incision and drainage alone does not address the underlying sinus tract disease and recurrence is common at the same site.

Patient Assessment and Diagnosis

Clinical examination identifies the location and extent of abscess formation. Ultrasound or MRI may delineate abscess size and identify associated sinus tracts. Assessment of systemic symptoms helps guide need for antibiotics and imaging. Culture of drainage material identifies causative organisms and guides antibiotic selection.

Incision and Drainage Technique

Preparation and Anesthesia

The site is cleaned and prepared with antiseptic solution. Local anesthesia with lidocaine and epinephrine is infiltrated around the abscess. For large abscesses or sensitive locations, conscious sedation or general anesthesia may be appropriate.

Incision Placement

The incision is made at the point of maximal fluctuance or at a natural site that follows anatomical planes. For axillary abscesses, the incision follows the direction of hair growth or natural skin creases. The incision should be large enough to allow adequate drainage and wound exploration.

Drainage and Wound Exploration

Once the abscess is entered, pus is expressed and sent for culture. The cavity is explored with a probe or finger to identify loculations and to ensure complete drainage. Blunt finger dissection breaks down loculations and ensures thorough drainage. The wall is not curetted or stripped, as this increases morbidity without improving outcomes.

Wound Management

The wound is left open to allow continued drainage and prevent re-accumulation. A sterile dressing is applied. Some practitioners place a drain to facilitate continued drainage, while others allow healing by secondary intention. Frequent dressing changes keep the wound clean and allow monitoring of drainage characteristics.

Post-operative Care

Dressing changes are performed daily or as needed depending on drainage volume. The wound is cleansed with sterile saline. Antibiotic ointment is applied and a fresh dressing placed. As drainage decreases, dressing frequency can be reduced. Patients should keep the area clean and dry between dressing changes.

Antibiotic Therapy

Culture results guide antibiotic selection. While awaiting culture, empiric antibiotics covering Staphylococcus and Streptococcus are typically started. Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole or doxycycline are common choices. Antibiotic duration is typically 7-10 days, though longer courses may be needed for extensive disease.

Recurrence and Definitive Treatment

Recurrence at the same site occurs in 40-60% of cases when incision and drainage is the only treatment. After resolution of acute symptoms, definitive surgical treatment such as deroofing or wide excision should be considered to prevent recurrence. Medical optimization with biologics or other agents also reduces recurrence risk.
