Nose Reconstruction After Mohs Micrographic Surgery

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Nasal Reconstruction: Complexity and Considerations

Nose reconstruction after Mohs micrographic surgery represents one of the most challenging reconstructive problems in dermatologic surgery. The nose has complex three-dimensional anatomy, prominent aesthetic importance, and functional requirements for breathing. Successful reconstruction requires careful planning and mastery of multiple surgical techniques.

Defect Assessment and Classification

Anatomical Zones

The nose is divided into aesthetic subunits: nasal dorsum, sidewalls, tip, columella, ala, and soft triangle. Defects are assessed by size, location within subunits, depth, and involvement of supporting structures (cartilage, bone). Subunit reconstruction principles guide approach—restoration within subunit lines produces superior aesthetic results.

Depth Classification

Superficial defects involve skin only. Intermediate defects involve subcutaneous tissue and may require cartilage support. Full-thickness defects involve mucosa or deeper structures and require complex reconstruction.

Reconstruction Techniques

Primary Closure and Direct Repair

Small defects on the nasal dorsum or sidewall can be closed primarily with careful attention to contour and tension distribution. The nose's curved surface requires design of closure lines that follow natural skin tension lines and aesthetic boundaries.

Skin Grafting

Full-thickness skin grafts are useful for moderate-sized defects. Nasal skin has characteristic color and texture; grafts should be harvested from similar regions—post-auricular, preauricular, or supraclavicular skin provides good color and texture match. Grafts are secured with tie-over bolsters for 7-10 days.

Local Flaps

Rotation, advancement, and transposition flaps using surrounding nasal and facial skin are powerful tools for nasal reconstruction. The nasal dorsum and sidewalls are particularly well-suited to flap reconstruction due to available adjacent tissue.

Nasolabial Flap

The nasolabial flap, based on branches of the superior labial artery, can reconstruct the nasal ala and lower nose defects. The flap is elevated from the cheek and upper lip region and transposed into the nasal defect. This flap provides excellent color and texture match for lower nasal reconstruction.

Forehead Flap

Large nasal defects, particularly involving the nasal tip, often require forehead flap reconstruction. The paramedian forehead flap, based on the supratrochlear artery, provides a robust blood supply and excellent tissue color match. The flap is elevated in a paramedian location, transposed into the nasal defect, and the pedicle is divided at 3 weeks allowing flap inset. This staged approach requires 2-3 procedures but achieves superior aesthetic results.

Cartilage Grafting

Defects involving loss of nasal cartilage or loss of nasal structure require cartilage grafting to restore contour and maintain airway patency. Cartilage is typically harvested from septal cartilage, ear cartilage, or rib cartilage depending on quantity and strength needed. The graft is sculpted to restore nasal contour and sutured securely.

Specific Site Considerations

Alar Reconstruction

Alar defects pose particular functional and aesthetic challenges. Loss of alar support can cause airway obstruction and distortion. Reconstruction requires cartilage grafting with flap coverage to restore both structure and function.

Columellar Reconstruction

The columella provides structural support and defines the nasal dorsum profile. Columellar defects require cartilage-containing reconstruction to restore proper support and aesthetic alignment.

Timing and Staging

Immediate reconstruction at the time of Mohs completion is often preferable when defect is well-defined. However, staged reconstruction may be necessary for very large defects or when flaps require pedicle division (as with forehead flap).

Post-operative Management

Nasal splinting for 1 week protects the reconstruction and maintains contour. Patients should avoid nasal manipulation and strenuous activity. Follow-up visits monitor for complications and assess healing. Secondary refinements may be necessary as edema resolves and scars mature.

Outcomes and Complications

Successful nasal reconstruction restores both aesthetic appearance and nasal function. Common complications include graft failure, flap necrosis, and secondary deformity. However, the rich blood supply of the nasal region generally supports good healing when proper technique is employed.
