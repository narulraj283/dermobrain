Local Anesthesia in Skin Surgery: Agents and Techniques

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Local Anesthetics: Overview and Selection

Local anesthetics block nerve conduction by inhibiting sodium channel activity in nerve fibers, preventing depolarization and action potential propagation. Different local anesthetic agents have varying potency, onset time, and duration of action. Appropriate selection and technique ensure patient comfort and optimal surgical conditions.

Common Local Anesthetic Agents

Lidocaine

Lidocaine is the most commonly used local anesthetic in dermatologic surgery. It has rapid onset (3-5 minutes), moderate duration of action (30-60 minutes), and good tissue penetration. Lidocaine is available as 1% or 2% solution. The maximum safe dose is 4.5 mg/kg without epinephrine and 7 mg/kg with epinephrine. Onset is too slow for topical application alone but excellent for infiltration and nerve blocks.

Epinephrine Addition

Adding epinephrine (adrenaline) to lidocaine causes vasoconstriction, reducing bleeding and extending duration of action. Epinephrine should not be used in areas with end-arterial blood supply (fingers, toes, ears, nose tip) due to risk of tissue necrosis from vasoconstriction. Typical concentration is 1:100,000 or 1:200,000.

Prilocaine

Prilocaine is similar to lidocaine but has slightly longer duration and more rapid onset. It carries a theoretical risk of methemoglobinemia at high doses but this is rarely clinically significant. Maximum dose is 6 mg/kg without epinephrine and 9 mg/kg with epinephrine.

Bupivacaine

Bupivacaine has slower onset (10-15 minutes) but much longer duration (4-8 hours). It provides prolonged post-operative anesthesia, reducing patient discomfort. However, slower onset means waiting time before surgery and cost is higher. Maximum dose is 2.25 mg/kg without epinephrine and 3 mg/kg with epinephrine.

Procaine

Procaine is rapid-acting but short-acting (30-45 minutes). It carries higher allergy risk than other agents and is rarely used in modern dermatologic surgery.

Topical Anesthetics

EMLA Cream

EMLA (Eutectic Mixture of Local Anesthetics) combines lidocaine and prilocaine in an emulsion. Applied under occlusion for 30-60 minutes, EMLA provides adequate topical anesthesia for superficial procedures. Onset is slower than infiltration but no needle sticks are required. Maximum dose is 10g for areas larger than 100cm².

Lidocaine Spray and Solutions

Topical lidocaine solutions (4%) or sprays provide rapid surface anesthesia useful for superficial procedures. However, penetration is limited without occlusion or chemical enhancers. Sprays are useful for cryotherapy or electrosurgery of small lesions.

Infiltration Techniques

Direct Infiltration

Direct infiltration of local anesthetic into or around the surgical area is the standard technique. The anesthetic should be infiltrated intradermally and subcutaneously to block pain fibers in the area. Slow injection with a small-gauge needle (27-30G) minimizes patient discomfort. The injection should be placed where it will provide block of sensory nerves serving the area.

Buffering Anesthetic

Adding sodium bicarbonate to local anesthetic increases pH, reducing acidity and discomfort on injection. Buffering also accelerates onset by 50-80% by allowing more anesthetic to exist in the non-ionized form. Typical ratio is 1mL of 8.4% sodium bicarbonate per 10mL of 1% lidocaine.

Warming Anesthetic

Warming local anesthetic to body temperature reduces injection discomfort. Anesthetic solutions can be warmed to 37°C before injection.

Nerve Block Techniques

Digital Block

Digital block provides anesthesia to the entire finger or toe by blocking the two digital nerves on either side of the digit. The anesthetic is injected on either side of the digit at the base of the digit or just proximal to it. Complete anesthesia is achieved with less total anesthetic volume than infiltration.

Supraorbital and Infraorbital Blocks

These blocks anesthetize the forehead and upper and lower eyelid regions respectively. Anesthetic is injected at the supraorbital or infraorbital foramen, blocking the branches of the trigeminal nerve (CN V). These techniques reduce anesthetic volume needed for significant facial areas.

Greater Occipital Nerve Block

This block anesthetizes the posterior scalp by injecting anesthetic around the greater occipital nerve as it emerges from between the trapezius and sternocleidomastoid muscles.

Safety Considerations

Dose Limits

Exceeding maximum recommended doses of local anesthetics risks systemic toxicity including cardiac arrhythmias, seizures, and cardiovascular collapse. Doses should be carefully calculated based on patient weight and the concentration of anesthetic used. Addition of epinephrine significantly increases maximum safe dose.

Allergy Considerations

True allergy to amide local anesthetics (lidocaine, prilocaine, bupivacaine) is rare but possible. Patients with documented allergy can usually use ester agents (procaine, benzocaine) as cross-reactivity is uncommon. If significant allergy history exists, testing with diluted anesthetic or non-anesthetic alternatives should be considered.

Epinephrine Contraindications

Epinephrine should not be used in areas of end-arterial blood supply. Additionally, epinephrine carries risk of cardiac effects in patients with significant cardiac disease, uncontrolled hypertension, or those taking certain medications (TCAs, some decongestants). Plain lidocaine without epinephrine should be used in these patients.

Post-operative Anesthesia Management

Extended anesthesia with bupivacaine provides post-operative pain relief, reducing patient discomfort. This can reduce or eliminate need for post-operative pain medications. Patients should be advised that the area will remain numb for several hours and they should avoid biting or traumatizing numb lips or mouth.
