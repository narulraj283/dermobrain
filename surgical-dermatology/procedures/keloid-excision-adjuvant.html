Surgical Procedures & Techniques

Keloid Excision with Adjuvant Therapy

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Keloids: Pathophysiology and Treatment Challenge

Keloids are abnormal scars that extend beyond the original injury boundaries, resulting from aberrant collagen deposition and excessive inflammatory response. Unlike hypertrophic scars which remain within original injury margins, keloids continue growing indefinitely without treatment. Surgical excision alone has recurrence rates of 40-50%, necessitating adjuvant therapies to improve outcomes.

Risk Factors and Patient Assessment

Higher risk for keloid formation exists in darker-skinned individuals, those with family history, and those with injuries in predisposed areas (shoulders, chest, earlobes). Keloid location affects treatment strategy—some sites have higher recurrence rates. Pre-operative photography and scar size documentation guide treatment planning.

Surgical Excision Technique

Primary Excision

The keloid is excised with margins that remain within the original scar borders or planned surgical site. Margins should not extend beyond necessary tissue. The wound is closed with intradermal sutures and skin closure, with meticulous attention to tension distribution. Excessive tension on closure must be avoided as high tension promotes keloid recurrence.

Wound Closure Methods

Intradermal closure with absorbable sutures reduces tension on the skin surface. Skin closure is typically performed with fine non-absorbable sutures or tissue adhesive. Sutures are removed at 3-4 weeks. Some surgeons prefer delayed closure or skin grafting to reduce tension, particularly for large keloids.

Adjuvant Therapies to Reduce Recurrence

Intralesional Corticosteroids

Intralesional corticosteroid injection immediately after excision or at the first post-operative visit reduces keloid recurrence. Triamcinolone 40 mg/mL is injected directly into the healing scar tissue at 4-6 week intervals. This disrupts collagen deposition and inflammatory response. Multiple injections are often necessary over 6-12 months.

Pressure Garments

Continuous pressure garment application over the healing scar provides mechanical suppression of collagen synthesis. Garments are worn for 12-24 hours daily for 3-6 months. While evidence for efficacy is moderate, they are non-invasive and commonly recommended.

Silicone Gel Sheeting

Silicone gel sheets applied to the scar provide occlusion, hydration, and potential modification of collagen deposition. Sheets are worn for 12 hours daily for several months. This approach is well-tolerated and safe but evidence for efficacy in keloid prevention is mixed.

Radiation Therapy

Post-operative radiation (typically 12-15 Gy in 2-3 fractions) significantly reduces keloid recurrence, with some studies reporting recurrence rates less than 10%. However, radiation carries long-term risks and is reserved for severe keloids or recurrent cases. The timing and dosing of radiation are critical—treatment is typically delivered within days after excision.

Laser Therapy

Pulsed dye laser or other lasers can be used post-operatively to reduce redness and flatten remaining scar tissue. Laser works synergistically with other adjuvant therapies. Multiple treatments spaced 4-6 weeks apart are typically necessary.

Combination Approach

Most effective outcomes result from combination therapy: surgical excision followed by intralesional corticosteroids, pressure therapy, and sometimes radiation for aggressive or recurrent keloids. This multimodal approach reduces recurrence rates significantly compared to surgery alone.

Outcomes and Realistic Expectations

With appropriate adjuvant therapy, recurrence rates can be reduced to 20-30%, compared to 40-50% with surgery alone. However, complete prevention is not always achievable. Patients should be counseled about the chronic nature of keloid disease and the need for long-term follow-up and additional treatment if recurrence occurs.
