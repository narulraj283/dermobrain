Scar Revision Surgery: Techniques and Outcomes

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Scar Revision: Goals and Principles

Scar revision surgery aims to improve the appearance of scars through various surgical and non-surgical techniques. The goals include improving color, texture, depth, width, and directional alignment. Effective scar revision requires understanding the scar pathophysiology and selecting appropriate techniques for each individual scar.

Scar Assessment

Scar Classification

Scars are classified as normotrophic (flat, in-plane with skin), hypertrophic (raised and thick), atrophic (depressed below skin surface), or keloid (extending beyond original injury boundaries). Treatment approach depends on scar type and depth.

Timing Considerations

Most scars mature over 12-18 months as collagen remodeling progresses. Revision should generally be deferred until maturation is complete. Early revision within 3-6 months of injury may be appropriate for poorly aligned scars or those that are severely cosmetically unacceptable.

Revision Techniques

Surgical Excision and Re-closure

For wide or poorly oriented scars, excision followed by re-closure along a more favorable tension line improves appearance. The scar is excised with minimal margins, and the wound is closed meticulously with attention to wound edge eversion and tension distribution. Multiple smaller scars are preferable to one large scar in many locations.

Z-plasty

Z-plasty is a useful technique for scars that restrict motion or are oriented contrary to skin tension lines. The scar is excised in the shape of a Z, with the central portion aligned with the scar and the lateral limbs at 60-degree angles. Transposition of the flaps redirects the scar orientation, reducing tension and improving appearance. Multiple Z-plasties can be used for longer scars.

W-plasty and Serial Revisions

W-plasty and similar techniques create multiple small irregular portions along the scar line rather than a single straight line. This breaks up the visual line of the scar, making it less noticeable. Multiple staged procedures may be necessary to achieve optimal results.

Subcutaneous Tissue Elevation

For depressed atrophic scars, elevation of the skin through subcutaneous scoring or suspension techniques can improve depression. Absorbable or non-absorbable sutures placed through the scar dermis and attached to deeper tissues or skin provide elevation. This technique is useful for rolling scars and minor atrophic depressions.

Adjunctive and Non-surgical Treatments

Laser Resurfacing

Fractional and ablative lasers improve scar appearance by inducing controlled collagen remodeling and removing surface irregularities. Multiple treatments are often necessary. Risks include post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and permanent hypopigmentation.

Dermal Fillers

Temporary fillers such as hyaluronic acid can elevate depressed scars, improving appearance temporarily. Repeated treatments are necessary as fillers resorb over months. This approach is useful for patients unwilling to undergo surgery or as adjunctive treatment.

Microneedling

Microneedling induces collagen production through controlled microtrauma, improving scar appearance over time. Multiple treatments are needed, typically spaced 4-6 weeks apart. Results are modest but accumulate over multiple sessions.

Expected Outcomes

Scar revision achieves improvement rather than complete elimination. Most patients experience significant improvement in scar appearance, with results most apparent at 3-6 months as collagen remodeling occurs. Complete scar elimination is not achievable, as the mark of injury always remains to some degree.
