Surgical Procedures & Techniques

Matricectomy: Chemical and Thermal Matrix Ablation

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Matricectomy: Definition and Surgical Goal

Matricectomy is the surgical ablation or removal of the nail matrix, the germinal tissue responsible for nail production. This procedure is performed in conjunction with nail plate avulsion when permanent prevention of nail regrowth is desired. The nail matrix consists of dorsal and ventral portions producing the hard nail plate and softer nail bed layers respectively.

Indications for Matricectomy

  • Ingrown toenails with chronic inflammation unresponsive to conservative care
  • Prevention of nail regrowth after onychomycosis treatment
  • Prevention of nail regrowth after nail bed tumor removal
  • Nail dystrophy from chronic paronychia with matrix involvement
  • Recurrent nail pathology affecting the nail unit

Chemical Matricectomy

Phenol Application

Phenol 88% is the most commonly used chemical agent for matrix ablation. After nail plate avulsion under tourniquet control, phenol is applied on gauze pads directly to the matrix area for 1-3 minutes depending on the size of area being treated. The contact time must be sufficient to achieve complete matrix destruction without being excessively prolonged.

Application Technique

Precise application is critical to avoid phenol contact with non-target tissues. Gauze soaked in 88% phenol is placed directly over the matrix area, and the surrounding nail bed and nail fold are protected with mineral oil or vaseline to prevent unintended chemical burn. After the desired contact time, the phenol is thoroughly irrigated away with isopropyl alcohol followed by copious saline irrigation.

Advantages and Limitations

Phenol is inexpensive and effective, with success rates exceeding 95% in most series. However, potential systemic toxicity exists if absorbed in large quantities, though this is rare with appropriate localized application. Residual chemical sensation may occur if irrigation is incomplete.

Thermal Matricectomy

Electrocautery

Electrocautery can be used for matrix ablation, providing controlled thermal destruction. After nail plate removal, the electrocautery device is applied directly to the nail matrix tissue with careful attention to depth and extent of destruction. Multiple passes may be necessary to ensure complete matrix elimination.

CO2 Laser Matricectomy

CO2 laser provides precise ablation of matrix tissue with minimal collateral thermal damage. The laser is directed at the matrix area under tourniquet control with magnification. Multiple passes may be required for complete destruction. This method provides excellent visibility and controllable depth of ablation.

Comparison of Ablation Methods

Success Rates

All three methods demonstrate high success rates (>95%) when properly applied. Phenol is most commonly used and well-studied. Electrocautery is readily available in most surgical settings. Laser offers enhanced precision but requires specialized equipment.

Cost Considerations

Phenol is the least expensive option. Electrocautery requires standard surgical equipment most facilities possess. Laser matricectomy is more costly but may reduce recurrence rates in high-risk cases.

Post-operative Care

Sutures are removed at 2-3 weeks. The nail bed epithelializes over 3-4 weeks. Patients should keep the area clean and dry. Antibiotic ointment is applied for comfort and infection prevention.

Complications and Outcomes

Incomplete ablation leads to nail regrowth, sometimes in abnormal patterns. Nail bed scarring may occur, potentially affecting appearance. Permanent loss of nail in the affected area is the expected outcome. Most patients are satisfied with the resolution of symptoms.
