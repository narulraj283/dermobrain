Electrosurgery in Dermatology: Techniques and Applications

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Electrosurgery: Physics and Mechanism

Electrosurgery uses electrical current to cut, coagulate, or ablate tissue. The electrical current creates thermal energy within the tissue, causing cells to heat and either vaporize (cutting mode) or denature proteins (coagulation mode). Electrosurgery is versatile, cost-effective, and widely available, making it a staple of dermatologic surgery.

Types of Electrosurgical Current

Monopolar Current

Monopolar current flows from the electrosurgical unit through the patient via an active electrode (handpiece) to a dispersive electrode (ground pad). The current passes through the body between these two electrodes. Monopolar current is effective but requires a large dispersive electrode to prevent burns under the pad. This mode is primarily used for cutting (unmodulated high-frequency current) and coagulation (modulated current).

Bipolar Current

Bipolar current flows between two closely spaced electrodes on the handpiece, limiting current flow to the immediate tissue between the electrodes. Bipolar electrosurgery eliminates the need for a dispersive electrode and reduces risk of unwanted current flow through the body. Bipolar systems are safer for use on digits and sensitive areas.

Cutting vs. Coagulation

Cutting Mode

Unmodulated high-frequency current causes rapid tissue vaporization and separation. The active electrode does not contact tissue—an arc bridges the gap, creating the cutting effect. This provides rapid tissue division with hemostasis as a secondary benefit. True cutting requires careful technique to avoid collateral thermal damage.

Coagulation Mode

Modulated (intermittent) current causes tissue protein denaturation without vaporization. The electrode contacts tissue directly, heating it to coagulation temperature. This mode is excellent for hemostasis and is commonly used for lesion removal where vaporization of the entire lesion is desired.

Blend Mode

Some electrosurgical units offer a blend mode combining cutting and coagulation current. This provides simultaneous cutting with hemostasis but with more collateral thermal damage than pure cutting mode. Blend mode is useful when cutting larger vessels that would bleed excessively in pure cutting mode.

Clinical Applications

Wart Removal

Electrocautery combined with curettage effectively removes warts. The wart is first debrided with a dermal curette, removing the hyperkeratotic surface. Electrocautery then ablates remaining wart tissue and any remaining hyperkeratotic material.

Skin Tag Removal

Small skin tags can be removed by grasping the base with forceps and applying electrocautery to cauterize the base. The tag separates within days or is removed with minimal bleeding. Larger tags require excision combined with cautery for hemostasis.

Lesion Ablation

Coagulation mode electrosurgery can ablate various benign skin lesions. The electrode tip contacts the lesion and tissue heating causes vaporization and ablation. Multiple passes may be necessary for complete lesion removal. The surrounding tissue is heated somewhat but generally heals well.

Hemostasis During Excision

Electrocautery provides excellent hemostasis during surgical excision. Small bleeders are touched with the electrode tip for a fraction of a second, cauterizing the vessel. This reduces operative bleeding and improves visibility.

Safety Considerations

Dispersive Electrode Placement

Proper placement of the dispersive electrode is critical for monopolar electrosurgery. The electrode should be placed on a large muscular surface area away from bony prominences, metal implants, and areas of poor blood supply. Inadequate pad contact can cause burns under the pad. Bipolar systems eliminate this risk.

Patient Selection

Patients with pacemakers or other electrical implants should not undergo monopolar electrosurgery, as the current may interfere with device function. Bipolar systems may be acceptable depending on the implant type—consultation with cardiologists is advised.

Smoke Evacuation

Electrosurgery creates a smoke plume containing vaporized tissue. Proper smoke evacuation reduces operator inhalation exposure and improves visibility.

Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantages include cost-effectiveness, availability, and versatility for multiple applications. Hemostasis is excellent. Disadvantages include thermal collateral damage potentially creating larger scars than laser surgery, inability to obtain histopathology of vaporized tissue, and patient discomfort from the burning smell and smoke. Care must be taken to control power settings to minimize collateral damage.
