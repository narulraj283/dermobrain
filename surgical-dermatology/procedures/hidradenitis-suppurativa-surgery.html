Hidradenitis Suppurativa Surgery: Overview and Approach

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Hidradenitis Suppurativa: Disease Understanding

Hidradenitis suppurativa (HS) is a chronic, recurrent inflammatory condition affecting apocrine gland-bearing areas (axillae, groin, inframammary regions). The condition causes suppurative nodules, chronic draining sinus tracts, and scars. Medical management with retinoids, antibiotics, and biological agents provides initial treatment, but surgical intervention is often necessary for disease control and symptom relief.

Indications for Surgery

  • Recurrent or uncontrolled disease despite medical therapy
  • Chronic drainage with significant functional or quality-of-life impairment
  • Extensive sinus tract formation
  • Significant scarring and contracture
  • Individual lesion management (incision and drainage, deroofing)
  • Definitive treatment of localized disease areas

Surgical Stages and Disease Classification

Hurley Staging

HS is staged using the Hurley classification: Stage I involves solitary or few nodules without sinus tracts or scarring; Stage II involves recurrent nodules with minimal tract formation in isolated areas; Stage III involves extensive sinus tracts and scarring involving entire anatomical regions. Surgical approach is guided by disease stage.

Affected Anatomical Regions

The axilla is the most commonly affected site. The groin, inframammary fold, and perineal regions are also frequently involved. Less commonly, the buttocks, belt line, and other areas are affected. Assessment of all disease areas is essential before surgical planning.

Surgical Treatment Options

Incision and Drainage

Acute abscess formation requires incision and drainage for symptom relief and infection control. However, this is not definitive treatment and recurrence at the same site is typical.

Deroofing Procedure

Unroofing or deroofing removes the roof of sinus tracts while preserving surrounding tissue. This approach is discussed separately but is useful for localized sinus tract disease.

Wide Local Excision

Complete excision of all diseased tissue including sinus tracts, their ramifications, and affected surrounding tissue is the most effective surgical approach. This is discussed in detail separately but represents definitive treatment for localized disease areas.

Staged vs. Single-Stage Procedures

Very extensive disease may require staged procedures to manage the total surgical burden and healing requirements. Multiple staged procedures at 4-6 week intervals allow complete disease control while managing anesthesia exposure and surgical time.

Role of Imaging

Pre-operative imaging with MRI or ultrasound delineates sinus tract extent and ramifications, informing surgical planning. Imaging may identify previously undetected tracts that would otherwise be missed during surgery, leading to incomplete treatment and recurrence.

Post-operative Management

Post-operative care varies with the extent of surgery. Wound care is essential for proper healing. Some wounds require frequent dressing changes for extended periods. Pain management in the immediate post-operative period is important as HS surgery sites are sensitive areas subject to friction and moisture.

Outcomes and Recurrence

Wider excision and ablation of entire diseased areas with healing by primary closure or skin grafting provides the best long-term outcomes. However, recurrence can occur in 10-40% of cases even with complete excision, reflecting the systemic nature of HS disease. Continued medical management reduces recurrence risk.
