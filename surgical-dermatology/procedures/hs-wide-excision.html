Surgical Procedures & Techniques

HS Wide Excision: Definitive Surgical Treatment

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Wide Excision as Definitive HS Surgery

Wide excision of all diseased tissue including sinus tracts, their ramifications, and affected surrounding skin and subcutaneous tissue represents the most effective surgical treatment for hidradenitis suppurativa. This approach provides the lowest recurrence rates and most durable disease control, though at the cost of larger defects requiring reconstruction.

Indications for Wide Excision

  • Extensive Stage II or Stage III disease
  • Recurrent disease after prior deroofing or incision and drainage
  • Multiple interconnected sinus tracts
  • Disease with significant functional impairment
  • Patient preference for definitive treatment
  • Disease unresponsive to medical therapy

Pre-operative Assessment

Imaging and Planning

Comprehensive imaging with MRI or ultrasound delineates the full extent of sinus tract disease. All branching patterns and extent are documented. This informs surgical planning regarding excision borders and reconstruction needs. Very extensive disease may require staged procedures.

Extent Definition

The excision includes all diseased tissue plus a margin (typically 0.5-1cm) of clinically normal tissue. For axillary disease, the entire affected axilla may need excision. For groin disease, the entire affected inguinal or genital area may be involved.

Wide Excision Technique

Anesthesia and Field Preparation

General anesthesia or conscious sedation with regional anesthesia is appropriate for extensive excision. The affected area is extensively prepped and draped. For axillary disease, the arm is positioned to provide optimal access and field visibility.

Excision Method

The excision is marked based on pre-operative planning. The incision is made around the affected area, following natural anatomical planes when possible. The diseased tissue is elevated as a specimen. Complete removal of all sinus tracts and their ramifications is essential. En bloc excision is preferable when possible, though the specimen should be carefully examined to ensure no disease is left behind.

Reconstruction Decision

Once the defect is created, a decision regarding reconstruction is made. Options include: primary closure with careful technique, healing by secondary intention with dressing care, skin grafting, or flap reconstruction depending on defect size and location.

Reconstruction Options

Primary Closure

For smaller defects, primary closure with careful tension management is possible. Intradermal sutures reduce tension on the skin surface. Multiple layers of closure improve durability. Tissue rearrangement with advancement or rotation may be necessary.

Skin Grafting

For medium to large defects, split-thickness skin grafts provide durable coverage with acceptable cosmetic results. The graft is sutured and secured with a tie-over bolster for 7-10 days. Graft take rates are generally high due to good blood supply of the underlying tissue.

Flap Reconstruction

For very large defects or defects in sensitive areas, local flaps such as advancement or rotation flaps can provide superior functional and cosmetic results. The blood supply must be maintained—axillary flaps are supplied by the axillary artery branches.

Post-operative Care

Post-operative care depends on the reconstruction method. Sutures or staples are removed at 2-3 weeks depending on location and healing. Skin grafts require immobilization and protection for 7-10 days. Wound care with dressing changes maintains cleanliness and monitors healing.

Pain Management

Wide excision can be painful post-operatively, particularly in the axilla and groin where movement and moisture are problematic. Adequate pain control with analgesics is essential. Compression garments can reduce swelling and provide comfort.

Outcomes and Recurrence

Recurrence rates after wide excision range from 5-30% depending on disease extent and follow-up duration. Complete excision of all diseased tissue minimizes recurrence. Continued medical management reduces recurrence risk further. Some patients may require staged procedures if disease extends beyond initially planned excision boundaries.

Return to Function

Full functional recovery takes 4-6 weeks depending on defect location and size. Axillary excision may limit arm mobility transiently. Groin excision may affect mobility and function. Progressive return to normal activities is typical as healing progresses.
