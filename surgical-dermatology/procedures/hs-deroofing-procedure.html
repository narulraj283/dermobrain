Surgical Procedures & Techniques

HS Deroofing Procedure: Sinus Tract Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Deroofing as Intermediate HS Surgery

Deroofing, or unroofing, is an intermediate surgical procedure for hidradenitis suppurativa that removes the roof of sinus tracts while preserving surrounding healthy tissue. This approach bridges simple incision and drainage and wide excision, offering effective disease control while minimizing tissue loss and morbidity. Deroofing is particularly useful for early-stage disease or limited sinus tract involvement.

Patient Selection

Deroofing is ideal for patients with Stage I or limited Stage II disease with discrete sinus tracts. It is less suitable for extensive Stage II or Stage III disease with extensive branching tracts. Imaging with ultrasound or MRI helps delineate sinus tract extent and guides patient selection.

Pre-operative Planning

Careful pre-operative assessment determines sinus tract location, extent, and ramifications. Probing can outline tract course, though blind probing may miss branches. Imaging provides superior delineation of tract anatomy. Multiple sinus tracts in close proximity may require treating all connected tracts in a single procedure.

Deroofing Technique

Anesthesia and Field Preparation

Local anesthesia with epinephrine is infiltrated around the sinus tract area. For extensive disease or sensitive areas, conscious sedation is preferred. The area is cleansed and draped in sterile fashion.

Tract Identification and Deroofing

The sinus tract is identified by palpation, probing, or the presence of drainage. A surgical blade or cautery device is used to open the tract from its superior surface to its deepest point. The goal is to convert the sinus tract from a closed cavity into an open wound that can epithelialize from the base upward.

The roof of the tract is removed, exposing the tract cavity. Care is taken to preserve the tract walls and surrounding tissue. Granulation tissue and debris within the tract are gently debrided or allowed to remain as granulation tissue promotes healing.

Ramification Management

Sinus tracts frequently have branches or ramifications. These must be identified and deroofed as well. The probe technique helps outline branches. Complete deroofing of all tracts and branches in the involved area is essential to prevent recurrence.

Wound Management

Immediate Post-operative Care

After deroofing, the wound is typically left open to heal by secondary intention. Dressings keep the wound clean and absorb drainage. Some practitioners apply antibiotic ointment; others use simple sterile gauze dressings.

Ongoing Wound Care

Dressing changes are performed daily or as needed based on drainage. As the wound epithelializes, dressing frequency can be reduced. Healing typically requires 4-12 weeks depending on wound size and depth. Patients should keep the area clean and dry.

Healing and Epithelialization

The wound epithelializes from the base upward and from the edges inward. Healthy granulation tissue indicates proper healing. Secondary infection should be managed with antibiotics if symptoms develop. As the wound heals, drainage decreases and dressing needs diminish.

Recurrence Rates and Long-term Outcomes

Recurrence after deroofing varies with disease extent and follow-up duration, ranging from 10-40% in various series. Complete deroofing of all tracts and branches reduces recurrence. Continued medical management with antibiotics or biological agents reduces recurrence risk. If recurrence occurs, repeat deroofing or progression to wide excision may be necessary.

Advantages and Limitations

Advantages of deroofing include preservation of tissue and function, lower morbidity than wide excision, and faster healing for limited disease. Limitations include incomplete disease eradication in extensive tracts and higher recurrence rates compared to wide excision. The procedure requires careful technique and understanding of sinus tract anatomy.
