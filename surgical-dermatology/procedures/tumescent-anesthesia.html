Tumescent Anesthesia: Infiltration Technique and Advantages

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Tumescent Anesthesia: Definition and Technique

Tumescent anesthesia is an infiltration technique that involves infusing large volumes of dilute local anesthetic solution (typically dilute lidocaine and epinephrine with saline and sodium bicarbonate) into subcutaneous tissue to achieve anesthesia, hemostasis, and expansion of the surgical field. The infusion creates a tumid (swollen) layer of tissue, hence the name.

Solution Composition

The typical tumescent solution contains 1000 mL normal saline, 1 mL of 1% lidocaine (10 mg), epinephrine 0.001% (0.1 mg), and 12.5 mL of 8.4% sodium bicarbonate. This creates a very dilute anesthetic solution suitable for large-volume infiltration.

Lidocaine in Tumescent Anesthesia

The dilute lidocaine concentration in tumescent solution (0.1%) is much lower than typical infiltration anesthetic (1%). However, the large volume infused provides sufficient anesthetic effect. Maximum recommended dose of lidocaine in tumescent anesthesia is approximately 55 mg/kg (compared to 7 mg/kg for conventional infiltration), allowing safe infiltration of very large areas.

Epinephrine Role

Epinephrine in the solution causes intense vasoconstriction, creating a bloodless surgical field and dramatically reducing bleeding. This allows visualization of deeper structures and reduces operative bleeding. Epinephrine also slows lidocaine absorption, extending duration and allowing higher total doses safely.

Sodium Bicarbonate

Sodium bicarbonate increases pH, reducing acidity of the solution. This allows more anesthetic to exist in the non-ionized, penetrating form. Buffering accelerates anesthetic onset significantly and reduces discomfort on injection.

Infiltration Technique

Solution Preparation

The solution should be prepared using sterile technique, adding components to normal saline in appropriate order and concentrations. Some surgeons purchase pre-made tumescent solutions from pharmaceutical suppliers. The solution is typically warmed to approximately 37°C before infiltration to reduce discomfort and accelerate onset.

Infiltration Method

The anesthetic solution is infiltrated using a small-gauge needle (typically 25-27G) or a small cannula. The infiltration is performed slowly, advancing the needle through the tissue while injecting to create a uniform anesthetic field. The tissue becomes visibly swollen and tumid, allowing visualization of the swelling indicating adequate infiltration.

Infiltration should be gentle and methodical, avoiding rapid injection which increases discomfort. Large volumes may require 20-30 minutes of infiltration time. The area being infiltrated should be massaged gently after infiltration to distribute the solution uniformly.

Waiting Time

After infiltration, 5-10 minutes should be allowed for anesthetic effect to fully develop and for vasoconstriction to become maximal. This waiting period is essential—operating before complete anesthetic effect results in inadequate anesthesia.

Clinical Applications

Liposuction and Lipoma Removal

Tumescent anesthesia is ideal for liposuction and removal of large lipomas. The dilute lidocaine allows infiltration of large areas without exceeding maximum safe doses. The vasoconstriction provides a bloodless surgical field and reduces operative complications. Tumescent anesthesia has become standard for liposuction procedures.

Extensive Surgical Procedures

Any extensive surgical procedure covering large areas (such as wide excision of HS or other conditions) can benefit from tumescent anesthesia. The large volume of solution infiltrates the entire surgical area and extends anesthesia into surrounding areas that may be encountered intraoperatively.

Hemostasis Enhancement

The vasoconstriction from epinephrine in tumescent solution is profound and extends anesthesia time significantly. Procedures can be performed in a relatively bloodless field, improving visualization and reducing operative bleeding complications.

Advantages of Tumescent Anesthesia

  • Allows infiltration of large surgical areas safely with standard lidocaine doses
  • Creates a bloodless field through intense vasoconstriction from epinephrine
  • Provides longer-duration anesthesia reducing post-operative pain
  • Can be performed with local anesthesia alone, avoiding general anesthesia risks
  • Reduces operative bleeding and blood loss
  • Improves visualization of surgical field
  • Less systemic absorption of lidocaine due to epinephrine effect

Potential Complications and Considerations

Fluid Overload

Large-volume infiltration can result in significant fluid absorption. Volumes greater than 4-5 liters may result in clinically significant fluid shifts. Monitoring for signs of fluid overload (peripheral edema, pulmonary edema, congestive heart failure) is important in patients with cardiac or renal disease.

Lidocaine Toxicity

Although tumescent anesthesia allows higher total lidocaine doses, systemic toxicity can still occur if maximum doses are exceeded. Careful calculation of total dose infused is essential. Signs of lidocaine toxicity include tinnitus, metallic taste, confusion, seizures, and cardiac arrhythmias.

Epinephrine Effects

The profound vasoconstriction from epinephrine carries theoretical risk of tissue necrosis or complications in patients with significant vascular disease. Patients should be assessed for peripheral vascular disease before tumescent procedures.

Post-operative Management

The extended anesthesia from tumescent solution provides substantial post-operative pain relief, often eliminating need for post-operative analgesics. Compression garments may be applied to reduce swelling and provide comfort. Patients should be advised to monitor for excessive swelling or signs of fluid overload in the days following surgery.
