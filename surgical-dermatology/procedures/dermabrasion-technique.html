Dermabrasion: Technique and Clinical Applications

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Dermabrasion: Definition and Mechanism

Dermabrasion is a mechanical resurfacing procedure that uses rotating abrasive surfaces to remove superficial skin layers. By removing epidermis and superficial dermis, dermabrasion reduces the appearance of scars, fine wrinkles, and other skin surface irregularities. The technique relies on controlled removal of damaged tissue to promote healing and remodeling.

Clinical Indications

  • Facial acne scars, particularly rolling and boxcar scars
  • Traumatic scars with superficial or mid-dermal involvement
  • Wrinkles and fine lines from sun damage or aging
  • Post-surgical scars in early healing phase
  • Removal of superficial skin lesions or tattoos
  • Pigmentary irregularities from burns or trauma

Patient Selection and Pre-operative Evaluation

Ideal candidates are those with facial scars that are superficial to mid-dermal in depth. Patients with darker skin types should be counseled about higher risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and hypopigmentation. Those with keloid history or poor wound healing capacity are poor candidates. Pre-operative facial photography documents baseline appearance.

Dermabrasion Technique

Anesthesia and Preparation

Dermabrasion is performed under general anesthesia or conscious sedation with local anesthesia. The face is cleaned thoroughly and marked to delineate treatment areas. A refrigerant spray is often applied to the skin to firm the dermal tissue and facilitate uniform abrasion.

Abrading Process

A rotating abrader with various grit sizes is used, with coarser grits for initial abrasion and finer grits for finishing. The device is moved slowly and steadily across the treatment area with light pressure, following natural contours and skin tension lines. The goal is uniform abrasion to the appropriate depth—typically removing epidermis and reaching the papillary or superficial reticular dermis.

Periorbital and perioral areas require extra caution due to thin skin and proximity to structures. The neck and cheeks are abraded with consistent pressure to avoid depressions or irregularities. Feathering of edges ensures smooth transition to untreated skin.

Depth Control

Proper depth control is essential to achieve the desired result without excessive injury. Bleeding indicates entry into the dermis—small capillary oozing is expected. Depth is assessed by visual landmarks and by the sensation of resistance. Over-abrasion increases scarring risk and prolongs healing.

Post-operative Care

Immediately after dermabrasion, the abraded surface is treated with antibiotic ointment and a protective dressing. Open-wound care with frequent cleansing and ointment application is necessary for 2-3 weeks until re-epithelialization is complete. Patients must scrupulously avoid sun exposure for months to years to prevent post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation.

Results and Complications

Improvement in scar appearance typically becomes apparent at 2-3 months as edema resolves and collagen remodeling progresses. Final results are evident at 6-12 months. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and hypopigmentation are the most common complications, particularly in darker skin types. Milia (small white bumps) may develop transiently. Scarring from over-aggressive abrasion can worsen appearance if excessive depth is achieved.
