Surgical Procedures & Techniques

Lipoma Removal: Surgical Extraction Techniques

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Lipomas: Definition and Clinical Characteristics

Lipomas are benign, slow-growing tumors composed of mature adipose tissue. They present as soft, mobile, painless subcutaneous nodules that can occur anywhere on the body. While benign, they warrant removal when cosmetically concerning, rapidly enlarging, or causing functional impairment.

Pre-operative Evaluation

Clinical examination with palpation determines the size, depth, and mobility of the lipoma. Imaging with ultrasound or MRI may be warranted for large lesions, deep lipomas, or those in sensitive anatomical locations. This ensures proper surgical planning and identifies any relationship to underlying structures.

Surgical Techniques for Removal

Open Excision with Primary Closure

Open excision remains the standard for most lipomas. After local anesthesia with epinephrine, an incision is made directly over the lesion or in a favorable skin tension line. The lipoma capsule is carefully identified and the lesion is mobilized using gentle traction and scissors dissection along the plane between capsule and surrounding tissue.

Blunt dissection with a periosteal elevator or similar instrument can facilitate removal. Once the entire lesion is freed, it is extracted and the wound is irrigated. Hemostasis is achieved with pressure and electrocautery as needed. The incision is closed with absorbable sutures in deeper layers and non-absorbable sutures in skin.

Minimal-Incision Liposuction Technique

For smaller superficial lipomas, a minimal-incision approach using suction-assisted lipectomy can be effective. A small incision is made and a small-diameter cannula is used to aspirate and remove the lipoma tissue. This technique reduces scarring but requires experience to ensure complete removal and carries higher recurrence risk if not thoroughly performed.

Tumescent Technique

Large lipomas may benefit from tumescent anesthesia, which involves infiltration of dilute epinephrine and lidocaine solution throughout the lesion and surrounding tissue. This provides profound anesthesia, hemostasis, and reduces blood loss. The lipoma can then be removed with conventional open excision using the tumescent field.

Size Considerations

Small lipomas (less than 2cm) can be removed with minimal scars using closed technique. Large lipomas (greater than 5cm) often require larger incisions and may need staged procedures if they are extremely large. The location and depth affect the approach—deep intramuscular lipomas may require referral to surgical colleagues.

Complications and Outcomes

Hematoma and seroma can occur postoperatively but usually resolve without intervention. Nerve injury is rare with careful technique. Incomplete removal leads to recurrence in 1-5% of cases. Patients should be counseled about expected scar appearance and healing timeline.
