Ear Reconstruction After Mohs Micrographic Surgery

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Ear Reconstruction Following Mohs: Challenges and Planning

Mohs micrographic surgery is frequently employed for skin cancers of the ear due to excellent cure rates and tissue preservation. However, Mohs surgery often results in significant defects that require reconstruction to restore ear structure and function. Successful ear reconstruction requires understanding ear anatomy, optimal timing, and appropriate reconstruction techniques.

Defect Assessment

Size and Location Classification

Ear defects are classified by location (helix, antihelix, concha, ear canal, earlobe) and size. Defects are further characterized by depth and involvement of underlying cartilage. The location determines reconstruction approach—cartilage involvement necessitates cartilage grafting or flap reconstruction.

Timing of Reconstruction

Immediate reconstruction at the time of final Mohs stage is ideal when defect size is known and margins are confirmed clear. This reduces total operative time and anesthesia exposure. However, some surgeons prefer staged reconstruction after histopathology is complete to ensure adequate margins.

Reconstruction Techniques

Primary Closure

Small defects involving skin only can often be closed primarily. Tension-free closure using advancement or rotation techniques preserves ear contour. Fine sutures and meticulous technique optimize cosmetic outcome.

Skin Grafting

For larger defects without cartilage involvement, full-thickness skin grafts provide durable reconstruction. Grafts are harvested from post-auricular skin or other areas with good color match. The graft is sutured to the defect edges, and a tie-over bolster provides immobilization and hemostasis for 7-10 days. Graft take rates exceed 95% when proper technique is used.

Cartilage Grafting

When cartilage is exposed or removed, cartilage grafting prevents collapse and deformity. Cartilage can be harvested from the opposite ear (if available), costochondral rib, or tragus. The graft is shaped to restore contour and sutured in place. A skin graft then covers the cartilage graft.

Local Flap Reconstruction

Rotation, advancement, or transposition flaps using adjacent ear and periauricular tissue can reconstruct helix and ear margin defects. These flaps maintain hair-bearing characteristics and sensory innervation better than grafts. Flap design must account for blood supply—the superior temporal artery supplies most ear flaps.

Composite Grafting

For very small defects, particularly of the ear margin, composite grafts combining skin and cartilage from the opposite ear or other sources restore contour and function. However, composite grafts have lower take rates than skin grafts alone due to limited vascular supply.

Post-operative Care and Outcomes

Careful post-operative wound care and protection is essential. Patients should avoid manipulation of the repair. Sutures are removed at 1-2 weeks depending on location. Long-term sun protection prevents recurrence of skin cancer in the remaining ear.

Complications

Graft failure is uncommon with proper technique but results in contraction and deformity. Hematoma under grafts should be aspirated early. Infection is prevented through sterile technique. Secondary deformities may require touch-up procedures.
