Suture Techniques in Dermatologic Surgery: Principles and Practice

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Suture Selection and Wound Closure Principles

Appropriate suture selection and meticulous wound closure technique are fundamental to achieving optimal cosmetic and functional results in dermatologic surgery. The choice of suture material, suture size, and closure technique depends on wound characteristics, location, and desired cosmetic outcome.

Suture Material Classification

Absorbable Sutures

Absorbable sutures are broken down by the body over time and do not require removal. Absorbable sutures lose tensile strength at varying rates depending on the material. Synthetic absorbable sutures (Vicryl, Monocryl) maintain strength for 2-4 weeks and are absorbed over 2-3 months. Natural absorbable sutures (chromic gut) are absorbed faster but are less commonly used in dermatologic surgery due to less predictable absorption and higher tissue reactivity.

Absorbable sutures are ideal for deep layer closure where removal is impractical. Intradermal (subcuticular) closure with absorbable sutures reduces tension on skin edges and minimizes suture mark formation.

Non-absorbable Sutures

Non-absorbable sutures maintain tensile strength indefinitely and must be removed. Silk and monofilament nylon are commonly used in dermatologic surgery. Silk is comfortable to handle and handles well, but has higher tissue reactivity and higher infection rates compared to monofilament materials. Monofilament sutures (nylon, polypropylene) have lower tissue reactivity and infection rates but can be more difficult to handle and can be subject to needle damage causing splitting.

Suture Size Selection

Fine sutures (5-0 and 6-0) are used for facial skin closure where minimal scarring is desired. The smaller suture creates smaller holes in tissue and smaller suture marks. Coarser sutures (3-0 and 4-0) are used for thicker skin or areas where fine sutures would be difficult to handle. The general principle is to use the finest suture that can be safely handled in the location being repaired.

Closure Techniques

Single-Layer Closure

Simple skin closure without deep layer sutures is appropriate for shallow wounds with minimal tension. The epidermis and dermis are approximated with interrupted or running sutures. Approximation should be precise, with wound edges everted slightly to account for tissue contraction during healing. Eversion ensures that the scar remains at the same level as surrounding skin rather than depressed.

Interrupted Closure

Individual sutures placed at regular intervals (approximately 3-5mm apart) allow removal of a single suture if there is infection or excessive tension. Interrupted sutures are standard for most dermatologic surgery. The suture loop is tied with a square knot to prevent slipping. Each suture should be placed perpendicular to the wound edge with equal distance from the wound edge on both sides.

Running Closure

A continuous suture line provides faster wound closure than interrupted sutures but removal of a single suture affects the integrity of the entire line. Running closure is less commonly used in dermatology but can be appropriate for some straight wounds with minimal tension.

Two-Layer Closure

For many dermatologic procedures, two-layer closure optimizes cosmetic results. Deep layer closure with absorbable sutures (intradermal layer) relieves tension from the skin surface, reducing suture marks and promoting even wound edge approximation. The skin is then closed with fine non-absorbable sutures for precise edge alignment and removal at appropriate timing.

The deep layer sutures are placed in the dermis, taking care not to penetrate the epidermis (which would leave marks). Sutures should be placed perpendicular to the wound and spaced to distribute tension evenly. The knot is tied deep in the wound, away from the surface.

Running Subcuticular (Intradermal) Closure

Running subcuticular closure using absorbable suture is an elegant technique for skin closure that avoids surface suture marks. A single suture is placed in the intradermal layer in a running fashion from one end of the wound to the other. The suture can be retrieved at the end and tied, or the knot can be tied after the suture is placed. This technique reduces wound edge tension and produces the most cosmetically pleasing results for many wounds.

Special Techniques

Mattress Sutures

Horizontal mattress sutures (also called figure-of-8 sutures) pass through tissue deeper than simple sutures, providing greater strength and better wound edge eversion. These sutures are useful for wounds under tension or locations where maximal wound edge eversion is desired. However, mattress sutures leave larger suture marks and are less cosmetically favorable.

Vertical Mattress Sutures

Vertical mattress sutures are placed perpendicular to the wound, passing through deeper tissue and then back through more superficial tissue. These sutures provide excellent edge eversion and are useful for wounds under tension or on high-tension areas such as the back.

Wound Edge Approximation

Edge Eversion

Proper edge eversion is critical for optimal cosmetic results. Wound edges should be everted slightly so that the final scar is at the same level as surrounding skin. Eversion is achieved by inserting the needle perpendicular to the wound edge and suturing with adequate depth to engage the dermis.

Tension Distribution

Uneven tension distribution causes dimpling and poor alignment. Sutures should be spaced evenly, and tension should be distributed equally across all sutures. Deep layer closure helps distribute tension from the skin surface.

Suture Removal Timing

Non-absorbable sutures should be removed at appropriate intervals to minimize suture mark formation while maintaining adequate wound strength. Face: 5-7 days; scalp: 7-10 days; trunk and extremities: 10-14 days; high-tension areas: 14-21 days. Very fine sutures on the face may be removed at 5 days to minimize marks, while coarser sutures on the back may remain longer.

Common Complications

Infection can occur from improper sterilization or technique. Suture abscess (foreign body reaction to suture material) causes localized inflammation. Suture marks result from too-tight sutures or prolonged suture retention. These are avoided through proper suture selection, adequate spacing, appropriate tension, and timely removal.
