Pre- & Post-Surgical Care

When to Call Your Surgeon After Skin Surgery: Red Flags and Concerns

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Recognizing Normal Post-Operative Symptoms

Many normal post-operative symptoms cause patient concern, though they represent expected healing responses rather than complications. Understanding what is normal helps patients distinguish between expected recovery and warning signs requiring urgent medical evaluation.

Normal Post-Operative Findings

Mild to moderate swelling peaks around days 2-3 after surgery and gradually resolves over 1-2 weeks. Redness around the incision is expected as part of normal healing inflammation. Mild pain or discomfort, managed with over-the-counter analgesics, is normal, as is minimal clear or pale yellow fluid drainage for the first 24-48 hours.

Bruising (ecchymosis) is common, particularly around the eyes and on the extremities. Bruising peaks at days 2-3 and gradually resolves over 7-10 days. Purple or yellow discoloration fading over time is normal. Itching at the incision site during healing is extremely common and indicates normal wound healing progression.

Fever and Systemic Symptoms

Low-grade fever (temperature less than 101°F) occasionally occurs immediately after surgery due to anesthesia and surgical inflammation. This typically resolves within 24-48 hours. However, fever greater than 101°F (38.3°C) appearing days after surgery, particularly with wound symptoms, suggests infection requiring urgent evaluation and possible antibiotic treatment.

Contact your surgeon immediately if fever persists beyond 48 hours, is accompanied by increasing pain or spreading redness around the wound, or is associated with systemic symptoms including chills, malaise, or weakness. These may indicate developing infection or other serious complications.

Increasing Pain

Mild pain immediately after anesthesia resolves within several hours to one day. Moderate pain responsive to over-the-counter analgesics for the first few days is normal. However, pain increasing after day 3-4, pain not adequately controlled with appropriate analgesics, or new onset severe pain requires prompt evaluation.

Increasing pain may indicate infection, hematoma (blood collection), or other complications. Localized severe pain, particularly with associated swelling or fluctuance, suggests abscess formation requiring urgent drainage. Do not dismiss pain as normal healing; contact your surgeon for evaluation.

Wound Appearance Changes Requiring Evaluation

Spreading erythema beyond the immediate wound margin, particularly if rapidly expanding, suggests cellulitis requiring antibiotics. Erythema that doesn't improve with local care after 5-7 days or worsens after initial improvement warrants medical assessment. Localized area of increasing induration and pain may indicate abscess formation.

Purulent (pus-like) drainage, foul odor, or color change from clear to yellow or green drainage indicates bacterial infection. Even small amounts of purulent drainage warrant prompt medical evaluation and possible culture to guide antibiotic selection. Complete lack of drainage combined with wound separation is also abnormal and requires evaluation.

Excessive Bleeding or Oozing

Light oozing of blood within the first 24 hours is normal, and spotting on the dressing commonly occurs. However, active bleeding persisting beyond the first 24 hours or bleeding resuming after initially stopping may indicate inadequate hemostasis or coagulopathy. Apply gentle pressure for 15-20 minutes; if bleeding continues, contact your surgeon.

Sudden onset of significant bleeding, particularly if accompanied by swelling suggesting hematoma formation, requires immediate medical evaluation. Patients on anticoagulants may experience more prolonged oozing; contact your surgeon if bleeding is heavier than expected.

Wound Separation and Dehiscence

Complete separation of wound edges (dehiscence) with gaping open areas requires urgent evaluation and often requires re-closure. Partial separation or opening along portions of the incline warrants prompt medical assessment. Wound separation significantly increases infection risk and healing time and often results in larger, more noticeable scars.

Signs of Allergic Reaction

Unexpected widespread rash, swelling beyond the immediate surgical area, or difficulty breathing suggests allergic reaction potentially to anesthesia, antibiotics, or other materials. Seek immediate medical attention for any symptoms suggesting systemic allergic reaction.

Neurologic Symptoms

Some numbness and unusual sensations are common immediately after surgery due to local anesthesia. However, numbness persisting beyond one week, increasing numbness, or sudden onset of neurologic symptoms including weakness or loss of function warrant evaluation for possible nerve injury. Contact your surgeon regarding persistent or progressive neurologic changes.

When to Seek Emergency Care

Seek immediate emergency care for difficulty breathing or swallowing, severe uncontrolled bleeding despite 20+ minutes of pressure application, signs of severe allergic reaction, chest pain or severe systemic symptoms, or any other life-threatening symptoms. For any concerns about your recovery, contact your surgeon; it's better to seek evaluation for potentially minor issues than to risk missing serious complications.
