Managing Surgical Scars: Silicone, Pressure Therapy, and Steroid Injections

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Scar Formation and Evolution

Surgical scars are inevitable consequences of skin surgery, though proper management during and after healing can significantly improve appearance. Understanding scar maturation and available management options helps optimize cosmetic outcomes. Scars continue evolving for 12-18 months after surgery, gradually fading as collagen remodels and vascularity decreases.

Early Scar Management: Massage and Moisture

Beginning scar management shortly after suture removal optimizes outcomes. Gentle massage using circular motions helps break up collagen and improve scar pliability. Begin massage gently 2-3 weeks after suture removal, gradually increasing pressure as healing progresses. Massage for 5-10 minutes several times daily for best results.

Maintaining scar moisture through ointment application promotes softer healing and may reduce hypertrophic (thickened) scarring. Continue topical ointment use for 2-3 weeks after suture removal. Some surgeons recommend scar-specific products containing onion extract (Mederma), though evidence for benefit is limited.

Silicone-Based Scar Treatments

Silicone gel sheeting and ointments have been extensively studied for scar management with evidence supporting their efficacy in improving appearance, particularly for hypertrophic scars. Silicone increases hydration of the stratum corneum (outer skin layer) and may modify collagen synthesis.

Silicone gel sheets: Specialized dressings worn over scars for 12 or more hours daily. These self-adhesive sheets are worn continuously for 12 weeks or longer. Results show improvement in scar thickness, color, and pliability with consistent use. Silicone sheets can be worn under clothing and are usually well-tolerated.

Silicone ointments and gels: Applied topically to scars several times daily, silicone ointments improve hydration and may reduce scar hypertrophy. These require more frequent application than gel sheets but offer flexibility for scars in difficult anatomic locations.

Pressure Garments and Dressings

Pressure therapy reduces scar hypertrophy by applying consistent compression, theoretically reducing collagen deposition and scar thickness. Specialized pressure garments providing 15-25 mmHg compression are available for various body locations. Pressure should be applied continuously for 12-23 hours daily for optimal benefit.

Effectiveness varies by individual and scar type. Pressure is most effective for large surface area scars from burns or extensive surgery. For linear surgical scars, evidence is more limited. Pressure garments are uncomfortable in hot climates and may be poorly tolerated for facial scars.

Intralesional Steroid Injections

Hypertrophic and keloid scars often respond well to intralesional corticosteroid injections, which reduce collagen synthesis and promote scar softening. Triamcinolone acetonide (40 mg/mL) is most commonly used, injected directly into the scar tissue monthly or every 6-8 weeks.

Steroid injections flatten raised scars, improve color, and increase pliability. Multiple injections are often required for optimal results, with improvement typically visible after 2-3 treatments. Potential side effects include temporary depression at injection sites, hypopigmentation, and atrophy (depression) if injected too superficially.

Laser and Light-Based Treatments

Pulsed dye lasers effectively treat erythematous (red) scars by targeting hemoglobin in new blood vessels. Treatment causes transient purpura (bruising) and gradually reduces scar redness. Vascular lasers may be particularly effective when started 4-6 weeks after surgery when scars are most vascular.

Ablative lasers (CO2 and erbium) resurface scar tissue, improving texture and appearance of depressed scars. Multiple treatments spaced 4-6 weeks apart are usually required. Non-ablative fractional lasers provide intermediate results with less downtime than ablative approaches.

Dermal Fillers

Injectable fillers such as hyaluronic acid (Restylane, Juvederm) provide temporary improvement for depressed scars by elevating the scar level to match surrounding skin. Results typically last 6-12 months and require repeat treatments. Fillers are particularly useful for facial scars in cosmetically sensitive areas.

Surgical Scar Revision

After complete scar maturation (12-18 months), surgical revision may improve appearance of scars remaining unacceptable despite conservative management. Scar revision techniques include excision with primary closure, Z-plasty or other geometric rearrangement, dermabrasion, or tissue expansion. These approaches are typically reserved for scars significantly impacting appearance or function.
