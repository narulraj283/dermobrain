Pre- & Post-Surgical Care

Surgical Site Infection Signs: Recognition and When to Seek Treatment

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview of Surgical Site Infections

Surgical site infections (SSIs) are infections localized to the surgical wound or surrounding tissue. Though relatively uncommon in dermatologic surgery (occurring in 1-5% of cases), SSIs require prompt recognition and treatment to prevent progression to deeper infection or systemic infection.

Timeline of Infection Development

Infections typically develop within 5-7 days after surgery, though they can appear any time before complete wound healing. Early infections (appearing within 48 hours) are usually caused by virulent organisms introduced during surgery or from skin flora despite sterile precautions. Delayed infections may result from hematoma formation, wound contamination, or immunocompromise.

Clinical Signs of Superficial SSI

Superficial incisional infections involve only skin and subcutaneous tissue. Classic signs include purulent drainage (pus), erythema beyond immediate incision margins, warmth, swelling, and pain. Drainage characteristics help identify infection: clear or pale yellow serous drainage is normal, while yellow, green, or brown thick drainage suggests bacterial infection.

Erythema extending more than 1-2 cm from incision margins particularly suggests infection. The erythema often rapidly spreads (cellulitis) if untreated. Foul odor from the wound strongly suggests bacterial infection. Some infections develop localized fluctuance (movement of fluid) indicating abscess formation with pus collection.

Systemic Signs of Infection

Fever greater than 101°F (38.3°C) appearing after the first 24-48 hours, particularly with local wound symptoms, indicates systemic infection. Other systemic signs include chills, malaise, fatigue, tachycardia (rapid heart rate), and general feeling of illness. Lymphadenopathy (swollen lymph nodes) draining the surgical area may develop as the body responds to infection.

Cellulitis vs. Localized Infection

Cellulitis presents as rapidly spreading erythema and edema extending well beyond the incision margins. Cellulitis typically has poorly demarcated borders and rapidly progresses without treatment. Cellulitis indicates systemic infection requiring oral or intravenous antibiotics. Localized infection confined to the wound vicinity may respond to local care and topical antibiotics alone, though systemic antibiotics are typically recommended.

Abscess Formation

Abscess formation represents localized collection of pus surrounded by inflammatory tissue. Abscesses appear as localized areas of fluctuance, increased swelling, and often severe localized pain. Fluctuance (wave-like movement of fluid under palpation) indicates pus collection and abscess formation. Abscesses require drainage for resolution, as antibiotics alone cannot penetrate the abscess cavity adequately.

Diagnostic Evaluation

For suspected infection, your surgeon will examine the wound and may obtain cultures of purulent drainage to identify causative organisms and guide antibiotic selection. Gram stain of drainage may provide rapid presumptive identification. White blood cell count and other laboratory studies may be obtained in patients with systemic symptoms to assess infection severity.

Treatment of Superficial SSI

Superficial infections typically respond to systemic antibiotics. For suspected Staphylococcus or Streptococcus (common skin flora), first-generation cephalosporin (cephalexin) or antistaphylococcal penicillin is typical initial therapy. For penicillin-allergic patients, clindamycin or fluoroquinolone may be used. Duration is typically 7-14 days.

MRSA (methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus) is increasingly common and requires coverage with clindamycin, doxycycline, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX), or linezolid depending on resistance patterns and allergy history. Cultures should be obtained to confirm organism and guide therapy.

Treatment of Abscess

Abscesses require drainage in addition to antibiotics. Small abscesses may respond to needle aspiration performed in the office under sterile conditions. Larger or recurrent abscesses often require formal incision and drainage. The abscess cavity is opened, pus is evacuated, and tissue is explored for loculations (pockets). The cavity may be left open to drain or loosely packed with gauze depending on size and surgeon preference.

Wound Care During Infection

Continue twice-daily wound cleaning even during infection treatment. For purulent drainage, cultures should be obtained before starting antibiotics to identify organisms. After culture, cleanse wounds thoroughly with soap and water, removing purulent material. Apply fresh dressing with antibiotic ointment. Increase dressing change frequency if drainage is significant.

Cellulitis Management

Cellulitis is a medical emergency requiring prompt systemic antibiotic therapy. For mild cellulitis in otherwise healthy patients, oral antibiotics may be adequate. For severe cellulitis, widespread distribution, systemic symptoms, or immunocompromised patients, intravenous antibiotics are indicated. Hospitalization may be necessary for severe infections with systemic toxicity.
