Pre- & Post-Surgical Care

Surgical Wound Care Instructions: Daily Care and Dressing Changes

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Immediate Post-Operative Wound Care

Proper wound care immediately following dermatologic surgery is critical for optimal healing and minimizing infection risk and scarring. Your surgeon will provide specific written instructions tailored to your particular procedure, surgical technique, and wound characteristics.

Initial Dressing and Bandage Management

Most dermatologic surgical wounds are covered with sterile gauze and tape or specialized wound dressings immediately after surgery. The initial dressing protects the wound from contamination and absorbs drainage. Your surgeon will specify when to remove this initial dressing, typically within 24-48 hours.

When removing the initial dressing, do so gently to avoid disrupting any healing tissue. If dressing adheres to the wound, moisten with sterile saline or clean water before removal to prevent tissue damage. If significant bleeding occurs during dressing removal, apply gentle pressure with clean gauze for 10-15 minutes.

Wound Cleansing Technique

Twice daily wound cleansing prevents bacterial overgrowth and promotes healing. Wash your hands thoroughly before touching the wound. Gently cleanse the wound with mild soap and lukewarm water or use sterile saline solution. Use a clean gauze pad or soft washcloth, making gentle circular motions around the wound.

For stitched wounds, cleanse around sutures thoroughly but gently, as debris can accumulate along suture lines creating infection risk. Do not submerge the wound in water (bathing or swimming) until sutures are removed unless your surgeon specifies otherwise, though gentle running water during shower cleansing is typically acceptable.

Topical Ointment Application

After cleansing, most surgeons recommend applying antibiotic ointment such as bacitracin or mupirocin to the wound. Ointment provides moisture, prevents drying and scabbing, and offers antimicrobial protection. Some surgeons prefer petrolatum (Vaseline) for very clean, well-healing wounds after initial antibiotic ointment use.

Apply ointment in a thin layer directly to the wound and surrounding area. More ointment is not better; excessive ointment can macerate (soften and weaken) surrounding skin. Use sterile cotton swabs or clean gloved fingers for application.

Redressing the Wound

After ointment application, most wounds benefit from continued dressing until sutures are removed. Use sterile gauze pads secured with medical tape. Non-adherent gauze (such as Teflon or Adaptic) prevents sticking to healing wound tissue. For wounds that are dry and well-healing, some surgeons permit leaving wounds uncovered (open to air) after the first few days.

Activity Restrictions

Avoid strenuous activity, heavy lifting, and excessive sweating for the first 1-2 weeks after surgery, as increased blood flow and mechanical stress can disrupt healing and increase bleeding. Gradually resume normal activities as wounds heal, following your surgeon's specific guidelines.

Managing Drainage and Bleeding

Minimal serous (clear or pale yellow) drainage is normal for the first 24-48 hours. Increased drainage suggests potential infection and requires medical evaluation. Light bleeding or oozing (spotting on dressing) is common immediately after surgery and typically resolves within 24 hours. Persistent bleeding requires pressure application and medical assessment.

Signs Requiring Medical Attention

Contact your surgeon immediately if you experience spreading erythema, increasing pain, purulent drainage, fever, chills, wound separation, or any concerning symptoms. These may indicate infection or other complications requiring prompt treatment.
