Nerve Damage After Skin Surgery: Prevention, Recognition, and Recovery

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Understanding Peripheral Nerve Injury in Cutaneous Surgery

Nerve injury is a potential complication of dermatologic surgery that can result in permanent sensory or motor changes. While dermatologic procedures have relatively low nerve injury rates compared to other surgical specialties, understanding nerve anatomy, injury mechanisms, and prevention strategies helps minimize complications.

Anatomy and Types of Nerves at Risk

Sensory nerves providing cutaneous innervation are the most commonly injured nerves in dermatologic surgery. Major nerves and areas where injury is most likely include the facial nerve (providing motor function to face), trigeminal nerve (providing facial sensation), supratrochlear nerve (forehead), supraorbital nerve (upper eyelid and forehead), and digital nerves (fingers and toes).

Motor nerve injuries are less common but more noticeable, as they result in muscle paralysis. Facial nerve branches (temporal, zygomatic, buccal, marginal mandibular, and cervical) supply facial musculature and are at risk during procedures on the face and neck. Accessory nerve injuries can result from neck dissections or extensive neck procedures.

Mechanisms of Nerve Injury

Direct laceration by surgical instruments or sutures is the most common cause of acute nerve injury. Lateral spreading technique for skin lesions may inadvertently lacerate nerve trunks if careful attention isn't paid to anatomy. Nerve entrapment by sutures occurs when nerves are incorporated into wound closure; sutures placed in deeper tissues may encircle nerves without recognizing their presence.

Traction injury occurs when excessive stretching or pulling stretches or damages nerve fibers. Thermal injury from electrocautery positioned too close to nerves causes tissue necrosis. Ischemic injury results from interrupted blood supply to nerves from cautery of feeding vessels or excessive pressure from hematomas or edema.

Clinical Manifestations of Nerve Injury

Sensory nerve injuries present with numbness, tingling, or abnormal sensations (paresthesias) in the distribution of the injured nerve. Numbness immediately after surgery is expected from local anesthesia; concerning numbness persists or progresses beyond anesthesia resolution (usually within hours). Hyperesthesia (increased sensitivity) or dysesthesia (burning or electric sensations) may develop during recovery.

Motor nerve injuries present with weakness or paralysis of muscles supplied by the injured nerve. Facial nerve injury causes facial droop, loss of smile, inability to close the eye, or loss of forehead wrinkles depending on which branch is injured. Finger movement loss occurs with digital nerve injury affecting motor branches.

Timing and Prognosis

Acute nerve injuries occurring during surgery may improve gradually if not complete transections. Partial injuries causing sensory changes or mild weakness often improve significantly over weeks to months as nerve inflammation resolves and axonal regeneration begins. Complete nerve transections causing dense numbness or paralysis have poorer prognosis.

Sensory recovery generally occurs over months, with progressive improvement in sensation over 6-12 months. Motor recovery follows similar timelines, though return of complete function may take 12-24 months or longer. Some degree of permanent sensory change or weakness may persist despite recovery.

Diagnosis and Evaluation

Clinical examination identifying sensory or motor deficits in a specific nerve distribution aids diagnosis. Electrodiagnostic testing (electromyography and nerve conduction studies) performed weeks after injury helps confirm nerve injury and assess injury severity and prognosis. Imaging (ultrasound or MRI) may identify nerve transection or large hematomas compressing nerves.

Conservative Management

For partial injuries or injuries suspected to be neurapraxia (temporary nerve injury from pressure or mild trauma), conservative management with observation is appropriate. Avoid further trauma to the area. Serial examinations assess for improvement. Physical therapy for motor injuries helps prevent contractures and muscle atrophy. Pain management is important, as nerve injuries are often painful.

For potential nerve compression from hematoma or swelling, rapid evacuation of the compressing fluid or swelling allows nerve decompression and may prevent permanent injury. Drainage procedures should be performed urgently if nerve compression is suspected.

Surgical Management

For suspected complete nerve transection, surgical exploration and repair is appropriate. If transection is recognized immediately during surgery, primary repair with end-to-end anastomosis provides the best outcomes. Transected nerve ends are freshly cut and precisely approximated with fine sutures or other techniques (nerve grafting for large gaps).

For transection recognized after surgery, delayed repair is appropriate after wound healing is established. Nerve exploration may reveal that apparent transection was actually severe compression with nerve continuity preserved, in which case decompression alone is appropriate.

Prevention of Nerve Injury

Thorough knowledge of anatomic nerve locations in different body areas is essential. Careful surgical technique with attention to anatomic landmarks helps avoid inadvertent nerve injury. Sutures placed in deeper tissues should be carefully positioned to avoid nerve incorporation. Electrocautery should be used judiciously in areas with important nerves, applying energy in short bursts to minimize thermal spread.

Gentle retraction and limited operative time reduce traction injury. Avoiding excessive pressure from hematomas through prompt hemostasis and drainage reduces compression injury. For extensive surgery in areas with important nerves, careful dissection and identification of nerves helps prevent injury.
