Suture Types and Removal Timeline: Absorbable vs. Non-Absorbable

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview of Surgical Sutures

Sutures (stitches) hold skin edges together, allowing organized healing and minimizing scarring. Different suture materials have distinct absorption timelines, handling characteristics, and cosmetic outcomes. Understanding your suture type helps you follow appropriate care instructions and anticipate removal timing.

Non-Absorbable Sutures

Non-absorbable sutures remain in place indefinitely unless surgically removed. These sutures provide excellent strength and do not lose tensile strength over time, making them ideal for areas requiring extended support. Common non-absorbable sutures include nylon, polypropylene, and silk.

Nylon sutures: Monofilament nylon provides excellent tensile strength and minimal tissue reactivity. It's the most commonly used suture in dermatologic surgery due to superior cosmetic outcomes and low infection rates. Nylon sutures require removal and should be left in place 7-14 days depending on location (face heals faster than trunk or extremities).

Polypropylene sutures: Similar to nylon with excellent strength and minimal tissue reaction. Polypropylene has slightly better handling characteristics than nylon and is preferred by some surgeons. Removal timing is identical to nylon.

Silk sutures: Braided silk provides easy handling and superior knot security. However, silk induces more tissue reaction than nylon and may result in slightly larger scars. Silk sutures should be removed in 7-10 days to minimize tissue reactivity.

Absorbable Sutures

Absorbable sutures gradually dissolve through enzymatic degradation and do not require removal. These sutures are useful for deep dermal closure and situations where suture removal is impractical (such as pediatric patients or inaccessible locations).

Vicryl (polyglactin 910): A braided synthetic suture losing strength over 2-3 weeks and absorbing completely over 60-90 days. Vicryl induces mild tissue reaction and is commonly used for deep dermal sutures in layered closures. Vicryl does not require removal.

Chromic catgut: A natural suture derived from animal intestine, chemically treated to resist enzymatic degradation. Chromic gut loses strength over 2-3 weeks and absorbs completely over 90 days. Chromic catgut induces moderate tissue reaction and is less commonly used in modern dermatologic surgery.

Monocryl (poliglecaprone 25): A monofilament synthetic suture losing strength over 1-2 weeks and absorbing completely over 90-120 days. Monocryl induces minimal tissue reaction and has excellent cosmetic characteristics. It's increasingly popular for dermal closure.

Gut Sutures

Plain catgut, made from untreated animal intestine, is rarely used in dermatology today. It loses strength over 7-10 days and absorbs completely over 30-40 days. Plain gut induces significant tissue reaction and is associated with increased scarring compared to modern synthetic alternatives.

Removal Timeline by Location

Non-absorbable suture removal timing depends on anatomic location and healing characteristics. Facial sutures typically remain 5-7 days, as facial wounds heal rapidly due to excellent blood supply. Scalp sutures should remain 7-10 days. Trunk sutures require 10-14 days. Extremity and lower leg sutures should remain 14-21 days due to slower healing in these areas.

Suture Removal Technique

Your surgeon will remove sutures in the office in a quick, minimally uncomfortable procedure. The area is cleansed, and each suture is lifted with forceps while the underlying strand is cut and pulled out. Occasionally, small pieces of suture material remain embedded; inform your surgeon if sutures appear incompletely removed.

Post-Removal Wound Care

After suture removal, continue wound cleansing and ointment application for several more days. Massage scars gently after suture removal to break up collagen and minimize scarring. The wound remains somewhat fragile immediately after suture removal, so avoid extreme stretching or activities that could separate the healing wound.

Absorbable Suture Aftercare

Absorbable sutures may remain visible at the skin surface for several weeks. Gentle cleansing and ointment application continues as with non-absorbable sutures. If absorbable suture material doesn't dissolve completely or causes persistent irritation, contact your surgeon for possible removal. Some patients require manual removal of incompletely absorbed sutures.
