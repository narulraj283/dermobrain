Wound Dehiscence After Surgery: Causes, Prevention, and Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Understanding Wound Dehiscence

Wound dehiscence is separation of wound edges, occurring either partially (incomplete separation) or completely (entire incision opens). Dehiscence is a serious post-operative complication requiring prompt intervention to prevent infection and minimize scarring. Understanding dehiscence mechanisms, risk factors, and management optimizes outcomes.

Incidence and Timeline

Dehiscence is relatively uncommon in dermatologic surgery (less than 2% incidence) but slightly more common in certain patient populations and surgical locations. Dehiscence typically occurs within the first 5-14 days after surgery when healing strength is minimal but inflammatory response is maximal. Early dehiscence appearing within 48 hours suggests technical surgical problems or excessive suture tension.

Risk Factors for Dehiscence

Multiple factors increase dehiscence risk. Wound tension from excessive suture tightness or poor surgical technique causes tissue ischemia and weakness. Infection increases local inflammation and tissue degradation, promoting separation. Seromas (fluid collections) or hematomas create increased pressure separating wound edges. Poor tissue approximation, particularly if wound edges are not precisely aligned, increases separation risk.

Patient factors include advanced age, malnutrition (particularly protein deficiency), chronic corticosteroid use, immunosuppression, diabetes, and other conditions impairing wound healing. Smoking reduces blood supply and impairs healing. Excessive tension, stretching, or movement of the surgical site postoperatively increases separation risk.

Clinical Presentation

Complete dehiscence presents with obvious opening of the incision with visible gap. Depending on depth, subcutaneous tissue, fat, or deeper structures may be visible. Partial dehiscence shows opening of only outer skin layers, while underlying deep sutures remain intact. Patients often notice wound opening, sometimes with drainage or bleeding.

Some dehiscence begins subtly with slight edge separation or opening at portions of the incline that progress if untreated. Daily wound inspection helps identify early dehiscence before complete separation occurs. Some patients report feeling a pop or tearing sensation when dehiscence occurs.

Infection and Complications

Dehiscence significantly increases infection risk by exposing deeper layers and removing the protective skin barrier. Even sterile-appearing open wounds require careful monitoring for signs of infection. Infection developing within dehisced wounds may spread rapidly into subcutaneous space and deeper tissues.

Dehiscence results in larger, more noticeable scars compared to wounds healing by primary intention. Wider scars with possible depression or hypertrophy result from delayed healing from wound edges inward. The time required for complete healing significantly extends when dehiscence occurs.

Management of Partial Dehiscence

For minor partial dehiscence affecting only small portions of the incision, conservative management with close observation and meticulous wound care is sometimes appropriate. Avoid suture removal in areas that haven't opened; these sutures provide necessary support. Clean the open area thoroughly and apply topical antibiotic ointment and dressing. Cultures should be obtained even if infection isn't yet apparent.

Strict activity restrictions are essential; even minor stretching or movement can propagate partial dehiscence into complete separation. Avoid strenuous activity, heavy lifting, and any activity pulling on the surgical site. Some surgeons prefer early intervention for partial dehiscence rather than risk progression to complete separation.

Management of Complete Dehiscence

Complete dehiscence typically requires re-closure. If dehiscence is recognized within 24-48 hours and infection is absent, primary re-closure under sterile conditions in the office or operating room can be performed. Using the same suture type and technique as the original closure, the surgeon carefully realigns wound edges and re-approximates tissues.

If infection is present or dehiscence is recognized after several days, allow the wound to heal by secondary intention (from inside out) rather than attempting re-closure. Infected wounds require drainage and antibiotic therapy; re-closure in infected wounds dramatically increases complications. Alternatively, delayed primary closure can be performed after infection clears and granulation tissue develops.

Healing by Secondary Intention

When primary re-closure isn't possible or is inappropriate, wounds heal from the bottom and sides inward through granulation tissue formation and epithelialization. This process is slower than primary healing, taking weeks to months depending on wound size. Regular wound care, dressing changes, and monitoring for complications is essential.

Prevention Strategies

Excellent surgical technique with appropriate suture tension is the primary dehiscence prevention strategy. Sutures should be tight enough to approximate edges without tension, but not so tight as to cause ischemia. Proper suture placement with adequate depth and spacing ensures adequate strength.

Layered closure for larger wounds distributes tension across multiple layers, reducing stress on skin closure. Avoiding excessive tension at skin closure is critical. Post-operative activity restrictions help prevent mechanical stress on healing wounds. Patients should strictly avoid heavy lifting, strenuous exercise, and any activity that pulls on the surgical site for appropriate healing period.
