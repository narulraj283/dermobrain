Nail Conditions

Nail Trauma: Assessment and Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Nail trauma encompasses various injuries to the nail unit including crush injuries, lacerations, avulsions, and underlying hematomas. Proper assessment and timely intervention prevent permanent nail deformity and functional impairment. The severity ranges from minor superficial injuries to complete nail loss requiring specialized reconstruction.

Classification of Nail Trauma

Nail injuries are classified by anatomic location and injury type:

  • Nail plate injuries: Breaks, cracks, lacerations, loss
  • Nail matrix injuries: Damage to matrix tissue with potential for permanent deformity
  • Nail bed injuries: Subungual hematoma, laceration, separation
  • Nail fold injuries: Laceration of proximal or lateral folds
  • Bone injuries: Fracture of distal phalanx with nail involvement

Injury severity determines treatment approach and prognosis for normal nail regrowth.

Clinical Assessment

Thorough examination includes assessment for: nail plate fracture or avulsion, matrix or bed laceration, subungual hematoma, distal phalanx fracture, and associated soft tissue injury. Plain radiographs confirm bone involvement. Assessment of nail bed integrity is critical, as unrepaired lacerations result in permanent nail deformity.

Subungual Hematoma Management

Subungual hematomas (blood beneath the nail plate) typically result from crush injuries. Small hematomas (less than 50% of nail surface) can be managed conservatively with elevation and observation. Larger hematomas or those causing significant pain may be evacuated by: trephination (burning small holes in the nail plate with electrocautery or heated paperclip) or removal of the nail plate for direct drainage and assessment of underlying nail bed injury.

Hematomas affecting more than 50% of the nail surface or accompanied by distal phalanx fracture suggest nail bed laceration requiring exploration and repair under direct visualization.

Nail Plate Laceration and Avulsion

Complete nail plate lacerations require nail removal to assess underlying structures. The nail plate is elevated carefully, preserving as much of the plate as possible if reinsertion is planned. Direct visualization allows assessment of nail matrix and bed damage. Nail bed lacerations are repaired with fine absorbable sutures (6-0 or 7-0) to prevent permanent split or ridge in the regenerating nail.

Avulsed nails can sometimes be replanted if the nail plate is not severely damaged and nail bed is suitable for reattachment. Thorough cleansing and nail bed assessment precede any attempt at reattachment. Even if replantation is unsuccessful, the nail matrix examination during reattachment provides useful information regarding future nail quality.

Matrix Injury and Deformity Prevention

Nail matrix lacerations require meticulous repair with fine sutures to prevent permanent nail deformity. Any defect in the matrix can create a permanent split, ridge, or dystrophic nail. Nail matrix damage cannot be fully corrected after healing; prevention through careful repair is essential.

Treatment Protocol

Comprehensive management includes:

  • Anesthesia: Digital block anesthesia provides adequate pain control for exploration and repair
  • Nail removal: Usually required for adequate visualization of matrix and bed
  • Exploration: Assessment of matrix, bed, and underlying bone
  • Repair: Meticulous closure of any lacerations
  • Nail replacement: If nail plate is preserved, reinsertion; if removed, new nail can be applied as dressing
  • Wound care: Sterile dressing, protection from trauma, elevation
  • Tetanus prophylaxis: If indicated by injury circumstance

Return to normal activities is gradual, typically 2-3 weeks for return to most activities, with complete healing by 6-8 weeks.

Prognosis and Complications

Complete nail replacement requires 3-6 months for fingernails and 6-12 months for toenails. Permanent deformity (splits, ridges, or dystrophy) may result from inadequately repaired matrix or bed injuries. Infection risk is reduced through appropriate wound care and tetanus prophylaxis. Functional impairment depends on severity and location of injury.
