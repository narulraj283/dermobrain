Nail Conditions

Paronychia: Acute and Chronic Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Paronychia is inflammation of the tissues surrounding the nail unit, including the lateral and proximal nail folds. The condition exists in acute and chronic forms with distinct etiologies and management approaches. Acute paronychia typically results from bacterial infection, while chronic paronychia involves irritant and allergic contact dermatitis with secondary colonization.

Acute Paronychia

Acute paronychia develops rapidly over hours to days, typically following nail trauma, hangnail manipulation, or manicure injury. Staphylococcus aureus and Streptococcus pyogenes are the most common bacterial pathogens. Clinical presentation includes localized erythema, edema, warmth, and tenderness at the nail fold. Fluctuance indicates abscess formation requiring drainage.

Management of acute paronychia includes: antibiotic soaks in warm saline, topical antibiotics, and oral antibiotics for systemic signs of infection. Abscess drainage via nail fold incision relieves pressure and promotes healing. Some cases require temporary removal of the lateral nail plate for adequate drainage. Recurrent acute paronychia suggests chronic underlying paronychia or nail-biting behavior.

Chronic Paronychia

Chronic paronychia develops insidiously over weeks to months, primarily affecting those with frequent water exposure, such as healthcare workers, dishwashers, and bartenders. The condition results from repeated irritant and allergic contact dermatitis to cleaning products, chemicals, and allergens. Loss of the cuticle seal allows water penetration and secondary microbial colonization (Candida albicans most common, though true Candida paronychia is uncommon).

Clinical features include nail fold edema, erythema, loss of cuticle, and nail plate involvement (thickening, ridging, discoloration). Symptoms include discomfort, particularly with water or cold exposure. The condition often affects multiple fingers, typically sparing the thumb (due to protective positioning).

Pathophysiology and Risk Factors

Chronic paronychia involves disruption of the protective seal provided by the cuticle. Repeated water exposure causes osmotic swelling and maceration of nail fold tissue. Chemical irritants (detergents, acids, solvents) damage the barrier. Secondary Candida colonization perpetuates inflammation but is not the primary cause.

Management of Chronic Paronychia

Treatment focuses on preventing water exposure and removing inciting allergens:

  • Protective measures: Cotton-lined gloves for water exposure, frequent glove changes
  • Moisture control: Keeping nail folds dry, avoiding prolonged water contact
  • Irritant avoidance: Discontinuing harsh soaps and chemicals
  • Nail care: Avoiding aggressive cuticle manipulation
  • Topical therapy: Potent corticosteroids twice daily for 2-4 weeks
  • Allergen testing: If contact dermatitis suspected, patch testing identifies specific allergens

Systemic antifungal therapy has limited evidence in chronic paronychia and is not routinely recommended. Severe cases refractory to conservative management may benefit from intralesional corticosteroid injections or brief course of systemic corticosteroids.

Prognosis

Acute paronychia resolves within 7-10 days with appropriate treatment. Chronic paronychia is prolonged, requiring months of consistent protective measures for resolution. Recurrence is common if preventive behaviors are abandoned. Patient education regarding cause and prevention is essential for long-term management success.
