Spoon Nails (Koilonychia): Causes and Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Spoon nails (koilonychia) are characterized by concave nail plate with loss of normal convexity. The nail appears flattened or concave when viewed from the side, resembling a spoon. While the condition is commonly associated with iron deficiency, multiple etiologies exist. Identifying the underlying cause guides appropriate management and prognosis.

Pathophysiology

Koilonychia results from altered nail matrix keratinization and reduced nail plate thickness. The mechanism is incompletely understood but likely involves: impaired collagen synthesis (iron-dependent), abnormal keratinization, and functional abnormalities of nail matrix keratinizing cells. The concavity develops gradually as affected nail plate grows distally and eventually is shed with normal nail plate replacement.

Etiologies

Multiple conditions produce spoon nails:

  • Iron deficiency: Most common cause, particularly with severe anemia
  • Hemochromatosis: Iron overload paradoxically causes koilonychia
  • Raynaud phenomenon: Vascular insufficiency contributes
  • Chronic hypoxia: Occupational or disease-related
  • Trauma: Repeated nail trauma can produce similar appearance
  • Chemical exposure: Alkali, caustic substances
  • Occupational: Among workers with chronic chemical or physical nail trauma
  • Idiopathic: Some cases have no identifiable etiology

Severity of nail changes may correlate with duration and severity of underlying condition.

Clinical Presentation

Patients typically notice progressive loss of normal nail curvature, with the nail becoming flattened or concave. Some patients report aesthetic concern. Others are asymptomatic, with koilonychia identified incidentally during examination for other conditions. Severe spoon nails may catch on objects or cause functional difficulty. Both fingernails and toenails may be affected.

Associated Clinical Findings

In iron deficiency anemia, associated findings include: pallor, dyspnea, tachycardia, and glossitis. Examination may reveal: pagophagia (ice eating), tachycardia, tachypnea, and other signs of chronic anemia. Addressing the underlying iron deficiency is essential.

Diagnostic Approach

Diagnosis is clinical based on characteristic morphology. Investigation focuses on identifying etiology: iron studies (serum iron, ferritin, TIBC) if iron deficiency suspected, and evaluation for occupational or chemical exposure history. Distinction from artifact or normal variation requires comparison with baseline nail appearance and assessment of other nails.

Differential Diagnosis

Distinguish true spoon nails from: normal nail variation (some individuals naturally have slightly flatter nails), artifact from nail cosmetics or trauma, and lateral nail curvature changes. Evaluation of multiple nails and temporal relationship to potential etiology clarifies diagnosis.

Management

Treatment depends on identified etiology:

  • Iron deficiency: Iron supplementation (ferrous sulfate 325 mg daily) with careful monitoring of iron stores
  • Hemochromatosis: Phlebotomy and iron chelation therapy
  • Chemical exposure: Protective measures, glove use, avoiding caustic substances
  • Occupational hazards: Modification of work practices, protective equipment
  • Idiopathic: Supportive care and reassurance regarding benign nature

With adequate iron repletion, spoon nail changes gradually improve over 3-6 months as normal nail plate replaces affected nail.

Prognosis

Prognosis depends on underlying etiology and treatability. In iron deficiency anemia, complete resolution occurs with iron supplementation and normalization of hemoglobin. In cases with chronic chemical exposure or occupational trauma, prevention of further exposure is essential. Nail changes resolve gradually as affected nail is shed and replaced by normally formed nail.
