Nail Conditions

Ingrown Toenails: Clinical Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Ingrown toenails (unguis incarnatus) occur when the lateral nail edge penetrates the surrounding nail fold, causing inflammation, pain, and potential infection. The condition most commonly affects the big toe and typically begins in adolescence or early adulthood. Approximately 10-12% of the population experiences ingrown toenails at some point.

Etiology and Risk Factors

Multiple factors contribute to ingrown nail development: improper nail trimming (curved rather than straight edges), tight-fitting shoes, excessive nail curvature, nail trauma, poor foot hygiene, and genetic predisposition. Recurrent microbial infection can create a cycle of inflammation and granulation tissue formation. Systemic factors including diabetes and vascular disease increase complication risk.

Clinical Stages

Stage 1 (inflammatory): Erythema and mild discomfort without infection. Stage 2 (abscess formation): Purulent drainage, increased pain, and possible fever. Stage 3 (granulation tissue): Chronic inflammation with excess tissue proliferation at the nail fold. Early intervention prevents progression to advanced stages requiring surgical management.

Conservative Treatment

Initial management includes: antibiotic soaks in warm saline or Epsom salt, proper nail trimming techniques, adequate footwear, and pain management. Some patients benefit from inserting a small cotton wisp under the nail edge to guide proper growth. Oral antibiotics are indicated if signs of systemic infection develop.

Surgical Interventions

Definitive treatment involves partial nail plate avulsion with or without nail matrix ablation. Techniques include: chemical cauterization using phenol, electrocautery, or radiofrequency ablation of the germinal matrix. Matrix destruction reduces recurrence from 10% (simple avulsion) to less than 2% with proper technique. Local anesthesia with digital block provides adequate pain control.

Surgical Procedure Details

The procedure involves nail elevation, removal of the affected portion (typically 4-5 mm lateral edge), and ablation of the corresponding nail matrix. Proper hemostasis and wound care are essential. Patients should avoid tight footwear and keep the area dry for 7-10 days. Return to normal activities occurs within 2-3 weeks, with complete healing in 4-6 weeks.

Complications and Prevention

Potential complications include infection, recurrence, and nail plate deformity. Prevention focuses on proper nail care: cutting nails straight across with slight lengthening beyond the toe, avoiding tight shoes, maintaining foot hygiene, and wearing open-toed footwear when possible. Patients with diabetes or peripheral vascular disease require close monitoring due to increased infection risk.
