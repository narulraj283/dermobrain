Managing Pemphigus Vulgaris: Evidence-Based Approaches

Definition and Epidemiology

Pemphigus vulgaris is an autoimmune blistering disorder with anti-desmoglein 3 (and anti-desmoglein 1 in some mucocutaneous cases) IgG antibodies causing acantholysis (loss of cell-to-cell adhesion). Incidence 0.5-4 per million annually; affects middle-aged and elderly primarily. Jewish, Mediterranean, and Indian ancestry at higher risk.

Pathophysiology

IgG autoantibodies against desmoglein 3 and/or desmoglein 1 (components of desmosomes connecting keratinocytes) bind to antigens causing loss of cell adhesion. Complement activation and phosphorylation of desmogleins lead to acantholysis. Loss of intact desmosomes results in flaccid intraepidermal blistering.

Clinical Features

Mucosal Involvement: Most common initial presentation (90% of patients). Erosions on buccal mucosa, palate, and tongue; painful, limiting eating and speaking. Erosions bleed easily; secondary infection risk.

Cutaneous Involvement: Flaccid blisters on erythematous base; rupture easily leaving painful erosions. Lesions typically appear on flexures, abdomen, and intertriginous areas. Positive Nikolsky sign (epidermal separation with gentle rubbing/pressure).

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis confirmed by: (1) histopathology showing intraepidermal acantholysis with "tombstone" appearance of basal cells, (2) direct immunofluorescence showing intercellular IgG deposition (pathognomonic), (3) serology with anti-desmoglein 3 and anti-desmoglein 1 IgG antibodies.

Treatment: Systemic Corticosteroids

First-Line: Prednisone 1-2mg/kg/day (typically 60-100mg) with gradual taper as clinical remission achieved. Takes 2-4 weeks to see response; slow taper over months to prevent relapse. Alternate-day dosing once-daily dosing reduced to 50% of previous dose (e.g., if on 60mg daily, alternate between 60mg one day and 30mg the next).

Long-Term Management: Most patients require prolonged therapy (years); discontinuation risks relapse in 50-70% of cases.

Steroid-Sparing Agents

Azathioprine: 1-2mg/kg/day allows corticosteroid dose reduction by 50% in many patients. Requires TPMT testing and baseline laboratory assessment.

Mycophenolate Mofetil: 2-3g daily in divided doses. Fewer drug interactions than azathioprine; emerging as preferred steroid-sparing agent.

Methotrexate: 7.5-25mg weekly for steroid-sparing effect, though less extensively studied than azathioprine/mycophenolate.

Biological Therapy

Rituximab: Anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody targeting B cells and plasma cells. Multiple dosing regimens studied (375mg/m² weekly × 4 weeks, or 1000mg IV days 1 and 15). Emerging evidence supports efficacy for severe, steroid-refractory pemphigus. Potential for remission even after discontinuation of corticosteroids.

Topical Therapy

Topical Corticosteroids: High-potency agents (clobetasol 0.05% cream) applied to skin lesions twice daily. Minimal systemic absorption; useful adjunct to systemic therapy.

Mouth Rinses: Topical anesthetic rinses (viscous lidocaine) for oral pain relief. Antimicrobial rinses (chlorhexidine 0.12%) prevent secondary infection.

Management of Complications

Secondary Infections: Monitor for bacterial superinfection of erosions; use topical antibiotics or systemic antibiotics if systemic infection develops.

Steroid Side Effects: Osteoporosis prevention (calcium, vitamin D, bisphosphonates), PCP prophylaxis if CD4 <200, GI protection (PPI for stress ulcer prophylaxis).
