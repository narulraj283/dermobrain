Managing Warts (Verruca Vulgaris): Causes, Symptoms, and Treatment Options

Definition and Epidemiology

Warts are benign proliferative skin lesions caused by human papillomavirus (HPV) infection. Prevalence 7-12% in general population; higher in children and young adults. Over 100 HPV types identified; common cutaneous types include HPV-1, HPV-2, HPV-4, HPV-27, HPV-29. Transmission through direct contact with infected individuals or contaminated surfaces; autoinoculation common.

Pathophysiology

HPV infects basal keratinocytes, triggering keratinocyte proliferation and hyperkeratinization. Viral presence stimulates cellular proliferation without cell transformation (benign lesions). Immune control determines resolution; most warts (90%) spontaneously resolve within 2 years, though may persist indefinitely.

Clinical Types

Common Warts: Firm, discrete, hyperkeratotic papules (2-8mm) typically on hands, fingers, elbows, knees. May have central "thrombosed vessels" (black dots).

Plantar Warts: Hyperkeratotic lesions on plantar surface often with central black dots. Painful due to pressure and location.

Palmoplantar Warts: Occur on palms and soles; mosaic pattern with multiple coalesced lesions.

Flat Warts: Flat-topped, small (1-3mm), numerous papules typically on face, hands, trunk; skin-colored or slightly hyperpigmented.

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis based on morphology and distribution. Dermoscopy shows characteristic vascular patterns (punctate, comma-like, or hairpin vessels). HPV typing unnecessary for cutaneous warts; reserved for genital/plantar types if malignancy suspected.

Treatment Options: First-Line

Cryotherapy: Liquid nitrogen applied every 2-4 weeks until resolution (average 3-4 treatments). Efficacy 50-70%; modest pain. Cryotherapy induces local immune response facilitating clearance.

Salicylic Acid: 17% cream or solution applied daily after soaking in water for 10 minutes. Cover with occlusive dressing. Treatment duration weeks to months. Efficacy 50-60%; excellent for widespread lesions. Caustic agent; protect surrounding skin.

Imiquimod: 5% cream applied 3-5 times weekly for 12-16 weeks. Toll-like receptor agonist stimulating local immune response. Efficacy 50-60%; slower onset than cryotherapy but good for difficult-to-treat areas.

Treatment Options: Second-Line

Podofilox: 0.5% solution or gel applied twice daily for 3 days weekly. Cytotoxic agent; efficacy 50-60%. Contraindicated in pregnancy and large surface area application.

Bleomycin: Intralesional injection (0.1-0.5mg per lesion) repeated every 3-4 weeks. Highly effective (90%) for resistant warts. Systemic absorption risk with extensive treatment; monitor renal function.

Treatment in Special Populations

Plantar Warts: Address causative factors (pressure, moisture). Cryotherapy, salicylic acid, or combination therapy. Pain management essential. Orthotic modifications (padding) provide symptomatic relief.

Extensive or Refractory Warts: Consider systemic retinoids (oral isotretinoin) or photodynamic therapy for extensive involvement unresponsive to standard modalities.

Natural History and Counseling

Majority (90%) spontaneously resolve within 2 years. Treatment accelerates resolution but high recurrence rates (20-50%) common. Set realistic expectations; reassurance about benign nature reduces anxiety. Avoid self-treatment beyond topical agents due to infection/scarring risk.

Prevention

HPV vaccines (Gardasil 9) prevent multiple cutaneous HPV types. Recommended for ages 11-45 years. Although primarily marketed for genital cancer prevention, emerging evidence suggests benefit for cutaneous warts.
