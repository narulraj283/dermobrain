Guttate Psoriasis: A Comprehensive Clinical Guide

By DermoBrain Medical Editorial Team Updated 2026-03-06 Medically Reviewed ⏱ 1 min read

Table of Contents

  1. Definition and Pathogenesis
  2. Clinical Features
  3. Diagnosis
  4. Treatment
  5. Prognosis
  6. References

Definition and Pathogenesis

Guttate psoriasis is an acute form of psoriasis characterized by sudden onset of numerous small (1-10mm), drop-like erythematous papules typically following group A streptococcal pharyngitis. The condition affects 1-2% of psoriatic patients. Approximately 80% of guttate psoriasis cases are preceded by streptococcal pharyngitis within 2-3 weeks of rash onset.

Clinical Features

Lesions appear acutely over 2-7 days, covering face, trunk, and proximal extremities. Individual papules are similar in size and morphology, distinguishing guttate from chronic plaque psoriasis. Pruritus and pain are variable. Natural history is favorable: 80-90% of patients achieve complete or near-complete resolution within 3-6 months without specific therapy.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is clinical based on acute onset after documented streptococcal infection and characteristic morphology. Laboratory confirmation: elevated anti-streptococcal titers (ASO, anti-DNase B) are present in 80% of cases.

Treatment

Streptococcal Management: Administer penicillin V (500mg 3-4 times daily for 10 days) or cephalexin (500mg 3-4 times daily for 10 days).

Topical Therapy: Mild cases respond to mid-potency topical corticosteroids (triamcinolone 0.1% cream) applied twice daily.

Phototherapy: NB-UVB phototherapy (2-3 times weekly) accelerates resolution with improvement visible within 4-8 weeks.

Prognosis

80-90% spontaneous resolution within 6 months but 30-40% develop chronic plaque psoriasis within 10 years. Long-term follow-up is recommended to monitor for emergence of plaque psoriasis.
