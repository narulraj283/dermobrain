Acne Vulgaris: Causes, Symptoms, and Treatment Options

Epidemiology and Clinical Impact

Acne vulgaris affects approximately 85% of adolescents aged 12-24 years, with peak incidence during teenage years. The condition persists into adulthood in 12-14% of adults, particularly women. Annual healthcare costs exceed $1.2 billion in the United States alone.

Pathophysiology of Acne Vulgaris

Acne develops through four interconnected pathophysiologic mechanisms: (1) increased sebum production stimulated by androgens, (2) follicular hyperkeratinization causing comedone formation, (3) bacterial colonization by Cutibacterium acnes, and (4) innate and adaptive immune-mediated inflammation. Androgens, particularly dihydrotestosterone (DHT), increase sebaceous gland size and sebum production. The bacterium's lipopolysaccharide activates toll-like receptors, triggering IL-6, IL-8, and TNF-α production.

Clinical Presentation and Morphology

Acne presents with distinctive lesion types: comedones (blackheads and whiteheads from follicular obstruction), inflammatory papules and pustules (1-5mm raised lesions), nodules (>5mm deeper lesions), and cysts (fluid-filled nodules). Lesions predominantly appear on the face, chest, shoulders, and back where sebaceous gland density is highest.

First-Line Treatment Approaches

Topical Retinoids: Tretinoin (0.025-0.1%), adapalene (0.1%), normalize follicular keratinization. Start with lowest concentrations applied 3 times weekly, increasing to nightly use.

Benzoyl Peroxide: 2.5%, 5%, and 10% formulations provide bactericidal activity. Effective monotherapy for mild acne; combines synergistically with topical antibiotics. Use 1-2 times daily.

Combination Therapy: Benzoyl peroxide (5%) with clindamycin 1% twice daily is superior to either agent alone. Adapalene 0.3% plus benzoyl peroxide 2.5% (Epiduo) reduces resistance and improves compliance.

Oral Antibiotic Therapy

Oral antibiotics address inflammatory acne through bacterial suppression and anti-inflammatory effects. Doxycycline (100mg daily) and minocycline (50-100mg daily) are first-line choices due to superior skin penetration and anti-inflammatory properties. Azithromycin (250mg 3 times weekly) provides alternatives for doxycycline-intolerant patients. Always combine oral antibiotics with benzoyl peroxide to prevent resistance; maximum duration is 12 weeks.

Hormonal Therapy

Oral contraceptives containing ethinyl estradiol with norgestimate, norethindrone, or drospirenone reduce androgens and sebum production. Norgestimate-containing pills show 31-35% acne improvement versus placebo. Spironolactone (50-200mg daily) effectively treats inflammatory acne in women by blocking androgen receptors on sebaceous glands.

Isotretinoin Therapy

Isotretinoin (0.5-1.0mg/kg/day, cumulative dose target 120-150mg/kg) is curative for severe nodular acne. Approximately 90% of patients achieve long-term remission or cure. Requires iPLEDGE registration, monthly pregnancy tests for females, and baseline lipid/liver function testing. Common side effects include cheilitis (100%), xerosis (80%), and arthralgias (25%).

Prevention and Lifestyle Management

Gentle twice-daily facial cleansing with non-comedogenic cleansers minimizes irritation. Non-comedogenic moisturizers and sunscreens (SPF 30+) address dryness from retinoids. Avoid excessive facial manipulation and oil-based cosmetics.
