Acne: Causes, Treatments, and What Actually Works

The Bottom Line

Acne is the most common skin condition in the world — if you have it, you're far from alone. It happens when oil glands in your skin overproduce, dead skin cells clog your pores, and bacteria trigger inflammation. Hormones (especially during puberty, periods, or stress) are a major driver.

The good news: acne is very treatable. Mild cases often respond to over-the-counter products with benzoyl peroxide or adapalene (Differin). Moderate acne may need prescription retinoids or antibiotics. For severe or scarring acne, isotretinoin (formerly known as Accutane) remains the most effective option — it clears acne in about 85% of patients after one course.

When to see a dermatologist: If over-the-counter products haven't helped after 8-12 weeks, if you're developing scars, or if acne is affecting your confidence or mood.

What Causes Acne?

Acne develops in your skin's oil glands (called pilosebaceous units). Four things happen together to create a breakout:

Excess oil production: Your sebaceous glands produce an oily substance called sebum. During puberty, hormones — particularly dihydrotestosterone (DHT) — cause these glands to enlarge and produce more oil. This is why acne peaks in the teenage years, affecting roughly 85% of people aged 12-24.

Clogged pores: Dead skin cells that normally shed get stuck together and block the hair follicle opening. This creates a plug called a comedone — the technical term for a whitehead (closed) or blackhead (open).

Bacterial growth: A bacterium called Cutibacterium acnes (C. acnes) lives naturally on everyone's skin. When pores get clogged, C. acnes multiplies inside the trapped oil. The bacteria release enzymes that break down sebum into inflammatory fatty acids.

Inflammation: Your immune system responds to the bacterial overgrowth by sending white blood cells to the area. This inflammatory response is what turns a clogged pore into a red, painful pimple. In severe cases, the inflammation goes deep, creating nodules and cysts that can scar.

Types of Acne

Dermatologists classify acne by the type and severity of lesions present:

Comedonal acne (mild): Mostly blackheads and whiteheads without significant redness. This is the earliest form and responds well to topical retinoids.

Inflammatory acne (moderate): Red papules (small bumps) and pustules (bumps with visible pus) alongside comedones. This is the classic "breakout" most people picture.

Nodulocystic acne (severe): Deep, painful nodules and cysts under the skin, often on the jawline, cheeks, and back. This type carries the highest risk of permanent scarring and typically needs systemic treatment.

Dermatologists use grading scales to track severity over time. The Global Acne Grading System (GAGS) scores lesions by location and type, helping your doctor measure whether treatment is working.

Who Gets Acne and Why?

Acne affects approximately 85% of teenagers, making it nearly universal during adolescence. But it's not just a teenage problem — 12-14% of adults aged 25-44 still deal with breakouts, and adult acne is more common in women due to hormonal fluctuations during menstrual cycles, pregnancy, and perimenopause.

Genetics play a significant role. If both parents had acne, you're roughly three times more likely to develop moderate-to-severe acne. Certain medications — including lithium, corticosteroids, and some anticonvulsants — can trigger or worsen breakouts. Despite popular belief, research has not confirmed that chocolate or greasy foods directly cause acne, though high-glycemic diets (lots of sugar and refined carbs) may contribute in some individuals.

The psychological impact is real and well-documented. Studies show depression and anxiety occur in about 27% of people with moderate-to-severe acne. If acne is affecting your mental health, that's a valid medical reason to pursue treatment — not something to dismiss.

Over-the-Counter Treatments

For mild acne, several effective products are available without a prescription:

Benzoyl peroxide (2.5-10%): Kills C. acnes bacteria and reduces inflammation. Research shows 2.5% is often just as effective as higher strengths but causes less dryness and irritation. Apply once daily to start, then increase to twice daily if tolerated. Expect some dryness and peeling in the first two weeks — this usually improves.

Adapalene 0.1% (Differin): This retinoid was prescription-only until 2016 and remains one of the most effective topical acne treatments available. It unclogs pores, reduces inflammation, and prevents new breakouts. Apply a pea-sized amount to the entire face (not just spots) at night. Your skin may get worse before it gets better — this "retinoid purge" typically lasts 4-6 weeks.

Salicylic acid (0.5-2%): A beta-hydroxy acid that penetrates into pores to dissolve the debris causing clogs. Best for blackhead-predominant acne. Gentler than benzoyl peroxide but generally less effective for inflammatory breakouts.

Important: Give any treatment at least 8-12 weeks before judging its effectiveness. Acne treatments work by preventing new breakouts, not by healing existing ones overnight.

Prescription Treatments

When over-the-counter products aren't enough, dermatologists have a strong toolkit of prescription options:

Topical retinoids (tretinoin, tazarotene): Stronger versions of adapalene. Tretinoin (Retin-A) comes in strengths from 0.025% to 0.1%. Your dermatologist typically starts at the lowest strength and increases as your skin adjusts. These are considered the foundation of most acne treatment plans.

Topical antibiotics: Clindamycin 1% lotion or gel kills acne-causing bacteria on the skin surface. Almost always prescribed alongside benzoyl peroxide to prevent antibiotic resistance — never as a solo treatment.

Oral antibiotics: Doxycycline (50-100mg daily) or minocycline are used for moderate inflammatory acne. These reduce the bacterial load and calm inflammation. Most dermatologists limit antibiotic courses to 3-4 months and transition to topical maintenance afterward.

Hormonal therapy (women): Combined oral contraceptives containing ethinyl estradiol with drospirenone (Yaz), norgestimate (Ortho Tri-Cyclen), or norethindrone acetate are FDA-approved for acne in women. Spironolactone (50-200mg daily) blocks androgen receptors and is widely used off-label for hormonal acne along the jawline and chin. Results take 2-3 months to appear.

Isotretinoin (Accutane): The most powerful acne medication available. It's the only treatment that addresses all four causes of acne simultaneously — it shrinks oil glands by up to 90%, normalizes skin cell shedding, reduces C. acnes populations, and calms inflammation. Prescribed at 0.5-1.0 mg per kilogram of body weight daily for 4-6 months, it produces lasting clearance in roughly 85% of patients after one course. Because isotretinoin causes birth defects, women must enroll in the iPLEDGE monitoring program and use two forms of contraception. Other side effects include very dry skin and lips, occasional muscle aches, and rarely, mood changes. Monthly blood tests monitor liver function and cholesterol.

What to Expect From Treatment

Acne treatment is a marathon, not a sprint. Most topical treatments take 6-12 weeks to show meaningful improvement. Isotretinoin typically begins visibly clearing skin around month 2-3. Your dermatologist will adjust your treatment plan based on how you respond, often combining approaches for the best results.

After acne clears, maintenance therapy (usually a topical retinoid applied nightly) prevents relapse. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (dark spots left after breakouts heal) fades over 3-12 months. For pitted scars, options include laser resurfacing, microneedling, and chemical peels — but these work best once active acne is controlled.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does diet cause acne?

Research hasn't proven that specific foods like chocolate or pizza directly cause acne. However, some studies suggest high-glycemic diets (heavy in sugar, white bread, and processed carbs) may worsen breakouts in acne-prone individuals. Skim milk has also been weakly associated with acne in some studies. The effect is modest though — diet changes alone rarely clear acne.

Can I pop my pimples?

Dermatologists strongly advise against it. Squeezing pushes bacteria and inflammation deeper into the skin, increasing the risk of scarring and prolonging healing. If you have a large, painful cyst, a dermatologist can inject it with a dilute corticosteroid for rapid relief — this is safe and usually flattens it within 24-48 hours.

Is Accutane safe?

Isotretinoin is safe when prescribed and monitored properly. The most common side effects — dry skin, dry lips, and occasional muscle soreness — are manageable and temporary. The concern about depression has been studied extensively, and large studies have not found a causal link; in fact, many patients report improved mood as their skin clears. The iPLEDGE program exists to prevent pregnancy during treatment, as the drug causes birth defects. Monthly bloodwork ensures safety throughout the course.

When should I see a dermatologist instead of treating acne at home?

See a dermatologist if over-the-counter products haven't improved your acne after 3 months, if you're developing scars (indentations or raised marks), if your acne is painful or cystic, or if breakouts are affecting your self-esteem or mental health. Early treatment prevents scarring — the sooner you address it, the better the outcome.
