Acne Fulminans: Acute Ulcerative Acne Emergency

Definition and Epidemiology

Acne fulminans is the most severe form of acne vulgaris, characterized by acute, explosive onset of severe nodular acne with systemic manifestations. Rare (0.1% of acne cases). Predominantly affects adolescent males. Often associated with isotretinoin initiation or withdrawal (particularly with concurrent systemic corticosteroids taper). Can occur spontaneously without preceding acne.

Pathophysiology

Severe abnormal immune response to Cutibacterium acnes with intense neutrophilic and lymphocytic infiltration. Follicular rupture produces extensive tissue necrosis and abscess formation. Systemic IL-6 elevation correlates with severity. Testosterone surge during puberty and genetic predisposition important cofactors.

Clinical Features

Skin Manifestations: Sudden eruption of large, hemorrhagic, often ulcerative nodules and cysts coalescing into extensive inflammatory plaques. Draining sinuses with purulent discharge. Predominantly on chest, shoulders, and back; face less affected. Severe pain, tenderness, and edema.

Systemic Symptoms: Fever (often >38.5°C), malaise, headache, arthralgia (particularly knees and elbows), myalgia. Hepatosplenomegaly possible. Constitutional symptoms profound; patients severely ill.

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis based on acute onset of severe ulcerative nodular acne with systemic symptoms. Elevated inflammatory markers: ESR elevated, CRP elevated, WBC often elevated (12,000-16,000). Chest imaging (X-ray, CT) if respiratory symptoms or pulmonary involvement suspected. Severe cases may present with sepsis-like picture requiring ICU admission.

Management: Systemic Corticosteroids

First-Line Therapy: Systemic corticosteroids are absolutely indicated and dramatically improve acne fulminans. Prednisone 0.5-1mg/kg/day (typically 40-80mg daily) for 1-4 weeks with gradual taper. Rapid improvement visible within 7-10 days; systemic symptoms resolve first, cutaneous lesions more gradual.

Dosing and Taper: Cannot be abruptly discontinued; slow taper over 2-4 weeks prevents rebound exacerbation. Typical approach: high-dose for 1-2 weeks, then reduce by 10% every 3-5 days.

Management: Isotretinoin

Timing: Initiate isotretinoin after systemic corticosteroids achieve disease control (typically 2-4 weeks of steroid therapy). Concurrent isotretinoin + systemic corticosteroids at high doses increases adverse effects risk; sequential therapy preferable.

Dosing: Standard acne conglobata dosing: 0.5-1mg/kg/day with cumulative target 120-150mg/kg. Essential therapy; prevents recurrence and halts progression to lifelong scarring.

Supportive Care

Pain management: NSAIDs, acetaminophen, opioids for severe cases. Cool compresses reduce discomfort. Psychological support essential given acute severity and distress. Nutritional support may be needed if systemic involvement severe.

Complications

Secondary bacterial infection common; broad-spectrum antibiotics if signs of infection. Arthritis develops in some cases (particularly knee arthritis); typically resolves with steroid therapy. Permanent hypertrophic/keloid scarring in 80%+ of cases despite treatment; requires scar revision later.

Prognosis

With appropriate systemic corticosteroid therapy followed by isotretinoin, most patients achieve disease control. However, extensive permanent scarring is common and requires revision procedures (subcision, laser, microneedling) 6-12 months after disease resolution. Psychosocial recovery may take years.
