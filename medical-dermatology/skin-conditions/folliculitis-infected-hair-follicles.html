Folliculitis: Infected Hair Follicles

Understanding Folliculitis

Folliculitis is inflammation of hair follicles from bacterial (typically Staphylococcus aureus or Pseudomonas aeruginosa), fungal (Malassezia, dermatophytes), or irritative causes, affecting 20-40% of population at some point. The condition presents as small pustules/papules centered on hair follicles, commonly on beard area, chest, back, and lower extremities. While usually self-limited, folliculitis causes cosmetic concern and discomfort; persistent cases suggest immunosuppression, colonization with pathogenic organisms, or underlying predisposing factors requiring investigation.

Epidemiology

Folliculitis affects 20-40% of population during lifetime. Pseudomonas aeruginosa folliculitis (hot tub folliculitis) affects 50-80% of users of inadequately chlorinated hot tubs/spas. Bacterial folliculitis most common (80%); fungal folliculitis (Malassezia, dermatophytes) accounts for 15-20%; irritative folliculitis (shaving/waxing-related) affects 30-40% of those performing regular hair removal.

Clinical Types

Superficial folliculitis: small pustules/papules on follicular openings, typically painless or mildly tender. Deep folliculitis/furuncles: larger nodules with central fluctuance (see furuncles/carbuncles section). Pseudomonas folliculitis: widespread pustules 24-72 hours post-exposure to contaminated water (characteristic distribution: areas covered by swimsuit). Fungal folliculitis (Malassezia): monomorphic 2-3 mm pustules on chest/back, mildly pruritic. Irritative folliculitis: post-shaving/waxing papules, typically painless, resolving with hair growth.

Diagnosis and Treatment

Diagnosis clinical; culture helpful if recurrent (identifies organism, guides therapy). Topical antibacterial agents: clindamycin lotion or benzoyl peroxide 2.5-10% applied twice daily for superficial folliculitis achieves 80-90% improvement within 2-4 weeks. Oral antibiotics for extensive/deep folliculitis: cephalexin 500-1000 mg four times daily or trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (if MRSA risk) for 10-14 days, achieving 90-95% cure. Pseudomonas folliculitis: usually self-limited (resolve within 1-2 weeks); symptomatic treatment with topical/oral antibiotics hastens recovery. Fungal folliculitis: topical azoles or oral itraconazole (see fungal section).

Prevention

Avoid shared razors/towels, maintain clean tools with regular disinfection, reduce friction irritation (electric razors preferable to blade shaving), apply warm compresses post-shaving. High-risk individuals benefit from prophylactic topical antibacterial use (benzoyl peroxide 2.5% nightly as cleanser) reducing recurrence from 40-50% to <10%.
