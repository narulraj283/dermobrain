Common Warts: HPV Types and Removal Options

Understanding Common Warts

Common warts (verruca vulgaris) are benign epithelial proliferations caused by human papillomavirus (HPV), particularly types 2, 4, and 27, affecting 20-40% of population at some point. Warts present as hyperkeratotic papules typically on hands/fingers, transmitted through direct skin contact or fomites. While entirely benign with no malignant potential (non-genital HPV types carry negligible cancer risk), warts cause cosmetic concern and occasional discomfort from trauma/ulceration. The condition is self-limited—95% of common warts spontaneously regress within 1-2 years as immune system clears HPV infection. Aggressive treatment may not improve this natural history, making conservative management or observation reasonable options. When treatment desired, multiple modalities offer 60-90% initial clearance, though recurrence rates 15-30% reflect immune incompleteness.

Epidemiology

Common warts affect 20-40% of population. Peak incidence age 15-35 years but occurs at any age. Children show 5-10% prevalence; increases through young adulthood, decreasing after age 40-50. Risk factors: immunosuppression, occupation (high trauma/minor cuts), genetic predisposition (HLA status influences susceptibility). Transmission requires direct contact with infected person or fomite contact; not highly contagious compared to molluscum contagiosum.

Pathophysiology

HPV infection establishes in basal keratinocytes with viral DNA typically episomal (non-integrated). Viral replication induces keratinocyte hyperproliferation, creating characteristic acanthosis and papillomatosis. Immune response (cell-mediated immunity) gradually recognizes infected keratinocytes and clears virus over months-years. Immune suppression prolongs wart persistence; immunocompromised patients develop more numerous and larger warts. CD4+ T cell response critical for viral clearance—those with adequate CD4+ response clear warts; those with impaired response develop persistent/recurrent warts.

Clinical Presentation

Common warts present as 2-10 mm hyperkeratotic papules with rough surface, discrete borders, typically on hands, fingers, knuckles. Characteristic "wart" appearance with visible vessels/pinpoint hemorrhages within keratin. Often solitary but multiple lesions common (50-60% of patients). Lesions stable or slowly enlarging. Associated trauma (picking, cutting) may cause painful bleeding/ulceration or viral spread to new areas through autoinoculation.

Treatment Approaches

Salicylic acid 17-40% applied topically daily for 4-12 weeks achieves 60-75% clearance through keratin digestion and direct antiviral effect. Superior results with occlusion (duct tape application showed similar efficacy in meta-analyses). Cryotherapy (liquid nitrogen application monthly) achieves 70-85% clearance within 3-4 months through cryonecrosis of infected tissue; post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation common (10-20% of treated lesions). Combination therapy (cryotherapy plus topical immunomodulator imiquimod) improves clearance to 85-95%. Curettage, CO2 laser, or pulsed dye laser alternatives for refractory cases but require anesthesia and leave small scars (2-5% of cases). Intralesional immunotherapy (imiquimod, interferon, candida antigen) shows 60-80% efficacy by stimulating local immune response.

Prognosis

Spontaneous regression occurs in 95% within 1-2 years as immune response clears HPV. Treatment accelerates clearance but doesn't improve ultimate cure rate (still 95% cleared by 2 years). Recurrence 15-30% within 1 year post-treatment due to persistent subclinical HPV or reinfection. Observation is reasonable for asymptomatic lesions given high spontaneous resolution rate.
