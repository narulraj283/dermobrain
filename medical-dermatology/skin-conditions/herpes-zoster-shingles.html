Herpes Zoster (Shingles): Clinical Presentation and Therapeutic Options

Definition and Epidemiology

Herpes zoster (shingles) results from reactivation of latent varicella-zoster virus (VZV) in dorsal root ganglia following primary varicella infection or vaccination. Incidence 1.2-3.4 per 1000 person-years in immunocompetent hosts; significantly higher in immunocompromised and elderly (>60 years). Lifetime risk of zoster approximately 30%. Recurrence uncommon (3-4% of cases).

Pathophysiology

VZV remains latent in sensory ganglia after primary infection. Immunological decline, stress, radiation therapy, or malignancy allows viral reactivation. Virus travels along sensory nerve distributions causing dermatomal vesicular eruption. Intense inflammation and nerve involvement result in severe pain (often the most distressing symptom).

Clinical Features: Early Stage

Prodromal phase (2-7 days) characterized by dermatomal pain, burning, pruritus, or paresthesias without visible rash. Dysesthesia and hyperesthesia of affected dermatome. Constitutional symptoms (fever, malaise, headache) common. Thoracic (55%) and cervical (15%) dermatomes most frequently affected.

Clinical Features: Vesicular Stage

Grouped vesicles on erythematous base in dermatomal distribution. Vesicles umbilicate and crust within 7-10 days. Pustulization from secondary bacterial infection common. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and hypopigmentation frequent. Unilateral involvement distinguishes zoster from disseminated varicella.

Complications

Post-Herpetic Neuralgia (PHN): Persistent pain (>3 months post-rash onset) in 10-50% of patients; increases with age (>50% risk in patients >60 years). Most bothersome complication causing significant quality-of-life impairment and disability.

Other Complications: Zoster ophthalmicus (V1 involvement) causing ocular complications (keratitis, uveitis, vision loss), Bell palsy (VII involvement), meningitis/encephalitis (rare).

Treatment: Antiviral Therapy

First-Line: Acyclovir 800mg five times daily for 7 days initiated within 72 hours of rash onset. Reduces acute pain duration and post-herpetic neuralgia risk by ~50%.

Alternative Options: Valacyclovir 1g three times daily × 7 days (superior bioavailability; dosing convenience) or famciclovir 500mg three times daily × 7 days (similar to valacyclovir).

IV Therapy: Acyclovir 10-15mg/kg every 8 hours for disseminated zoster, ophthalmic zoster, meningitis, encephalitis, or immunocompromised patients.

Adjunctive Treatments for Pain

Systemic Corticosteroids: Prednisone 30mg daily × 7 days, then taper over 2 weeks reduces acute pain and improves function; minimal impact on PHN development. Benefit greatest if initiated within 72 hours.

Gabapentinoids: Gabapentin starting 100mg three times daily escalating to 1800-3600mg daily in divided doses or pregabalin 150mg daily escalating to 600mg daily. Most effective for PHN prevention if initiated early (during acute phase).

Topical Agents: Capsaicin cream 0.075% applied 3-5 times daily to healed lesions provides PHN relief. Lidocaine patches applied to painful areas. Avoid application to open lesions.

Management of Post-Herpetic Neuralgia

Preventive approach superior to treatment. Early antiviral therapy, adjunctive corticosteroids, and gabapentinoids initiated during acute phase reduce PHN incidence and severity. Established PHN treated with gabapentinoids, tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline 25-75mg nightly), topical capsaicin or lidocaine patches, and potentially opioids for refractory cases.

Vaccination

Zoster vaccine (Shingrix, recombinant subunit vaccine) recommended for all adults ≥50 years and immunocompromised age ≥19 years. Two-dose series 2-6 months apart. Efficacy >90% in preventing zoster and >90% in preventing PHN. Two doses provide durable protection.
