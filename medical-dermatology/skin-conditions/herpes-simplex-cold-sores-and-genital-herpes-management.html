Herpes Simplex: Cold Sores and Genital Herpes Management

Understanding Herpes Simplex

Herpes simplex virus (HSV) types 1 and 2 affect 50-90% of population globally. HSV-1 causes oral cold sores (70-80% of primary infections); HSV-2 causes genital herpes (90% of genital ulcers). Following primary infection, virus establishes latency in sensory neurons with periodic reactivation (50-80% of infected individuals experience recurrence within 12 months of primary infection). Recurrent episodes typically milder than primary infection (reduced severity/duration/viral shedding). Antiviral therapy within 24 hours of symptom onset reduces lesion duration by 24-48 hours and recurrence frequency 40-50% with suppressive therapy. While entirely benign in immunocompetent individuals, dissemination to CNS (meningitis/encephalitis risk 0.1-1%), eyes (keratitis/uveitis risk 0.5-2%), and neonatal herpes (maternal-neonatal transmission risk 30-40% with active genital lesions at delivery) require serious management.

Epidemiology

HSV-1: 50-90% of population (varies by region/SES); primary infection typically childhood, 5% of those infected develop symptomatic cold sores. HSV-2: 15-25% of population; sexually transmitted, 10-15% of infected experience clinically evident genital herpes. Recurrence rates: 30-50% experience recurrence within 12 months of primary infection; frequency decreases over years (5-10 recurrences/year initially, declining to <1 recurrence annually after 5-10 years in most individuals).

Clinical Manifestation

Primary infection: fever, malaise, regional lymphadenopathy followed by grouped vesicles on erythematous base progressing to pustules, crusting, ulcers within 7-10 days. Recurrent episodes milder: prodrome (tingling, burning 24-48 hours before rash), vesicles typically <5 mm, fewer lesions, faster healing (5-7 days versus 10-14 days primary). Associated pain, particularly in genital herpes affecting urination. Systemic toxicity minimal in recurrences; primary infection may cause severe systemic symptoms in 20-30% of patients.

Treatment Protocols

Primary oral HSV-1 (cold sores): acyclovir 400 mg five times daily or valacyclovir 1000 mg three times daily for 7-10 days, achieving 25-30% duration reduction if initiated within 24 hours. Genital HSV-2 primary: acyclovir 400 mg five times daily for 7-10 days (IV form if severe). Recurrent episodes: topical acyclovir 5% cream applied five times daily or valacyclovir 500 mg twice daily for 3-5 days achieves 1-2 day duration reduction if initiated during prodrome. Suppressive therapy (acyclovir 400-800 mg twice daily, valacyclovir 500-1000 mg daily, famciclovir 250 mg twice daily) reduces recurrence frequency 75-80% and severity 50-70%; continued 12 months minimum, then reassess given declining recurrence with time.

Prevention and Complications

Sexual abstinence during prodrome/active lesions reduces transmission risk 95% versus 70% with asymptomatic shedding during suppressive therapy. Condom use 60-80% protective. Pregnant women with genital HSV: cesarean delivery recommended if active lesions at delivery (neonatal transmission risk 30-40% with vaginal delivery, <2% with cesarean). Antiviral prophylaxis (acyclovir 400 mg three times daily starting 4 weeks before expected delivery) reduces recurrence risk 75-80%, reducing need for cesarean to <5% of cases.
