Nail Psoriasis: How Psoriasis Affects Your Nails

Understanding Nail Psoriasis

Nail psoriasis represents a significant clinical manifestation occurring in 10-55% of patients with psoriasis, with nail involvement often preceding cutaneous disease by 5-10 years in approximately 15% of cases. The condition affects both fingernails and toenails, impacting quality of life beyond cosmetic concerns. When nail psoriasis occurs alongside joint involvement (psoriatic arthritis), the prognosis becomes more complex, with approximately 80% of patients experiencing nail changes having concurrent or future joint disease. The severity of nail involvement correlates poorly with skin disease severity, requiring independent assessment and management.

Epidemiology and Nail Changes

Approximately 50 million people worldwide have psoriasis, with 5-12 million experiencing nail involvement. Among psoriasis patients with nail disease, 60-70% have fingernail involvement, while 35-50% experience toenail disease. The prevalence increases significantly in patients with generalized psoriasis (40-80%) compared to localized disease (5-10%). Mean age of onset is 30-50 years. Nail psoriasis occurs more frequently in males (55-60% of cases) and shows increased prevalence in individuals of Caucasian descent. Patients with psoriatic arthritis experience nail psoriasis in 70-80% of cases, making nail examination critical for disease classification.

Pathophysiology and Nail Changes

Nail psoriasis involves inflammation of the nail matrix (producing nail pitting and other surface changes) and nail bed (causing onycholysis, discoloration, and subungual hyperkeratosis). The nail matrix undergoes disruption by psoriatic inflammation, creating characteristic nail pitting—depressions measuring 0.5-2 mm in diameter arranged in rows. Nail bed inflammation causes onycholysis (separation of nail from bed) and oil drop discoloration (yellowish-brown patches resulting from thinning of the nail plate over subungual exudates). Subungual hyperkeratosis represents thickening of stratum corneum beneath the nail, creating white opaque discoloration. TNF-alpha, IL-17, and IL-23 overexpression in nail tissue perpetuates inflammation, explaining responsiveness to biologic agents.

Clinical Manifestations

Nail pitting represents the most common finding (75-90% of nail psoriasis cases), appearing as punctate depressions following the growth pattern of the nail matrix. Pits range from fine (0.5 mm) to coarse (2 mm), affecting single to multiple nails. Onycholysis (40-60% of cases) presents as nail detachment starting at the distal or lateral margins, creating air-filled spaces beneath the nail that appear as yellow-white patches. The nail-free margin may demonstrate red or brown discoloration. Oil drop changes occur in 40-50% of patients, appearing as well-demarcated yellowish-brown patches under the nail. Nail thickening and crumbly texture affect 20-40% of cases, with progressive destruction sometimes necessitating nail loss. Splinter hemorrhages (2-5% of cases) appear as linear streaks beneath the nail from capillary damage.

Diagnostic Approach

Diagnosis is clinical, based on characteristic nail changes in the context of psoriasis history. Dermoscopy reveals pitting pattern, onycholysis extent, and subungual hyperkeratosis distribution. Nail clipping and fungal culture should be obtained to exclude concurrent onychomycosis, which affects 5-10% of nail psoriasis patients and requires separate antifungal therapy (terbinafine 250 mg daily for 6-12 weeks). Nail biopsy, rarely necessary, confirms psoriasiform dermatitis in the nail matrix or bed. X-ray imaging may be indicated if osteolysis (bone resorption affecting 2-5% of severe cases) is suspected, showing tuft erosion at distal phalanges.

Treatment Strategies

Treatment depends on disease severity, extent, and impact on function. Topical corticosteroids (Class III-IV potency intralesional injection) provide modest benefit; intralesional triamcinolone 2.5-5 mg injected into the nail fold monthly shows efficacy in 30-40% of cases. Calcipotriol 50 mcg/g ointment applied under occlusion 3-5 times weekly benefits nail bed disease in 20-30% of patients. Clobetasol 0.05% under occlusion with topical retinoids (tazarotene 0.1%) addresses matrix disease, though improvement takes 3-6 months reflecting nail growth rate (fingernails grow ~3 mm/month).

Systemic therapy addresses moderate-to-severe nail psoriasis: methotrexate (10-25 mg weekly) benefits 50-70% of patients with clinical improvement visible after 8-12 weeks. Acitretin (0.5-1 mg/kg/day) shows efficacy in 40-60% of cases within 3-4 months. Biologic agents demonstrate superior efficacy: TNF-inhibitors (etanercept 50 mg weekly, infliximab 5 mg/kg at weeks 0,2,6 then every 8 weeks) benefit 60-75% of patients with nail clearance in 24-48 weeks. IL-17 inhibitors (secukinumab 300-350 mg) and IL-23 inhibitors (risankizumab 150 mg IV) show 70-85% efficacy with faster onset (8-16 weeks). JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib 5 mg twice daily) demonstrate emerging benefits in 50-65% of refractory cases.

Prognosis and Long-term Outcomes

Untreated nail psoriasis persists indefinitely, with complete spontaneous remission occurring in <5% of cases. With systemic therapy, 50-70% of patients achieve ≥50% improvement within 6-12 months. Complete nail clearance requires 12-24 months even with effective systemic treatment, reflecting nail plate growth kinetics (full fingernail replacement requires 4-6 months). Approximately 20-30% of treated patients experience recurrence within 12 months of therapy discontinuation. Biologic therapy maintains remission in 75-85% of patients with continued treatment, compared to 30-40% with conventional systemic agents.

Patient Management and Prevention

Patients must understand the slow pace of improvement (nails grow only 3 mm monthly) and avoid applying nail cosmetics containing acrylates, formaldehyde, or dibutyl phthalate which exacerbate inflammation. Regular nail trimming with appropriate care prevents secondary nail trauma. Avoidance of harsh chemicals and excessive water exposure benefits nail health. Recognition that nail disease may precede arthritis development necessitates annual assessment for joint involvement. Counseling regarding realistic expectations (complete clear nails may require 6-12 months) improves treatment compliance and patient satisfaction.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can nail psoriasis be completely cured?

Complete cure of nail psoriasis is rare, but 80-90% improvement is achievable with modern biologics or systemic treatments. Intralesional triamcinolone injected into the nail matrix (2.5 mg every 4-6 weeks for 6-12 months) shows 60-70% clearance rates. Biologics targeting TNF-alpha or IL-17/23 provide best long-term outcomes. Once therapy discontinues, relapse risk is high (50-75%).

Is nail psoriasis actually fungal infection?

No — nail psoriasis is immune-mediated inflammation, not fungal. Accurate differentiation is critical: antifungals are ineffective for psoriasis. Dermoscopy and KOH preparations can distinguish them. Co-infection with fungus occurs in 5-10% of nail psoriasis cases. Biopsy shows fungal hyphae in tinea; nail psoriasis shows inflammatory infiltrates without organisms.

Will my nails grow back normal after treatment?

Nails grow from the matrix — once damaged, changes persist until replaced. With treatment, new nail growth (beginning at cuticle) is normal. Complete nail replacement takes 3-6 months for fingernails, 6-12 months for toenails. Approximately 70-80% of patients achieve normal nail appearance once clear nail matrix resumes growth. Scarring or permanent changes are uncommon with early intervention.

What are the best treatments for nail psoriasis?

Intralesional corticosteroids (triamcinolone acetonide 2.5-5 mg) are first-line, with 60-70% response rates. Topical clobetasol propionate 0.05% under occlusion helps mild cases. Systemic therapies: methotrexate, cyclosporine, and biologics (TNF inhibitors, IL-17 inhibitors) are reserved for severe cases. Biologics show superior efficacy (80-90% clearance) but require systemic disease presence.

Does nail psoriasis mean I'll develop psoriatic arthritis?

Nail involvement increases psoriatic arthritis (PsA) risk significantly — approximately 25-30% of nail psoriasis patients develop PsA, compared to 5-10% of skin-only psoriasis. The presence of nail changes plus polyarticular symptoms (knees, ankles, small joints) warrants rheumatologic evaluation. Early detection and biologic therapy can prevent irreversible joint damage.

Should I avoid nail polish and manicures?

Avoid harsh chemicals and nail trauma during active disease — these worsen inflammation and slow healing. If nails clear, careful manicuring is acceptable. Use hypoallergenic polish without formaldehyde, toluene, or dibutyl phthalate. Avoid gel manicures (UV exposure and acetone soak trauma). Keep nails trimmed short to minimize mechanical stress during treatment phases.
