Inverse Psoriasis: What You Need to Know

By DermoBrain Medical Editorial Team Updated 2026-03-06 Medically Reviewed ⏱ 1 min read

Table of Contents

  1. Definition
  2. Pathophysiology
  3. Clinical Features
  4. Treatment
  5. Infection Management
  6. References

Definition

Inverse (flexural) psoriasis is a distinct variant affecting intertriginous areas: axillae, groin, inframammary folds, and intergluteal cleft. It represents 3-7% of psoriasis cases overall but accounts for 20% of psoriasis in obese patients and up to 50% in elderly populations.

Pathophysiology

Inverse psoriasis develops in genetically predisposed individuals. The intertriginous location creates unique conditions: high humidity and moisture retention promote fungal overgrowth (Candida species). Obesity, poor hygiene, and heat are risk factors.

Clinical Features

Lesions present as erythematous plaques and macules with minimal or absent scale due to moisture and friction. Plaques are often shiny and well-demarcated. Pruritus and pain are prominent. Secondary Candida infection is common.

Treatment

Topical Therapy: Avoid superpotent agents; use low-to-mid potency preparations (hydrocortisone 1-2.5%, triamcinolone 0.1% cream). Tacrolimus 0.1% ointment is valuable lacking atrophy risk.

Moisture Management: Frequent drying with soft cloths, loose-fitting clothing, and powder-based products reduce moisture and fungal overgrowth.

Systemic Therapy: TNF-alpha inhibitors show 70-80% efficacy for inverse psoriasis.

Infection Management

Secondary Candida infection is present in 30-40% of cases. Evidence of infection warrants topical antifungal therapy: miconazole or clotrimazole cream applied 2-3 times daily for 2-4 weeks.
