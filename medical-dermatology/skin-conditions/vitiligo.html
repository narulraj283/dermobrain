Vitiligo: Diagnosis and Management Strategies

Definition and Epidemiology

Vitiligo is an acquired depigmentary disorder from selective melanocyte loss, resulting in white patches. Affects 0.5-2% globally, with higher prevalence in darkly pigmented individuals. Onset typically before age 40; 50% appear before age 20. Genetic predisposition strong; 30-40% report positive family history.

Pathophysiology

Vitiligo results from: (1) autoimmune destruction of melanocytes by CD8+ cytotoxic T cells, (2) oxidative stress from toxic metabolite accumulation, (3) genetic susceptibility, (4) neurogenic factors with altered neuropeptide signaling. Approximately 60% have positive anti-melanocyte antibody serology (anti-tyrosinase, anti-gp100).

Clinical Features

Sharply demarcated depigmented macules and patches, often symmetrical. Common sites: face (greatest psychosocial impact), hands, feet, genitals, axillae. Three patterns: generalized (acrofacial), segmental (unilateral, non-progressive), and focal. Depigmented patches may develop erythema and pruritus before depigmentation.

Diagnosis

Clinical confirmed by Wood lamp examination (highlights depigmented areas as bright white). Dermoscopy shows loss of pigmentation pattern. Histology demonstrates complete melanocyte absence.

Topical Therapies

Potent Topical Corticosteroids: Clobetasol 0.05% ointment twice daily achieves repigmentation in 60-75% when used 3-6 months. Intermittent use maximizes benefit while minimizing atrophy.

Calcineurin Inhibitors: Tacrolimus 0.1% ointment and pimecrolimus 1% cream valuable for face, eyelids. Lack atrophy risk; improvement requires 2-3 months. Efficacy comparable to mid-high potency TCS.

Phototherapy

NB-UVB: 311nm wavelength 2-3 times weekly. Requires 2-3 months to observe repigmentation; best at 6-12 months. Response rates 60-75% with complete repigmentation in 40%.

Excimer Laser (308nm): Monochromatic UV targeted therapy particularly valuable for localized lesions. Biweekly treatments; treats only affected areas.

JAK Inhibitors

Ruxolitinib cream 1.5% FDA-approved in 2022 for vitiligo. Clinical trials: 75-88% significant repigmentation in facial vitiligo. Breakthrough therapy addressing underlying immune dysregulation. Onset 12-24 weeks; continues improving through 48 weeks.

Surgical Options

For stable, localized vitiligo unresponsive to medical therapy: autologous skin grafting (split-thickness or punch), cultured autologous melanocyte transplantation, laser-assisted cellular grafting. Success rates 70-90% with experienced surgeons. Perform when disease stable (no new lesions 6-12 months).
