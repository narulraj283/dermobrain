Lichen Planus: Epidemiology, Pathophysiology, and Treatment

By DermoBrain Medical Editorial Team Updated 2026-03-06 Medically Reviewed ⏱ 1 min read

  3. Diagnosis
  4. Cutaneous Lichen Planus Treatment
  5. Oral Lichen Planus Treatment

Definition and Epidemiology

Lichen planus is a chronic inflammatory disorder affecting skin and mucous membranes. Prevalence 0.5-2% globally. Typically affects adults aged 40-60 years; rare in children. Female-to-male ratio approximately 1.4:1. Exact etiology unclear; likely involves T cell-mediated autoimmunity against basal keratinocytes.

Clinical Features

Skin lesions: small (1-3mm), pruritic, purple (violaceous), polygonal papules. Typically symmetrical involving flexor forearms, wrists, shins, and lower back. Classic mnemonic "6 P's": Purple, Pruritic, Planar (flat-topped), Polygonal, Papules, Plaques.

Oral involvement in 50% of cases: white reticular (lacey) patterns on buccal mucosa, tongue, and palate. May be asymptomatic or cause burning, pain, bleeding with eating.

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis confirmed by histology showing lichenoid infiltrate at dermal-epidermal junction with basal layer vacuolization and "colloid bodies" (apoptotic keratinocytes).

Cutaneous Lichen Planus Treatment

Topical Corticosteroids: Mid-to-high potency agents (triamcinolone 0.1% cream, clobetasol 0.05%) applied twice daily. Clinical improvement within 2-4 weeks; maintenance may be needed.

Phototherapy: NB-UVB (2-3 times weekly) or PUVA effective for generalized disease. Response rates 60-70%.

Intralesional Corticosteroids: Triamcinolone acetonide 5-10mg/mL injected into resistant papules; repeated every 4-6 weeks. Highly effective for localized disease.

Retinoids: Topical tretinoin or oral acitretin for extensive disease. Oral retinoids reserve for severe, extensive lichen planus.

Oral Lichen Planus Treatment

Topical corticosteroids (fluocinonide gel 0.05%) applied 3-4 times daily. Intralesional steroid injections for erosive lesions. Topical retinoids as adjunct. Severe erosive disease may require systemic corticosteroids or cyclosporine.
