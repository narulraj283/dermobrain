Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV): A Comprehensive Clinical Guide

By DermoBrain Medical Editorial Team Updated 2026-03-03 Medically Reviewed

Herpes simplex virus causes recurrent blistering infections; two species HSV-1 (usually oral) and HSV-2 (usually genital) can affect either site.

Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) represents a multisystem concern requiring integrated treatment approaches.

Epidemiology and Clinical Significance

Demographic factors significantly influence the development and severity of Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV).

  • Factor 1: HSV-1 or HSV-2 infection
  • Factor 2: Transmission by direct contact
  • Factor 3: Latency in sensory nerve ganglia
  • Factor 4: Recurrence triggers: stress, immunosuppression, fever, menstruation, sun exposure

Causes and Risk Factors

The pathophysiology of Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) involves multiple interconnected biological pathways.

  • Prodrome: tingling, burning in area
  • Vesicles on erythematous base
  • Painful ulceration after rupture
  • Systemic symptoms in primary infection (fever, lymphadenopathy)
  • Heals with crust in 7-10 days

Pathophysiology and Disease Mechanisms

Understanding the molecular and cellular mechanisms underlying Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) guides precision therapeutics.

  • More localized and milder than primary
  • Fewer vesicles, shorter duration
  • Usually unilateral in same area

Symptoms and Clinical Presentation

Symptom progression in Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) correlates with underlying pathophysiological changes.

  • Topical antivirals (acyclovir, penciclovir) - modest benefit if early
  • Oral antivirals (acyclovir, valacyclovir, famciclovir) - reduces duration and severity
  • Prophylactic antivirals for frequent recurrences
  • Topical anesthetics for pain
  • Cool compresses
