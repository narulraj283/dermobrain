Chloracne: Industrial Chemical-Induced Acne

Definition and Epidemiology

Chloracne is a severe follicular disease caused by exposure to halogenated aromatic hydrocarbons, particularly polychlorinated dibenzodioxins (dioxins), dibenzofurans, and biphenyls. Occurs in occupational settings (chemical manufacturing, waste incinerators, thermal decomposition). Notable historical exposure: Seveso disaster (1976); Vietnam veterans exposed to Agent Orange dioxin contaminant.

Pathophysiology

Dioxins and related compounds activate aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) pathways triggering keratinocyte proliferation, sebaceous gland alteration, and profound inflammation. Dose-dependent; severity correlates with exposure level. Systemic absorption causes widespread effects beyond skin (hepatotoxicity, immunosuppression, endocrine disruption).

Clinical Features

Severe comedonal and papular acne with predominantly closed comedones (milium-like lesions). Facial involvement predominant, though can affect trunk and extremities. Associated follicular hyperkeratosis and pilosebaceous inflammation. Often monomorphic with uniform lesion appearance. Severe pruritus and pain common.

Systemic Manifestations

Beyond skin: hepatomegaly and abnormal liver function (elevation in liver enzymes), altered lipid metabolism, immune suppression (reduced T cell function), endocrine disruption (reproductive toxicity), and increased cancer risk with chronic exposure.

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis based on characteristic acneiform eruption following documented chemical exposure. Confirmation: serum dioxin levels (exceptionally expensive and not routinely performed). Histopathology shows follicular hyperkeratinization and inflammation.

Management

Primary Intervention: Remove from exposure source immediately. Cessation of exposure prerequisite for resolution; continued exposure prevents healing.

Supportive Skin Care: Gentle cleansing with non-irritating cleansers. Avoid extracting comedones (high risk of scarring). Emollients provide barrier support.

Topical Therapy: Topical retinoids (tretinoin 0.025-0.1%) can improve comedone formation and follicular plugging. Start low concentration given severe irritation risk. Benzoyl peroxide less effective than in typical acne.

Systemic Therapy: Limited efficacy; oral retinoids (isotretinoin, acitretin) reserved for severe, unresponsive cases. Requires careful monitoring given systemic toxicity already present from dioxin exposure.

Medical Surveillance

Comprehensive medical evaluation at exposure documentation: baseline liver function tests, lipid panel, immune function assessment, reproductive assessment if applicable. Long-term follow-up given delayed manifestation of dioxin toxicity. Cancer surveillance per occupational exposure guidelines.

Prognosis

Slow resolution after exposure cessation (may take months to years for complete clearance). Residual post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and atrophic scars common. Systemic health effects may persist indefinitely.

References

  2. Jones KC, et al. Human exposure to persistent organic pollutants. Environ Int. 2005;31(2):209-218.
