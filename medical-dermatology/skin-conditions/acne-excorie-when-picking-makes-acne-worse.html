Acne Excoriée: When Picking Makes Acne Worse

Definition and Pathophysiology

Acne excoriée (also acne excoriée des jeunes filles) refers to acne lesions that become severely damaged through repetitive picking, scratching, or manipulation by the patient. Often associated with body-focused repetitive behaviors (BFRBs), obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), anxiety, or dermatillomania. Mechanical trauma produces larger, deeper lesions at risk for secondary infection and severe scarring.

Epidemiology

Predominantly affects adolescent girls and young women (hence historical name), though increasingly recognized in men. Often begins with mild acne that patient exacerbates through picking. Associated with perfectionism, anxiety, stress, and body image concerns.

Clinical Features

Acne lesions with evidence of picking: erosions, crusts, excoriations, scabs. Lesions typically worse than underlying acne severity would suggest. Distribution often shows more trauma on accessible areas (face, chest) versus less accessible sites. Secondary infection common. Significant scarring potential from repeated trauma.

Psychological Assessment

Screen for underlying anxiety, OCD, depression. Assess triggers: stress, anxiety, boredom. Determine if picking is conscious or unconscious/habitual. Evaluate impact on self-esteem and psychosocial functioning. Referral for mental health evaluation appropriate if significant psychiatric component identified.

Dermatologic Treatment

Treat Underlying Acne: Benzoyl peroxide 5%, topical retinoids, or oral antibiotics address acne. Reducing acne burden decreases picking triggers.

Topical Corticosteroids: Low-to-mid potency (hydrocortisone 1%, triamcinolone 0.1%) applied to excoriated areas reduces inflammation and pruritus, decreasing picking urge.

Barrier Protection: Occlusive dressings (hydrocolloid patches, bandages) over excoriated lesions prevent access for picking. Often surprisingly effective behavioral intervention.

Behavioral Interventions

Habit Reversal Training: Identify triggers and substitution behaviors. Alternative tension-releasing activities: stress ball, fidget toy, hand clenching, exercise.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Address underlying anxiety, perfectionism, body image concerns. Helpful for conscious picking driven by anxiety/stress.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT): Acceptance of urge without acting on it; mindfulness-based approaches.

Pharmacotherapy

For OCD-related picking: selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) like sertraline (50-200mg daily) or paroxetine. Augmentation with medications targeting anxiety may benefit. Dermatologist-psychiatrist collaboration optimal.

Monitoring and Prognosis

Combination of dermatologic treatment, behavioral modification, and mental health support most effective. However, recurrence of picking behavior common if underlying anxiety/OCD not adequately addressed. Long-term psychosocial support and periodic dermatologic follow-up recommended.
