Necrotizing Fasciitis: Understanding Flesh-Eating Bacteria

Understanding Necrotizing Fasciitis

Necrotizing fasciitis is a rapidly progressive deep soft tissue infection with mortality 20-40% even with appropriate treatment, and 80-90% without intervention. Characterized by rapid tissue destruction, systemic toxicity, and dissemination along fascial planes, necrotizing fasciitis represents a dermatologic emergency requiring immediate recognition, aggressive debridement, and systemic antibiotics. Group A Streptococcus (50% of cases), Staphylococcus aureus (30%), and polymicrobial infections (20%) cause disease. The condition progresses from initial mild skin changes to severe systemic toxicity within 24-48 hours if untreated, making early clinical suspicion essential for survival.

Epidemiology and Risk Factors

Necrotizing fasciitis affects <1% of population annually (rare condition); mortality 20-40% with treatment, 80-90% without. Risk factors: diabetes (15-20 fold increased risk), immunosuppression, IV drug use (injection sites), varicella (breaks in skin), prior surgery/trauma, obesity. Mortality correlates with delayed diagnosis (diagnosis delay >24 hours increases mortality from 20-30% to 50-70%) and age >50 years (mortality 40% versus 20% in younger patients).

Clinical Presentation and Progression

Initial: localized pain out of proportion to visible skin changes—key diagnostic finding. Skin appears erythematous/edematous initially, rapidly progressing to crepitus (air in tissues from gas-producing organisms—primarily polymicrobial strains), bullae, skin necrosis within 24-48 hours. Systemic toxicity: fever >39°C, tachycardia, tachypnea, hypotension, shock. Mental status changes, renal failure, DIC in severe cases. Pain with passive stretch of affected area (distinguishing from cellulitis) is classic sign.

Diagnosis and Imaging

Clinical suspicion must trigger aggressive workup: CT/MRI showing fascial edema/gas, elevated muscle compartment pressures on compartment syndrome testing. Immediate surgical consultation critical—definitive diagnosis made intraoperatively with finding of gray/tan necrotic fascia with minimal inflammation, allowing "finger dissection" through tissue planes (tissue separation without resistance, unlike normal tissue planes).

Treatment

EMERGENT surgical debridement (within hours of diagnosis, not delayed for imaging) removes all necrotic tissue; multiple operations frequently necessary (average 2-3 debridements, sometimes 5-10 total) as demarcation of viable tissue becomes clear over 24-48 hours. IV antibiotics immediately (before surgery if diagnosis established): vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg every 8-12 hours PLUS clindamycin 600-900 mg every 6-8 hours (clindamycin inhibits toxin production in streptococcal infection, improving survival). Polymicrobial coverage adds fluoroquinolone or aminoglycoside. Supportive care: aggressive fluid resuscitation, vasopressor support if hypotensive, blood product replacement for DIC. Beta-hemolytic streptococci survival with aggressive early debridement approaches 70-75%; delayed treatment survival drops to 20-30%.

Prognosis

Early recognition and surgical debridement critical—survival correlates inversely with diagnosis delay. Survivors may require extensive reconstructive surgery/skin grafting. Limb amputation necessary in 20-30% of cases. Psychological impact substantial in survivors.
