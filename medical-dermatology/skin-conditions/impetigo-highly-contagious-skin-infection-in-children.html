Impetigo: Highly Contagious Skin Infection in Children

Understanding Impetigo

Impetigo is a highly contagious superficial bacterial skin infection affecting 3% of children globally (peak age 2-6 years), caused primarily by Group A Streptococcus (50-60%), Staphylococcus aureus (30-40%), or both organisms. The condition presents as vesicles progressing to golden-yellow honey-colored crusts, typically on exposed areas (face, legs). Impetigo spreads rapidly through schools and daycare (secondary attack rate 40-50% in close contacts without prophylaxis). Unlike deeper cellulitis, impetigo affects only epidermis, allowing topical antibiotic treatment in localized cases. Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis occurs in 1-5% of untreated streptococcal impetigo, warranting prompt treatment.

Epidemiology

Impetigo affects 3% of children <6 years globally (up to 8-10% in tropical regions). Streptococcal impetigo accounts for 70-80% of cases historically; MRSA-associated impetigo increasing (now 20-30% of cases in many regions). Risk factors: warm/humid climate, poor hygiene, malnutrition, crowding, minor skin trauma. Secondary impetigo (arising on pre-existing dermatitis) accounts for 20-30% of cases.

Clinical Types and Presentation

Non-bullous impetigo (70-80%): vesicles/pustules rapidly progressing to honey-colored crusts on erythematous base, typically on face/lower extremities. Bullous impetigo (20-30%): flaccid blisters from Staphylococcus aureus toxin (exfoliative toxin A/B), rapidly rupturing leaving crusts. Associated regional lymphadenopathy in 80-90% of cases. Systemic symptoms absent unless severe (bacteremia rare, <1%).

Treatment

Localized impetigo (<5 lesions, 2-5 cm involved area): topical mupirocin 2% ointment applied three times daily for 5-7 days achieves 90-95% cure rates. For MRSA suspected: topical retapamulin or clindamycin cream alternative. Extensive impetigo (>5 lesions, large involved areas, systemic symptoms): systemic antibiotics: amoxicillin-clavulanate 875-125 mg twice daily or cephalexin 500-1000 mg four times daily for 7-10 days both achieve 95-98% cure. MRSA coverage: trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole or clindamycin if MRSA suspected.

Complications and Prevention

Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis occurs in 1-5% of streptococcal impetigo if untreated. Cellulitis progressing from impetigo occurs in <1% of cases. School exclusion: children treated appropriately return to school after 24-48 hours of antibiotic therapy (lesions still contagious but treated). Household prophylaxis: skin care education, treatment of minor wounds, identification and treatment of secondary cases.
