Molluscum Contagiosum: Clinical Presentation and Therapeutic Options

Definition and Epidemiology

Molluscum contagiosum is a benign viral infection caused by molluscipoxvirus (genus Molluscipoxvirus). Affects 1-2% of dermatology clinic patients; higher in children and sexually active individuals. Transmission through direct contact or contaminated fomites (towels, clothing, toys). Most lesions spontaneously resolve within 6-12 months in immunocompetent patients; may persist indefinitely in immunosuppressed.

Pathophysiology

Virus replicates in keratinocytes, producing characteristic inclusion bodies (molluscum bodies) composed of viral particles. Lesions represent proliferative response with virus-laden cytoplasm. Immune response typically clears infection; persistent lesions suggest incomplete immune control.

Clinical Features

Dome-shaped, flesh-colored to pink papules (2-5mm, occasionally larger) with characteristic central umbilication containing waxy material. Lesions often numerous (5-20+), clustered or scattered. Mild pruritus or surrounding inflammation indicates host immune response approaching resolution. Common sites: face, neck, trunk, extremities; sexually transmitted form on genitals/inner thighs.

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis based on characteristic morphology (dome-shaped, umbilicated papules). Histology shows characteristic intracytoplasmic inclusion bodies (molluscum bodies). Electron microscopy or PCR useful if diagnosis uncertain but unnecessary for routine cases.

Natural History

Self-limited in immunocompetent individuals; 90% resolve within 6-12 months. Spontaneous immune response produces localized inflammation around lesions ("molluscum dermatitis") heralding resolution. Lesions may cluster at site of irritation (autoinoculation through scratching, shaving, etc.). Secondary bacterial infection possible.

Treatment: Observation

Watchful waiting appropriate for asymptomatic lesions in immunocompetent patients given natural resolution. Explain to patient that lesions typically resolve within 6-12 months. However, many patients prefer active treatment for cosmetic reasons or to prevent spread, particularly in children and athletes.

Treatment: Topical Options

Imiquimod: 5% cream applied 3-5 times weekly under occlusion for 12-16 weeks. Activates toll-like receptors stimulating local immune response. Efficacy 60-90%; well-tolerated. Excellent for extensive disease.

Tretinoin: 0.025-0.1% cream applied nightly. Modest efficacy; useful as adjunctive agent. Causes irritation; initiate at low concentration.

Salicylic Acid: 17% solution applied daily to lesions. Modest efficacy; economical option.

Treatment: Destructive Options

Cryotherapy: Liquid nitrogen applied every 2-4 weeks until resolution. Efficacy 50-70%; modest pain and post-inflammatory changes. Average 3-4 treatments for resolution.

Curettage: Mechanical removal using sharp curette under local anesthesia or with topical anesthetic cream (eutectic mixture of lidocaine and prilocaine, EMLA). Highly effective single treatment; leaves small punctate scars. May be combined with topical cidofovir or potassium hydroxide.

Topical Potassium Hydroxide (KOH): Application of dilute KOH solution causing chemical destruction. Efficacy variable; risk of irritation and scarring limits widespread use.

Special Populations

Severe Immunosuppression (HIV/AIDS): Lesions may become numerous and persistent. HAART therapy often results in immune reconstitution and lesion clearance. Treatment options same but may require more aggressive intervention.

Atopic Individuals: Higher risk of spread and secondary infection; active treatment often recommended.

Prevention

Avoid sharing personal items (towels, clothing, razors). Cover lesions to prevent transmission. Avoid shaving or manipulation of lesions. Athletes should avoid skin-to-skin contact sports until lesions treated.
