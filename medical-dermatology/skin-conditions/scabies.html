Scabies: Understanding Pathophysiology and Treatment

Definition and Epidemiology

Scabies is a parasitic infestation caused by the mite Sarcoptes scabiei var. hominis. Affects approximately 300 million people annually worldwide. Transmission through prolonged skin-to-skin contact (sexual contact, sharing bedding) or rarely through contaminated fomites. Incubation period 4-6 weeks for primary infestation; 1-3 days for reinfection (due to prior sensitization).

Pathophysiology and Clinical Features

Female mites burrow into stratum corneum creating tunnels where they lay eggs. Intense pruritus results from allergic hypersensitivity reaction to mite, feces, and secretions. Characteristic S-shaped burrows visible on skin surface, particularly on hands, fingers, wrists, genitals, buttocks. Lesions often excoriated from scratching; secondary bacterial infection common.

Clinical forms: (1) typical scabies with classic burrows and pruritus; (2) bullous scabies with blistering lesions; (3) Norwegian scabies (crusted scabies) in immunosuppressed patients with thick crusts harboring thousands of mites.

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis based on distribution, pruritus (particularly nocturnal and bilateral involvement), and family/contact history. Confirmation: (1) dermoscopy showing characteristic "jet with contrail" sign (mite and burrow), (2) scabies preparation (mineral oil mount of scale; shows mites, eggs, or feces), (3) dermoscopy showing mite, eggs, or feces.

Treatment: Topical Therapy

Permethrin 5% Cream: Gold standard for typical scabies. Apply from neck to toes (including scalp for children <2 years) after bathing; leave on 8-14 hours (typically overnight), then wash off. Repeat application 7-14 days later to kill newly hatched mites. Efficacy >95%; safe even in infants >2 months.

Sulfur 5-10% Ointment: Alternative for infants and pregnant women. Apply nightly for 3 consecutive nights. Less effective than permethrin; preferred in special populations.

Benzyl Benzoate 10-25%: Less commonly used in US but effective. Apply nightly for 3 nights. Caustic; irritating to skin and mucous membranes.

Treatment: Systemic Therapy

Ivermectin: Oral dose 200 mcg/kg, repeated in 1-2 weeks. Two-dose regimen required to kill mites from newly hatched eggs. Efficacy 95% when given with topical permethrin; less effective as monotherapy. FDA-approved for scabies; particularly valuable for Norwegian scabies and mass outbreaks (institutional settings).

Management of Contacts and Environment

Critical Point: Treat all household members and sexual contacts simultaneously, even if asymptomatic, to prevent reinfestation. Failure to treat contacts major cause of treatment failure and recurrent scabies.

Fomites: Wash all bedding, clothing, and towels in hot water and dry on high heat. Items not washable placed in sealed bags for 2 weeks (mites cannot survive >3 days off skin). Vacuum carpets and furniture.

Treatment of Complications

Pruritus and Secondary Eczema: Topical corticosteroids (mid-potency) and antihistamines for pruritus relief during healing. Post-treatment pruritus may persist 2-4 weeks despite successful mite elimination.

Secondary Bacterial Infection: Treat with appropriate antibiotics (cephalexin or clindamycin). Reteat scabies if evident after antibiotic therapy.

Norwegian/Crusted Scabies

Severe form with thick crusts containing millions of mites. Presents in immunosuppressed (HIV/AIDS, post-transplant), elderly, or institutionalized patients. Highly contagious. Requires aggressive treatment: permethrin 5% cream applied more frequently (24-48 hours), oral ivermectin (200 mcg/kg on days 1, 2, 8, 9, 15), and topical keratolytics (salicylic acid, urea) to remove crusts.
