Molluscum Contagiosum: Pearly Bumps in Children and Adults

Understanding Molluscum Contagiosum

Molluscum contagiosum is a common viral infection caused by a poxvirus, affecting 1-10% of children and 1-20% of immunocompromised adults (particularly HIV+ patients with CD4 <50 cells/mcL). The condition presents as characteristic 2-5 mm pearly, umbilicated papules typically on face, trunk, and extremities, spreading through direct contact (sexually transmitted in adults, contact/fomite transmitted in children). While cosmetically concerning, molluscum contagiosum is entirely benign and self-limited, spontaneously resolving in 95% of immunocompetent individuals within 6-12 months (average 13-20 months). Excessive treatment often causes more irritation than benefit; management should balance cosmetic concerns against treatment side effects.

Epidemiology

Molluscum contagiosum affects 1-10% of children (higher in tropical regions, 5-10%), 1-4% of general adult population. In HIV+ patients, prevalence correlates with CD4 count: 1-5% if CD4 >200 cells/mcL, 20% if CD4 <50 cells/mcL. Sexually acquired molluscum affects 2-3% of sexually active young adults. Transmission: direct skin contact (children), sexual contact (adults), shared towels/equipment (athletes). Infectivity moderate—household contacts show 30-40% secondary attack rate. Water-borne transmission disputed; pools unlikely major transmission source.

Clinical Presentation

Molluscum contagiosum presents as 2-5 mm pearly umbilicated papules (central dimple/core most distinctive feature), typically appearing in clusters on face, trunk, extremities. Lesions may appear inflamed/erythematous if secondarily infected or if host immune response heightening (suggesting resolution imminent). Associated regional lymphadenopathy in 10-20% of cases. Pruritus in 30-40% of patients, occasionally prompting scratching/spread to new areas. Asymptomatic in 60-70%. Widespread disease (>30 lesions) suggests immunosuppression; CD4 count <100 in HIV+ patients with extensive molluscum is classic finding.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis clinical; highly characteristic umbilication distinguishes molluscum from other papules. Histology shows central caseous material filled with viral particles. PCR not necessary for diagnosis. Presence of extensive molluscum in adult without known HIV warrants HIV testing.

Treatment Approaches

Observation: 95% of immunocompetent individuals spontaneously clear molluscum within 6-12 months; conservative management avoids treatment-related irritation. Cryotherapy: liquid nitrogen applied to each lesion monthly achieves clearance in 60-70% within 3-4 months, though post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation common (20-30% of treated areas). Curettage: sharp curettage of umbilicated core offers rapid removal but leaves small scars in 10-20% of cases. Topical agents: imiquimod (immune modulator) applied 3-5 times weekly for 4-6 weeks, podofilox 0.5% solution twice daily, cantharidin (blistering agent applied by provider, patient washes off 6-8 hours later) all show 60-80% efficacy within 4-8 weeks but require repeated applications. Systemic therapy: cidofovir IV (5 mg/kg weekly for 4-6 weeks) for severe immunocompromised patients; oral valganciclovir shows emerging promise but not approved for molluscum. Most effective approach for extensive disease: combination of curettage plus topical immunomodulator (imiquimod) achieving 90-95% clearance within 8-12 weeks.

Prognosis

Complete spontaneous resolution occurs in 95% within 6-12 months. Recurrence rare <5%. Immune reconstitution (CD4 recovery >200 cells/mcL on antiretroviral therapy) leads to rapid molluscum clearance in HIV+ patients as immune system recognizes virus. Scarring risk low if treated conservatively; higher with aggressive curettage or repeated cryotherapy.
