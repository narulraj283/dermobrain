Herpes Zoster (Shingles): Causes and Modern Treatment

Understanding Herpes Zoster

Herpes zoster (shingles) is reactivation of latent varicella-zoster virus (VZV) in dorsal root ganglion causing dermatomal eruption in 1-1.5 million Americans annually. Age >50 shows dramatically increased incidence (10-fold increase compared to age 20-40), reflecting declining VZV-specific immunity. The condition causes acute pain, followed by post-herpetic neuralgia (persistent pain lasting >30 days post-eruption) in 30-50% of older adults (>60 years), significantly impacting quality of life. Early antiviral therapy within 72 hours of symptom onset reduces acute pain, herpes zoster-associated pain duration, and post-herpetic neuralgia incidence by 40-50%; delaying treatment beyond 72 hours dramatically reduces efficacy.

Epidemiology and Risk Factors

Herpes zoster affects 1-1.5 million Americans annually with dramatic age-related increase: 2-3 per 1000 person-years age <30, 10-15 per 1000 person-years age 60-70, 20-30 per 1000 person-years age >80. Lifetime risk 25-30%. Risk factors: age >50 (dominant), immunosuppression (10-15 fold increased risk in HIV+, transplant recipients, those on chronic immunosuppression), diabetes, psychological stress, prior herpes zoster (recurrence 1-4%). Varicella vaccination (chickenpox vaccine) reduces zoster risk 50-60% and post-herpetic neuralgia 65-70%. Zoster vaccine (Shingrix, recombinant) >90% effective in preventing zoster and post-herpetic neuralgia in those >50 years; superior to older live-attenuated vaccine (Zostavax) now discouraged.

Pathophysiology and VZV Reactivation

VZV reactivates from latency in dorsal root ganglia, travels along sensory nerve axon to skin in dermatomal distribution. Initial prodrome (burning pain, paresthesias along future rash distribution) lasts 1-7 days preceding visible rash. Innate immune response creates inflammatory cascade with lymphocyte infiltration causing acute pain and vesiculation. VZV-specific T cell immunity declines with age, explaining age-related incidence increase. Immunosuppression/stress reduces T cell surveillance allowing reactivation. Post-herpetic neuralgia develops from chronic inflammation in affected ganglia and altered pain signal processing from persistent virally-induced nerve damage.

Clinical Presentation

Herpes zoster presents with dermatomal distribution (unilateral, restricted to single or adjacent dermatomes in 95% of cases), characteristic progression: prodrome (1-7 days burning pain), erythema/edema (1-2 days), vesicles in clusters (days 3-7), pustulation, crusting (7-14 days), resolution over 3-4 weeks. Associated regional lymphadenopathy, fever, constitutional symptoms in 40-50% of cases. Acute post-herpetic pain (during active infection) correlates strongly with older age and delayed treatment. Post-herpetic neuralgia (pain persisting >30 days post-rash onset) affects 30-50% of those >60 years, 50-75% of those >70 years; characterized by burning, shooting, throbbing pain continuing months-years, significantly impairing quality of life in 20-30% of affected patients.

Diagnosis and Antiviral Therapy

Diagnosis clinical based on dermatomal vesicular eruption. PCR of vesicular fluid provides rapid confirmation (>95% sensitive). Viral culture less sensitive but possible. Antivirals initiated within 72 hours of symptom onset reduce acute pain 25-40%, decrease zoster-associated pain duration 40-50%, and reduce post-herpetic neuralgia incidence 40-50% if given early. First-line: acyclovir 800 mg five times daily for 7-10 days (IV form for ophthalmologic zoster/immunocompromised). Valacyclovir 1000 mg three times daily for 7 days (superior bioavailability, less frequent dosing) preferred in most cases achieving similar efficacy to IV acyclovir with better compliance. Famciclovir 500 mg three times daily alternative with similar efficacy.

Pain Management and Post-Herpetic Neuralgia

Acute pain management: NSAIDs 400-600 mg every 4-6 hours, opioids for severe pain (but limited by tolerance/side effects with long-term use). Topical lidocaine patches (5% patches applied 12 hours daily over affected area) provide 20-30% pain relief through local anesthetic action. Post-herpetic neuralgia treatment: gabapentin starting 100 mg three times daily escalated to 3600 mg daily divided doses (effective in 70% achieving 50% pain reduction), pregabalin 150-600 mg daily alternative. Topical capsaicin 0.075% cream applied four times daily provides modest benefit (30-40% improvement) through substance P depletion. Combination therapy superior to monotherapy in refractory cases.

Prognosis and Prevention

Complete skin healing occurs in 95% of cases within 3-4 weeks, though pain may persist. Secondary bacterial infection occurs in <5% if skin care maintained. With early antiviral therapy, 50-60% of older adults avoid post-herpetic neuralgia; without treatment, 30-50% develop persistent pain. Zoster vaccine (Shingrix) >90% effective in preventing zoster and post-herpetic neuralgia; CDC recommends vaccination in all adults >50 years regardless of prior zoster history.
