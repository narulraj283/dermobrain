Molluscum Contagiosum in Children

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Molluscum Contagiosum: Poxvirus Skin Infection

Molluscum contagiosum is benign viral skin infection by poxvirus. Most common viral skin condition in children aged 1-10 years. Presents with characteristic umbilicated papules. Highly contagious but benign; significant cosmetic and social impact.

Epidemiology

Worldwide distribution with higher prevalence in tropical/subtropical climates. Highly contagious through direct contact, fomites, possibly autoinoculation. Spreads readily in childcare/schools. Increased incidence in immunocompromised.

Pathophysiology

Molluscipoxvirus replicates in keratinocyte cytoplasm. Causes keratinocyte proliferation and central invagination filled with viral-laden material (molluscum bodies). Host immune response gradually controls infection.

Clinical Presentation

Flesh-colored, dome-shaped, firm papules 2-5 mm diameter. Pathognomonic central umbilication with white, cheese-like core. May appear clustered or scattered. Common face, trunk, extremities, axillae, groin. Usually non-pruritic.

Natural History

Lesions persist weeks to months before spontaneous resolution without scarring. Total course prolonged, with new lesions appearing while old resolve. Lasts months to years in some. Giant molluscum (>5 mm) may indicate immunocompromise.

Immunocompromised Patients

HIV/AIDS (CD4 <100): extensive, numerous large lesions, higher scarring risk. Other immunocompromised states: more widespread, persistent. Improves with immune reconstitution.

Diagnosis

Clinical based on umbilicated papules. Dermoscopy shows central umbilication and keratin debris. Histopathology: Henderson-Patterson bodies (cytoplasmic inclusions with viral particles).

Differential Diagnosis

Distinguish from warts (no umbilication), varicella vesicles, comedones, milia. Umbilication highly specific for molluscum.

Treatment Options

Most cases self-limited in immunocompetent children. Many parents desire treatment for cosmetic/contagious concerns. Options: topical agents (tretinoin, imiquimod, cantharidin), mechanical removal (curettage, cryotherapy, laser), rare systemic therapy.

Topical Therapies

Tretinoin nightly; imiquimod 3-5 times weekly. Both require sustained weeks-to-months application. Cantharidin causes blister formation and mechanical removal but not FDA-approved.

Ablative Therapies

Cryotherapy effective but uncomfortable. Curettage removes lesion core under topical anesthetic. Laser therapy for extensive disease but costly, may scar.

Prognosis

Excellent. Most self-resolve within 6 months to 2 years without scarring. Recurrence possible as immunity develops slowly.
