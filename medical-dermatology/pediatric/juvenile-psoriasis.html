Juvenile Psoriasis

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Juvenile Psoriasis: Chronic Inflammatory Skin Disease in Children

Juvenile psoriasis accounts for 30-40% of all psoriasis cases. Presents with characteristic erythematous, scaly plaques. Significant impact on quality of life and psychosocial well-being. Understanding unique pediatric features and treatment considerations essential for optimal management.

Epidemiology

Onset ranges infancy to late adolescence; bimodal distribution (peaks 8-11 and 15-18 years). 10-15% cases begin before age 10. Strong genetic predisposition: 70-90% family history. MZ twin concordance ~70% vs DZ ~30%.

Clinical Presentation

Most common: plaque psoriasis with well-demarcated, erythematous plaques with silvery-white scale. Distribution differs from adults; predilection face, trunk, intertriginous areas. Younger children: intertriginous onset with centrifugal spread. Inverse psoriasis (intertriginous without scale) more common in children than adults.

Pathophysiology

Complex genetic-environmental interplay. HLA-CW6 strongly associated, particularly early-onset. Dysregulated innate and adaptive immunity; Th1/Th17-mediated inflammation. TNF-alpha, IL-23, other cytokines drive inflammation and keratinocyte proliferation.

Triggers

Infections (particularly Group A Strep) trigger/exacerbate. Viral infections (varicella, respiratory) frequently precede onset. Trauma (Koebner) and stress recognized. Medications (beta-blockers, NSAIDs, lithium) may exacerbate.

Clinical Variants

Guttate psoriasis: common in children, small droplet-like papules (2-10 mm) in rapid onset, often streptococcal-triggered. Pustular psoriasis: rare in children. Erythrodermic: severe, requiring hospitalization.

Systemic Manifestations

Psoriatic arthritis less common in children than adults. Nail involvement (pitting, onycholysis, hyperkeratosis) in 25-50%. Scalp involvement frequent with significant scaling.

Diagnosis

Clinical based on characteristic erythematous well-demarcated plaques with scale. Dermoscopy or biopsy confirms in atypical cases. Elevated streptococcal titers indicate recent infection in guttate presentations.

Psychosocial Impact

Visible skin disease significantly impacts self-esteem, social integration. Depression, anxiety, poor self-esteem risks. Bullying concerns. Mental health professional involvement and peer support beneficial.

Management: General Measures

Gentle cleansing, liberal moisturizing, irritant avoidance foundational. Trigger identification/avoidance important. Group A Strep treatment may abort guttate disease. Stress management and psychological support.

Topical Therapies

Low-to-moderate potency corticosteroids first-line for localized disease. Calcineurin inhibitors for facial and intertriginous areas without steroid side effects. Vitamin D analogs combined with corticosteroids.

Systemic Therapies

Extensive disease: narrowband UVB phototherapy (first-line), conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, acitretin), biologic therapies targeting TNF-alpha, IL-23, IL-17 increasingly used in pediatrics.

Prognosis

Early-onset may have more aggressive course, higher persistent disease risk into adulthood. Variable course with remission/exacerbation periods. Appropriate management maintains quality of life effectively.
