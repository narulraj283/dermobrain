Milia in Newborns

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Milia: Benign Epidermal Inclusion Cysts in Infants

Milia are common benign tiny white papules in majority of newborns, representing epidermal inclusion cysts. Typically 1-2 mm pearly white papules from keratin retention. Completely benign, asymptomatic, spontaneously resolving without treatment.

Epidemiology

Occur approximately 50% of newborns. Equal male-female distribution. No ethnic predilection. Present at birth or appear first weeks. Most common facially, especially nose, cheeks, upper lip.

Pathophysiology

Represent microscopic epidermal inclusion cysts filled with keratin. Etiology incompletely understood but likely involves incomplete sebaceous gland duct maturation with keratin retention. Not related to hygiene, sunburn, or pathological process.

Clinical Characteristics

Tiny (1-2 mm), firm, pearly white or yellowish papules. Discrete lesions without surrounding erythema or inflammation. Asymptomatic and non-interfering with function. Variable number from few scattered to more numerous distribution.

Distribution and Variants

Most commonly facial (nose, cheeks, upper lip, chin). Can appear anywhere. Intraoral milia (Epstein pearls on hard palate or gums) particularly common. Distinguish from neonatal herpes (vesicles/ulcerations with systemic symptoms).

Histopathology

Small epidermal cysts lined by stratified squamous epithelium with granular layer containing keratin. Cyst wall lacks associated sebaceous glands, distinguishing from epidermoid cysts in older individuals. Follicular infundibulum origin characteristic.

Natural History

Transient and self-limited. Most lesions disappear within 2-4 weeks to several months of age. Spontaneous resolution occurs as keratin gradually expelled or cyst ruptures. Rarely, lesions may persist several months, but complete resolution expected without treatment.

Parental Education

Reassure parents that milia are benign, normal neonatal findings requiring no treatment. Asymptomatic and self-resolving. Attempting removal or manipulation not indicated and may introduce infection or cause scarring. Pearly appearance, though cosmetically bothersome to some parents, requires no intervention.

Management

Observation and reassurance only. No treatment indicated or necessary. Attempting extraction or manual expression not recommended as may introduce infection, cause scarring, or post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. Topical treatments not indicated.

Differential Diagnosis of Persistent Lesions

Lesions persisting beyond 6 months or with atypical features (surrounding erythema, drainage, symptoms) warrant evaluation for alternative diagnoses including ectopic sebaceous tissue, steatocystoma, dermoid cyst, other cystic lesions. Dermatologic evaluation appropriate.

Prognosis

Excellent with expected complete spontaneous resolution without scarring or sequelae. No treatment ever required. No impact on long-term skin health or appearance.
