Pediatric Dermatology

Childhood Vitiligo

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Childhood Vitiligo: Acquired Depigmentation Disorder

Vitiligo is chronic depigmentation disorder from melanocyte destruction. 25% of cases begin in childhood with unique challenges in management and psychosocial support. Significant impact on self-esteem during critical developmental years.

Epidemiology

Affects 0.5-2% population; 25% with childhood onset. Peak onset ages 10-30 years. Equal male-female distribution. Approximately 30% report positive family history.

Pathophysiology

Multiple mechanisms: autoimmune melanocyte destruction (circulating anti-melanocyte antibodies), oxidative stress with cytotoxic metabolite accumulation, loss of growth factors, melanocyte migration/differentiation abnormalities.

Clinical Presentation

Circumscribed depigmented macules and patches. Normal skin surface without atrophy, scale, or inflammation (though inflammation may precede depigmentation). Well-demarcated, sharp borders. Distribution varies localized to generalized (>50% BSA). Common sites: hands, feet, face.

Distribution Patterns

Localized (one area), segmental (unilateral, dermatomal), generalized (bilateral, symmetric), acrofacial (face and hands). Segmental: earlier onset, faster progression, earlier stabilization. Generalized: unpredictable progression.

Associated Conditions

Thyroid disease (hypo/hyperthyroidism): 20-30% of patients. Pernicious anemia, Addison disease, lupus, type 1 diabetes associated. Screen for these conditions in pediatric vitiligo.

Psychosocial Impact

Cosmetic impact profound in children. Social stigma, bullying, reduced quality of life, depression, anxiety. Early intervention and psychological support essential.

Diagnosis

Clinical based on characteristic depigmented patches. Dermoscopy shows reduced pigmentation with hyperpigmented macules at borders. Wood's lamp enhances visualization. Histopathology: absent or near-absent melanocytes.

Differential Diagnosis

Pityriasis alba (atopic hypopigmentation with fine scale, less distinct borders). Tinea versicolor (fine scale). Post-inflammatory hypopigmentation. Other hypopigmentation causes.

Assessment

Document extent (VASI or similar), pattern, progression rate. Thyroid testing recommended. Screen for autoimmune conditions. Age-appropriate discussion of associated health risks.

Photoprotection

Essential: depigmented areas lack melanin, prone to sunburn. SPF 50+ broad-spectrum sunscreen applied liberally and frequently. Protective clothing, hats, midday sun avoidance recommended.

Topical Therapies

Mid-to-potent topical corticosteroids first-line for localized disease; twice daily application. Topical calcineurin inhibitors increasingly used, especially facial/intertriginous disease, avoiding steroid side effects. Combination therapy may improve response.

Phototherapy

Narrowband UVB phototherapy for generalized/segmental vitiligo. 2-3 times weekly with dose escalation. Response rates 50-75%. Targeted/excimer laser options. Requires multiple office visits.

Cosmetic Options

Extensive disease: depigmentation (monobenzone, laser-assisted) for uniform tone. Waterproof makeup camouflage (Dermablend, Microskin). Temporary/semi-permanent makeup options for motivated patients.

Emerging Therapies

JAK inhibitors show promise in early studies. Systemic corticosteroids for rapidly progressive disease. Systemic immunosuppressants for severe disease resistant to other therapies.

Prognosis

Chronic with variable course. One-third stable, one-third slow progression, one-third rapid. Early-onset may be more favorable than late-onset. Spontaneous repigmentation uncommon (10-20% of cases).

Psychosocial Support

Comprehensive management includes psychology referral for significant distress. Support groups and peer connections reduce isolation. Family education for appropriate support.
