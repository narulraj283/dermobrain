Measles Rash (Rubeola)

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Measles: Highly Contagious Viral Exanthem

Measles (rubeola) is highly contagious viral infection by paramyxovirus. Presents with characteristic prodromal symptoms, Koplik spots, and maculopapular rash spreading cephalocaudally. Vaccine-preventable but remains important in undervaccinated populations.

Epidemiology

R0 of 12-18 (each infected person infects 12-18 susceptibles). Respiratory droplet transmission. Virus shed from 4 days before to 4 days after rash onset. Vaccination dramatically reduced incidence.

Pathophysiology

Replicates in respiratory epithelium and lymphoid tissues. Viremia with dissemination to skin and organs. Characteristic rash represents immune response to viral antigens.

Clinical Course: Prodromal Phase

Prodrome 3-4 days: fever (often 39-40°C), "3 Cs"—cough, coryza, conjunctivitis. Koplik spots appear (small white spots with red halos on buccal mucosa opposite molars). Pathognomonic for measles.

Rash Development

Appears 3-4 days after fever onset. Begins at hairline and behind ears, spreading cephalocaudally to face, trunk, extremities over 3 days. Maculopapular, blanching eruption that may coalesce.

Rash Characteristics

2-3 mm erythematous macules and papules. Typically non-pruritic. Blanches initially; may develop petechial component in severe cases. Enanthem common. Rash intensity correlates with illness severity.

Diagnosis

Clinical: fever with "3 Cs," Koplik spots, and characteristic rash. Laboratory: serologic testing (IgM), viral culture, RT-PCR.

Complications

Otitis media, sinusitis, pneumonia (1-6%, most common cause of death), encephalitis (1 per 1000). Immune suppression increases secondary infection risk. SSPE: rare fatal complication years after infection.

Treatment

Supportive care. Vitamin A supplementation (200,000 IU 2 days, repeated 2 weeks later) recommended. Reserve antibiotics for documented secondary infection.

Prognosis

Excellent with appropriate care. Case fatality ~2 per 1000 in developed countries. Higher in malnourished children in developing countries.
