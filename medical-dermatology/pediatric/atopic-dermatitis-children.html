Atopic Dermatitis (Eczema) in Children

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Atopic Dermatitis in Children

Atopic dermatitis (AD), commonly known as eczema, is a chronic inflammatory skin condition affecting 10-20% of children. It's characterized by intense pruritus, xerosis, and characteristic inflammation patterns varying by age.

Pathophysiology

AD results from impaired skin barrier function due to reduced ceramide levels and filaggrin mutations, combined with Th2-skewed immune response and elevated IgE levels.

Clinical Presentation by Age

In infants, eczema appears on face, scalp, and extensor surfaces. In older children, it shifts to flexural surfaces including antecubital and popliteal fossae. Adolescents may present with facial or hand dermatitis.

Diagnostic Criteria

Diagnosis is clinical, based on Hanifin and Rajka or UK Working Party criteria. Key features include onset before age 2, pruritus, chronic relapsing course, xerosis, and characteristic morphology.

Management Strategies

First-line treatment focuses on emollients and skin care. Topical corticosteroids or calcineurin inhibitors treat mild to moderate disease. Severe cases may require systemic corticosteroids, phototherapy, or dupilumab.

Trigger Management

Common triggers include harsh soaps, fragrances, dust mites, animal dander, and stress. Bathing in lukewarm water with mild cleansers, soft breathable clothing, and humidity maintenance help prevent flares.

Prognosis

Many children experience improvement or remission by adolescence. Complications include secondary bacterial infection and increased risk of other atopic conditions.
