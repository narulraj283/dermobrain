Eczema in Children: A Parent's Guide to Atopic Dermatitis

The Bottom Line

Eczema (atopic dermatitis) is the most common skin condition in children, affecting up to 20% of kids. It shows up as dry, itchy, red patches — often on the cheeks, elbows, and behind the knees. It's not contagious, and it's not caused by poor hygiene.

The foundation of treatment is simple: moisturize generously and consistently. Thick creams and ointments applied right after bathing help repair the skin barrier and prevent flares. When flares happen, short courses of prescription steroid creams bring relief quickly and are safe when used as directed by your pediatrician or dermatologist.

When to see a doctor: If your child's eczema isn't controlled with moisturizers and over-the-counter hydrocortisone, if the skin looks infected (oozing, crusting, or warm), or if itching is disrupting sleep.

What Is Eczema?

Atopic dermatitis — eczema for short — is a chronic condition where the skin's protective barrier doesn't work as well as it should. Think of healthy skin as a brick wall: skin cells are the bricks, and natural fats (lipids) are the mortar holding everything together. In children with eczema, the "mortar" is incomplete. This lets moisture escape (causing dryness) and allows irritants and allergens to sneak in (triggering inflammation and itching).

In many children, the root cause involves variations in a gene called filaggrin, which produces a protein essential for building that skin barrier. About 30% of children with moderate-to-severe eczema carry a filaggrin gene mutation, though you don't need a genetic test to diagnose or treat the condition.

Eczema typically appears in the first year of life. In babies, it often starts on the cheeks and scalp. In toddlers and older children, it shifts to the creases — inside the elbows, behind the knees, around the wrists and ankles. Most children outgrow eczema or see significant improvement by their teenage years, though some carry it into adulthood.

Common Triggers

While eczema has a genetic and immune basis, flare-ups are often set off by environmental triggers. Every child's triggers are different, but common ones include:

Dry air and low humidity: Winter heating dries indoor air, which dries skin. Many families notice eczema worsens in cold months.

Irritants: Fragranced soaps, bubble baths, laundry detergents with dyes or perfumes, and rough fabrics (especially wool) can irritate sensitive skin. Switch to fragrance-free products labeled "for sensitive skin."

Heat and sweat: Overheating — from overdressing, hot baths, or active play — can trigger itching. Dress children in breathable cotton layers.

Allergens: Dust mites, pet dander, pollen, and certain foods (most commonly milk, eggs, peanuts, wheat, and soy) can worsen eczema in some children. Food triggers are more relevant in infants and toddlers with severe eczema — talk to your doctor before eliminating foods from your child's diet.

Stress and illness: Even in young children, stress and infections can trigger flares. The itch-scratch cycle — where itching leads to scratching, which damages skin, which causes more itching — is the most common driver of ongoing flares.

Daily Skin Care: The Foundation

Consistent skin care prevents more flares than any medication. Here's what dermatologists recommend:

Bathing: Lukewarm (not hot) baths for 5-10 minutes daily. Use a small amount of fragrance-free, soap-free cleanser only where needed (diaper area, hands, feet). Long soaks and harsh soaps strip natural oils from the skin.

Moisturize immediately: Within 3 minutes of getting out of the bath — while skin is still damp — apply a thick moisturizer everywhere. This "soak and seal" technique traps moisture in the skin. Ointments (like petroleum jelly/Vaseline) and thick creams (like CeraVe Moisturizing Cream, Vanicream, or Eucerin) are significantly more effective than lotions, which are mostly water.

Moisturize again: Apply moisturizer at least twice a day total — after the bath and at least once more during the day. During flares, you may apply it 3-4 times. There is essentially no such thing as too much moisturizer for eczema-prone skin.

Clothing: Soft cotton clothing next to skin. Remove tags. Wash new clothes before wearing. Use fragrance-free, dye-free laundry detergent. Skip fabric softener and dryer sheets.

Medical Treatments

When good skin care alone isn't enough, several safe and effective medications can help:

Topical corticosteroids (steroid creams): The first-line medical treatment for eczema flares. These reduce inflammation and itching, usually within days. They come in a range of strengths:

For the face and skin folds: mild steroids like hydrocortisone 1% or 2.5% cream, applied twice daily for up to 2 weeks. For the body: moderate-strength options like triamcinolone 0.1% cream. Your doctor will prescribe the right strength for the location and severity. The key is to use them early in a flare (don't "save" them for when it's really bad) and to keep moisturizing alongside them.

A note about "steroid fear": Many parents worry about side effects from steroid creams. Used as directed by your doctor — the right strength, right location, right duration — topical steroids are very safe, even in children. Undertreating eczema causes more harm (infection, sleep loss, skin damage from scratching) than appropriately used steroid creams. If you have concerns, discuss them with your dermatologist rather than skipping treatments.

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory creams: For sensitive areas like the face and eyelids, or for long-term maintenance, your doctor may prescribe tacrolimus (Protopic) ointment or pimecrolimus (Elidel) cream. These calm inflammation without the concerns associated with long-term steroid use on delicate skin. They may cause a brief burning or stinging sensation when first applied — this usually fades after the first few days.

Crisaborole (Eucrisa): A newer non-steroidal ointment approved for mild-to-moderate eczema in children aged 3 months and older. Applied twice daily, it works by blocking an enzyme (PDE4) involved in inflammation.

Antihistamines: Oral antihistamines like cetirizine (Zyrtec) or hydroxyzine may help with nighttime itching, though they don't directly treat the skin inflammation. Sedating antihistamines (hydroxyzine, diphenhydramine) are sometimes used at bedtime when itching disrupts sleep.

When Eczema Is More Severe

For children with eczema that doesn't respond to topical treatments, newer options are available:

Dupilumab (Dupixent): An injectable biologic medication approved for moderate-to-severe eczema in children aged 6 months and older. It targets specific immune proteins (IL-4 and IL-13) that drive eczema inflammation. Given as an injection every 2-4 weeks, clinical trials showed 75% improvement in skin clearance in the majority of patients. Side effects are generally mild — the most common is injection site reactions and occasional eye inflammation (conjunctivitis).

Wet wrap therapy: For severe flares, your doctor may recommend applying medication and moisturizer, then wrapping the area in damp gauze or cotton, followed by a dry layer. This is done at home, usually at bedtime, and can dramatically reduce inflammation and itching within a few days. Your medical team will show you the technique.

Phototherapy: Narrowband UVB light therapy, administered 2-3 times per week in a dermatologist's office, can significantly improve eczema in older children and adolescents. Each session lasts only minutes, and improvement typically appears after 4-6 weeks of consistent treatment.

Watching for Infection

Eczema-damaged skin is vulnerable to bacterial and viral infections. Watch for these signs that your child's eczema may be infected:

Bacterial infection: Honey-colored crusting, oozing, increased redness spreading beyond the usual eczema patches, warmth, or pain. The most common culprits are Staphylococcus aureus and Streptococcus. Your doctor may prescribe topical or oral antibiotics. Bleach baths (¼ cup regular household bleach in a full bathtub, twice weekly for 10 minutes) can help prevent recurrent skin infections — this is a well-studied, dermatologist-recommended practice equivalent to swimming pool chlorine levels.

Eczema herpeticum: If your child develops clusters of small, painful, fluid-filled blisters that look different from their usual eczema — especially with fever — seek medical attention promptly. This is a herpes virus infection of eczema-damaged skin that requires antiviral medication.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will my child outgrow eczema?

Many children do. Studies show that about 60-70% of children with eczema see significant improvement or complete resolution by their teenage years. Children with mild eczema are more likely to outgrow it than those with severe disease. Even if eczema persists, it often becomes easier to manage with age.

Should I test my child for food allergies?

Food allergies and eczema do overlap — about 30% of children with moderate-to-severe eczema have a food allergy. However, removing foods from your child's diet without testing can cause nutritional problems and unnecessary stress. Talk to your doctor, who may recommend specific allergy testing if your child's eczema is severe, started very early, or seems to worsen after certain foods. Blanket "allergy panels" often produce misleading results.

Are steroid creams safe for my baby?

Yes, when used appropriately. Low-potency steroids like hydrocortisone 1% are routinely used on infants. Your doctor will match the steroid strength to the location and severity — milder for the face and skin folds, slightly stronger for the body. The risks of undertreated eczema (infection, chronic scratching, sleep disruption, skin thickening) are greater than the risks of properly supervised steroid use.

Is eczema related to asthma?

Yes — eczema, asthma, food allergies, and hay fever are all part of the "atopic march," a progression of allergic conditions that often develops in childhood. About 50% of children with moderate-to-severe eczema will develop asthma, and 75% will develop hay fever. Managing eczema well may help reduce the severity of these related conditions.
