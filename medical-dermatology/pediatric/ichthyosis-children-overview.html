Ichthyosis in Children: Overview and Types

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Ichthyosis: Hereditary and Acquired Keratinization Disorders

Ichthyosis encompasses inherited and acquired keratinization disorders characterized by abnormal scale accumulation. Most common is ichthyosis vulgaris affecting ~1 in 250 individuals, presenting in early childhood. Understanding different types crucial for appropriate diagnosis and management.

Classification

Ichthyosis vulgaris (most common, autosomal dominant, filaggrin mutations). X-linked ichthyosis (males affected). Autosomal recessive ichthyosis (more severe). Epidermolytic ichthyosis. Various syndromic forms. Acquired ichthyosis secondary to malignancy, thyroid disease, medications.

Ichthyosis Vulgaris

Most common, autosomal dominant inheritance. Filaggrin gene mutations impair epidermal barrier. Typically ages 1-4 years with increased scale on extensor surfaces. Small, quadrilateral polygonal scale resembling fish scales. Flexural areas spared. Associated atopy, keratosis pilaris, atopic dermatitis predisposition.

X-Linked Ichthyosis

Affects males predominantly. STS gene mutations (steroid sulfatase). Presentation typically within first month with dark brown scales. Progressive over weeks. Particularly flexural involvement. Associated cryptorchidism and increased attention deficit disorder risk. Female carriers minimal cutaneous findings.

Autosomal Recessive Congenital Ichthyosis (ARCI)

More severe phenotypes than ichthyosis vulgaris. Infants present at birth with collodion membranes (shiny, translucent covering) or severe scaling/erythema. Progressive improvement over weeks to months. Associated ectropion, eclabium, increased infection risk from compromised barrier.

Pathophysiology

All forms: disrupted epidermal barrier function and abnormal keratinocyte differentiation. Filaggrin-related forms lose important barrier protein with increased TEWL. Compensatory hyperkeratinization and scale accumulation. Other genetic mutations affecting lipid metabolism and barrier proteins contribute to other forms.

Clinical Manifestations

Hallmark: abnormal scaling varying in pattern and severity among types. Associated pruritus, erythema, hyperkeratosis, follicular hyperkeratosis (keratosis pilaris). Severe forms: systemic complications including infection and temperature dysregulation.

Diagnosis

Clinical based on characteristic scale and distribution. Age of onset, inheritance pattern, associated features differentiate types. Genetic testing confirms some forms. Histopathology rarely required. Laboratory studies exclude acquired causes.

Management: General Principles

Focus on maintaining skin barrier and managing symptoms. Frequent bathing in lukewarm water with mild cleansers. Generous emollients applied especially post-bathing. Keratolytic agents (salicylic acid, lactic acid) when appropriate. Maintain adequate humidity.

Topical Therapies

Emollients first-line, applied liberally post-bathing. Heavy occlusives (petrolatum, ointments, creams) superior to light lotions. Urea-containing products (10-40%) provide hydration and keratolytic effects. Lactic acid creams (5-12%) promote desquamation. Topical retinoids or salicylic acid with caution regarding potency and duration.

Systemic Therapies

Severe disease unresponsive to topical treatment: systemic retinoids (acitretin) considered. Particularly useful for severe forms including epidermolytic ichthyosis. Requires careful monitoring for side effects and teratogenicity. Reserved for significant disease causing functional impairment.

Management of Associated Conditions

Ichthyosis vulgaris: concurrent atopic dermatitis managed aggressively. X-linked: screen for associated conditions. Secondary infection prevention important given compromised barrier.

Genetic Counseling and Family Support

Appropriate for hereditary ichthyosis families. Understanding inheritance patterns, recurrence risk, genetic testing helps families make informed decisions. Prenatal diagnosis possible for some severe forms. Support organization connection reduces isolation.

Prognosis

Varies by type. Ichthyosis vulgaris: mild, typically stable or improves with age. X-linked: chronic but not life-threatening. ARCI: variable, may improve over time as infants transition through severe neonatal period. Quality of life significantly impacted by pruritus and cosmetic concerns, though effective management addresses most symptoms.
