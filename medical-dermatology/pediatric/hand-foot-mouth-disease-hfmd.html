Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease: Enteroviral Exanthem

HFMD is an acute, self-limited viral illness caused primarily by enterovirus 71 (EV-A71) and coxsackievirus A16 (CVA16). Presents with vesicular eruptions on hands, feet, and mouth in young children (typically <5 years). Highly contagious, spreads rapidly in childcare/school settings.

Epidemiology

Worldwide distribution with summer/fall predominance in temperate climates, year-round in tropics. Peak ages 1-4 years. Transmission via fecal-oral (most common), respiratory, and contact with vesicular fluid.

Pathophysiology

Virus replicates in oropharynx and GI tract. Viremia leads to dissemination. Vesicular lesions result from viral invasion of epithelial cells with inflammatory response.

Clinical Presentation

Prodrome of fever (38-39°C), malaise, anorexia, upper respiratory symptoms lasting 1-2 days. Followed by characteristic oral ulcers and vesicular eruptions on hands, feet, extending to dorsal surfaces.

Oral Manifestations

Small red macules rapidly progressing to painful vesicles. Rupture leaving painful ulcers with erythematous halos. Common on buccal mucosa, tongue, gums, hard palate. Pain may interfere with eating and drinking.

Cutaneous Manifestations

Small (3-8 mm), fluid-filled vesicles with erythematous bases on palms and soles. May extend to dorsal surfaces. Lesions remain vesicular without crusting, distinguishing from chickenpox. Buttocks and genital areas may be affected.

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis. PCR testing of nasopharyngeal or stool samples confirms. Distinguish from chickenpox (progressive lesion stages), aphthous ulcers, other viral exanthems.

Complications

EV-A71 infection risks viral meningitis, encephalitis, myocarditis, pulmonary edema. Brainstem encephalitis in young children carries mortality. Post-infectious myalgias and neuropathies reported.

Management

Supportive care with antipyretics and analgesics. Soft, cool foods better tolerated. Maintain hydration. Gentle oral hygiene. Antimicrobial mouthwash prevents secondary infection.

Prognosis

Excellent for uncomplicated cases. Lesions resolve within 7-10 days. Full recovery expected.
