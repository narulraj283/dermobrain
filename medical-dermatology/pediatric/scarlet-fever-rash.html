Scarlet Fever Rash

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Scarlet Fever: Group A Streptococcal Toxin-Mediated Rash

Scarlet fever is acute bacterial infection by Group A Streptococcus (GAS) producing erythrogenic toxin. Presents with pharyngitis followed by distinctive fine papillary erythematous rash with sandpaper-like texture.

Epidemiology

Most common ages 5-15 years. Year-round but increased winter/early spring incidence. Transmission via respiratory droplets or contaminated food. Reportable in many jurisdictions.

Pathophysiology

GAS produces virulence factors including erythrogenic toxins (SPEs) acting as superantigens. This massive immune activation produces characteristic rash and systemic symptoms.

Clinical Presentation

Acute pharyngitis with sore throat, fever (38.5-39.5°C), headache, malaise. Intense pharyngeal erythema with exudate and cervical lymphadenopathy. Within 12-72 hours: characteristic rash develops.

Rash Characteristics

Fine, papillary, blanching erythema with sandpaper-like texture. Lesions 1-2 mm diameter. More prominent in skin folds (Pastia lines). Circumoral pallor with flushed cheeks and bright red lips. Palms and soles spared.

Associated Features

"Strawberry tongue"—erythematous with prominent papillae. Early: white-coated with red papillae (white strawberry); later: beefy red. Petechiae in skin folds. Generalized lymphadenopathy.

Diagnosis

Positive throat culture or RADT demonstrating GAS plus characteristic rash. CDC criteria: GAS infection AND fine, papillary, blanching rash with sandpaper texture.

Complications

Suppurative: abscess, otitis media, sinusitis. Non-suppurative: acute rheumatic fever (ARF), post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis (PSGN). Rapid diagnosis and antibiotics prevent ARF.

Treatment

Penicillin V 250 mg orally 2-3 times daily for 10 days (first-line). Macrolides or first-generation cephalosporins for allergic patients. Alleviates symptoms within 24 hours and prevents complications.

Prognosis

Excellent with appropriate antibiotics. Symptoms resolve within 24-48 hours. Long-term complications depend on treatment adequacy.
