Ringworm in Children (Tinea Corporis)

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Ringworm in Children: Dermatophyte Infection

Ringworm (tinea corporis) is common fungal infection in children caused by dermatophytes, most commonly Trichophyton tonsurans in North America. Presents with characteristic annular (ring-shaped) scaly erythematous lesions. Highly contagious through direct contact, fomites, and potentially animal contact.

Epidemiology

Common in children aged 3-14 years. Higher incidence in warm, humid climates. Transmission through direct contact with infected skin, fomites (towels, bedding, clothing), animals (especially cats and dogs). Increased risk in atopic dermatitis patients and immunocompromised.

Pathophysiology

Dermatophytes invade stratum corneum, utilizing keratin as nutrient source. Trigger inflammatory response from fungal antigens. Trichophyton tonsurans most common in North America; Microsporum canis from animal contact.

Clinical Presentation

Characteristic annular scaly erythematous patches with central clearing, creating "ring-shaped" lesions. Well-demarcated borders, often advancing centrifugally. Most common trunk, extremities, face. Usually mildly pruritic. Lesions vary 0.5-5 cm diameter. May be solitary or multiple.

Diagnosis

Clinical appearance highly suggestive. Confirm with KOH preparation of scale showing fungal elements or fungal culture and identification. Dermoscopy may aid diagnosis showing scaly appearance and follicular involvement. Wood's lamp not helpful (most tinea corporis non-fluorescent).

Differential Diagnosis

Distinguish from nummular eczema (more pruritic, less defined borders, history of atopy), psoriasis (different distribution, thicker scale), pityriasis rosea (larger herald patch, widespread distribution), or other scaling lesions.

Treatment

Topical antifungals first-line for limited disease: azoles (clotrimazole, miconazole), allylamines (terbinafine), or polyenes. Apply twice daily for 2-4 weeks. Continue for 1-2 weeks after clinical clearance. Systemic antifungals (griseofulvin, terbinafine) for extensive disease or treatment failure.

Infection Control

Avoid direct contact with infected skin. Do not share towels, clothing, or personal items. Wash infected areas regularly. Remove contaminated clothing from other family members' contact. Screen family and pets for infection.

Prognosis

Excellent with appropriate treatment. Most lesions clear within 3-4 weeks with topical antifungals. Complete resolution expected. Recurrence possible from reexposure to source.
