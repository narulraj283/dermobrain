Roseola Infantum (Exanthem Subitum)

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Roseola Infantum: Sudden Rash Following High Fever

Roseola infantum (exanthem subitum, sixth disease) is caused by human herpesvirus 6 (HHV-6) or HHV-7. Characteristically presents with high fever in infants/toddlers followed by rapid defervescence and exanthem.

Epidemiology

Typically ages 6 months to 3 years; peak 6-15 months. More common in males. Seasonal variation with increased spring/fall incidence. Approximately 95% of children infected by age 2.

Pathophysiology

Lymphotropic viruses replicate in lymphoid tissues. Viremia leads to disseminated infection. Rash appears as fever resolves, representing immune response.

Clinical Presentation

Abrupt high fever onset (39-40.5°C, reaching 40-41°C) in otherwise well-appearing infant. Minimal systemic symptoms relative to fever degree. Febrile seizures in 10-15% of patients.

Natural Course and Rash

Fever 3-5 days (range 3-7). Once fever resolves, characteristic rash rapidly develops within hours to 1-2 days. Classic "rash as fever breaks."

Rash Characteristics

Rose-pink, maculopapular, non-pruritic, blanching rash. Lesions 2-3 mm diameter. Most prominent on trunk and neck, lighter on face and proximal extremities. Non-pruritic. No enanthem.

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis based on characteristic presentation: high fever in infant followed by defervescence and rose-pink rash. PCR or serology confirmation rarely necessary.

Complications

Febrile seizures primary complication (10-15%). Status epilepticus and recurrent seizures rare. Severe complications uncommon in immunocompetent children.

Management

Supportive care: antipyretics and analgesics. Fever management and seizure precautions important. Antibiotics not indicated.

Prognosis

Excellent. Self-limited, typically 3-7 days total. Lifelong immunity usually develops.
