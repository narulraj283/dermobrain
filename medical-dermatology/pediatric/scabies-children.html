Scabies in Children

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Scabies in Children: Mite Infestation

Scabies is parasitic skin infestation by Sarcoptes scabiei mite, highly contagious through direct contact. Presents with intense pruritus and characteristic burrow lesions. Affects all ages but features may differ in children. Requires prompt diagnosis and treatment of infected individual and close contacts.

Epidemiology

Occurs worldwide with higher prevalence in crowded conditions, childcare, schools, institutions. Transmission through prolonged direct skin-to-skin contact (most common), occasionally through fomites. Peak incidence in children 2-6 years. Can occur at any age. More cases in winter in temperate climates.

Pathophysiology

Female mites burrow into stratum corneum, laying eggs. Burrows contain mites, feces, and eggs. Host immune response to mite antigens causes intense pruritus, particularly Th1-mediated reaction. Begins 2-6 weeks after initial infestation (longer if previously sensitized).

Clinical Presentation

Intense pruritus (often worse at night when mites more active). Characteristic burrows (linear or S-shaped, 2-3 mm) visible, most common on hands, wrists, interdigital spaces, feet, genital area. In children, may also involve face, scalp, trunk. Secondary excoriations from scratching common.

Atypical Presentations

Bullous scabies: large fluid-filled bullae from intense inflammation. Crusted scabies: widespread crusted lesions with higher mite burden. Nodular scabies: excoriated nodules on genitalia and axillae. Infants and young children: may involve face, neck, scalp (unlike adults).

Diagnosis

Clinical based on characteristic pruritus and burrows. Confirm by scrapings examined with KOH prep or microscopy showing mites, eggs, or feces. Dermoscopy may visualize mite in burrow. Diagnosis often clinical without confirmation.

Differential Diagnosis

Distinguish from atopic dermatitis (different distribution, chronicity), insect bites, contact dermatitis, urticaria, or other pruritis causes. Presence of burrows and involvement of characteristic sites helps identify scabies.

Treatment: Permethrin

First-line: permethrin 5% cream applied to entire body (excluding face, scalp in older children) for 8-14 hours, then wash off. Repeat in 7 days. Apply from neck down in children (avoid face/scalp with topical agents; may use sulfur ointment 5-10% for scalp/face).

Alternative Agents

Sulfur ointment (5-10%) safe in infants and pregnant women; apply nightly for 3 nights. Crotamiton 10%: less toxic alternative but lower efficacy. Ivermectin (systemic): 150 mcg/kg orally twice, spaced 7-14 days apart. Benzyl benzoate: effective but may cause irritation.

Management of Contacts

All household members and close contacts should be treated simultaneously to prevent reinfection. Clothing, bedding, towels washed in hot water. Items not washable sealed in bags for 72+ hours (mites cannot survive long off host).

Prognosis

Excellent with appropriate treatment. Pruritus may persist 2-4 weeks even after mites eliminated (due to residual inflammation). Recurrence common from inadequate initial treatment or reexposure.
