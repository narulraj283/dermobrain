Head Lice (Pediculosis Capitis) in Children

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Head Lice: Pediculosis Capitis in Children

Head lice (Pediculus humanus capitis) are parasitic insects infesting scalp hair in children, particularly ages 3-12 years. Highly transmissible through direct head-to-head contact and fomite transmission. Causes intense pruritus and social embarrassment. Effective treatment available with proper application and nit removal.

Epidemiology

Most common parasitic infestation in school-aged children. Peak ages 3-12 years. More common in girls. Transmission via direct head-to-head contact (most common) and potentially through shared combs, pillows, hats, headphones. Does not indicate poor hygiene. Affects all socioeconomic levels.

Life Cycle

Adult female lice lay eggs (nits) cemented to hair shaft. Nits hatch in 7-10 days. Nymphs mature to adults in 7-10 days. Adults live 3-4 weeks on host. Can survive only 24-48 hours off host. Entire life cycle 3-4 weeks.

Clinical Presentation

Pruritus (particularly occiput and nape of neck) most common symptom. Visible lice crawling on scalp or in hair. Nits visible attached to hair shafts. Secondary bacterial infection from scratching may develop. Occipital/posterior cervical lymphadenopathy common.

Nit Identification

Nits are oval, firmly attached to hair shaft, tan to whitish color. Distinguished from dandruff (flakes off easily) and hair casts (move freely on hair). Most abundant on hair close to scalp, particularly occiput.

Diagnosis

Clinical: visual identification of lice and/or nits. Use magnification to examine hair shafts. Distinguish from other causes of pruritus and scalp findings.

Treatment: Pediculicides

First-line: over-the-counter pyrethrins or permethrin 1%. Apply to scalp and hair, leave 10 minutes, rinse thoroughly. Repeat in 7-10 days to kill newly hatched nymphs. Malathion, benzyl alcohol, or spinosad for resistant cases. Ivermectin (systemic) for severe infestations.

Nit Removal

Essential for treatment success. After pediculicide treatment, comb through hair with fine-toothed nit comb to mechanically remove nits. Repeat daily for 7-10 days. Use vinegar rinse before combing to help loosen nits.

Environmentalcontrol

Wash clothing, bedding, pillows in hot water. Seal non-washable items in plastic bags for 2+ weeks (lice die without host). Soak combs and brushes in hot water or pediculicide solution for 5-10 minutes. Vacuum furniture.

School Policies

Many schools allow return once first treatment completed and child is nit-free. Check individual school policies. Exclusion policies have been questioned as unnecessary if treated.

Prognosis

Excellent with appropriate treatment and nit removal. Recurrence common from reinfection. Prevention through education, checking for signs regularly, treating promptly important.
