Melasma in Darker Skin Tones: Etiology, Diagnosis, and Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Melasma is an acquired, symmetric hypermelanosis of the face characterized by patches of hyperpigmentation. While melasma can affect individuals of all skin types, it is most common in individuals with darker skin, particularly those of Latin American, Asian, Indian, and Middle Eastern descent. The prevalence in these populations reaches 40-50% in women, making it one of the most common dyschromias in ethnic dermatology.

Pathophysiology

The etiology of melasma is multifactorial, involving complex interactions between UV exposure, genetic predisposition, hormonal factors, and inflammatory processes. Enhanced melanin synthesis results from activation of melanocytes by various triggers. Increased melanin transfer to keratinocytes and deposition in both epidermal and dermal layers contribute to the visible hyperpigmentation. In darker skin types, the baseline melanin content and enhanced melanocyte responsiveness create conditions favoring melasma development.

Triggering Factors

  • UV radiation (both UVA and UVB)
  • Hormonal factors (oral contraceptives, hormone replacement therapy)
  • Genetic predisposition
  • Pregnancy (often called chloasma)
  • Cosmetic products and fragrances
  • Photosensitizing medications
  • Thyroid disease

Clinical Features

Melasma presents as symmetric macules or patches with sharp borders, typically affecting the cheeks, nose, forehead, chin, and upper lip. Color ranges from light brown to deep gray-brown, often matching or exceeding the patient's baseline skin tone. Three morphologic patterns are recognized: centrofacial (60%), malar (30%), and mandibular (10%). The disorder is significantly more common in women, with male-to-female ratio of approximately 1:10.

Diagnostic Considerations

Clinical diagnosis is usually straightforward based on presentation. Wood lamp examination reveals that epidermal melasma appears darker and more prominent, while dermal melasma becomes less apparent or may disappear, helping determine the distribution of pigment. Dermoscopy can further characterize pigment location and pattern.

Evidence-Based Treatment

Photoprotection

Strict sun protection with broad-spectrum SPF 50+ sunscreen is essential and non-negotiable. Additional sun-protective measures (clothing, hats, avoidance of peak sun hours) should be recommended.

Topical Depigmenting Agents

Hydroquinone (2-4%) remains the gold standard, though long-term use requires monitoring for ochronosis risk (though rare in darker skin types). Tretinoin (0.025-0.1%) enhances the effect of hydroquinone and addresses photodamage. Combination therapies are often more effective than monotherapy. Alternative agents include azelaic acid, kojic acid, vitamin C, niacinamide, and licorice extract.

Chemical Peels

Glycolic acid peels, salicylic acid peels, and mandelic acid peels can help improve melasma when applied at appropriate concentrations. Peels should be conservative in darker skin to avoid post-peel complications. TCA peels carry higher risk in skin of color and should be used cautiously.

Laser and Light-Based Therapies

Fractional laser treatments can improve melasma, though caution is needed regarding post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation or hypopigmentation. Low fluence Nd:YAG laser has shown promising results with reduced complication rates. Multiple sessions are typically required.

Combination Approaches

Superior results are achieved with combination therapy: rigorous photoprotection plus topical depigmenting agents plus chemical peels or laser therapy. Treatment duration is typically 3-6 months minimum.

Patient Counseling

Realistic expectations are critical. Melasma improvement can be slow, and recurrence following sun exposure is common. Long-term maintenance therapy is usually necessary. Patients should understand that although various treatments are available, complete resolution may not be achievable.
