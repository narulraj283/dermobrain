Eczema and Atopic Dermatitis in Skin of Color: Phenotypes and Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Atopic dermatitis (AD) and other forms of eczema affect individuals of all racial and ethnic backgrounds, but the disease burden in individuals with darker skin is disproportionately high. Black Americans experience 1.5-3 times higher prevalence of AD compared to Caucasians, with more severe disease manifestations, higher rates of flares, and worse treatment outcomes. Understanding the phenotypic differences of eczema in skin of color and providing culturally-informed management is essential for dermatologists caring for diverse populations.

Epidemiology

African American children have approximately 2-3 times higher prevalence of AD compared to Caucasian children. Disease burden persists into adulthood, with Black adults showing continued higher prevalence compared to other racial/ethnic groups. Hispanic and Asian populations also show elevated AD prevalence in some studies. Factors contributing to disparities include genetic predisposition, environmental triggers, healthcare access barriers, and inadequate treatment access.

Clinical Phenotypes in Darker Skin Types

Morphologic Features

Eczema in darker skin often presents with lichenification, follicular prominence, and papular morphology rather than the classic erythematous plaques and scaling seen in lighter skin. Hyperpigmented, hypopigmented, or pigmented papules may be the dominant feature. These atypical presentations often lead to underrecognition of AD in people of color, delaying diagnosis and treatment initiation.

Sensitive Distribution

Face and neck involvement is more common in darker skin, whereas extensor surface involvement is more common in lighter skin. Hand dermatitis and follicular lesions are frequent manifestations in people of color. These distributional differences may influence quality of life impacts and treatment strategies.

Barrier Dysfunction

Individuals with darker skin may have inherent differences in epidermal barrier structure and function. Studies suggest differences in lipid composition, stratum corneum thickness, and water loss patterns. These differences may contribute to increased disease severity and altered treatment responses.

Pathophysiology Specific to Dark Skin

The 2+2 hypothesis (filaggrin mutations plus allergen sensitization) explains AD development in lighter skin, but this model may not fully explain AD in darker skin populations. Genetic factors distinct to individuals of African descent may influence filaggrin expression, immune responses, and barrier function. Environmental triggers (irritants, allergens, microbes) and psychosocial stressors may play amplified roles in disease pathogenesis in certain populations.

Diagnostic Considerations

Underdiagnosis of AD in skin of color results from limited recognition of atypical presentations and potential bias in provider assessment. Clinical criteria (Hanifin and Rajka, UK Working Party) apply across skin types but require cultural knowledge to interpret correctly. The atypical appearance of eczema in darker skin should not delay diagnosis or treatment initiation.

Treatment Principles

Emollients and Skin Care

Daily emollient use is foundational for all AD management. Emollients should be selected based on individual skin response, as preferences vary. Heavier formulations may be preferred by some patients but should not contribute to folliculitis. Frequent emollient application and liberal use during and after bathing optimize barrier support.

Topical Corticosteroids

Potency should be matched to body area and disease severity. Medium to high potency corticosteroids are often needed for flares, though careful monitoring for side effects is essential. Atrophy risk is lower on face and intertriginous areas, allowing appropriate use in these sensitive sites. Clear guidance on application frequency and duration minimizes misuse.

Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors

Tacrolimus and pimecrolimus are particularly valuable for facial and intertriginous AD where prolonged corticosteroid use is undesirable. These agents provide long-term anti-inflammatory benefits and are safe for extended use. Black box warning regarding potential malignancy is controversial and not supported by clinical evidence.

Phototherapy

Narrowband UVB phototherapy is effective for generalized AD in individuals with darker skin. Treatment twice weekly for 8-12 weeks can significantly improve disease. Safety monitoring for potential hyperpigmentation should be performed, though benefits typically outweigh risks. Excimer laser therapy targets localized areas.

Systemic Therapy

For moderate-to-severe AD inadequately controlled by topical therapies, systemic agents are indicated. Dupilumab (anti-IL-4 receptor antibody) has shown efficacy across racial/ethnic groups with improved outcomes in Black patients in some studies. Cyclosporine, mycophenolate, and azathioprine are additional systemic options. Referral to specialists experienced in managing severe AD is appropriate.

Complication Management

Secondary Infection

Bacterial colonization and infection are common complications of AD in all populations but may occur more frequently in darker skin. Appropriate antibiotic therapy for infected eczema is essential. Long-term low-dose antibiotics may reduce flare frequency in some patients.

Post-Inflammatory Changes

PIH following eczema flares is particularly common and visible in darker skin, creating additional cosmetic concern. Aggressive anti-inflammatory therapy to minimize inflammation-induced hyperpigmentation is important. Sun protection and topical depigmenting agents support management of established PIH.

Healthcare Access and Disparities

Barriers to specialist care, high out-of-pocket costs for biologic therapies, and transportation challenges limit treatment access for many Black and brown patients with AD. Insurance barriers prevent access to newer, highly effective biologic therapies. Systemic changes to expand access are necessary to reduce treatment disparities.

Culturally Informed Patient Education

Discussions regarding bathing frequency and water temperature, emollient selection, and clothing should account for cultural practices and individual preferences. Hair care practices should be integrated with skin care recommendations rather than viewed as competing priorities. Clear explanation of disease pathophysiology in languages patients understand improves adherence and outcomes.
