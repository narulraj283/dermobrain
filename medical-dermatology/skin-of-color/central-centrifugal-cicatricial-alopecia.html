Central Centrifugal Cicatricial Alopecia: Diagnosis and Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Central centrifugal cicatricial alopecia (CCCA) is a progressive scarring alopecia that predominantly affects women of African descent, with prevalence estimated at 3.4% in this population. The condition presents with hair loss beginning at the crown and expanding centrifugally (outward), causing permanent baldness in affected areas. CCCA represents a significant dermatologic concern requiring early recognition and aggressive intervention.

Epidemiology

CCCA occurs almost exclusively in women of African descent, with typical onset in the third to fourth decade of life, though cases in younger women have been reported. The female-to-male ratio approaches 100:1. Potential triggering factors include tight hairstyles, chemical hair treatments, heat-based styling, and possibly genetic predisposition, though the precise etiology remains incompletely understood.

Pathophysiology

CCCA involves primary inflammation of the hair follicle, leading to destruction of the follicular stem cells and permanent loss of follicular function. The mechanism is not fully characterized but likely involves multiple contributing factors: mechanical trauma from styling practices, chemical injury from hair treatments, inherent susceptibility of follicles in textured hair to damage, and possibly genetic predisposition affecting follicular stability. Histopathologic examination shows follicular inflammation with eventual fibrosis replacing the follicular structure.

Clinical Presentation

CCCA begins as alopecia at the crown vertex with gradual expansion in a circular pattern. Initially, affected hair appears shortened (broken) rather than lost at the root. Progressive scarring causes baldness with loss of follicular markings, creating a smooth, shiny scalp. Symptoms may include pruritus, tenderness, or burning at the margin of expanding alopecia. Rate of progression varies, with some patients experiencing rapid progression and others slower progression over years.

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis is based on characteristic presentation: scarring alopecia beginning at the crown and expanding centrifugally in women of African descent. Dermoscopy shows loss of follicular ostia and erythema in active disease. Scalp biopsy may be performed if diagnosis is uncertain, showing lymphocytic infiltration in active disease and fibrosis in established scarring.

Differential Diagnosis

CCCA must be differentiated from androgenetic alopecia, which is non-scarring; traction alopecia, which localizes to areas of mechanical stress; and other cicatricial alopecias such as lichen planopilaris or discoid lupus erythematosus. Clinical history and scalp examination typically allow distinction.

Management Strategies

Early Intervention

Early recognition and treatment are crucial to arrest progression before extensive scarring develops. First-line treatments target inflammation: intralesional triamcinolone acetonide injected into the scalp at the margin of active disease, topical corticosteroids and calcineurin inhibitors applied to affected areas.

Systemic Therapy

Oral antibiotics with anti-inflammatory properties (particularly doxycycline) may be used as adjunctive therapy. Hydroxychloroquine has shown benefit in some cases. Systemic corticosteroids may be considered in rapidly progressive disease, though long-term use is limited by side effects.

Adjunctive Treatments

Topical minoxidil may slow progression and support hair regrowth. Scalp care with gentle shampooing and moisturizing supports scalp health. Avoidance of tight hairstyles and chemical treatments is recommended, though evidence for direct causation in established CCCA is debated.

Procedural Options

Hair transplantation can restore hair to areas of permanent scarring after disease stabilization. This requires confirmation of disease quiescence before surgical intervention to ensure transplanted hairs persist.

Monitoring and Prognosis

Regular scalp examination to assess disease activity and progression is essential. Photographs document changes over time. Response to treatment varies; some patients stabilize with early intervention while others continue to progress despite therapy. Long-term treatment may be necessary to maintain disease control.

Patient Education

Patients should be counseled regarding disease nature, potential triggers, importance of early treatment, and realistic expectations regarding outcomes. Regular dermatologic follow-up is necessary to assess treatment response and modify therapy as needed.
