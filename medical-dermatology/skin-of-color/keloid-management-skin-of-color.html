Ethnic & Skin of Color Dermatology

Keloid Management in Skin of Color: Comprehensive Clinical Guidelines

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Keloids are a form of pathologic scarring characterized by excessive fibroblast proliferation and abnormal collagen synthesis. The incidence of keloid formation is 10-15 times higher in individuals with darker skin types compared to Caucasians, particularly in individuals of African descent. This increased predisposition reflects genetic and immunologic differences in wound healing mechanisms.

Epidemiology and Risk Factors

Keloids can develop following minor trauma, including ear piercing, vaccination, insect bites, acne lesions, and surgical procedures. However, the triggers are not predictable in all cases—some keloids develop spontaneously without identifiable trauma. Individuals with a family history of keloids have significantly increased risk. Age of onset is typically between 10-30 years, with higher incidence in women and in certain anatomic locations such as the chest, shoulders, and earlobes.

Pathophysiology

The exact mechanisms underlying increased keloid susceptibility in skin of color remain incompletely understood but likely involve: altered transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β) signaling pathways, increased collagen synthesis by fibroblasts, impaired collagen catabolism, abnormal inflammatory response, and genetic predisposition affecting wound healing regulation. Aberrant Wnt/β-catenin and hedgehog signaling pathways have also been implicated.

Clinical Presentation

Keloids present as firm, rubbery, nodular lesions that extend beyond the original wound boundaries. They may be flesh-colored, pink, red, or hyperpigmented. Common symptoms include pruritus and pain. Unlike hypertrophic scars, keloids continue to grow over months to years and do not regress spontaneously.

Evidence-Based Treatment Options

Intralesional Corticosteroids

Triamcinolone acetonide (10-40 mg/mL) remains the first-line treatment. Multiple injections spaced 2-4 weeks apart over 3-6 months achieve the best results. Success rates range from 50-80% with combination therapies.

Surgical Intervention

Excision alone carries high recurrence rates (45-100%). However, excision combined with other modalities significantly improves outcomes. Combination approaches include excision followed by intralesional corticosteroid injections or radiation therapy.

Laser Therapy

Pulsed dye lasers and Nd:YAG lasers can reduce erythema and flatten keloids. These modalities work best when combined with other treatments. Multiple sessions are typically required.

Cryotherapy

Liquid nitrogen application causes controlled tissue destruction and may help flatten keloids, particularly small lesions. Cryotherapy can be combined with intralesional injections for enhanced efficacy.

Radiation Therapy

Brachytherapy or external beam radiation applied immediately following surgical excision reduces recurrence rates. Typical doses range from 15-20 Gy. Radiation is particularly effective for recurrent or large keloids but requires careful consideration of long-term risks.

Combination Approaches

The most successful outcomes occur with combination treatments: surgical excision followed by intralesional corticosteroid injections and/or laser therapy, with or without adjunctive radiation.

Prevention Strategies

Counseling patients about increased keloid risk is essential. Minimization of unnecessary procedures, careful surgical planning with tension-reducing techniques, and use of topical or intralesional corticosteroids immediately after procedures may reduce keloid formation risk. For elective procedures in high-risk individuals, consideration should be given to delaying non-essential surgeries or pursuing alternative management strategies.
