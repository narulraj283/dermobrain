Ethnic & Skin of Color Dermatology

Skin Cancer Detection in Dark Skin: Challenges and Clinical Guidance

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Skin cancer incidence is lower in individuals with darker skin types due to protective melanin, yet melanoma in this population carries significantly worse prognosis due to delayed diagnosis and advanced stage at presentation. Early recognition of skin cancer in dark skin is complicated by atypical presentations, lower clinical suspicion, and inadequate dermatologic training regarding skin cancer in people of color. Increased awareness and systematic screening approaches are essential to reduce disparities in cancer outcomes.

Epidemiology and Disparities

While melanoma incidence is 10-20 times lower in Black and Hispanic populations compared to Caucasians, mortality rates approach or exceed those in lighter-skinned populations due to later stage at diagnosis. Five-year survival for Black Americans with melanoma is 65-70% compared to >90% for Caucasians. Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders also experience worse melanoma outcomes than expected based on incidence. These disparities reflect delayed diagnosis, later stage at presentation, and potentially biologic differences in aggressive subtypes.

Anatomic Differences in Melanoma Presentation

Melanoma in dark skin often occurs on acral sites (palms, soles, nail beds)—sites that historically received less screening attention and have inherently worse prognosis. Acral lentiginous melanoma (ALM), which tends to be more aggressive, is proportionally more common in people of color. Oral, genital, and mucosal melanomas occur more frequently in darker skin types, presenting additional diagnostic challenges.

Clinical Presentation of Melanoma in Dark Skin

Cutaneous Melanomas

While typical ABCDE criteria (Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color variation, Diameter >6mm, Evolving/changing) apply to melanoma in all skin types, application in dark skin requires modification. Lesions on dark skin may appear as darkly pigmented, asymmetric macules or patches rather than the variegated lesions typical in lighter skin. Small lesions (<6 mm) can be melanoma. Any changing lesion warrants concern.

Acral Lesions

Melanomas of palms, soles, and nail beds present differently. Palmoplantar melanomas appear as dark macules or patches with irregular borders and variable pigmentation. Nail melanomas (melanonychia) present as longitudinal dark streaks in the nail plate. Size, change over time, and asymmetry of pigment distribution are concerning features. Subungual melanomas may present with pigmentation extending to the nail fold (Hutchinson sign).

Mucosal Melanomas

Melanomas of oral mucosa, genital skin, and anus are more common in people of color than in Caucasians. These present as dark, irregular macules or nodules with rapid growth. Any pigmented lesion in these locations warrants close evaluation.

Differential Diagnosis

Benign Pigmented Lesions

Dermatosis papulosa nigra, lentigos, lichen planus pigmentosus, erythromelanosis follicularis faciei, and other benign conditions occur commonly in dark skin and must be distinguished from melanoma. Clinical history, dermoscopy, and biopsy when uncertain help differentiate benign from malignant lesions.

Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation

PIH following inflammation can resemble melanoma in appearance. Clinical history of preceding inflammation and dermoscopic features help differentiate.

Screening and Early Detection

Clinical Examination

Comprehensive skin examination should be performed on all patients, including examination of acral sites, nails, mucous membranes, and intertriginous areas. Adequate lighting, magnification, and time allow identification of subtle lesions. Photography documents changing lesions over time.

Dermoscopy

Dermoscopic examination enhances visualization of pigment patterns and structural features. However, dermoscopic patterns are less well-characterized in dark skin compared to light skin, potentially reducing sensitivity. Practitioners should be trained specifically in dermoscopy of dark skin.

Biopsy

Any lesion with features concerning for melanoma should be biopsied. Shave biopsies are acceptable for non-nodular lesions and are convenient, though they may not provide adequate depth assessment. Excisional biopsies allow complete lesion evaluation and appropriate depth assessment.

Risk Factors for Melanoma in Dark Skin

  • Family or personal history of melanoma
  • Presence of atypical nevi
  • Numerous ordinary nevi
  • Fair skin features within context of dark skin (e.g., light patches)
  • Large acral nevi
  • Longitudinal melanonychia

Non-Melanoma Skin Cancers

Basal Cell Carcinoma

While less common in dark skin, BCC does occur and often presents with less obvious erythema and scaling than in light skin. Lesions may appear as subtle, shiny, indurated plaques or nodules. Dark-pigmented BCC can mimic melanoma clinically and dermoscopically.

Squamous Cell Carcinoma

SCC occurs less commonly in dark skin but when present may be associated with chronic inflammation, scars, or previous injury. Keratoacanthomas occur more commonly in dark skin and must be distinguished from SCC, though they have similar clinical appearance and biologic potential.

Management of Confirmed Melanoma

Once melanoma diagnosis is confirmed, standard management applies: wide excision with appropriate margins (typically 1-3 cm based on depth), sentinel lymph node biopsy if indicated, and staging evaluation. Referral to oncology for systemic therapy decisions is appropriate for advanced disease. Close follow-up surveillance is essential given higher mortality rates in dark skin.

Addressing Healthcare Disparities

Efforts to reduce melanoma disparities include: education of both patients and healthcare providers regarding cancer risk in dark skin, improved training in skin cancer detection in people of color, implementation of systematic screening protocols, and addressing healthcare access barriers. Shared decision-making and culturally-informed communication enhance patient engagement in screening and early detection efforts.
