Ethnic & Skin of Color Dermatology

Acne in Skin of Color: Epidemiology, Presentation, and Therapeutic Considerations

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Acne affects individuals of all races and ethnicities, but presentation, severity, and response to treatment vary significantly by skin type. In individuals with skin of color, acne often presents with distinct morphologic patterns and is frequently complicated by post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and keloid formation, making appropriate management crucial.

Epidemiology

Contrary to historical misconceptions, acne prevalence is comparable across racial and ethnic groups, affecting approximately 40-50 million Americans regardless of skin tone. However, in individuals with darker skin types, acne tends to be more severe, to persist longer into adulthood, and to result in greater rates of permanent scarring and dyspigmentation.

Clinical Presentation in Darker Skin Types

Acne in skin of color often presents with predominance of inflammatory lesions (papules, pustules, and nodules) rather than comedones. Hyperpigmented macules may be particularly visible against darker skin backgrounds. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation often persists for extended periods, leading patients to perceive acne as more severe than in lighter skin types. Perioral and jaw acne is more common in women of color.

Pathophysiology Considerations

The four hallmarks of acne pathogenesis are: increased sebum production, follicular hyperkeratinization, Cutibacterium acnes colonization, and inflammation. In darker skin types, the inflammatory component appears more pronounced, and the transition from acne to post-inflammatory pigmentary changes is more dramatic, creating both therapeutic and cosmetic challenges.

Treatment Principles

Topical Therapies

Benzoyl peroxide (2.5-5%) is highly effective and well-tolerated. Lower concentrations minimize irritation while maintaining efficacy. Retinoids (tretinoin, adapalene, retinol) reduce follicular hyperkeratinization and prevent post-inflammatory pigmentary changes. Use should be initiated at lower concentrations (0.025% tretinoin or lower) and increased gradually. Niacinamide offers anti-inflammatory benefits with minimal irritation.

Oral Medications

Oral antibiotics (doxycycline, minocycline) provide systemic anti-inflammatory benefits. Doxycycline monohydrate (50-100 mg daily) offers additional sun protection and reduced phototoxicity compared to other doxycycline formulations. Combined oral contraceptive pills regulate sebum production in females.

Isotretinoin Considerations

Severe, scarring acne warrants isotretinoin therapy. Darker skin types may require more careful monitoring, as both retinoid side effects and post-inflammatory pigmentary changes can be more pronounced.

Preventing Complications

Aggressive early treatment of inflammatory lesions reduces risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and scarring. Strict sun protection (SPF 50+) is essential, as sun exposure exacerbates post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. Patients should be counseled against skin picking and manipulation of lesions, which increases risk of traumatic PIH and keloid formation.

Procedural Interventions

Chemical peels with glycolic or salicylic acid at conservative concentrations can improve acne and reduce post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. Laser therapies and extractions under proper guidance may be incorporated into comprehensive treatment plans.
