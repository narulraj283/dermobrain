Hypertrophic Scars in Dark Skin: Pathophysiology and Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Hypertrophic scars are thickened, raised scars that remain confined within the boundaries of the original wound. While they can occur in all skin types, they occur more frequently in individuals with darker skin and occur with greater severity. Unlike keloids, hypertrophic scars typically remain within wound margins and may undergo some spontaneous regression over years.

Pathophysiology

Hypertrophic scar formation results from excessive collagen deposition during the remodeling phase of wound healing. The process involves: prolonged inflammatory phase, increased myofibroblast activation and differentiation, excessive extracellular matrix production, and reduced collagen degradation. In darker skin types, these processes appear to be exaggerated, suggesting underlying differences in wound healing biology related to genetic factors and inherent immune response characteristics.

Risk Factors

  • Depth of initial wound or trauma
  • Wound tension and location (especially over joints)
  • Delay in wound healing
  • Age (increased risk in younger individuals)
  • Genetic predisposition in darker skin types
  • Prolonged inflammatory phase

Clinical Characteristics

Hypertrophic scars present as raised, firm, sometimes nodular lesions that remain within wound margins. They may be red, purple, or hyperpigmented. Symptoms can include pruritus, pain, and functional impairment. Onset is typically within weeks to months following injury, with gradual improvement possible over 1-2 years, though some scars persist indefinitely.

Management Strategies

Conservative Approaches

Silicone gel products and pressure garments can help flatten and soften hypertrophic scars with consistent use. Sun protection prevents hyperpigmentation that may accentuate scars in darker skin types.

Intralesional Treatments

Triamcinolone acetonide injections reduce scar bulk and improve appearance. 5-fluorouracil (5-FU) injections can inhibit fibroblast proliferation. Bleomycin has shown efficacy in some resistant cases. These are often combined for optimal results.

Laser and Light-Based Therapies

Pulsed dye lasers reduce erythema and improve texture. Fractional laser resurfacing remodels collagen. Nd:YAG lasers penetrate more deeply to address scar volume. Multiple sessions spaced 4-6 weeks apart are typically needed.

Surgical Options

Surgical scar revision can reorient scars to better align with natural skin lines. Excision followed by careful layered closure reduces scar prominence. Z-plasty and W-plasty techniques redistribute tension and improve appearance.

Combination Therapy

Best results are achieved with combination approaches: surgical revision combined with laser therapy and/or intralesional injections provides superior outcomes compared to single modalities.

Preventive Measures

Optimal wound care during initial healing phase is crucial. Tension-reducing closure techniques, appropriate wound support, and early attention to infection prevention can minimize subsequent hypertrophic scar formation. Patient education regarding realistic timelines and the natural history of scar remodeling is essential.
