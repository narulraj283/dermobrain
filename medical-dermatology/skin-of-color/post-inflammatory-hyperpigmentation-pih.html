Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation: Understanding PIH in Skin of Color

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) is one of the most common sequelae of cutaneous inflammation in individuals with darker skin types (Fitzpatrick IV-VI). Unlike post-inflammatory erythema seen in lighter skin, PIH represents increased melanin deposition in the epidermis and dermis following skin injury or inflammation. The condition is particularly prevalent in patients of African, Asian, Hispanic, and Mediterranean descent.

Pathophysiology

When inflammation occurs in the skin, keratinocytes and fibroblasts release inflammatory mediators, including tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α) and interleukin-1 (IL-1), which upregulate tyrosinase activity in melanocytes. This increased enzymatic activity leads to enhanced melanin production. Additionally, the transfer of melanosomes to surrounding keratinocytes increases, resulting in visible hyperpigmentation that can persist for months to years.

Clinical Presentation

PIH typically manifests as symmetric macules or patches that are darker than the patient's baseline skin tone. The color may range from light brown to deep black, depending on the severity of the initial inflammation and the baseline skin tone. Common trigger sites include the face, neck, chest, and areas prone to friction or trauma.

Common Triggers

  • Acne and acne medications (especially retinoids)
  • Eczema and irritant contact dermatitis
  • Laser treatments and chemical peels
  • Surgical procedures and traumatic injuries
  • Insect bites and infections
  • Photosensitizing medications

Management Strategies

Treatment should focus on preventing further inflammation and gradually reducing existing hyperpigmentation. First-line approaches include strict sun protection with SPF 50+ broad-spectrum sunscreen, as UV exposure can darken PIH. Topical depigmenting agents such as hydroquinone (2-4%), tretinoin, and combination formulations have demonstrated efficacy. Kojic acid, azelaic acid, and niacinamide offer alternative or adjunctive options.

For persistent cases, procedural interventions may be considered. Low-energy laser treatments and chemical peels with lower concentrations of glycolic acid or salicylic acid can help, though caution is needed to avoid triggering additional inflammation. Some dermatologists report success with fractional laser therapy at conservative settings.

Prevention

The most effective management of PIH is prevention through prompt treatment of triggering conditions and appropriate skin care. Patients should be counseled on gentle skin handling, avoidance of irritating products, and early recognition of inflammation to minimize cascade effects.
