Acne Keloidalis Nuchae: Pathophysiology and Therapeutic Approaches

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Acne keloidalis nuchae (AKN) is a chronic folliculitis of the posterior neck characterized by inflammation, keloid-like scar formation, and progressive alopecia. Despite its name, AKN is not true acne but rather a form of folliculitis exacerbated by cutaneous trauma and characteristic hair anatomy. The condition overwhelmingly affects men of African descent, with prevalence ranging from 0.6-3% in this population compared to less than 0.1% in Caucasians.

Pathophysiology

AKN results from a combination of factors: (1) Tight curl pattern of hair creates mechanical stress at follicles; (2) Close haircuts or frequent shaving cause traumatic folliculitis; (3) Follicular rupture releases keratin and hair material into the dermis, triggering foreign body inflammatory response; (4) Chronic inflammation leads to fibrosis and keloid-like scar formation; (5) Repeated trauma perpetuates the cycle. Unlike pseudofolliculitis barbae, which typically improves with modified grooming, AKN often progresses despite hair care modifications.

Clinical Features

AKN presents with painful, inflamed papules, pustules, and keloid-like nodules on the posterior and lateral neck. The condition typically manifests in adolescence or early adulthood following onset of hair shaving. Progressive scarring alopecia develops over time, creating permanent baldness in severely affected areas. Drainage of purulent material is common. Pruritus and pain may be severe, affecting quality of life.

Classification

Early-stage AKN shows inflammation and folliculitis with minimal scarring. Intermediate-stage presents with established keloid-like scar formation and moderate fibrosis. Late-stage demonstrates extensive cicatricial alopecia, extensive scarring, and severe inflammation that may be difficult to treat.

Management Strategies

Early Intervention Approach

Hair management modifications to minimize mechanical trauma are important: allowing longer hair length, avoiding close haircuts, using electric clippers instead of razors, and avoiding friction from tight clothing. However, these measures alone often fail to arrest progressive disease.

Topical and Intralesional Treatments

Intralesional triamcinolone injections (40 mg/mL) reduce inflammation and scar bulk. Retinoids and topical antibiotics address underlying folliculitis. Combination therapies improve efficacy compared to monotherapy.

Systemic Therapy

Extended courses of oral antibiotics (typically doxycycline or minocycline) provide anti-inflammatory benefits. Long-term therapy (6-12 months) may be necessary. Isotretinoin has been used in severe cases with variable success, though significant systemic side effects limit its use.

Procedural Interventions

Surgical options include: excision with primary closure (carries high recurrence rates), excision with skin grafting (lower recurrence), laser ablation, and cryotherapy. Combination approaches using excision followed by intralesional corticosteroids or laser therapy yield better outcomes. Early surgical intervention before extensive scarring develops optimizes results.

Laser Hair Removal

Permanent hair reduction using laser therapy may help prevent recurrence of folliculitis, especially when combined with surgical approaches. Multiple sessions over 6-12 months are required.

Complications

Progressive cicatricial alopecia is the most significant complication, resulting in permanent baldness in the affected area. Secondary bacterial infection and abscess formation may occur. Some cases progress to extensive keloid-like scarring requiring aggressive intervention.

Prognosis

Without treatment, AKN typically progresses. Early recognition and aggressive multimodal intervention provide the best outcomes. Referral to dermatologists experienced in managing ethnic skin conditions is recommended for optimal care.
