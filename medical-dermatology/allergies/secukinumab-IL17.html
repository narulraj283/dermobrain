Ustekinumab (Stelara): IL-23p40 Inhibition for Psoriasis and IBD

Clinical Overview

Ustekinumab (Stelara) is a fully human monoclonal antibody targeting the IL-23p40 subunit shared by both IL-23 and IL-12 cytokines. This dual targeting provides a broader mechanism than IL-23-only inhibitors while maintaining favorable safety. Ustekinumab was the first IL-23 pathway inhibitor approved for moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis (2009) and is now FDA-approved for psoriasis, psoriatic arthritis, Crohn's disease, and ulcerative colitis. Clinical efficacy shows PASI-75 achievement in 68.1% of patients at week 12 in the PHOENIX 1 trial. Unlike IL-17A inhibitors, ustekinumab demonstrates better gastrointestinal tolerability and lower infection risk, making it an excellent option for patients with IBD comorbidities.

Epidemiology and Clinical Context

Psoriasis and IBD frequently co-occur (2-10% comorbidity). TNF-α inhibitors are contraindicated in psoriasis due to paradoxical psoriasiform reactions in 5-20% of patients. IL-17A inhibitors may exacerbate IBD through IL-17A's protective mucosal barrier function. Ustekinumab's dual IL-12/IL-23 inhibition provides both psoriasis benefit and Crohn's disease efficacy, making it ideal for patients with both conditions. Among biologic-naïve patients with moderate-to-severe psoriasis, ustekinumab induces PASI-75 in 68% and PASI-90 in 44%, comparable to TNF-α inhibitors and IL-17A inhibitors.

Mechanism of Action

Ustekinumab binds to the p40 subunit of IL-12 and IL-23 (Kd = 0.0043 nM). IL-23 drives Th17 cell differentiation and expansion, producing IL-17, IL-22, and TNF-α. IL-12 drives Th1 differentiation, producing IFN-γ. By blocking both pathways, ustekinumab inhibits both Th1 and Th17 differentiation, reducing epidermal hyperplasia and immune infiltration. In Crohn's disease, IL-23 inhibition reduces pathogenic Th17 responses in gut-associated lymphoid tissue, explaining its therapeutic benefit across both indications.

Clinical Presentation and Baseline Assessment

Candidates for ustekinumab typically have moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis (PASI ≥12 or ≥10% BSA) unresponsive to topical therapy or phototherapy. Before initiation, obtain: TB screening, CBC with differential, CMP, hepatitis B/C serology (critical), lipid panel, and vaccination status. Document baseline PASI score, BSA involvement, and quality of life impact (DLQI score).

Dosing and Administration

Weight-based dosing:

  • ≤45 kg: 45 mg SC at weeks 0, 4, then every 12 weeks
  • 46-90 kg: 90 mg SC at weeks 0, 4, then every 12 weeks
  • >90 kg: 90 mg SC at weeks 0, 4, then every 12 weeks (may escalate to 130 mg every 8-12 weeks if inadequate response)

Intravenous administration (45 mg or 90 mg based on weight) is available as alternative. Injections use 0.5-1 mL prefilled syringe; patient self-injection is feasible after training. Rotate injection sites.

Efficacy in Psoriasis

PHOENIX 1: PASI-75 at week 12 was 68.1% (45 kg: 45 mg) and 67.1% (>90 kg: 90 mg) vs 3.1% placebo. PASI-90 in 44.8% vs 0.8% placebo.

PHOENIX 2: PASI-75 at week 12 was 69.5% vs 3.3% placebo; sustained response through 52 weeks in >80%.

Timeline: Slower than IL-17A inhibitors; significant improvement by weeks 4-8, maximal response by week 16-24.

Psoriatic arthritis: ACR-20 response in 42% vs 15% placebo at week 24; ACR-50 in 25% vs 7%.

Durability: Discontinuation studies show relapse within 12-16 weeks in 50%, but continuous therapy maintains response. Loss of efficacy <5%.

Efficacy in IBD

Ustekinumab is FDA-approved for moderate-to-severe Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis. UNITI trials showed induction therapy resulted in clinical remission in 34% of Crohn's patients vs 21% placebo. Maintenance therapy sustains remission in >50%. This makes ustekinumab valuable for psoriasis patients with concurrent IBD.

Safety and Monitoring

Common adverse events: Infection (6-8%), injection site reactions (4-7%), headache (2%), fatigue (2%).

Serious monitoring points: TB reactivation risk exists; TB testing mandatory. Hepatitis B reactivation can occur in HBsAg+ patients; prophylactic antivirals essential. Rare but serious: reversible posterior leukoencephalopathy syndrome (RPLS). Live vaccine contraindication during therapy and 15 weeks after discontinuation.

Baseline and ongoing: TB screening, CBC, CMP, hepatitis B/C serology. Clinical assessment at weeks 0, 4, 12, 16, then every 12 weeks. If inadequate response at week 16, reassess diagnosis and consider dose escalation or alternative therapy.

When to Switch or Discontinue

Inadequate response: If PASI-75 not achieved by week 16-20, assess compliance and consider alternate biologic. TNF-α inhibitors, IL-17A inhibitors, and IL-23p19-specific inhibitors are alternatives.

Loss of efficacy: Secondary loss is uncommon; if it occurs, assess for superimposed infection or drug interaction. Switching to IL-23p19-specific inhibitors may restore response.

Discontinuation: If complete remission achieved and maintained ≥24 weeks, consider de-escalation to longer intervals or drug holiday. However, expect relapse in 50% within 12-16 weeks; maintenance typical.

Prognosis and Long-Term Outcomes

Long-term data (5-10 years) show sustained PASI-75 in 60-70% with continuous therapy. Serious infection rates <2% annually; TB reactivation rare with proper screening. Tachyphylaxis uncommon. Patients with concurrent psoriasis and IBD often achieve simultaneous improvement in both.

Drug Interactions and Special Populations

Drug interactions: Minimal CYP450 interaction. Avoid combining with other immunosuppressants unless indicated.

Pregnancy: Animal data show no teratogenicity; limited human pregnancy data. Women should use effective contraception.

Elderly patients: No dose adjustment needed; assess baseline infections carefully.

When to Refer

Refer for moderate-to-severe psoriasis unresponsive to topical/phototherapy, psoriasis comorbid with IBD, psoriatic arthritis, or complex diagnostic questions.

FAQ

Is ustekinumab slower than other biologics?

Yes, compared to IL-17A inhibitors, ustekinumab shows slower onset. While IL-17A inhibitors achieve PASI-75 by week 12 in >80%, ustekinumab reaches PASI-75 in about 68%, with maximal response by week 16-24. However, response rates and durability are comparable once fully achieved. The tradeoff is better infection safety and IBD compatibility.

Can I use ustekinumab if I have Crohn's disease?

Yes—ustekinumab is FDA-approved for both Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis. This makes it an excellent choice if you have psoriasis AND inflammatory bowel disease, as other biologics may worsen GI disease. Ustekinumab will treat both conditions simultaneously. Coordinate care between your dermatologist and gastroenterologist.

What if I'm overweight? Does higher dose work better?

Ustekinumab dosing is weight-based: 45 mg for ≤45 kg, and 90 mg for >45 kg. For patients >90 kg with suboptimal response at week 16, higher doses (130 mg) can be considered. However, higher weight alone doesn't determine response; disease severity and individual immune response matter more. Most patients >90 kg respond well to standard 90 mg dosing.

I have hepatitis B. Can I use ustekinumab?

If you are HBsAg positive, ustekinumab can be used with prophylactic antiviral therapy (lamivudine, entecavir, or tenofovir) to prevent reactivation. Your dermatologist and hepatologist should coordinate care. Regular monitoring of liver function and hepatitis B viral load is essential. If you are only hepatitis B antibody positive, ustekinumab can be used without special precautions.
