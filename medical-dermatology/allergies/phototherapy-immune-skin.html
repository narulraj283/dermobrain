Anaphylaxis: Recognition, Immediate Management, and Skin Manifestations

Clinical Overview

Anaphylaxis is a severe, potentially life-threatening systemic allergic reaction occurring within seconds to minutes of exposure to a triggering antigen. Diagnosis and management require rapid recognition and immediate intramuscular epinephrine administration. Skin manifestations (urticaria, angioedema, flushing) are present in 80-90% of anaphylactic reactions, making dermatologists important front-line recognizers. Untreated anaphylaxis can progress to cardiovascular collapse, loss of airway, and death within 15-30 minutes. However, prompt epinephrine administration dramatically improves outcomes, with mortality <1% in patients receiving timely treatment.

Epidemiology and Incidence

Annual anaphylaxis incidence: 1.6-2 per 100,000 population, rising. Lifetime risk: 1-3% of population. Peak incidence: food-related in children; medications and insect stings in adults. Common triggers: foods (peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, milk, eggs—account for 90% of food-related anaphylaxis in children and 50% in adults), medications (penicillin, cephalosporins, NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors—account for 10-15% of anaphylaxis), insect venom (bee, wasp, fire ant—account for 20-30%), idiopathic (10-15%). Severity ranges from mild reactions (cutaneous symptoms alone) to severe (cardiovascular collapse, respiratory failure).

Pathophysiology and IgE-Mediated Mechanisms

Classic IgE-mediated anaphylaxis occurs through the following mechanism:

Sensitization: Initial antigen exposure to IgE-producing B cells; IgE antibodies bind to high-affinity IgE receptors (FcεRI) on mast cells and basophils.

Re-exposure and degranulation: Subsequent antigen exposure cross-links FcεRI on mast cell/basophil surface, triggering rapid degranulation and release of: (1) preformed mediators (histamine, tryptase, leukotrienes, heparin—released in seconds to minutes), (2) newly synthesized mediators (prostaglandins, leukotrienes—released within minutes), (3) cytokines (TNF-α, IL-4, IL-5—causing late-phase reactions).

Mediator effects: Histamine binds H1 and H2 receptors on endothelial cells and smooth muscle, causing vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, smooth muscle contraction, and itching. Leukotrienes (C4, D4, E4) are potent vasoconstrictors and cause bronchoconstriction and mucus secretion. Tryptase activates protease-activated receptor 2 (PAR-2) on endothelial cells, contributing to vascular permeability and hypotension.

Non-IgE mechanisms: Some anaphylaxis occurs independent of IgE: direct mast cell activation (opioids, vancomycin, contrast agents), complement activation (IVIg, dialyzers), or bradykinin-mediated (ACE inhibitor angioedema, though not true IgE anaphylaxis). These atypical mechanisms require different management approaches.

Clinical Presentation and Cutaneous Manifestations

Skin findings (80-90% of cases): Pruritus (often first symptom, starting at site of antigen exposure and generalizing), urticaria (erythematous wheals, transient, migratory), flushing (erythema without raised lesions), angioedema (facial, laryngeal, tongue—non-pitting edema involving deeper dermis and subcutis).

Cardiovascular symptoms: Hypotension, tachycardia or bradycardia (vagal response), syncope, shock, cardiac arrhythmias, myocardial infarction (in severe cases).

Respiratory symptoms: Stridor (laryngeal involvement), dyspnea, bronchospasm (wheezing), respiratory failure (severe cases).

Gastrointestinal symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, cramping, diarrhea.

CNS symptoms: Dizziness, altered mental status, loss of consciousness (due to hypotension or hypoxia).

Timeline: Symptoms typically onset within 15-30 minutes of antigen exposure (most within 5 minutes); faster onset = more severe reaction. Some biphasic reactions occur, with recrudescence of symptoms hours after initial response.

Diagnosis: Clinical Recognition and Triptase Testing

Diagnosis is clinical, based on rapid onset of symptoms and signs characteristic of anaphylaxis. No single test confirms anaphylaxis in real-time; however, serum tryptase (released by mast cell degranulation) can support diagnosis if obtained within 15-60 minutes. Tryptase >11.4 ng/mL (upper limit normal typically 11.4 ng/mL) at time of reaction supports anaphylaxis diagnosis. Peak tryptase occurs 15-30 minutes after onset; a second tryptase at 2 hours may be obtained if first was normal and suspicion remains high.

Skin testing: After acute reaction resolves (≥4-6 weeks), skin prick testing or intradermal testing (with appropriate safeguards) can identify specific allergen triggers for IgE-mediated anaphylaxis. Serologic IgE testing (ImmunoCAP, RAST) can also quantify allergen-specific IgE. These tests aid in future trigger avoidance.

Immediate Management: Epinephrine Administration

FIRST-LINE TREATMENT: Intramuscular epinephrine is the definitive treatment. Delay in administration increases mortality. Autoinjectors (EpiPen, Auvi-Q) are preferred in pre-hospital settings and outpatient environments due to rapid administration.

Dosing:

  • Adults: Epinephrine 0.3-0.5 mg IM (1:1000 concentration, 0.3-0.5 mL) to lateral thigh (vastus lateralis muscle) for rapid absorption and access through clothing.
  • Pediatric: Epinephrine 0.01 mg/kg IM (up to 0.3-0.5 mg maximum) based on weight and age; typical dosing: 0.15 mg for <25 kg, 0.3 mg for 25-50 kg, 0.5 mg for >50 kg.

Timing: Administer immediately; every minute of delay worsens outcomes. Repeat dose every 5-15 minutes if inadequate response (persistent hypotension, worsening symptoms) for maximum 3-4 doses.

IV epinephrine: Reserved for ICU/hospital settings if cardiovascular collapse present or IV access immediately available. Dose: 0.3-0.5 mg slow IV push (over 1-5 minutes) diluted to 1:10,000 concentration. Risk of arrhythmia and hypertension if IV route used; requires cardiac monitoring.

Adjunctive Management After Epinephrine

IV access and fluids: Establish IV access; infuse normal saline (bolus of 1-2 L over 15-30 minutes) to counteract histamine-induced vasodilation and maintain blood pressure.

Antihistamines: Diphenhydramine 25-50 mg IV/IM (or cetirizine/loratadine orally if mild and responding to epinephrine). These reduce urticaria and pruritus but do NOT replace epinephrine in treatment of anaphylaxis.

Corticosteroids: Methylprednisolone 125 mg IV or prednisone 40-60 mg PO. These help prevent late-phase and biphasic reactions but have no acute effect and do not replace epinephrine.

H2 blockers: Ranitidine 50 mg IV (if available; may not be needed) or omeprazole. H2 receptors also mediate some histamine effects; blockers may provide adjunctive benefit.

Airway management: If stridor or respiratory compromise: oxygen, position of comfort, prepare for intubation if worsening. Epinephrine reduces airway edema rapidly.

Position and monitoring: Place patient supine with legs elevated (Trendelenburg) to improve cerebral perfusion if hypotensive. Monitor vitals continuously (BP, HR, O2 saturation, ECG if available).

Observation and Biphasic Reactions

All anaphylaxis patients require observation for 4-24 hours (depending on severity and response). Biphasic anaphylaxis—recrudescence of symptoms after initial improvement—occurs in 1-3% of cases, typically 4-12 hours after onset. This may be due to persistent antigen, ongoing mediator release, or inadequate initial treatment. Extended observation, corticosteroid prophylaxis, and availability of epinephrine are warranted.

Differential Diagnosis

Acute urticaria vs. anaphylaxis: Acute urticaria presents with urticaria/pruritus but lacks systemic symptoms (cardiovascular, respiratory, GI). Some urticaria cases progress to anaphylaxis, but most resolve with antihistamines alone.

Angioedema (ACE inhibitor-related): Non-IgE mediated bradykinin-related swelling. Does not respond to epinephrine or antihistamines. Requires fresh frozen plasma or C1-esterase inhibitor for airway-threatening cases. Lacks other anaphylaxis features (urticaria typically absent, no gastrointestinal/cardiovascular collapse unless airway critical).

Hereditary angioedema: Recurrent episodic swelling of deeper layers, often without urticaria. Triggered by stress, trauma, or estrogens. Treatment with C1 inhibitor or kallikrein inhibitors; epinephrine ineffective.

Scombroid reaction (histamine poisoning from spoiled fish): Presents with flushing, urticaria, hypotension; mimics anaphylaxis. History of ingestion of spoiled scombroid fish (tuna, mackerel) is key. Responds to antihistamines; less likely to require epinephrine.

Systemic mastocytosis: Recurrent anaphylaxis-like episodes; diagnosed by elevated baseline serum tryptase (often >15-20 ng/mL). Evaluated with bone marrow biopsy if suspected.

Long-Term Management and Trigger Avoidance

Allergen identification: After acute reaction, skin prick testing or serology identifies IgE-mediated triggers. Comprehensive history (food, medications, insect exposure, occupational exposures) essential.

Trigger avoidance: For food allergens, strict avoidance and label reading essential; cross-reactivity risks must be understood (e.g., 50% cross-reactivity between tree nuts). For medication-related, allergy labels in medical records prevent accidental re-exposure; skin/graded challenge testing may identify safe alternatives.

Epinephrine auto-injector prescription: All anaphylaxis patients (or those with strong history of IgE-mediated reactions) should be prescribed epinephrine auto-injector(s) for self-carry. Typical prescription: two auto-injectors (0.3 or 0.5 mg). Training on use is critical; many patients/families underuse or misuse auto-injectors.

Venom immunotherapy: For insect sting anaphylaxis, venom immunotherapy (VIT) desensitizes the patient to insect venom, reducing risk of future sting-related anaphylaxis from 50-60% to <5%. Indicated for systemic anaphylaxis reactions to stings.

Oral immunotherapy: For food allergies, ongoing research on oral immunotherapy (OIT) and sublingual immunotherapy (SLIT) shows promise in desensitizing patients; however, these remain experimental/not standard care except in research settings.

When to Refer to Allergy/Immunology

All anaphylaxis patients warrant allergy specialist evaluation for trigger identification, testing, and long-term management planning. Dermatologists encountering anaphylaxis should recognize it immediately and refer for emergency/ICU management, then to allergist for follow-up.

FAQ

If I have anaphylaxis, what should I do immediately?

CALL 911 (emergency services) immediately. If you have an epinephrine auto-injector, use it right away (do not hesitate waiting for EMS). Sit or lie down. If available, have someone stay with you. Do NOT drive yourself. After epinephrine, all anaphylaxis patients require hospital observation, even if symptoms improve, due to risk of biphasic reactions (worsening 4-12 hours later). Remove any triggering antigen (remove bee stinger, stop eating the food, etc.) if safe to do so. Inform responding EMS/hospital of the trigger and that you have used epinephrine.

Why epinephrine and not antihistamines first?

Epinephrine is the only medication that reverses the core pathophysiology of anaphylaxis. It acts on alpha-adrenergic receptors (causing vasoconstriction and improving blood pressure) and beta-adrenergic receptors (causing bronchodilation and increasing heart strength). Antihistamines (like diphenhydramine) block only histamine's effects on skin and do nothing for cardiovascular collapse, airway edema, or shock. Antihistamines are slow-acting (30-60 minutes for IV); by then, irreversible damage may have occurred. Epinephrine works in 5-15 minutes and is life-saving in severe anaphylaxis. Antihistamines are given AFTER epinephrine for comfort.

If I survive anaphylaxis once, will it happen again?

Risk of recurrent anaphylaxis depends on trigger avoidance. If the trigger is identified and strictly avoided, risk of future anaphylaxis is low (except insect stings—risk of sting-related anaphylaxis is 50-60% if stung again without venom immunotherapy). For food allergies, accidental ingestion risk is 10-30% per year (depending on food, allergen labeling practices, dining out). For medication anaphylaxis, avoiding the medication prevents recurrence, but you must ensure all your healthcare providers know of the allergy and document it. Carrying epinephrine auto-injectors and having an allergy action plan significantly improve outcomes if anaphylaxis occurs again.

What is a biphasic reaction? How long do I need to be observed?

Biphasic anaphylaxis is a second wave of symptoms occurring hours (typically 4-12 hours, sometimes up to 48 hours) after initial symptoms have improved or resolved. This occurs in 1-3% of anaphylaxis cases. Risk factors include severe initial reaction, treatment delay, and inadequate initial corticosteroid dosing. All anaphylaxis patients should be observed for at least 4-6 hours after symptom onset; high-risk cases warrant 24-hour observation. Discharge instructions must include signs of recurrence (wheezing, swelling, dizziness) and instructions to return immediately if symptoms develop.
