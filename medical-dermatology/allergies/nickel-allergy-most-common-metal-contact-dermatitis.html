Latex Allergy: Natural Rubber Skin Reactions and Cross-Reactivity

Clinical Overview

Latex allergy is an IgE-mediated hypersensitivity reaction to proteins derived from the rubber tree (Hevea brasiliensis). Affecting 0.5-1% of the general population but up to 10-17% of healthcare workers, latex allergy manifests with cutaneous reactions (urticaria, eczema), respiratory symptoms (asthma, rhinitis), and rarely, anaphylaxis. Latex exposure occurs through contact with gloves, catheters, medical equipment, and in the occupational setting (rubber manufacturing, healthcare). The advent of latex-free glove programs in hospitals has reduced incidence, though continued vigilance is needed. Accurate diagnosis via clinical history and IgE serology (latex-specific IgE), combined with avoidance and patient education, prevents serious reactions.

Epidemiology and Risk Factors

Latex allergy prevalence: 0.5-1% in general population; 5-10% in healthcare workers; 10-17% in atopic individuals with multiple allergies. Higher prevalence in spina bifida patients (40-60%) due to multiple surgeries exposing them to latex. Peak incidence: 1980s-1990s when unsterilized latex glove use increased substantially; incidence has declined significantly since latex-free alternatives became widespread. Risk factors: (1) Occupational exposure (healthcare workers, rubber industry workers, people with hobbies involving latex-containing equipment), (2) Atopy and allergic rhinitis (increased risk), (3) Spina bifida or other conditions requiring frequent surgeries, (4) Prior allergic reactions to latex.

Latex Proteins and Pathophysiology

Hevea brasiliensis latex contains over 200 proteins; approximately 15 are allergenic and can trigger IgE responses. Major latex allergens: Hev b 1, Hev b 3, Hev b 5, Hev b 6 (prohevein), Hev b 7, Hev b 11. IgE antibodies bind to these proteins on mast cells and basophils. Upon latex contact, IgE cross-linking triggers degranulation and release of histamine, leukotrienes, and other mediators, causing immediate hypersensitivity reactions. Aerosolized latex particles (especially from powdered gloves during donning/doffing) can enter respiratory tract, causing inhalation reactions and asthma. Type IV delayed hypersensitivity to latex can also occur, causing contact dermatitis, distinct from IgE-mediated allergy.

Clinical Manifestations

Immediate reactions (IgE-mediated, onset within minutes to 1-2 hours):

  • Localized contact urticaria: Itching, erythema, and wheals at sites of glove contact (hands, wrists, genitals if condom use). Most common presentation. Resolves within 24-48 hours if contact avoided.
  • Inhalation asthma: Dyspnea, wheezing, bronchospasm triggered by inhalation of aerosolized latex particles (powdered glove particles). Occupational asthma in healthcare workers or rubber plant workers. Symptoms may be delayed, developing hours after exposure and worsening throughout workshift.
  • Anaphylaxis: Rare but possible, particularly with extensive skin contact (e.g., latex balloon explosion exposing large skin area) or IV latex exposure (latex-containing catheters during surgery). Presents with urticaria, angioedema, bronchospasm, hypotension, cardiovascular collapse. Immediate epinephrine required.
  • Angioedema: Swelling of lips, tongue, throat, particularly after latex exposure (balloons, gloves near face). Respiratory compromise possible.

Delayed-type hypersensitivity (Type IV, 24-72 hours): Contact dermatitis (erythema, edema, eczema) at glove contact site. May occur concurrently with IgE reactions (especially if sensitized to both rubber proteins and accelerators like thiuram compounds used in latex processing).

Oral Allergy Syndrome and Latex-Fruit Cross-Reactivity

Cross-reactivity with fruits: Certain latex proteins are homologous to proteins in certain fruits, causing oral allergy syndrome (OAS). Patients with latex IgE antibodies may react to these foods: avocado, banana, kiwi (classic association), chestnut, papaya. Reactions manifest as oral itching, swelling of lips/tongue/oropharynx, particularly with raw fruits. Usually resolves with swallowing due to gastric acid denaturation of proteins. Systemic reactions (anaphylaxis from fruit ingestion in latex-allergic patients) are rare but possible.

Latex-fruit cross-reactivity pattern: Approximately 30-50% of latex-allergic patients have concurrent fruit allergy. The correlation is strongest for kiwi, banana, and avocado. Not all latex-allergic patients have fruit allergy, and not all fruit-allergic patients have latex allergy.

Diagnosis and Testing

Clinical history: Key diagnostic element. Timing of reactions relative to latex exposure (glove donning, balloon contact, medical procedures). Associated symptoms (respiratory, GI, systemic). History of occupational exposure or frequent medical procedures. Concurrent fruit reactions (kiwi, banana, avocado).

IgE serology: Latex-specific IgE (ImmunoCAP latex) quantifies IgE antibodies to whole latex extract. Sensitivity 80-95% for clinical latex allergy. Negative test does not rule out allergy if clinical suspicion high (sensitivity limitation). Positive test confirms latex sensitization but does not diagnose clinical allergy (can be positive without symptoms). Specific IgE to individual latex allergens (Hev b 5, Hev b 6) may have higher specificity but are less commonly measured.

Skin prick test: Fresh latex extract or latex glove protein extract applied to skin; positive if wheal develops within 15 minutes. Sensitivity 80-95% for latex-allergic individuals. Requires expertise and risk of anaphylaxis; performed in allergist's office with epinephrine available.

Use test (challenge test): Wearing latex glove and observing for reactions within 15-30 minutes. Can be performed in clinic setting under supervision but carries anaphylaxis risk; best reserved for diagnostic uncertainty after serology/history.

Management

Avoidance (primary management): Most effective approach. For occupational exposure: use latex-free gloves (nitrile, vinyl, synthetic polymer), equipment, catheters. Many hospitals have transitioned to 100% latex-free programs. For personal use: avoid latex gloves (use nitrile), latex balloons, condoms (use polyurethane), certain medical devices.

Acute reaction treatment:

  • Localized urticaria: Antihistamines (cetirizine 10 mg, diphenhydramine 25-50 mg). Topical steroids if localized eczema. Cool compresses.
  • Anaphylaxis: Immediate IM epinephrine 0.3-0.5 mg, IV fluids, antihistamines, corticosteroids. Emergency care and observation required.
  • Asthma: Inhaled beta-agonist (albuterol), potentially inhaled corticosteroid if persistent.

Epinephrine auto-injector prescription: For patients with history of anaphylaxis or severe reactions, prescribe epinephrine auto-injector for self-carry and use if exposure occurs.

Desensitization and immunotherapy: Not routinely offered for latex allergy; efficacy limited. Avoidance remains standard.

Occupational Considerations

Healthcare workers with latex allergy can continue working in latex-free environment. Employer accommodation (latex-free gloves, equipment) is legal requirement in US (ADA accommodations). Some highly sensitive individuals may experience reactions to aerosolized latex from colleagues' powdered latex gloves; portable HEPA filtration units or institution-wide latex-free policies provide further protection. Early detection and workplace accommodations prevent progression to occupational asthma.

When to Refer

Refer to allergist if: clinical history suggestive of latex allergy but IgE serology negative (to perform skin testing), severe reactions (anaphylaxis history), occupational exposure requiring accommodation planning, or if fruit allergy workup needed.

FAQ

If I have latex allergy, are all plastics and rubber dangerous?

No. Latex allergy is specific to natural rubber proteins from Hevea brasiliensis. Synthetic plastics (vinyl, polyurethane, nitrile, silicone) and synthetic rubbers do not contain the allergenic proteins and are safe for latex-allergic individuals. Latex (natural rubber) products are the only concern. Nitrile and vinyl gloves, for example, are completely safe alternatives for latex-allergic people.

If I have latex allergy, what fruits should I avoid?

The main fruits with cross-reactivity to latex are kiwi, banana, avocado, and chestnut. About 30-50% of latex-allergic patients have concurrent fruit allergy. However, not all latex-allergic patients react to these fruits, and not all people allergic to these fruits are latex-allergic. Start by avoiding kiwi, banana, and avocado if you have latex allergy. If you don't experience oral itching or swelling with these fruits, you likely don't have cross-reactivity and can safely eat them. If reactions occur, consult your allergist for specific testing to fruits you're concerned about.

I'm a nurse with latex allergy. Can I keep working?

Yes, absolutely. Your employer is legally required (under the Americans with Disabilities Act in the US) to provide latex-free gloves and equipment for you. Many hospitals have already transitioned to 100% latex-free supplies, making this accommodation straightforward. Inform your occupational health department or employer about your allergy; they will provide nitrile or other latex-free gloves and ensure your workspace is latex-free. If colleagues continue using powdered latex gloves nearby (which can aerosolize particles), you may need portable air filtration or request an institution-wide latex-free policy to prevent inhalation reactions.

Can I get tested for latex allergy before I have a severe reaction?

Yes. If you suspect latex allergy (itching with glove use, symptoms with balloon exposure, family history of latex allergy), ask your doctor for a latex-specific IgE blood test (ImmunoCAP latex). This test is safe and can be done anytime. A positive test means you're sensitized to latex. Even with a positive test, not everyone develops clinical symptoms; however, a positive test warrants avoidance of latex exposure to prevent reactions. Skin prick testing can also be performed by an allergist if needed, though it carries a small risk of reaction and requires expert supervision.
