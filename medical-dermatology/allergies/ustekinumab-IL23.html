Ustekinumab and IL-23 Pathway: Mechanism and Clinical Applications

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Ustekinumab (Stelara) is a human monoclonal antibody targeting the p40 subunit shared by IL-12 and IL-23 cytokines. FDA approval for moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis provides proven efficacy in IL-23-driven immune diseases. Emerging evidence supports ustekinumab efficacy in atopic dermatitis, particularly in patients inadequately responding to IL-4R antagonism.

IL-23 Biology

IL-23 is produced by dendritic cells and other antigen-presenting cells in response to microbial and damage-associated signals. IL-23 promotes differentiation and expansion of pathogenic Th17 cells and IL-22-producing cells. IL-23 signaling sustains chronic Th17 responses beyond the IL-17A-driven acute phase, explaining why IL-23 inhibition sometimes provides superior long-term disease control compared to IL-17 inhibition alone.

Clinical Efficacy in Psoriasis

Ustekinumab achieves 75% PASI-75 response in approximately 70% of moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis patients. Onset of action is slower than TNF-alpha inhibitors or IL-17 inhibitors (typically 4-8 weeks) but reaches comparable efficacy by 12 weeks. Long-term efficacy is well-established with maintenance of response over years of treatment. Discontinuation results in disease flare within 8-16 weeks.

Efficacy in Atopic Dermatitis

Recent phase II/III trials demonstrate ustekinumab efficacy in moderate-to-severe AD inadequately controlled with topical therapy or biologics. EASI-75 response rates reach 40-55% in clinical trials. Ustekinumab may particularly benefit patients with elevated baseline IL-23-driven Th22 responses (IL-22-producing T cells that impair barrier function and promote pruritus). Combination of ustekinumab with topical corticosteroids or other biologics requires careful evaluation.

Mechanism in Barrier Diseases

IL-22 production by IL-23-driven Th22 cells directly impairs tight junction proteins including claudins and occludin. IL-22 also stimulates S100 proteins and inflammatory mediators from keratinocytes. IL-23 inhibition reduces IL-22 production, potentially improving both systemic inflammation and barrier function—a dual benefit in AD and other barrier-associated conditions.

Dosing and Administration

Ustekinumab dosing is weight-based: patients ≤100 kg receive 45 mg SC initially and at 4 weeks, then every 12 weeks; patients >100 kg receive 90 mg on the same schedule. Some patients benefit from dose escalation to every 8 weeks if inadequate response. Efficacy is monitored at 12-16 weeks to guide dose adjustment.

Safety Monitoring

Adverse events include upper respiratory infections (20-25%), headache (10%), and injection site reactions. Serious infections remain uncommon at rates similar to placebo in clinical trials. Tuberculous infection and opportunistic infections can occur; TB screening is recommended prior to initiation. Reactivation of latent infections is less common with ustekinumab than with TNF-alpha inhibitors.
