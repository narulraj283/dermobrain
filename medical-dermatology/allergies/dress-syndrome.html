DRESS Syndrome: Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms (DRESS syndrome) represents a potentially life-threatening hypersensitivity reaction characterized by fever, rash, lymphadenopathy, and systemic involvement. Unlike SJS/TEN with rapid onset, DRESS typically develops 2-8 weeks after drug exposure, and the presence of eosinophilia, atypical lymphocytes, and reactivated herpes viruses distinguishes this entity.

Etiology and Risk Factors

Anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine, phenobarbital) account for 50% of DRESS cases, while antibiotics (sulfonamides, beta-lactams), NSAIDs, and other medications contribute to remaining cases. Genetic factors including defective detoxification and HLA associations predispose certain populations. Viral reactivation, particularly human herpesvirus-6 (HHV-6) and to lesser extent EBV and CMV, characterizes DRESS and may trigger or perpetuate disease.

Pathophysiology

DRESS involves abnormal drug metabolism creating reactive metabolites that bind to proteins and activate T cells. Impaired metabolite clearance allows accumulation and repeated immune stimulation. Viral reactivation likely results from drug-induced immune dysregulation rather than primary viral infection triggering DRESS. The resulting Th1 and Th2 immune activation produces widespread inflammation and tissue damage.

Clinical Features

Patients present with fever (often high spiking), characteristic facial edema with periorbital involvement, and generalized erythematous to urticarial-papular rash. Lymphadenopathy affects cervical, axillary, and inguinal nodes. Hepatosplenomegaly occurs in 50-75% of cases. Cutaneous eruption varies from morbilliform to exfoliative in severe cases. Mucosal involvement is less prominent than in SJS/TEN.

Systemic Manifestations

Hepatic involvement with elevated transaminases occurs in 80% of cases and may progress to acute liver failure. Hematologic abnormalities include eosinophilia (1,500-40,000/μL), atypical lymphocytosis, and thrombocytopenia. Nephritis, pneumonitis, myocarditis, and encephalitis indicate multisystem disease. Constitutional symptoms including persistent fever, fatigue, and weight loss may persist for weeks to months despite treatment.

Diagnosis

RegiSCAR scoring system incorporates clinical features (fever, rash distribution), systemic symptoms, and laboratory abnormalities (eosinophilia, atypical lymphocytes, hepatic enzyme elevation) to establish probable or definite DRESS diagnosis. Skin biopsy shows superficial dermatitis with lymphocytic infiltration and variable eosinophilia. Viral reactivation detection supports diagnosis.

Management

Immediate drug discontinuation is essential, though symptoms often persist 4-8 weeks after drug withdrawal. Systemic corticosteroids (methylprednisolone 0.5-2 g daily or prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day) form first-line treatment, with tapering over weeks to months guided by clinical improvement and laboratory parameters. Hepatic function and hematologic indices require close monitoring. Refractory cases may require additional immunosuppressants including azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil, or cyclosporine.
