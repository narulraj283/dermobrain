Stevens-Johnson Syndrome: Severe Cutaneous Drug Reaction

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) represents a severe cutaneous adverse reaction (SCAR) characterized by widespread mucocutaneous necrosis and systemic involvement. Mortality rates reach 10-50% depending on disease extent and treatment initiation timing, making recognition and prompt management critical for patient outcomes.

Epidemiology and Etiology

Incidence varies from 1-6 cases per million person-years depending on geographic location and drug exposure patterns. Medications account for 80-90% of cases, with NSAIDs, anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine), antibiotics (sulfonamides, beta-lactams), and allopurinol representing the highest-risk drugs. Infections, particularly herpes simplex virus in children and Mycoplasma pneumoniae in adults, trigger 5-10% of cases. Genetic predisposition, particularly HLA polymorphisms, influences individual susceptibility.

Pathophysiology

Cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CD8+) recognize altered self-antigens presented by HLA molecules, triggering massive keratinocyte apoptosis through Fas-FasL and perforin-granzyme B pathways. Unlike typical delayed hypersensitivity, SJS involves rapid, extensive cell death without prominent inflammatory infiltration, distinguishing it from other severe drug reactions.

Clinical Presentation

Prodromal symptoms including fever, malaise, arthralgia, and respiratory symptoms appear 1-8 weeks after drug exposure. Cutaneous lesions begin as fixed erythematous macules or targetoid lesions on flexural areas and spread centrifugally within 48-72 hours. Oral erosions, genital ulceration, and ocular involvement occur early. SJS diagnostic criteria require less than 10% body surface area involvement; toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN) involves greater than 30% BSA with overlap syndrome defining 10-30% involvement.

Systemic Complications

Mucosal involvement affects gastrointestinal, respiratory, and genitourinary tracts, impairing oral intake and increasing infection risk. Hepatitis, myocarditis, nephritis, and pulmonary involvement indicate multisystem disease with poor prognosis. Secondary infection through denuded skin and mucous membranes remains the leading cause of morbidity and mortality.

Management and Treatment

Immediate offending medication discontinuation is essential. Systemic corticosteroids (methylprednisolone 0.5-2 g daily) given early may reduce disease progression, though evidence remains debated. Supportive care in burn units or ICU settings with careful fluid and electrolyte management, nutritional support, and infection prevention is essential. Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) may reduce disease progression through multiple mechanisms. Plasmapheresis remains controversial but potentially beneficial in rapidly progressive cases.
