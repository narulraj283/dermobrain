Patch Testing: Methodology, Interpretation, and Clinical Application

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Patch testing is the gold standard diagnostic method for identifying contact allergens responsible for allergic contact dermatitis. This controlled epicutaneous testing reproduces the patient's dermatitis by applying allergens directly to the skin under occlusion.

Test Methodology

Standard patch tests use allergens in appropriate vehicles (petrolatum, water, or ethanol) applied to filter paper or aluminum chambers fixed to adhesive tape. The TRUE Test system offers pre-loaded panels with 23-30 common allergens. Tests are applied to the upper back, left uncovered for 48 hours, then read at 48 and 96 hours for delayed hypersensitivity reactions.

Interpretation Standards

The International Contact Dermatitis Research Group uses standardized scoring: negative (-) shows no reaction, irritant (IR) shows erythema only, doubtful (+/-) shows faint erythema, weak (+) shows erythema and infiltration, strong (++) shows erythema, infiltration, and vesicles, and extreme (+++) shows extensive vesiculation and bullae. Repeat open application tests (ROAT) confirm relevant allergens by reproducing the patient's dermatitis.

Relevance Assessment

A positive patch test result must be clinically relevant—the allergen must be present in products the patient contacts and the location of dermatitis must match exposure sites. Irritant reactions and excited skin syndrome create false positives requiring careful interpretation. Detailed occupational and environmental histories help distinguish relevant from irrelevant reactions.

Clinical Applications

Patch testing guides allergen avoidance counseling, identifies occupational hazards, documents legal liability in occupational cases, and facilitates desensitization protocols. Results should be provided with a comprehensive allergen list, alternatives, and product recommendations to enable effective avoidance and symptom resolution.
