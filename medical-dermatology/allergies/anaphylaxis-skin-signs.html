Anaphylaxis: Cutaneous Signs and Systemic Manifestations

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Anaphylaxis represents a severe, potentially life-threatening systemic allergic reaction occurring minutes after exposure to triggers. Cutaneous manifestations including urticaria, angioedema, and flushing represent the most common early signs, appearing in 80-90% of cases and often prompting medical evaluation that prevents progression to shock.

Pathophysiology

IgE-mediated mast cell and basophil degranulation releases preformed mediators (histamine, tryptase, heparin) and newly synthesized mediators (leukotrienes, prostaglandins, PAF) causing systemic inflammation and cardiovascular collapse. Direct T cell activation can trigger non-IgE-mediated anaphylaxis-like reactions (anaphylactoid reactions). Regardless of mechanism, cascade of vascular permeability increase, bronchospasm, and cardiovascular dysfunction produces the characteristic biphasic or protracted reactions seen in severe cases.

Cutaneous Manifestations

Pruritus often precedes visible cutaneous lesions by minutes, providing early warning of systemic reaction. Urticaria appears as pruritic wheals distributed over trunk and extremities, often becoming confluent. Flushing produces erythema particularly of face and upper trunk. Angioedema affects face, lips, tongue, and potentially airways. Post-reaction hyperpigmentation can develop at sites of severe urticaria. Absence of cutaneous manifestations does not exclude anaphylaxis; 5-10% of cases present without skin findings.

Systemic Manifestations Timeline

Within 5-30 minutes of exposure, gastrointestinal symptoms (cramping, diarrhea, vomiting), respiratory symptoms (stridor, wheezing, dyspnea), and cardiovascular symptoms (hypotension, shock) can develop rapidly. Laryngeal edema causing stridor or glottal swelling presents as life-threatening airway emergency. Bronchospasm produces wheezing and respiratory distress. Cardiovascular collapse appears as hypotension, tachycardia, and loss of consciousness. Biphasic anaphylaxis occurs when symptoms recur 1-72 hours after resolution of initial reaction despite no additional exposure, requiring prolonged observation.

Common Triggers

Foods (particularly shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, milk) cause 30-40% of cases. Medications including penicillin, contrast agents, and other drugs account for 20-30%. Insect stings, particularly hymenoptera (bees, wasps, hornets), trigger 15-20%. Latex and other allergens account for remaining cases. In 15-20% of cases, trigger remains unidentified (idiopathic anaphylaxis).

Diagnostic Evaluation

Clinical history and timing of symptom development diagnose anaphylaxis; laboratory confirmation includes elevated serum tryptase (>11.4 ng/mL), plasma histamine (requires immediate processing), and elevated 24-hour urine histamine or methylhistamine. Tryptase levels peak 15-30 minutes after symptom onset and decline over hours; serial measurements may be required. Normal tryptase does not exclude anaphylaxis; absent tryptase elevation occurs in 20-30% of IgE-mediated cases.

Emergency Management

Epinephrine 0.3-0.5 mg intramuscularly (1:1000 dilution) represents first-line, dose-dependent treatment. Subcutaneous or intravenous administration are less effective than IM; IM administration provides rapid onset and adequate blood levels. Patients should be positioned supine with legs elevated to prevent shock worsening; IV access should be established for medications. Antihistamines (H1 receptor antagonists) and corticosteroids provide adjunctive benefit by preventing late-phase reactions and biphasic recurrence. Oxygen and IV fluids address respiratory and hemodynamic compromise.

Prevention and Long-Term Management

Identified trigger avoidance prevents recurrence. Patients with anaphylaxis history should carry epinephrine auto-injectors (EpiPen, Auvi-Q) and training in proper administration. Antihistamines (H1 and H2 receptor antagonists) provide some protection in food-allergic patients but do not prevent anaphylaxis. Oral immunotherapy and biologics including omalizumab show promise for desensitization in food-allergic patients with severe reactions. β-blockers can interfere with epinephrine response and should be avoided in patients at high anaphylaxis risk.
