Allergies & Immune Skin Disorders

Atopic Dermatitis Immunology: Th2 Inflammation and Barrier Dysfunction

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Atopic dermatitis (AD) represents a chronic inflammatory skin disease resulting from complex interplay between genetic predisposition, immune dysregulation, and impaired skin barrier function. Understanding the underlying immunology informs targeted biologic and small molecule therapies that address specific inflammatory pathways.

Genetic and Barrier Factors

Filaggrin gene mutations and other structural protein abnormalities impair skin barrier function, increasing transepidermal water loss and allergen penetration. Barrier dysfunction alone is insufficient to cause AD; genetic predisposition for Th2 immune responses represents the critical additional factor. Twin studies demonstrate high heritability (60-80%); sibling risk reaches 50% when one parent is affected.

Th2 Cell-Mediated Inflammation

Altered dendritic cell activation in AD promotes naive T cell differentiation toward Th2 phenotype. CD4+ Th2 cells produce IL-4, IL-5, and IL-13, which promote IgE class switching, eosinophil recruitment, and mast cell activation. Reduced IL-12 production and altered Th1 responses fail to provide immune tolerance. Th2 cytokines drive mast cell, basophil, and eosinophil recruitment; these cells amplify inflammation through mediator release.

Cytokine Networks

IL-4 and IL-13 signaling through IL-4 receptor-alpha promotes Th2 differentiation and IgE production. IL-31 produced by Th2 cells and other sources directly stimulates sensory nerves causing pruritus—a hallmark of AD. IL-22 produced by Th22 and other cells impairs tight junction proteins causing barrier disruption. TNF-alpha and IL-6 sustain chronic inflammation. JAK proteins mediate signaling for multiple these cytokines, explaining JAK inhibitor efficacy.

Impaired Regulatory Function

Reduced regulatory T cell (Treg) numbers and function fail to suppress effector T cell responses. Loss of IL-10 and TGF-beta production by Tregs permits unopposed Th2 inflammation. Allergen-specific tolerance mechanisms are defective in AD, promoting IgE responses to common environmental and food allergens. This fundamental Treg dysfunction permits disease perpetuation despite triggers removal.

Skin Barrier Defects

Epidermal filaggrin degradation produces natural moisturizing factor; filaggrin deficiency reduces skin hydration and stratum corneum integrity. AD skin shows increased TEWL and increased allergen penetration. Th2 cytokines including IL-13 further impair tight junction proteins (claudins, occludin), creating feed-forward barrier dysfunction. Scratching disrupts barrier, allowing allergen penetration and additional immune activation.
