Photoallergy vs Phototoxicity: Distinguishing Photosensitive Reactions

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Photosensitive skin reactions result from interactions between exogenous or endogenous photosensitizing agents and ultraviolet radiation. Two distinct mechanisms—photoallergy and phototoxicity—produce superficially similar presentations but require different diagnostic approaches and management strategies.

Phototoxicity Mechanism

Phototoxic reactions result from direct chemical injury caused by photosensitizing agents activated by UVA radiation. These reactions resemble exaggerated sunburns, appearing within 24 hours of sun exposure without requiring prior sensitization. Any exposed individual develops reactions when exposed to sufficient doses of photosensitizer and UV radiation. Common phototoxic agents include psoralens (furocoumarins in citrus fruits, parsley, celery), NSAIDs, tetracyclines, and thiazides.

Photoallergy Mechanism

Photoallergic reactions involve delayed-type hypersensitivity to ultraviolet-altered antigens. The photosensitizer absorbs UV radiation, creating reactive intermediates that combine with skin proteins to form complete antigens. Sensitized T cells recognize these photomodified antigens, triggering inflammatory responses 24-72 hours after exposure. Prior sensitization is required; only susceptible individuals develop reactions. Common photoallergens include sunscreens (benzophenones, cinnamates), fragrances, and systemic medications.

Clinical Differentiation

Phototoxic reactions typically present as erythema, edema, and blistering confined to sun-exposed areas, with sharp demarcation at clothing lines. Photoallergic reactions produce eczematous dermatitis with vesiculation that may extend beyond sun-exposed areas, reflecting lymphocyte migration. Photoallergic patients often report pruritus preceding visible lesions, while phototoxic reactions primarily cause pain or burning sensation.

Diagnostic Approach

Photo patch testing with suspected photoallergens determines photoallergic sensitization. Phototoxic reactions are diagnosed by clinical history and reproduction with the inciting agent. Phototesting (UVA/UVB exposure to suspected areas) may reproduce phototoxic but not photoallergic reactions acutely.

Management

Both conditions require strict sun avoidance and broad-spectrum SPF 50+ sunscreen with UVA protection. Patients must identify and eliminate the offending photosensitizer. Topical and systemic corticosteroids manage acute inflammation. Photoallergy may resolve with allergen avoidance, while phototoxicity risk persists as long as the photosensitizer and UV exposure coexist.
