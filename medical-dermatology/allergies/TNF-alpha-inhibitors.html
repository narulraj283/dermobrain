Systemic Immunosuppressants in Dermatology: Methotrexate, Cyclosporine, Acitretin

Clinical Overview

Systemic immunosuppressants form the backbone of traditional dermatology therapy for severe psoriasis, atopic dermatitis, lichen planus, and other inflammatory skin conditions. Three agents are most commonly used: methotrexate (MTX), cyclosporine, and acitretin. These agents predate biologics and, despite widespread adoption of newer agents, remain first-line or second-line options due to lower cost, extensive safety data, and oral convenience. This section focuses on their mechanisms, clinical use, monitoring requirements, and role in modern biologic-era dermatology.

Methotrexate (MTX)

Mechanism of Action

Methotrexate is a folate antagonist that inhibits dihydrofolate reductase, blocking thymidylate synthase and reducing DNA synthesis. At low doses used in dermatology (10-25 mg weekly), MTX exerts both antiproliferative effects (suppressing keratinocyte hyperplasia) and immunomodulatory effects (reducing T cell proliferation, macrophage activation, and TNF-α/IL-1 production). Onset is gradual; clinical improvement typically begins at weeks 4-8, with maximal response by 12-16 weeks. PASI-75 is achieved in 50-60% of psoriasis patients.

Dosing and Administration

Starting dose: 5-10 mg weekly (either as single dose or split into three doses 12 hours apart on one day). Dose escalation to 15-25 mg weekly based on response, tolerability, and monitoring labs. Typically administered once weekly either orally or SC. Folic acid supplementation (1 mg daily or 5 mg on non-MTX days) is mandatory to reduce hematologic and GI toxicity.

Efficacy and Response

PASI-75 response: 50-60% at 16 weeks. PASI-50: 70-80%. Response rates are comparable to or slightly lower than biologics, but cost is substantially lower. Efficacy is maintained long-term with continuous therapy; tachyphylaxis is uncommon.

Safety Profile and Monitoring

Common adverse effects: Gastrointestinal upset (nausea, mouth ulcers: 10-30%), elevated transaminases (ALT/AST elevation in 10-20%, usually mild and reversible). Folate deficiency symptoms: megaloblastic anemia, mouth sores, increased infection risk.

Serious adverse effects: Hepatic cirrhosis (rare with modern monitoring), bone marrow suppression (anemia, thrombocytopenia, leukopenia: <1% with monitoring), renal dysfunction (MTX is excreted renally; caution in baseline renal impairment), opportunistic infections (TB rare but possible).

Baseline assessment: CBC, CMP (particularly creatinine, ALT, AST), hepatitis B/C serology, HIV status, pregnancy test (women of childbearing age). Baseline chest X-ray if TB risk factors present.

Monitoring during therapy: CBC, CMP at baseline, 4-8 weeks after initiation, then every 8-12 weeks. Hepatitis B surface antigen annually. If ALT/AST elevated >3x ULN or platelet count <100K, hold MTX until improvement. Patient education: Avoid alcohol (increases hepatotoxicity), use effective contraception (highly teratogenic), avoid live vaccines.

Cyclosporine

Mechanism of Action

Cyclosporine is a calcineurin inhibitor that blocks IL-2 and other Th1 cytokine production by inhibiting T cell activation. In dermatology, cyclosporine is highly effective for T-cell mediated diseases. Onset: improvement by weeks 2-4, maximal response by 8-12 weeks. PASI-75 achieved in 70-80% of psoriasis patients, higher than MTX or systemic corticosteroids.

Dosing and Administration

Initial dose: 2.5-3 mg/kg/day orally, divided into two doses. Dosage escalation in 0.5-1 mg/kg increments every 2-4 weeks, with maximum dose 5 mg/kg/day. Dosing must be individualized based on clinical response and tolerability. Absorption is variable; consistent administration with or without food is important. Blood level monitoring (trough levels 75-200 ng/mL) is not routinely performed in dermatology but may be useful for dosing optimization.

Efficacy and Rapid Response

PASI-75 response: 70-80% at 8 weeks. Faster onset than MTX. Excellent for acute flares or severe disease. However, efficacy wanes upon discontinuation; relapse occurs in 50-75% within 2-6 weeks of stopping. Long-term maintenance is problematic due to nephrotoxicity and hypertension risks.

Safety and Nephrotoxicity

Nephrotoxicity: The most significant long-term limitation. Dose-dependent and reversible at higher doses; however, chronic use (>2 years continuously) can result in irreversible renal dysfunction. Creatinine elevation >30% from baseline requires dose reduction or discontinuation. Hypertension occurs in 30-50% of patients on cyclosporine; monitoring and antihypertensive therapy often needed.

Other common effects: Gingival hyperplasia (20-30%, reversible with discontinuation or meticulous oral hygiene), tremor, headache, hirsutism.

Baseline assessment: Serum creatinine, estimated GFR, blood pressure, CBC, CMP, lipid panel, hepatitis B/C serology, TB screening.

Monitoring: Serum creatinine and BP every 2-4 weeks initially, then every 4-8 weeks. Discontinue if creatinine elevation >30% from baseline, new hypertension unresponsive to antihypertensives, or signs of serious infection. Live vaccines contraindicated.

Acitretin (Retinoid)

Mechanism of Action

Acitretin is a third-generation retinoid (isomer of isotretinoin) that acts as a retinoic acid receptor (RAR) agonist, regulating gene transcription in keratinocytes and immune cells. Reduces keratinocyte proliferation and differentiation, suppresses Th17 cells, and improves epidermal barrier function. Onset slow: improvement by weeks 4-8, maximal response by 12-24 weeks.

Dosing and Administration

Starting dose: 10-25 mg daily with a meal (food enhances absorption). Maintenance: 25-50 mg daily. Doses >50 mg daily increase risk of adverse effects without proportional benefit. Peak serum levels achieved 2-4 hours after dosing; half-life 48-96 hours.

Efficacy

PASI-75 response: 50-60% at 12-16 weeks. Less effective than methotrexate or cyclosporine as monotherapy for severe psoriasis, but often used in combination with phototherapy (PUVA or NB-UVB) for enhanced efficacy. Particularly useful for pustular psoriasis and palmoplantar psoriasis.

Teratogenicity and Pregnancy

CRITICAL: Acitretin is extremely teratogenic. Contraindicated in pregnancy; significant risk of craniofacial, cardiac, thymic, CNS, and urinary tract malformations. All women of childbearing potential must use two forms of effective contraception during therapy and for 3 years after discontinuation (due to long tissue half-life). Monthly pregnancy tests required. Ethanol consumption increases teratogenicity (acitretin metabolized to isotretinoin); patients must abstain from alcohol.

Common Adverse Effects

Mucocutaneous dryness (lip dryness, dry mouth, dry eyes: 50-100%), elevated cholesterol/triglycerides (70-80%), elevated liver enzymes (10-30%, reversible), alopecia, photosensitivity. More severe: hepatotoxicity (rare, <2%), skeletal toxicity with long-term use (ankylosed spinal ligaments, ossification of spinal ligaments, diffuse idiopathic skeletal hyperostosis—DISH—with prolonged use >1 year).

Baseline assessment: Pregnancy test (women of childbearing age), lipid panel, LFTs, CBC. Pregnancy test prior to initiation and monthly during therapy.

Monitoring: Lipid panel and LFTs at baseline, 4-8 weeks, then every 12 weeks. Pregnancy tests monthly for women of childbearing age. Skeletal imaging (spine X-ray) if continuous use exceeds 12-24 months (look for DISH or other skeletal changes).

Comparative Efficacy and Selection

First-line choice: In many centers, biologics have largely replaced systemic immunosuppressants as first-line for moderate-to-severe psoriasis due to superior efficacy and more favorable long-term safety profiles. However, systemic agents remain important for: (1) patients with contraindications to biologics (e.g., active TB, significant infection), (2) cost-constrained settings, (3) patients intolerant to or failing biologics, (4) certain disease phenotypes (acitretin for pustular psoriasis).

Methotrexate: Best long-term safety profile; suitable for maintenance therapy. Slower onset than cyclosporine but faster than acitretin.

Cyclosporine: Fastest-acting; best for acute flares and severe disease. Limited by nephrotoxicity and hypertension; typically used for short-term (weeks to months) courses.

Acitretin: Useful as adjunct to phototherapy, particularly for pustular or palmoplantar psoriasis. Strict teratogenicity requirements limit use in women of childbearing age.

When to Use Systemic Immunosuppressants in Biologic Era

Indications: Active TB or serious infections (contraindication to biologics), patient preference for oral non-biologic agent, cost constraints, failure or intolerance to two or more biologics (as salvage therapy), pregnancy (methotrexate relatively safe if indicated; cyclosporine category C; acitretin contraindicated), specific disease phenotypes (e.g., acitretin for pustular forms).

When to Refer

Refer for moderate-to-severe disease requiring systemic therapy, complex medication interactions, or uncertainty regarding monitoring.

FAQ

How long until systemic immunosuppressants work?

Timeframe varies: Cyclosporine is fastest, with visible improvement by weeks 2-4 and PASI-75 in 70-80% by 8 weeks. Methotrexate is slower, with improvement starting at weeks 4-8 and maximal response by 12-16 weeks. Acitretin is slowest, requiring 12-24 weeks for full response. Biologics (IL-17A, IL-23 inhibitors) are typically faster than methotrexate but slower than cyclosporine.

Is methotrexate safe long-term? What should I watch for?

Methotrexate has been safely used for decades in dermatology when properly monitored. The main concerns are hepatotoxicity (cirrhosis risk, especially with concurrent alcohol or obesity), bone marrow suppression (anemia, low platelets), and kidney problems. With regular blood tests (CBC, liver function, kidney function) every 8-12 weeks and folic acid supplementation, serious complications are rare (<1%). You should avoid alcohol, use reliable contraception, and take folic acid daily to minimize risks.

Why is acitretin so teratogenic? Can men take it?

Acitretin and its metabolite isotretinoin are highly teratogenic (cause major birth defects in >25% of exposed pregnancies). Women of childbearing age face strict requirements: two forms of contraception, monthly pregnancy tests, and abstinence from alcohol (which increases conversion to more teratogenic isotretinoin). Men do not have teratogenicity restrictions and can safely use acitretin; however, women whose partners use acitretin should NOT worry—systemic absorption in partners is negligible.

What if I develop elevated kidney function on cyclosporine?

Cyclosporine can cause dose-dependent kidney problems. If your creatinine rises >30% above your baseline (measured by a blood test), your dermatologist will typically reduce the dose or discontinue the medication. Most kidney function returns to baseline after stopping. However, continuous high-dose cyclosporine for >2 years can cause permanent kidney damage, which is why cyclosporine is usually limited to short-term use (weeks to a few months) or used intermittently with breaks. Your doctor will monitor your kidney function regularly with blood tests.
