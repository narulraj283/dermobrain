Dermatitis Herpetiformis: Gluten-Sensitive Autoimmune Blistering

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Dermatitis herpetiformis (DH) represents a gluten-sensitive autoimmune blistering disorder characterized by intensely pruritic papulovesicular eruption. Virtually all DH patients have underlying celiac disease (gluten-sensitive enteropathy) on duodenal biopsy, though many remain asymptomatic. Strict gluten avoidance and dapsone treatment provide symptomatic relief and potential disease remission.

Epidemiology and Pathophysiology

DH affects 15-25 per 100,000 individuals in Northern Europe and similar populations; lower prevalence occurs in Southern Europe, Asia, and African populations. Nearly 100% of DH patients carry HLA-DQ2 or DQ8 alleles. Gluten ingestion triggers intestinal inflammation with altered intestinal permeability allowing bacterial translocation and immune system activation. Tissue transglutaminase (tTG) modification of gluten peptides promotes autoreactive T cell responses. IgA antibodies against epidermal and tissue transglutaminase deposit at the dermal-epidermal junction and at the basement membrane zone, triggering complement activation and neutrophil infiltration causing vesiculation.

Clinical Features

Intensely pruritic papules and vesicles on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees), buttocks, scalp, and lower back characterize typical presentation. Lesions are monomorphic, symmetric, and tightly grouped. Severe scratching often obscures primary lesions; erosions and excoriations predominate. Pruritus typically precedes visible lesions by hours, and scratching lesions can trigger new eruption at trauma sites. Associated celiac disease causes gastrointestinal symptoms in 50-75% of DH patients, including diarrhea, bloating, and weight loss.

Diagnosis

Skin biopsy from perilesional normal-appearing skin shows granular IgA deposits at the dermal-epidermal junction on direct immunofluorescence microscopy, which is pathognomonic for DH. Serologic testing reveals IgA anti-tTG and anti-endomysium antibodies in most cases. Duodenal biopsy identifies villous atrophy (Marsh grade 3a-3c) consistent with celiac disease in 75-95% of patients with established DH, even without gastrointestinal symptoms.

Management

Gluten-free diet represents definitive treatment, producing gradual improvement in skin manifestations over weeks to months and allowing dapsone dose reduction and eventual discontinuation. Dapsone at 50-100 mg daily provides rapid symptomatic relief within 24-72 hours for most patients, though it does not prevent intestinal damage. Sulfapyridine offers alternative for dapsone-intolerant patients. Strict gluten avoidance eventually produces complete remission of skin disease and intestinal healing; some patients achieve remission on gluten-free diet alone without dapsone.
