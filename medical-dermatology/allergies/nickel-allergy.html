Nickel Allergy: Epidemiology, Avoidance, and Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Nickel is the most common contact allergen worldwide, affecting 10-15% of the general population with higher prevalence in women. Nickel sensitization typically occurs through pierced ears, jewelry contact, or occupational exposure, with the prevalence increasing significantly after ear piercing in adolescence.

Sources of Nickel Exposure

Common sources include costume jewelry, watch backs, belt buckles, eyeglass frames, buttons, zippers, and coins. Nickel leaches from stainless steel products when chromium protective layers deteriorate through scratching or corrosion. Occupational exposure occurs in metalworkers, hairdressers (from scissors and clips), and healthcare workers (from instruments).

Clinical Presentation

Nickel dermatitis typically appears on the earlobes, neck (from necklaces), wrists (from bracelets), lower abdomen (from jean buttons), and feet (from shoe hardware). Lesions include erythema, vesiculation, scaling, and lichenification depending on exposure duration. Widespread involvement or systemic symptoms suggest nickel ingestion in sensitive individuals, though this remains controversial.

Diagnostic Considerations

Patch testing with nickel sulfate 5% confirms sensitization, though a positive test doesn't automatically predict clinical reactivity. Nickel spot tests (dimethylglyoxime tests) identify nickel in specific objects and guide avoidance strategies. Contact with nickel-plated items lasting longer than 4-6 hours typically provokes reactions in sensitized individuals.

Management and Prevention

Treatment involves allergen avoidance, with protective strategies including barrier creams, fabric liners under jewelry, and selecting nickel-free alternatives (titanium, surgical stainless steel, gold-plated items). Affected individuals should apply clear nail polish to hardware and remove rings before handwashing. Systemic corticosteroids help acute flares; topical steroids and calcineurin inhibitors manage dermatitis. Occupational workers benefit from frequent hand washing and protective gloves.
