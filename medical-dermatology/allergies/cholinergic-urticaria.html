Cholinergic Urticaria: Exercise-Induced and Heat-Induced Reactions

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Cholinergic urticaria (COU) represents a physical urticaria triggered by exercise, heat, or emotional stress through mechanisms involving acetylcholine and sympathetic nervous system activation. Distinct from allergic urticaria by characteristic morphology and triggers, COU typically affects young adults and may significantly limit physical activity and quality of life.

Pathophysiology

Exercise and stress increase core body temperature and trigger sweating; cholinergic activation of M3 muscarinic receptors on mast cells causes local mediator release. Increased serum IgE levels in some patients suggest allergic component, though IgE specificity remains unclear. Direct mast cell activation by acetylcholine or heat rather than immune recognition likely predominates.

Clinical Presentation

During or shortly after exercise/heat exposure or emotional stress, patients develop pruritus followed by characteristic small (1-3 mm) monomorphic, punctate erythematous papules predominantly on trunk and proximal extremities. Lesions typically spare mucous membranes and palms/soles. Surrounding erythema may occur. Individual lesions persist 15-30 minutes; widespread involvement resolves within 1-2 hours after stimulus cessation. Rarely, systemic symptoms including hypotension and angioedema occur with intense exercise (anaphylaxis-like syndrome).

Triggering Factors

Physical exercise remains the most common trigger; sweating from any cause (hot environment, warm clothing, hot beverages) can precipitate reactions. Emotional stress and anxiety trigger urticaria through sympathetic nervous system activation. Food intake (particularly spicy foods) enhances predisposition to cholinergic reactions, likely through temperature increase and stress response. Individual variation in exercise tolerance and trigger threshold is significant.

Diagnosis

History of urticaria precipitated by exercise or heat is typically diagnostic. Exercise challenge testing produces characteristic papular eruption but is rarely necessary. Prick skin testing to allergens is negative; ice cube testing remains negative (distinguishing COU from cold urticaria). Negative serum tryptase helps exclude systemic mastocytosis.

Management

First-line approach involves trigger avoidance, gradual exercise acclimatization, and environmental temperature control. Antihistamines (particularly first-generation sedating antihistamines like hydroxyzine) provide variable benefit, with dose optimization necessary. Cetirizine and loratadine offer non-sedating alternatives. Patients should avoid exercise for 4 hours after food intake. Aspirin at 650 mg BID shows benefit in some patients through unclear mechanisms. Systemic corticosteroids and omalizumab address refractory cases. Patients should maintain awareness of rare anaphylaxis risk during intense exercise and carry epinephrine auto-injectors if at high risk.
