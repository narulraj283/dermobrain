Chronic Urticaria: Pathophysiology and Long-term Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Chronic urticaria (CU) persists for more than 6 weeks with daily or near-daily wheals and pruritus. Affecting 0.5-1% of the population, CU significantly impacts quality of life through sleep disruption and functional impairment. Unlike acute urticaria, CU rarely identifies specific triggers and requires systematic management with escalating therapies.

Etiology and Pathophysiology

Approximately 50% of CU cases involve autoimmunity with circulating IgE or IgG antibodies targeting high-affinity IgE receptors on mast cells or to IgE itself, causing chronic mast cell activation. Remaining cases likely involve non-IgE mechanisms including complement activation, direct mast cell activation, or tissue inflammation. Th2 cell infiltration and altered regulatory T cell function contribute to persistent inflammation. Most patients lack identifiable external triggers, distinguishing CU from acute urticaria.

Clinical Features

Patients experience daily wheals with variable morphology and distribution, often accompanied by pruritus severe enough to disrupt sleep and work. Associated angioedema occurs in 40% of cases. Systemic symptoms are absent unless accompanying anaphylaxis. Disease activity fluctuates unpredictably, with periods of remission interspersed with exacerbations.

Diagnostic Evaluation

Standard allergy testing yields false positives and rarely identifies clinically relevant triggers in CU. Routine laboratory testing (CBC, metabolic panel, ESR) screens for underlying systemic disease in 5-10% of cases. C1-esterase inhibitor levels should be measured if angioedema is disproportionate to urticaria severity, suggesting hereditary angioedema. Autologous serum skin testing (ASST) and histamine release testing identify autoimmune CU but are not universally available and don't alter management.

First-Line Treatment

Modern approach recommends second-generation antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine, or desloratadine) as first-line therapy, with dose escalation up to 4-fold maximum recommended doses for inadequate response. Approximately 45% of patients achieve adequate control with standard antihistamine dosing; an additional 35% respond to high-dose antihistamines. Sedating first-generation antihistamines are avoided due to central nervous system effects.

Second-Line Therapy

Omalizumab (anti-IgE monoclonal antibody) and cyclosporine represent FDA-approved second-line options. Omalizumab provides benefit in 70-80% of inadequately controlled CU patients, particularly those with IgE elevation, though optimal dosing remains debated. Cyclosporine at 3-5 mg/kg/day produces response in 75% of patients but requires monitoring for nephrotoxicity and hypertension.

Emerging Therapies

JAK inhibitors including ruxolitinib show promising results in refractory CU. Dupilumab (anti-IL4-receptor) benefits patients with concurrent atopic dermatitis. Ligelizumab (anti-IgE monoclonal antibody with higher binding affinity) and other biologics under investigation may expand treatment options. Antihistamine combinations, intravenous immunoglobulin, and plasmapheresis address refractory cases in specialized centers.
