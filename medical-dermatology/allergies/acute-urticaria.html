Acute Urticaria: Pathophysiology, Diagnosis, and Treatment

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Acute urticaria consists of urticarial eruption with or without angioedema lasting less than 6 weeks. Most acute urticaria cases are self-limited, resolving within 2-4 weeks despite unknown etiology in 50% of cases. Identification of specific triggers enables effective prevention, while symptomatic management controls symptoms during acute episodes.

Pathophysiology

Urticaria results from mast cell and basophil degranulation in response to IgE-mediated mechanisms (allergic), non-IgE mechanisms (pseudo-allergic), or direct mast cell activation. IgE-mediated reactions involve cross-linking of high-affinity IgE receptors on mast cells with antigen, triggering immediate release of preformed mediators (histamine, tryptase) and generation of newly synthesized mediators (leukotrienes, prostaglandins, PAF). These mediators increase vascular permeability, producing plasma extravasation into dermis and characteristic wheals.

Common Triggers in Acute Urticaria

IgE-mediated food allergies represent the leading identified cause, particularly shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, and milk in children. Drug reactions account for 10% of cases, with NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, and antibiotics most commonly implicated. Infections (viral, bacterial) provoke acute urticaria through multiple mechanisms. Insect stings/bites, contact reactions, and latex exposure cause acute IgE-mediated urticaria. Pseudo-allergic reactions to additives and dyes trigger non-IgE urticaria.

Clinical Presentation

Pruritic wheals appear within minutes to hours of exposure, remain visible for less than 24 hours at individual sites, and may coalesce into larger plaques. Associated angioedema produces deeper, more sustained swelling of dermis and subcutaneous tissue. Systemic symptoms including respiratory compromise or hypotension indicate anaphylaxis requiring emergency treatment.

Diagnostic Approach

Detailed history targeting potential triggers is most valuable; routine allergy testing often yields false positives. Prick skin testing identifies IgE-mediated allergies. Oral challenge testing confirms food allergy but carries anaphylaxis risk. Most acute cases resolve without specific trigger identification, limiting diagnostic yield of extensive testing.

Treatment

Second-generation antihistamines (cetirizine 10 mg daily, loratadine 10 mg daily, desloratadine 5 mg daily) provide first-line treatment. Doses can be increased two-fold if inadequate response; up to 4-fold increases remain within safety guidelines. Systemic corticosteroids (methylprednisolone dose pack) provide adjunctive therapy for severe cases. Epinephrine intramuscularly (0.3-0.5 mg IM) is essential for anaphylaxis. Triggers should be avoided when identified; re-exposure counseling and emergency action plans help prevent recurrence.
