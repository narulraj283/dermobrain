Secukinumab (Cosentyx): IL-17A Inhibition for Psoriasis and Inflammatory Skin Disease

Clinical Overview

Secukinumab (Cosentyx) is a fully human monoclonal antibody that selectively inhibits interleukin-17A (IL-17A), a key pro-inflammatory cytokine in psoriasis and related inflammatory skin conditions. Unlike TNF-α inhibitors or IL-23 inhibitors, secukinumab represents the first-in-class IL-17A inhibitor and provides rapid clinical improvement with PASI-75 (75% improvement in Psoriasis Area and Severity Index) achieved in 81.6% of patients at week 12 compared to 4% with placebo in the FIXTURE trial. The drug is FDA-approved for moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis, psoriatic arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, and Behçet's disease with genital ulcers.

Epidemiology and Clinical Significance

Psoriasis affects approximately 3.2% of the US population, with 75% having plaque psoriasis. Among patients with moderate-to-severe disease requiring systemic therapy, only about 20% achieve adequate disease control with conventional topical and phototherapy approaches. The IL-17A pathway is dysregulated in psoriasis, with elevated IL-17A levels found in psoriatic lesions, serum, and synovial fluid. Secukinumab's specific targeting of this pathway offers an alternative for patients who fail or are intolerant to TNF-α inhibitors or other biologics.

Mechanism of Action and Pathophysiology

IL-17A is a key pro-inflammatory cytokine produced by Th17 cells, γδ T cells, and innate lymphoid cells. In psoriasis, IL-17A drives acanthosis (epidermal hyperplasia), infiltration of immune cells, and induction of antimicrobial peptides and chemokines. Secukinumab binds to human IL-17A with high affinity (Kd = 0.23 pM), preventing interaction with the IL-17A receptor on keratinocytes, fibroblasts, and endothelial cells. This blocking suppresses downstream inflammatory mediators including IL-6, TNF-α, and IL-8, resulting in reduced epidermal proliferation and immune infiltration. The mechanism is distinct from TNF-α inhibitors and IL-23p19 inhibitors.

Clinical Presentation and Diagnosis

Patients with moderate-to-severe psoriasis typically present with plaques affecting ≥10% body surface area (BSA) and/or BSA ≥2% in high-impact areas (face, hands, feet, intertriginous regions). The Psoriasis Area and Severity Index (PASI) score ranges from 0-72, with scores ≥12 indicating moderate-to-severe disease. Diagnosis is clinical; however, secukinumab is reserved for patients who have failed conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, acitretin, cyclosporine) or phototherapy. Before initiating secukinumab, obtain baseline TB screening (tuberculin skin test or interferon-gamma release assay), CBC, CMP, lipid panel, and hepatitis B/C serology.

Dosing and Administration

Loading Phase: 300 mg subcutaneous injection administered as 3 x 100 mg injections at weeks 0, 1, 2, 3, and 4. Alternatively, an intravenous loading dose of 10 mg/kg at weeks 0, 2, and 4 is available for rapid disease control.

Maintenance Phase: 300 mg SC every 4 weeks beginning at week 8. Some patients (<90 kg) may benefit from a 150 mg maintenance dose every 4 weeks, assessed after 12 weeks of therapy.

Injections are administered using 0.5 mL prefilled pen or syringe; patients can self-inject after proper training. Rotate injection sites to minimize localized reactions.

Treatment Response and Efficacy

Response rates in pivotal trials were impressive:

  • FIXTURE trial: PASI-75 at week 12 was 81.6% (300 mg) and 71.6% (150 mg) vs 4% placebo
  • FEATURE trial: PASI-75 at week 12 was 76.4% vs 3% placebo
  • Peak efficacy: PASI-90 achieved in 50-60% of patients; complete clearance (PASI-100) in 25-35%
  • Timeline: Significant improvement begins by week 2-4, with maximal response by week 12

Efficacy is maintained with continued therapy, with sustained PASI-75 in >80% through 52 weeks. Some patients may experience response plateau at week 24; dose escalation rarely improves outcomes. Switching to an alternative biologic or combining with topical agents may be considered.

Safety Profile and Adverse Effects

Common adverse events (≥5% vs placebo):

  • Infection (upper respiratory tract infections, oral candidiasis, nasopharyngitis): 3-5% increased risk
  • Injection site reactions: Erythema, pain, or induration at injection sites (8-15%)
  • Headache: 2-3%

Important safety considerations:

  • Infections: IL-17A inhibition impairs neutrophil recruitment; monitor for signs of infection. Avoid in patients with active TB. Cases of Candida infections, atypical mycobacterial infections, and CMV have been reported.
  • Neutrophil monitoring: Some patients develop elevated neutrophil counts; assess if clinically indicated. Cases of neutropenia are rare.
  • IBD: IL-17A has protective effects in the gut; secukinumab may exacerbate or unmask latent IBD. Avoid in patients with active IBD history unless benefits outweigh risks.
  • Pregnancy: Insufficient data; use only if benefit justifies potential fetal risk.

Monitoring Requirements

Baseline: TB screening, CBC, CMP, hepatitis serology. During therapy: Clinical response assessment at weeks 4, 8, 12, then every 12 weeks. If inadequate response at week 12, consider alternative diagnosis or biologic. Annual TB screening for high-risk patients. Patient education: Teach self-injection technique, proper storage (2-8°C), and infection precautions. Avoid live vaccines during therapy and for 16 weeks after discontinuation.

When to Switch or Discontinue

Switching: If PASI-75 not achieved by week 12-16, consider alternate IL-17A inhibitors (ixekizumab, brodalumab) or switch to IL-23 inhibitors (guselkumab, tildrakizumab). If intolerable infection or candidiasis develops, switch to TNF-α inhibitor or IL-23 inhibitor.

Discontinuation: If complete remission achieved and maintained for ≥12 weeks, consider de-escalation to lower dose or drug holiday. However, most patients relapse within 4-12 weeks of discontinuation; maintenance therapy is typically recommended.

Prognosis and Long-Term Outcomes

With sustained therapy, 60-70% of patients maintain PASI-75 or better indefinitely. The drug demonstrates durable efficacy without apparent tachyphylaxis over 5+ years of follow-up. Progression to psoriatic arthritis may occur despite skin disease control; concurrent joint assessment is warranted. Cardiovascular risk remains independently important in psoriasis patients regardless of skin control.

Comparison with Other IL-17A and IL-23 Inhibitors

Other IL-17A inhibitors: Ixekizumab (Taltz) and brodalumab (Siliq) also target IL-17A with comparable efficacy. Brodalumab targets IL-17 receptor A (blocking IL-17A, IL-17C, IL-17F), providing broader IL-17 pathway inhibition; may be superior for some patients but carries increased infection risk. Ixekizumab may be preferred in patients with IBD history (less IBD exacerbation than secukinumab).

IL-23 inhibitors: Guselkumab, tildrakizumab, and risankizumab target IL-23p19 and are effective for plaque psoriasis with perhaps lower infection risk. However, IL-17A inhibitors may provide faster onset and higher PASI-90/100 rates.

When to Refer to Dermatology

Refer if moderate-to-severe psoriasis fails topical/phototherapy, patient is candidate for systemic therapy, or if complex diagnostic issues exist. Specialist assessment is critical before biologic initiation.

FAQ

How quickly will my skin clear on secukinumab?

Patients typically notice improvement by week 2-4, with 80% achieving PASI-75 by week 12. Individual variation exists; some patients reach PASI-90 by week 8, while others plateau at PASI-50. Your dermatologist will assess response at weeks 4 and 12 to determine if the drug is working adequately.

Can I get infections while on secukinumab? What should I watch for?

Yes, IL-17A inhibition increases infection risk, particularly fungal infections (oral thrush, nail candidiasis) and some bacterial infections. Watch for fever, persistent cough, oral plaques, painful swallowing, unusual rashes, or swollen lymph nodes. Seek care immediately if signs of infection develop. Avoid live vaccines (varicella, MMR, rotavirus, zoster) while on therapy. Annual TB screening is important for early detection.

What if secukinumab stops working after 6-12 months?

Secondary loss of efficacy is uncommon but can occur. First, confirm compliance with injections and dosing. If compliance is verified, assess for superimposed irritant contact dermatitis or infection. If true secondary loss develops, switching to an alternative IL-17A inhibitor (ixekizumab, brodalumab) or to IL-23 inhibitors often restores response. Some patients benefit from combination therapy with topical potent steroids or vitamin D analogs.

Can I use secukinumab if I have a history of TB or hepatitis?

If you have a history of tuberculosis, secukinumab is generally contraindicated due to increased TB reactivation risk. If you have hepatitis B surface antigen positivity, secukinumab can be used with caution and requires antiviral prophylaxis (lamivudine, entecavir, or tenofovir). Hepatitis C (antibody positive without active infection) is not a contraindication. Consult your dermatologist and infectious disease specialist before initiating therapy if you have any history of TB or chronic hepatitis.
