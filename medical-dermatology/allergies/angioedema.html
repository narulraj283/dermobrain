Angioedema: Deep Tissue Swelling and Clinical Management

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Angioedema consists of deeper dermal and subcutaneous swelling, typically affecting face, lips, tongue, and potentially airways. While commonly accompanying urticaria, angioedema can occur independently and requires specific classification to guide treatment, particularly when associated with ACE inhibitor use or hereditary factors.

Classification and Mechanisms

Angioedema is classified as histamine-mediated (IgE-mediated mast cell activation), bradykinin-mediated (including hereditary angioedema variants), or idiopathic. Histamine-mediated angioedema responds to antihistamines and corticosteroids. Bradykinin-mediated angioedema, including hereditary forms and ACE inhibitor-induced angioedema, does not respond to standard treatments and requires specific therapies.

Hereditary Angioedema Types

Type I HAE (80% of cases) and Type II HAE (15%) result from C1-esterase inhibitor deficiency or dysfunction respectively, leading to uncontrolled bradykinin generation through contact system activation. Type III HAE with normal C1 inhibitor levels involves mutations in factor XII or other pathway components. Acquired angioedema shares biochemical features with hereditary forms but develops in adults without family history, often associated with lymphoproliferative disease.

Clinical Presentation

Lesions present as non-pitting, non-urticarial swelling lasting 24-72 hours. Facial involvement produces characteristic facial edema; oral and pharyngeal involvement poses airway obstruction risk. Gastrointestinal involvement causes abdominal pain simulating acute abdomen. Unlike urticaria, angioedema doesn't improve with antihistamines or corticosteroids when bradykinin-mediated.

ACE Inhibitor-Associated Angioedema

Occurring in 0.1-0.5% of patients, ACE inhibitor angioedema results from decreased bradykinin degradation by ACE. Reactions typically appear within first month of therapy but can occur at any time. African-American patients show 3-4 fold increased incidence. Management requires ACE inhibitor discontinuation and switching to alternative antihypertensive agents; cross-reactivity between ACE inhibitors and ARBs occurs in 25-50% of cases.

Diagnosis and Management

C1-esterase inhibitor levels and function testing confirm hereditary angioedema. Bradykinin-mediated angioedema requires avoidance of ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs, and certain triggers. Acute treatment for histamine-mediated angioedema includes antihistamines and corticosteroids. Bradykinin-mediated angioedema requires C1-esterase inhibitor replacement, kallikrein inhibitors (ecallantide), or bradykinin receptor antagonists (icatibant). Patients with airways-threatening angioedema require emergency airway management and specific medical therapies based on mechanism.
