Fixed Drug Eruption: Localized Recurrent Reaction Pattern

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Fixed drug eruption (FDE) represents a distinctive drug reaction characterized by reproducible recurrence at identical anatomic sites with each drug exposure. This predictable localization pattern, coupled with a typical clinical appearance and mechanism, makes FDE identifiable and manageable through allergen avoidance.

Epidemiology and Common Offenders

FDE accounts for 10% of cutaneous drug reactions, with highest incidence in young adults and slightly higher prevalence in women and dark-skinned individuals. Antimicrobials represent 50% of cases, with sulfonamides (particularly trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole) and NSAIDs being the most common triggers. Acetaminophen, oral contraceptives, barbiturates, and antimalarials account for remaining cases.

Pathophysiology

The mechanism involves tissue-resident memory T cells (TRM) localized to sites of previous drug exposure. Upon drug re-exposure, these TRM cells rapidly recognize antigen and produce local inflammatory mediators, recruiting additional effector T cells and causing localized inflammation. The presence of TRM cells explains the predictable recurrence at identical sites and the rapid onset (30 minutes to 48 hours) compared to typical delayed hypersensitivity.

Clinical Presentation

Solitary or few sharply demarcated erythematous to violaceous lesions appear at consistent anatomic sites, most commonly lips, genitals, hands, feet, and around orifices. Typical lesions are round or oval, 1-3 cm in diameter, and may show central dusky discoloration or blister formation. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation commonly develops, particularly in darker-skinned individuals. Pruritus, burning, or pain at lesion sites often precedes visible eruption.

Diagnosis and Confirmation

Clinical history of recurrent lesions at identical sites with drug exposure is suggestive; the strongest diagnostic feature is reproducibility with drug re-challenge (though intentional re-challenge is rarely performed). Oral challenge testing can document FDE when diagnosis is uncertain. Skin biopsy shows localized interface dermatitis with lymphocytic infiltration and variable keratinocyte necrosis but less extensive than SJS.

Management

Drug avoidance is essential and definitive treatment. Symptomatic management with potent topical corticosteroids or topical calcineurin inhibitors addresses active lesions. Antihistamines may help pruritus. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation may persist for months but eventually resolves. Patients benefit from maintaining a medication list identifying their specific trigger to prevent recurrence with unaware clinicians.
