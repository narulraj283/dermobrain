Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Merkel Cell Carcinoma

Merkel cell carcinoma (MCC) is a rare but highly aggressive neuroendocrine skin cancer arising from Merkel cells in the epidermis. Although MCC accounts for less than 1% of skin cancers, it carries substantial mortality, with 5-year survival rates of approximately 50% even with appropriate treatment. Early detection and aggressive multimodal therapy are essential.

Epidemiology

MCC predominantly affects elderly individuals, with median age of diagnosis around 70 years. The disease is more common in fair-skinned populations. Immunosuppression substantially increases risk, with organ transplant recipients developing MCC at 40-fold increased rate. Merkel cell polyomavirus (MCPyV) is detected in 70-80% of cases and is considered causative.

Clinical Presentation

MCC typically presents as a rapidly enlarging, firm, painless nodule on sun-exposed skin, most commonly on the head and neck region. The lesion may appear skin-colored, red, or violaceous and can be mistaken for benign growths including cysts or lipomas. Rapid growth over weeks to months is characteristic. Regional lymphadenopathy is often present at diagnosis.

Risk Factors

UV exposure is an important risk factor, with most MCCs developing on sun-exposed skin. Age is a significant risk factor, with disease predominantly affecting individuals over 60. Immunosuppression, including post-organ transplantation or advanced HIV/AIDS, dramatically increases risk. Merkel cell polyomavirus infection plays a crucial pathogenic role in most cases.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis requires histopathologic examination. Merkel cell carcinoma demonstrates small, round, blue cells arranged in nests or trabecular patterns. Neuroendocrine differentiation is characteristic, with positive immunostaining for cytokeratin 20 (CK20) and chromogranin. Staging includes physical examination, imaging (CT or PET), and sentinel lymph node biopsy.

Staging and Prognostic Factors

Breslow thickness influences prognosis, with tumors ≤1mm having better outcomes than thicker lesions. Sentinel lymph node involvement represents stage III disease and significantly impacts prognosis. Distant metastases (stage IV) carry poor prognosis with median survival of 4-12 months. Immune status and completeness of treatment influence outcomes.

Treatment Approach

Wide local excision is standard primary treatment, typically with 1-2cm margins and full thickness excision. Sentinel lymph node biopsy is recommended for all but the smallest lesions. Adjuvant radiation therapy improves local control and overall survival. Chemotherapy may be administered for advanced disease or as adjuvant treatment. Immunotherapy with checkpoint inhibitors shows promise for advanced disease.

Prognosis

MCC carries poor prognosis despite treatment. Five-year survival for stage I disease is approximately 75-80%, declining to 60% for stage II and 25-30% for stage III. Stage IV disease has median survival of less than one year. Recent advances in immunotherapy have improved outcomes for selected patients with metastatic disease.

Follow-up and Surveillance

Regular clinical surveillance is essential given high recurrence risk. Imaging surveillance including CT or PET is recommended for detecting metastatic disease. Patients require close monitoring for locoregional and systemic recurrence. Early detection of recurrence may allow salvage therapy in selected cases.
