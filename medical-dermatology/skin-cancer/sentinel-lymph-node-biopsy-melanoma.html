Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy in Melanoma

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy in Melanoma

Sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB) is a minimally invasive procedure that identifies the first lymph node or nodes to which melanoma cells drain. This technique provides crucial staging information, guides treatment decisions, and improves prognostication. SLNB has become standard of care for intermediate and thick melanomas.

Indications for SLNB

SLNB is recommended for melanomas with Breslow thickness ≥1.0mm. For tumors 0.8-1.0mm, SLNB may be considered if additional adverse features are present including ulceration, high mitotic rate (≥1 mitosis/mm2), or Clark level IV-V invasion. Breslow thickness >4mm warrants SLNB unless patient refuses or has significant comorbidities precluding regional lymphadenectomy if nodes are positive.

Procedure Technique

SLNB begins with lymphoscintigraphy, injecting radioisotope (technetium-99m sulfur colloid) intradermal around the primary tumor. A gamma probe locates the sentinel node(s). Intraoperative mapping uses blue dye (isosulfan blue or methylene blue) injected similarly. Both radioisotope and dye together improve sentinel node identification rates exceeding 95%.

Surgical Technique

Under general or local anesthesia, the surgeon identifies the sentinel node using the gamma probe and blue dye visualization. The lymph node basin is entered, and the sentinel node is carefully excised. Careful dissection avoids injury to surrounding structures. Multiple sentinel nodes may be identified and excised if present. The specimen is sent for detailed histopathologic examination.

Histopathologic Examination

Sentinel nodes are examined for presence of melanoma metastases. Standard H&E staining identifies obvious metastases. Serial sectioning detects micrometastases (small groups of tumor cells). Immunohistochemistry using melanoma-specific antibodies (S-100, Melan-A, HMB-45) increases sensitivity for detecting occult micrometastases. RT-PCR may further increase detection sensitivity.

Staging Implications

Sentinel node status profoundly affects melanoma stage and prognosis. Detection of nodal metastases upstages patients to stage III. The number of involved nodes influences stage within stage III category. Even micrometastases alter prognosis compared to node-negative disease. This prognostic information guides adjuvant therapy decisions.

Prognosis with Positive Sentinel Node

Detection of sentinel node metastases indicates approximately 50% five-year survival, substantially worse than node-negative disease. The number of involved nodes, tumor burden within nodes, and primary tumor characteristics further refine prognosis. Patients with micrometastases have better prognosis than those with larger nodal deposits.

Complete Lymph Node Dissection

Detection of sentine node metastases traditionally prompted complete lymph node dissection (CLND) of the involved basin. However, recent evidence suggests CLND may not improve survival. Many centers now observe patients with micrometastases and perform imaging surveillance. CLND remains standard for clinically apparent nodal disease.

Complications

SLNB is generally safe with low complication rates. Lymphedema occurs in <5% of patients and is usually mild. Nerve injury causing sensory changes can develop in up to 10% but is usually transient. Infection is rare. Hematoma and seroma formation may occur but are usually minor. Blue dye reactions are rare.

Accuracy and Limitations

SLNB correctly identifies the sentinel node in >95% of cases. False-negative rates (missed nodal metastases) range from 5-10%. Factors including surgeon experience and tumor location influence accuracy. Even with negative SLNB, some patients develop nodal recurrence, necessitating ongoing surveillance.

Follow-up After SLNB

Patients with positive sentinel nodes typically undergo additional staging imaging (CT, PET) and consideration of adjuvant therapy. Those with negative sentinel nodes undergo clinical surveillance. Imaging surveillance may be recommended for high-risk patients. Regular self-examination and dermatologic evaluation remain essential for detecting recurrence.
