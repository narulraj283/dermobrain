Acral Lentiginous Melanoma

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Acral Lentiginous Melanoma

Acral lentiginous melanoma (ALM) is the rarest melanoma subtype, representing 2-10% of cases, but is the most common type in individuals with dark skin. It develops on palms, soles, and subungual areas—locations of high mechanical stress. ALM tends to be diagnosed at advanced stages with worse prognosis than other melanoma types.

Location and Presentation

ALM characteristically arises on acral surfaces including palms, soles, and nail beds. Subungual melanoma presents with pigmentation in the nail matrix or plate, often extending into the nail fold (Hutchinson sign). The lesion appears as a pigmented macule or patch with irregular borders and variegated coloration.

Why These Locations?

The predilection for acral sites is not fully understood. These areas experience significant mechanical stress and have unique dermal structure with higher density of rete ridges. Unlike other melanoma types, ALM is not clearly associated with sun exposure, though some evidence suggests cumulative UV damage plays a role.

Diagnostic Challenges

Early detection is challenging because acral locations are often overlooked during skin self-examination. Subungual melanomas are frequently misdiagnosed as fungal infections or simple pigmentation. Any brown or black longitudinal band in the nail, especially with involvement of the nail fold, warrants dermatologic evaluation. Dermoscopy shows irregular parallel ridge pattern with marked asymmetry.

Histopathology

ALM demonstrates lentiginous growth pattern with significant junctional component. Cells may be arranged along dermal-epidermal junction and extending into surrounding dermis. The subungual variant originates in the matrix and may extend into the nail bed and surrounding tissue.

Treatment

Surgical excision with appropriate margins is essential. For subungual melanomas, nail bed excision is typically required. Amputation may be necessary for extensive disease. Sentinel lymph node biopsy is generally performed given the high risk of nodal metastasis at presentation.

Prognosis and Outcomes

ALM carries poor prognosis due to delayed diagnosis, resulting in thicker tumors at presentation. Five-year survival rates are significantly lower than other melanoma types. Close follow-up and surveillance for recurrence and metastasis are essential components of management.
