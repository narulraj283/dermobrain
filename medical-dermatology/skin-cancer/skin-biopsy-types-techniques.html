Skin Biopsy: Types and Techniques

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Skin Biopsy: Types and Techniques

Histopathologic examination via skin biopsy remains the gold standard for diagnosis of skin cancer. Multiple biopsy techniques are available, each suited to particular clinical scenarios. Appropriate technique selection ensures adequate tissue for diagnosis while minimizing scarring and optimizing healing.

Shave Biopsy

Shave biopsy uses a razor blade or specialized shave tool to remove a shallow specimen. The blade is held tangentially to skin surface and moved horizontally beneath the lesion. This technique is quick, requires minimal anesthesia, and leaves minimal scarring. However, shave biopsy provides limited depth, precluding assessment of deep dermal involvement.

Shave Biopsy Indications

Shave biopsy is appropriate for suspected melanoma if confined to epidermis, though full-thickness excisional biopsy is preferred. Shave biopsy is suitable for non-melanoma skin cancers including BCC and SCC. It is adequate for benign lesions. Shave biopsy should be avoided if melanoma is suspected and depth assessment is critical for staging.

Punch Biopsy

Punch biopsy uses a circular cutting tool (typically 3-4mm diameter) to obtain full-thickness dermal specimen. The punch is advanced perpendicular to skin, advanced through dermis, and the specimen is freed with scissors. This technique provides adequate depth for most diagnostic purposes and leaves a round defect closed with sutures.

Punch Biopsy Advantages

Punch biopsy provides full-thickness specimens enabling assessment of invasion depth, important for melanoma staging. It is suitable for most skin lesions and can be performed efficiently in office setting. The round defect heals predictably with minimal scarring. Multiple punches can be obtained if needed for extensive lesions or special studies.

Excisional Biopsy

Excisional biopsy removes the entire lesion with surrounding normal skin margin. The specimen is typically 5-10mm larger than the lesion itself. This technique provides complete histologic assessment and enables measurement of margins. Excisional biopsy is preferred for suspected melanoma to obtain complete staging information.

Excisional Biopsy Advantages

Excisional biopsy provides definitive diagnosis and treatment simultaneously for benign lesions. For melanoma, it allows complete assessment of Breslow thickness, mitotic rate, and margin status. It eliminates concern for incomplete sampling. The specimen orientation is clear for histopathologic assessment.

Incisional Biopsy

Incisional biopsy removes a representative portion of a large lesion without excising the entire lesion. This technique is useful for large lesions that would require extensive reconstruction if completely excised. However, it has limitations including potential sampling error and inability to assess margins definitively.

Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy

SLNB is a specialized procedure identifying the first lymph node to which a tumor drains. A radioisotope and/or blue dye is injected at the tumor site. The sentinel node (or nodes) is identified and excised for histologic examination. This technique identifies occult nodal metastases staging melanoma and guiding treatment decisions.

Special Stains and Studies

Standard hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining is used for most biopsies. Immunohistochemistry may be utilized to identify melanoma cells (S-100, Melan-A, SOX10) or other characteristics. Special studies including Mohs layer-by-layer examination provide margin assessment. Genetic testing may be performed for prognostic or therapeutic information.

Patient Preparation and Aftercare

Biopsies require informed consent and understanding of potential risks including infection, bleeding, and scarring. Local anesthesia is administered before biopsy. Proper wound care including cleansing and antibiotic ointment promotes healing. Sutures are typically removed in 7-14 days depending on location. Patients should avoid strenuous activity and water immersion during healing.
