Melanoma Staging and Prognosis

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Melanoma Staging and Prognosis

Melanoma staging utilizes the TNM classification system, incorporating tumor characteristics (T), regional lymph node involvement (N), and distant metastasis (M). Accurate staging guides treatment decisions and provides prognostic information essential for patient counseling and follow-up planning.

Tumor Stage (T)

Primary tumor staging is based on Breslow thickness, measured in millimeters from the granular layer or base of the lesion to the deepest invasion point. Thin melanomas (T1) measure ≤1.0mm, with further substratification at 0.8mm. T2 (1.01-2.0mm), T3 (2.01-4.0mm), and T4 (>4.0mm) categories reflect increasing depth. Ulceration presence elevates stage within thickness category.

Breslow Thickness Significance

Breslow thickness is the single most important prognostic factor. Melanomas ≤1mm thick have excellent prognosis with 95%+ five-year survival. Thickness of 1-2mm demonstrates 80-90% five-year survival. Tumors 2-4mm have 60-75% survival, while those >4mm show 50% or less five-year survival. Mitotic rate, ulceration, and Clark level provide additional prognostic refinement.

Lymph Node Stage (N)

Regional lymph node involvement indicates metastatic disease. N0 represents no nodal involvement. N1 (1 node), N2 (2-3 nodes), and N3 (4 or more nodes) reflect increasing nodal tumor burden. Micrometastases (clinically occult, identified by sentinel lymph node biopsy) carry better prognosis than clinically apparent nodal disease.

Distant Metastasis (M)

M0 indicates absence of distant metastasis. M1a denotes skin, subcutaneous, or distant lymph node metastases. M1b includes lung metastases, while M1c comprises other visceral metastases. The presence of elevated lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) indicates higher stage M1c disease. Median survival for stage IV melanoma ranges from 6-20 months depending on metastatic site and burden.

Overall Stage Grouping

Stage 0 represents melanoma in situ. Stage I includes thin melanomas without ulceration (I A: ≤0.8mm; I B: 0.81-1.0mm or ulcerated ≤1.0mm). Stage II includes intermediate to thick melanomas. Stage III encompasses regional lymph node metastases. Stage IV represents distant metastases.

Prognostic Factors Beyond TNM

Mitotic rate (number of mitoses per mm2) significantly impacts prognosis, with increased mitotic activity indicating worse outcome. Ulceration denotes poor prognosis within TNM categories. Clark level (I-V) describing depth relative to skin structures provides prognostic information. Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes, when present, may indicate better prognosis.

Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy

SLNB is recommended for melanomas with Breslow thickness ≥1.0mm, or 0.8-1.0mm with additional risk factors including ulceration, high mitotic rate, or Clark level IV-V. SLNB identifies occult nodal metastases affecting staging and guiding adjuvant therapy decisions. Detection of micrometastases impacts survival outcomes and treatment planning.

Prognosis by Stage

Stage IA melanomas have >95% five-year survival. Stage IB and IIA have 85-90% survival. Stage IIB and IIC show 60-75% survival. Stage III (nodal disease) demonstrates 40-70% five-year survival depending on nodal burden. Stage IV carries 15-20% five-year survival, though immunotherapy has improved outcomes for selected patients.

Follow-up and Surveillance

Close surveillance is essential given recurrence risk. Patients should perform monthly skin self-examination and undergo periodic dermatologic evaluation. Imaging surveillance for stage III and higher may include regular imaging. Patient education regarding sun protection and early recognition of warning signs is paramount.
