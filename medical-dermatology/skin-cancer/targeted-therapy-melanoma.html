Targeted Therapy for Melanoma: BRAF and Beyond

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Targeted Therapy for Melanoma: BRAF and Beyond

Targeted therapies exploit genetic mutations driving melanoma growth. BRAF mutations present in 40-50% of melanomas provide an ideal target. BRAF inhibitors combined with MEK inhibitors have dramatically improved outcomes for BRAF-mutant melanomas. These agents work rapidly but resistance often develops, necessitating combination therapy and ongoing research.

BRAF Mutations in Melanoma

BRAF V600E mutations are present in 40-50% of cutaneous melanomas. This mutation constitutively activates BRAF kinase, driving oncogenic signaling through the MAPK pathway. BRAF mutations are less common in acral and mucosal melanomas. Testing for BRAF mutations guides therapy selection, as BRAF inhibitors are only effective for BRAF-mutant tumors.

BRAF Inhibitors

Vemurafenib and dabrafenib are selective BRAF inhibitors FDA-approved for BRAF V600E-mutant melanoma. These agents block BRAF kinase, halting aberrant signaling. As monotherapy, BRAF inhibitors produce response rates of 50-70% for metastatic disease, but responses are often short-lived with median progression-free survival of 5-7 months.

MEK Inhibitors

MEK inhibitors block the MAPK pathway downstream of BRAF. Trametinib and cobimetinib are FDA-approved MEK inhibitors. When combined with BRAF inhibitors, response rates improve to 70-80%, and progression-free survival doubles to 10-11 months. The combination is more effective than either agent alone.

BRAF/MEK Combination Therapy

Combining BRAF and MEK inhibitors (vemurafenib plus cobimetinib, dabrafenib plus trametinib) provides superior outcomes. Response rates of 70-80%, with 40-50% complete response rates, are achieved. Five-year overall survival approaches 50% with combination therapy. This combination has become standard first-line therapy for BRAF-mutant metastatic melanoma.

Mechanisms of Resistance

Resistance to BRAF/MEK inhibitors develops through multiple mechanisms. Secondary BRAF, NRAS, or MAP2K mutations restore MAPK signaling. PTEN loss facilitates resistance through alternative pathway activation. Tumor heterogeneity with selection of resistant clones occurs with treatment. Understanding resistance mechanisms guides development of novel combinations.

Combination with Immunotherapy

Combining targeted therapy with immunotherapy explores synergistic approaches. Sequential therapy (targeted therapy followed by immunotherapy) is often used. Concurrent combination may enhance toxicity without clear benefit. Clinical trials continue evaluating optimal sequencing and combination strategies.

Non-BRAF Mutations

NRAS mutations occur in 25-30% of melanomas and were historically difficult to target. Novel NRAS inhibitor sotorasib shows promise for NRAS-mutant melanomas. KIT mutations occur in acral and mucosal melanomas and are treatable with KIT inhibitors. Identifying mutations guides personalized therapy selection.

Adjuvant Targeted Therapy

BRAF inhibitors combined with MEK inhibitors are approved as adjuvant therapy for stage III BRAF-mutant melanoma. One year of adjuvant therapy reduces recurrence risk. Long-term follow-up data support meaningful recurrence-free survival benefit. Targeted therapy represents an alternative to immunotherapy for appropriate patients.

Side Effects and Management

BRAF inhibitors commonly cause photosensitivity and squamous cell carcinomas requiring strict sun protection and skin monitoring. MEK inhibitors cause diarrhea, rash, and cardiac toxicity. Combination therapy increases toxicity. Dose modifications and supportive care manage most side effects. Severe toxicity occasionally requires therapy discontinuation.

Prognosis with Targeted Therapy

BRAF-mutant melanomas treated with BRAF/MEK combination therapy achieve substantially better outcomes than BRAF wild-type melanomas. Five-year overall survival exceeds 50% for metastatic BRAF-mutant disease. However, many patients eventually develop resistance. Combination with immunotherapy or other novel agents are being studied to improve long-term outcomes.
