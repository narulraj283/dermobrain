Radiation Therapy for Skin Cancer

Radiation therapy (RT) represents an effective treatment for skin cancers, particularly when surgery is not possible or has failed. External beam radiation therapy (EBRT) delivers targeted radiation to tumors, destroying cancer cells through DNA damage. Radiotherapy is particularly useful for advanced, unresectable, or recurrent skin cancers and brain metastases.

Indications for Radiation Therapy

Radiotherapy is indicated for inoperable advanced skin cancers including unresectable regional disease and brain metastases. It may be used adjuvantly for high-risk features including positive margins, lymph node involvement, or perineural invasion. Palliation of symptoms from advanced disease is another important indication. RT complements surgery or systemic therapy in comprehensive treatment plans.

Mechanisms of Action

Radiation damages cellular DNA, leading to cell death. High doses produce acute effects including inflammation and tissue damage. Fractionated therapy (divided into multiple treatments) allows normal tissue recovery between fractions. Different dose fractionation schedules optimize tumor control while minimizing normal tissue toxicity.

Photon Radiation

External beam photon radiotherapy uses high-energy X-rays focused on the tumor. Multiple beams from different angles concentrate dose within the tumor while reducing dose to surrounding normal tissues. Intensity-modulated radiotherapy (IMRT) modulates beam intensity to conform dose precisely to tumor shape. Three-dimensional conformal therapy improves target coverage and normal tissue sparing.

Electron Radiation

Electron beam therapy is particularly useful for superficial skin cancers. Electrons deposit dose near the skin surface with limited depth penetration, ideal for cutaneous lesions. Electron therapy produces minimal dose to underlying structures. This approach is often used for head and neck skin cancers, providing good tumor control with limited normal tissue effects.

Stereotactic Radiosurgery

For brain metastases from melanoma and other skin cancers, stereotactic radiosurgery (SRS) delivers high-dose focused radiation in one to few fractions. SRS controls brain metastases effectively, with response rates of 80-90%. Combination with systemic therapy or additional whole brain radiation is individualized based on number and size of lesions.

Dosing and Fractionation

Conventional fractionation typically delivers 2 Gy daily to total doses of 50-70 Gy over 5-7 weeks. Hypofractionation uses larger daily doses over shorter periods. Stereotactic approaches deliver very high doses in few fractions. Dose selection balances tumor control probability with normal tissue toxicity risk.

Acute and Late Toxicity

Acute toxicity during treatment includes erythema, dry desquamation, and moist desquamation of skin. Hair loss occurs in treated areas. Mucositis develops if mucosal surfaces are irradiated. Most acute effects resolve within weeks after treatment. Late toxicity including fibrosis and chronic ulceration may develop months to years after treatment.

Adjuvant Radiation

Adjuvant RT improves local control for high-risk melanomas and SCCs with positive margins, deep invasion, or perineural invasion. It reduces recurrence risk but impact on overall survival is variable. Adjuvant RT is typically offered when surgical re-excision is not feasible. Planning encompasses primary site and nodal basins at risk.

Combination with Systemic Therapy

Combining radiotherapy with checkpoint inhibitors or targeted therapy is increasingly explored. Radiation may enhance immunotherapy response through tumor antigen release. Sequencing of RT and systemic therapy is individualized. Some evidence suggests concurrent combination may improve outcomes compared to sequential therapy.

Palliative Radiation

For advanced unresectable disease, palliative RT reduces symptomatic burden. Short courses of 5-10 Gy in 1-2 fractions relieve pain, bleeding, and drainage from advanced lesions. Brain metastases causing symptoms are effectively palliated. Palliative intent focuses on symptom relief rather than curative goals.
