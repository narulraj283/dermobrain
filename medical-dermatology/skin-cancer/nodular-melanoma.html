Understanding Nodular Melanoma: Clinical Considerations and Management

Overview

Nodular melanoma (NM) represents 15-20% of all melanomas but accounts for a disproportionately high percentage of melanoma deaths. This aggressive variant is characterized by a vertical growth phase from its inception, with minimal or no radial growth component. Unlike superficial spreading melanoma, nodular melanoma grows primarily downward into deeper skin layers and subcutaneous tissue from the beginning, which explains why these lesions are typically thicker at diagnosis (median Breslow depth 2.0+ mm versus 0.6-0.8 mm for SSM) and often diagnosed at more advanced stages.

Clinical Features and Presentation

Nodular melanoma typically presents as a raised, pigmented dome-shaped nodule or tumefaction. The lesion is often darkly pigmented (dark brown, black), but approximately 5-10% of nodular melanomas are amelanotic (flesh-colored, red, or pink), making them particularly difficult to diagnose clinically. These lesions frequently show symmetry and uniform coloration, which can be deceptive—they may not trigger the ABCDE alarm bells that alert patients to superficial spreading melanoma.

Nodular melanomas commonly bleed, itch, or crust—symptoms that should always prompt urgent dermatologic evaluation. Many patients report that the lesion was present for only a few months before diagnosis, reflecting the rapid vertical growth rate. The mean thickness at diagnosis is approximately 2.5-3.0 mm, making early detection critically important given the exponential increase in metastatic risk with increasing depth.

Aggressive Growth Pattern

The defining feature of nodular melanoma is its immediate vertical growth phase. There is no extended radial growth phase as seen in SSM; instead, melanoma cells invade downward through the dermis and into subcutaneous fat from the outset. This rapid vertical invasion means:

  • Higher Breslow depths at presentation
  • Greater likelihood of lymph node involvement at diagnosis (Stage III disease in 20-30% of cases)
  • Higher mitotic rates (typically >5 mitoses/mm², sometimes >10/mm²)
  • More frequent presence of ulceration (30-50% of nodular melanomas show ulceration)
  • Higher incidence of Clark level IV-V involvement

AJCC Staging and Prognosis

Nodular melanoma stage-specific survival rates are significantly lower than other subtypes:

  • Stage IA (≤0.8 mm, no ulceration): 97% 5-year survival (rare for NM)
  • Stage IB-IIA (0.8-2.0 mm range): 92-95% survival
  • Stage IIB-IIC (2.01+ mm): 70-85% survival (NM typically presents at this stage or higher)
  • Stage III (with regional lymph node involvement): 40-75% survival depending on node burden
  • Stage IV (distant metastases): ~35% 5-year survival with modern immunotherapy

The presence of ulceration significantly worsens prognosis—it carries approximately 10-15% additional reduction in 5-year survival compared to non-ulcerated lesions of the same thickness.

Histopathological Features

Pathological diagnosis of nodular melanoma requires:

  • Exophytic nodular lesion with vertical growth phase
  • Absence of significant radial growth component (though small amounts may be present)
  • Infiltration of tumor cells into dermis and often into subcutaneous tissue
  • High mitotic count (often >5 mitoses/10 high-power fields)
  • Possible ulceration or surface erosion
  • Accurate Breslow depth measurement
  • Clark level assessment (often IV-V)
  • Assessment for regression, necrosis, and lymphocytic infiltrate

Diagnostic Challenges

Nodular melanomas are frequently misdiagnosed clinically because:

  • Symmetry and uniform color do not trigger ABCDE suspicion
  • Amelanotic variants are easily mistaken for benign lesions like basal cell carcinoma or dermatofibroma
  • Small initial lesions may appear innocuous despite rapid growth
  • The short duration of lesion may make it seem "new" rather than dangerous

Dermoscopic features of nodular melanoma include starburst or globular patterns, homogeneous blue or dark coloration, and lack of typical superficial melanoma patterns. Any nodular lesion, especially if asymmetric, changing, or with concerning dermoscopic features, warrants excisional biopsy.

Surgical Management

Excisional biopsy with appropriate margins is essential. Excision margins for nodular melanoma depend on Breslow depth:

  • ≤1.0 mm: 1 cm margins (rare for nodular melanoma at this depth)
  • 1.01-2.0 mm: 1-2 cm margins
  • 2.01-3.0 mm: 2 cm margins
  • >3.0 mm: 2 cm margins (some specialists advocate Mohs surgery for improved precision)

Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy

SLNB is recommended for essentially all nodular melanomas given the high frequency of lymph node involvement. Studies show that 20-40% of nodular melanomas with Breslow depth greater than 1.0 mm have positive sentinel nodes. Given the advanced stage at presentation for many nodular melanomas, baseline staging with cross-sectional imaging (CT chest/abdomen/pelvis, brain MRI for higher-risk lesions) should be considered before SLNB, as overt metastatic disease may alter management.

Adjuvant and Neoadjuvant Therapy

For Stage IIB-IIC nodular melanoma, adjuvant pembrolizumab (Keytruda, 200 mg IV every 3 weeks for 1 year) is standard of care, reducing recurrence risk by approximately 35% based on KEYNOTE-716 trial data.

For Stage III nodular melanoma with resectable lymph node disease, neoadjuvant pembrolizumab followed by surgery and adjuvant therapy is increasingly preferred based on SWOG S1801 and NADINA trial data. For patients with unresectable Stage III or Stage IV disease, first-line immunotherapy options include:

  • Nivolumab + relatlimab (Opdualag): PD-1 and LAG-3 blockade with ~60% response rates
  • Nivolumab + ipilimumab: PD-1 and CTLA-4 blockade with ~55% response rates but higher toxicity
  • For BRAF-mutant nodular melanoma (40-50% of cases): Dabrafenib + trametinib or encorafenib + binimetinib with response rates of 70-75%

Surveillance and Follow-up

Aggressive surveillance is particularly important for nodular melanoma patients:

  • Clinical examination every 3 months for the first 2-3 years
  • Lymph node examination at each visit (nodular melanoma has higher recurrence risk)
  • High-risk patients should have baseline and periodic (every 3-6 months for 2-3 years) cross-sectional imaging
  • Monthly self-examination using ABCDE and color photography for comparison
  • Annual full-body skin examination by dermatologist indefinitely

Prognosis and Outcomes

Despite being only 15-20% of melanomas, nodular melanoma accounts for 25-30% of melanoma deaths due to its aggressive nature and advanced stage at presentation. However, patients diagnosed with localized nodular melanoma (Stage I-II) have 5-year survivals exceeding 90%, emphasizing the importance of early detection. The advent of immunotherapy has dramatically improved outcomes for Stage III-IV disease, with median overall survival for Stage IV melanoma now approaching 4+ years with modern combination therapies compared to 6-12 months in the pre-immunotherapy era.

Conclusion

Nodular melanoma is an aggressive variant that demands prompt recognition and surgical intervention. The lack of extended radial growth phase means lesions are typically thicker at diagnosis, but early-stage nodular melanomas still have excellent prognoses with surgical excision alone. Any raised, growing pigmented lesion—especially if amelanotic, bleeding, or changing rapidly—warrants urgent dermatologic evaluation. For advanced nodular melanoma, modern immunotherapy and targeted therapies offer genuine hope; achieving long-term survival rates unimaginable a decade ago.
