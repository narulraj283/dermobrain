Acral Lentiginous Melanoma: Presentation, Diagnosis, and Treatment Strategies

Clinical Overview

Acral lentiginous melanoma (ALM) is a distinct clinicopathologic variant of melanoma arising on the palms, soles, nail apparatus, and other acral (distal) surfaces. ALM is characterized by an in situ intraepidermal proliferative phase with pagetoid spread followed by dermal invasion, though the transition to vertical growth phase may occur earlier than in other melanoma subtypes. ALM represents 5-15% of melanomas overall but accounts for 35-60% of melanomas in individuals with darker skin phototypes (Fitzpatrick IV-VI), making it proportionally more common in African American, Asian, and Hispanic populations. Despite being less common than superficial spreading melanoma, ALM carries distinctly poorer prognosis due to delayed diagnosis, acral anatomy limiting surgical margins, and biologically aggressive behavior.

Epidemiology & Risk Factors

ALM occurs across all age groups but most commonly presents in middle-aged to elderly patients (median age 50-70 years). No strong gender predominance is documented. Notably, ALM shows a striking predilection for individuals with skin of color—representing the most common melanoma subtype in East Asian, African, and Latin American populations. Unlike other melanoma variants, ALM does NOT strongly associate with ultraviolet radiation exposure or chronic sun damage. Instead, ALM appears to arise de novo without apparent precursor lesions in most cases. Risk factors include: genetic predisposition (familial melanoma syndrome), immunosuppression (particularly transplant recipients), and previous history of melanoma. The lack of strong UV association explains the equal distribution of ALM across different latitudes and in populations with minimal sun exposure.

Pathophysiology

ALM arises through malignant transformation of melanocytes within acral epithelium, but the exact etiologic triggers remain incompletely understood. Histologically, ALM demonstrates extensive pagetoid spread with melanocytes infiltrating all epidermal levels, particularly prominent in the acral variant compared to other melanoma types. The lesion shows marked cytologic atypia, variable pigmentation, and often a dense inflammatory lymphocytic infiltrate. Dermal invasion is characterized by spindle-shaped or epithelioid melanocytes arranged in fascicles or storiform patterns. Molecular alterations differ from non-acral melanomas: BRAF V600E mutations are less common (~25%), while KIT mutations are significantly more frequent (~30-40%). NRAS mutations occur in ~20% of cases. The high frequency of KIT alterations in ALM (compared to 5-10% in non-acral melanoma) has led to investigation of KIT inhibitors as therapeutic options for KIT-mutant acral melanomas.

Clinical Presentation & Classification

ALM on plantar or palmar surfaces presents as an asymmetric, irregularly bordered, hyperpigmented macule or plaque with color variation (tan, brown, black). The lesion may demonstrate the ABCDE features (Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color variegation, Diameter >6mm, Evolution). Subungual ALM initially appears as a longitudinal brown-black band in the nail (melanonychia) with irregular borders and color variegation. The pigmented band may extend into the nail fold (Hutchinson sign), representing periungual invasion—a sign of advanced disease. Perilesional skin involvement may include dystrophy of the nail or ulceration. Palmar presentation is less common than plantar but carries similar implications. Interdigital or periungual location is associated with higher risk. The distinguishing feature of ALM is its location on acral surfaces combined with delayed presentation (often 1-2 years after symptoms first noted) due to difficulty distinguishing melanoma from benign pigmentation on hands and feet.

Diagnosis & Staging

Clinical diagnosis requires high suspicion for any new or enlarging pigmented lesion on palms, soles, or nail apparatus. Dermoscopy reveals asymmetric pattern, irregular dots and globules at the periphery, irregular streaks, and parallel ridge pattern (in non-nail lesions). For nail lesions, dermoscopy shows irregular pigmentation with unclear lateral borders extending into the nail matrix. Longitudinal melanonychia with Hutchinson sign (pigmentation in nail fold) is highly suspicious for subungual melanoma. Biopsy is essential: excisional biopsy with 3-5mm margins allows complete histopathologic assessment. For subungual lesions, biopsy of the nail matrix through a proximal nail fold access or nail plate removal is required. Breslow depth is the most critical prognostic factor: unfortunately, >50% of ALM cases present as Stage IIB-III (Breslow >2mm) due to delayed diagnosis. Clark level, mitotic rate (≥1/mm²), and ulceration status determine final TNM stage. Sentinel lymph node biopsy is recommended for all invasive ALM ≥0.8mm Breslow depth.

Treatment Algorithm

Surgical excision with negative margins is the primary treatment. Standard margins are 1-2cm for ALM, though acral anatomy (particularly on digits and sole) may limit margin adequacy. For palmoplantar lesions, margins of 1cm are often achievable, but for subungual ALM, amputation at the distal interphalangeal joint or higher is frequently necessary to achieve adequate margins, especially if the lesion involves the proximal nail matrix. Amputation through the digit at a joint that allows functional and cosmetic preservation is preferred; ray amputation (removal of entire digit with metacarpal) is reserved for extensive disease. Sentinel lymph node biopsy is indicated for all invasive ALM with Breslow depth ≥0.8mm. For SLN-positive disease, completion lymph node dissection should be performed. Adjuvant systemic therapy is strongly considered for Stage IB-III ALM: ipilimumab 3mg/kg IV every 3 weeks for 4 induction doses followed by maintenance dosing (EORTC 18071 trial showed improved RFS in Stage III patients). Alternatively, pembrolizumab 200mg IV every 3 weeks for up to 1 year is an option (KEYNOTE-054). For KIT-mutant metastatic ALM, imatinib mesylate 400-600mg daily, sunitinib 50mg daily (4 weeks on/2 weeks off), or nilotinib 400mg twice daily provides potential benefit. For BRAF V600E-mutant metastatic ALM, dabrafenib 100mg twice daily plus trametinib 2mg daily achieves response rates of 50-70%. For wild-type or BRAF/NRAS-negative disease, pembrolizumab or nivolumab plus ipilimumab is appropriate.

Prognosis & Survival

ALM prognosis is substantially worse than non-acral melanoma subtypes. Five-year survival for Stage IA (Breslow ≤0.8mm) is 90-95%, but only 30-40% of ALM patients present at this stage. Stage IIA (Breslow 1.01-2.0mm) has 5-year survival of 70-80%; Stage IIB (Breslow 2.01-4.0mm) is 50-65%; and Stage IIC (>4mm) is 30-40%. The poor overall prognosis reflects delayed presentation—median Breslow depth at diagnosis is 2.5-3.5mm compared to 1.0-1.5mm for other subtypes. Presence of ulceration reduces 5-year survival by ~15%. SLN positivity reduces 5-year survival by 30-40% depending on tumor burden. For metastatic ALM, median overall survival with single-agent immunotherapy is 10-14 months; combination nivolumab-ipilimumab improves median OS to 18-24 months. KIT-mutant metastatic ALM treated with KIT inhibitors achieves response rates of 40-50% with median progression-free survival of 9-11 months.

When to See a Dermatologist

Any new or changing pigmented lesion on palms, soles, or nail apparatus warrants urgent dermatologic evaluation. Longitudinal melanonychia (brown-black band in nail) with Hutchinson sign, irregular borders, or nail dystrophy is a red flag requiring nail biopsy. Lesions that have enlarged over weeks to months or changed color require evaluation. Dermoscopy by an experienced dermatologist is essential to differentiate benign melanonychia (seen in up to 20% of individuals with skin of color) from melanoma. Regular surveillance of acral skin is particularly important in individuals with family history of melanoma or prior personal history. Given the high proportion of melanomas in dark-skinned individuals that are acral, dermatology education emphasizing ALM awareness is critical to reduce disparities in melanoma outcomes.

Frequently Asked Questions

I have a dark band under my fingernail. Is this melanoma?

Longitudinal melanonychia (dark band in the nail) is common, especially in individuals with darker skin tones, and is benign in >95% of cases. However, features concerning for melanoma include: irregular borders, Hutchinson sign (pigmentation in the nail fold), nail dystrophy/thickening, bleeding from the nail, width >3mm, and rapid growth. Dermoscopy by a dermatologist can often distinguish benign from malignant melanonychia. If concerning features are present, nail biopsy is warranted to establish diagnosis with certainty.

If acral melanoma is diagnosed, will I need to have my finger or toe amputated?

Amputation may be necessary for subungual melanoma if the lesion involves the nail matrix (proximal nail) and standard excision with adequate margins cannot be achieved. However, not all acral melanomas require amputation—lesions on the palm or sole distant from joints may be excised with intact limb preservation. Amputation through the interphalangeal joint is preferred over ray amputation when feasible. The surgical plan depends on the exact location, size, and depth of your lesion. Your surgeon will discuss the options to maximize both cure and functional/cosmetic outcomes.

I was diagnosed with Stage II acral melanoma with a 2.8mm Breslow depth. What's my prognosis?

Stage IIB melanoma (Breslow 2.01-4.0mm) has approximately 50-65% 10-year survival. With adjuvant immunotherapy (pembrolizumab or ipilimumab for 1-2 years), recurrence-free survival improves to approximately 65-70% at 2 years. Regular surveillance with clinical examination and imaging (ultrasound or CT depending on risk factors) is indicated. SLN biopsy status is important: if your sentinel node was negative, prognosis is somewhat better; if positive, more intensive adjuvant therapy is warranted. Discuss specific prognosis with your oncologist, as individual factors influence outcomes.

Why is acral melanoma more common in people with darker skin?

Acral lentiginous melanoma accounts for 35-60% of melanomas in individuals with darker skin versus only 5-15% in lighter-skinned populations. Unlike other melanoma subtypes that correlate strongly with sun exposure, ALM appears to arise independently of UV radiation. The reason for higher ALM prevalence in darker-skinned populations remains unclear—it may reflect both genetic/molecular predisposition and diagnostic bias (other melanoma subtypes are more easily recognized clinically on lighter skin). Recognition of ALM in all populations is critical to avoid delays in diagnosis.
