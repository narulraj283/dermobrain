Basal Cell Carcinoma: Types and Overview

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Basal Cell Carcinoma: Types and Overview

Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is the most common human malignancy, originating from basal keratinocytes at the dermal-epidermal junction. Although BCC rarely metastasizes, it can cause significant local tissue destruction if left untreated. Multiple subtypes exist, each with distinct clinical and histologic characteristics influencing treatment approach and prognosis.

Epidemiology and Risk Factors

BCC incidence has increased dramatically over recent decades, with over 3 million cases diagnosed annually in the United States alone. Chronic and cumulative UV exposure, particularly UVB radiation, is the primary risk factor. Fair-skinned individuals, those with impaired ability to tan, and residents of sunny climates face increased risk. Immunosuppression, prior radiation exposure, and genetic syndromes including Gorlin syndrome predispose to multiple BCCs.

Pathophysiology

BCC arises from malignant transformation of pluripotent cells in the basal layer. The Sonic Hedgehog (SHH) signaling pathway is frequently dysregulated in BCC, driving tumorigenesis. Mutations in PTCH1 (Patched 1) gene occur in approximately 85% of BCCs. Other mutations including p53 inactivation and RAS mutations contribute to malignant transformation.

Clinical Presentation

BCC typically presents as a slow-growing lesion on sun-exposed skin, most commonly the head and neck region. Appearance varies by histologic type but often includes a pearly, shiny surface with telangiectasia (dilated blood vessels). Some lesions demonstrate a central ulceration with rolled borders ("rodent ulcer"). Bleeding, crusting, and lack of healing despite extended duration are characteristic.

Nodular Basal Cell Carcinoma

Nodular BCC, the most common subtype (60-80% of cases), presents as a firm, pearly or skin-colored nodule with a glistening surface and visible telangiectatic vessels. The lesion may demonstrate surface ulceration or crusting. Nodular BCC grows slowly but can achieve significant size and depth if untreated, causing local tissue destruction.

Superficial Basal Cell Carcinoma

Superficial BCC represents 15-35% of cases and presents as a thin, erythematous, scaly patch or plaque. The lesion often appears eczematous or psoriasiform, potentially causing diagnostic confusion. Superficial BCC typically occurs on the trunk and shoulders, areas exposed to sun but not receiving intense focused UV. This subtype has the lowest risk of aggressive behavior.

Morpheaform (Infiltrative) Basal Cell Carcinoma

Morpheaform BCC, representing 5-10% of cases, is clinically aggressive and often underestimated in size. It presents as a poorly demarcated, scar-like plaque with indistinct borders. Significant subclinical extension is characteristic, making complete removal challenging. This subtype carries higher recurrence rates and requires careful assessment of margins.

Pigmented Basal Cell Carcinoma

Pigmented BCC contains melanin-producing cells and presents with brown to black coloration. The lesion may clinically resemble melanoma, requiring histologic confirmation. Pigmented BCC has similar behavior to nodular type but can be more difficult to distinguish from other pigmented lesions clinically.

Treatment Considerations

Treatment selection depends on BCC subtype, size, location, and patient factors. Standard excision with histologic assessment is appropriate for most BCCs. Mohs micrographic surgery is preferred for morpheaform, large, or high-risk location lesions. Radiation therapy, topical immunotherapy (imiquimod), and photodynamic therapy represent alternatives in selected cases.

Prognosis and Follow-up

Local recurrence rates range from 1-5% after standard excision and <1% after Mohs surgery. Metastasis is rare but possible with large, long-standing, or high-risk BCCs. Patients with history of BCC face increased risk of developing additional BCCs, necessitating long-term surveillance and skin protection counseling.
