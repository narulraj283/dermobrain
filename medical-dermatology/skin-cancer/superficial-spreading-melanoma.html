Lentigo Maligna Melanoma: Epidemiology, Clinical Features, and Management

Clinical Overview

Lentigo maligna melanoma (LMM) represents a distinct clinicopathologic variant of melanoma arising in sun-damaged skin, primarily affecting the face, ears, and neck of elderly patients. LMM evolves through a prolonged intraepidermal phase known as lentigo maligna (LM), which can persist for 10-30 years before transition to invasive melanoma (vertical growth phase). The extended horizontal growth phase offers a critical window for detection and treatment before dermal invasion occurs. While LMM accounts for only 5-10% of cutaneous melanomas, it is the most common histologic type in patients over 60 years old and represents a significant proportion of melanomas in chronically sun-exposed populations.

Epidemiology & Risk Factors

LMM typically develops in patients aged 65-80 years with pronounced chronic sun exposure history, particularly those with fair skin phototypes (Fitzpatrick I-III). The condition is more common in populations living in high-UV index regions: Australia reports an incidence of approximately 15-20 cases per 100,000 person-years in patients >60 years. Male predominance is observed (male-to-female ratio approximately 2:1), reflecting cumulative sun exposure patterns. Primary risk factors include: cumulatively high ultraviolet A (UVA) and ultraviolet B (UVB) exposure, solar elastosis evident on clinical examination, presence of multiple solar lentigines and actinic keratoses on face and neck, fair skin phototype, and weakened immune system (particularly in transplant recipients). Unlike superficial spreading melanoma, LMM does NOT strongly associate with dysplastic nevus syndrome or sudden intermittent sunburns; rather, it correlates with chronic occupational sun exposure.

Pathophysiology

LMM arises through malignant transformation of melanocytes in severely photodamaged skin. The dermis demonstrates classic solar elastosis with solar-damaged collagen appearing as blue-staining amorphous material on histology. In the lentigo maligna (in situ) phase, neoplastic melanocytes infiltrate the full thickness of the epidermis with basilar proliferation, showing marked cytologic atypia, irregular nests, and pagetoid spread. Unlike in superficial spreading melanoma, the inflammatory infiltrate in LMM is often sparse or minimal. Progression to invasive melanoma (LMM proper) occurs with breach of the dermoepidermal junction and expansion into the dermis. Molecular alterations include high frequency of NF1 mutations (40-50% of cases), BRAF V600E mutations (30-40%), and NRAS mutations (10-20%). Notably, copy number variations affecting chromosome 9p and loss of chromosome 10 occur more frequently in LMM compared to other melanoma subtypes.

Clinical Presentation & Classification

LM (in situ) presents as an ill-defined, flat, hypo- to hyperpigmented macule or patch on sun-exposed skin, typically the face or ears. The lesion gradually enlarges over years, with centrifugal growth pattern. Early lesions may be subtle—appearing as slight darkening of sun-damaged skin with indistinct borders. The color is often variegated, ranging from tan to dark brown to black, with areas of regression appearing as white or hypopigmented zones. Unlike superficial spreading melanoma, LM rarely achieves >6mm in early years and may achieve several centimeters diameter before the patient seeks consultation. The presence of confetti-like pigmentation (punctate dark spots within lighter areas) is characteristic. Transition to invasive LMM is heralded by nodule formation, surface elevation, darkening of a portion of the lesion, or development of ulceration—though some invasive LMM remains relatively flat.

Diagnosis & Staging

Clinical diagnosis requires high suspicion in elderly patients with large, ill-defined, hyperpigmented patches on face or neck. Dermoscopy reveals asymmetric pigmentation distribution, granular pattern, with irregular streaks and dots at the periphery extending into surrounding normal-appearing skin. Diagnosis is confirmed by biopsy, preferably excisional biopsy with adequate margins, as punch or shave biopsies may miss invasive dermal component. Histopathologic assessment determines critical prognostic parameters: Breslow depth (if invasive), Clark level, and presence/absence of ulceration. In situ lentigo maligna (Clark level I) has an excellent prognosis with complete surgical excision, while invasive LMM with Clark level IV-V or Breslow >4mm represents Stage IIC disease with significant recurrence risk. Sentinel lymph node biopsy is indicated for invasive LMM with Breslow depth ≥0.8mm to detect occult regional metastases.

Treatment Algorithm

For lentigo maligna (in situ), the goal is complete eradication of melanocytes. Surgical excision with histologically confirmed clear margins remains the gold standard. Standard excision margins of 0.5-1.0cm are adequate for in situ disease, but larger margins (1-1.5cm) may be needed given often-extensive subclinical disease. Mohs micrographic surgery has gained popularity for LM on face/ears because it allows for paraffin-fixed sections and real-time margin assessment, potentially reducing recurrence rates and minimizing tissue loss. Cryotherapy, topical imiquimod (5%), or photodynamic therapy (PDT) may be considered for selected patients unwilling or unable to undergo surgery, though these modalities have higher recurrence rates (15-30% at 5 years) compared to excisional surgery (5-10%). For invasive LMM with Breslow depth ≤1.0mm, wide local excision with 1.0cm margins and consideration of SLN biopsy is standard. For Breslow >1.0mm, margins increase to 1-2cm. SLN-positive invasive LMM should be managed with completion lymph node dissection and consideration of adjuvant immunotherapy. For metastatic LMM, treatment parallels other melanoma subtypes: ipilimumab 3mg/kg IV every 3 weeks for 4 doses (CheckMate 069 trial demonstrated improved RFS), or pembrolizumab 200mg IV every 3 weeks (up to 1 year). For BRAF-mutant metastatic LMM, dabrafenib 100mg twice daily plus trametinib 2mg daily is an alternative, achieving response rates of 64-70%.

Prognosis & Survival

In situ lentigo maligna (LM) has an excellent prognosis when completely excised, with 5-year melanoma-specific survival >95% and 10-year survival >90%. Recurrence rates for in situ LM treated with surgical excision are 5-15% depending on margin adequacy and follow-up duration. Invasive LMM (Stage IA-IIB) has 5-year survival rates of 85-95% for thin lesions (Breslow ≤1mm), 70-85% for intermediate thickness (1.01-4mm), and 40-60% for thick lesions (>4mm). Unfavorable prognostic factors include Breslow depth >2mm, Clark level IV-V, presence of ulceration, mitotic rate ≥1/mm², and advanced age. SLN positivity reduces 5-year survival by 25-35% depending on tumor burden. Distant metastatic disease has median survival of 12-18 months with immunotherapy.

When to See a Dermatologist

Any enlarging pigmented lesion on sun-exposed skin in patients ≥50 years warrants dermatologic evaluation. Particular attention should be paid to ill-defined, large hyperpigmented patches on face, ears, temple, or anterior neck. Total-body skin examination is indicated to assess for other field-effect changes including actinic keratoses, basal cell carcinomas, and squamous cell carcinomas. Patients with history of lentigo maligna should undergo surveillance every 3-6 months due to risk of recurrence at treatment site (5-15%) and development of new primary melanomas (lifetime risk 3-5%). Patients with multiple solar lentigines and actinic keratoses should have annual dermatology evaluation to detect LM in early stages before invasive transformation.

Frequently Asked Questions

If my lentigo maligna is still in situ (Clark level I), can I avoid surgery?

While topical treatments like imiquimod 5% cream or photodynamic therapy may eliminate some lentigo maligna without surgery, they carry higher recurrence rates (15-30% vs. 5-10% with excision). Surgical excision with confirmed clear margins remains the gold standard for maximizing cure rates. Mohs micrographic surgery is particularly useful on the face because it allows tissue-sparing while ensuring complete margin assessment. Your dermatologist can discuss your specific situation and preferences.

How much tissue will my surgeon remove if I have lentigo maligna on my face?

Surgical margins for in situ lentigo maligna are typically 0.5-1.0cm beyond visible disease, though 1.5cm margins may be used if clinically appropriate. Mohs surgery often requires narrower margins with superior margin assessment. The final defect size depends on the original lesion size, location, and aesthetic/functional considerations. Many defects can be closed with careful planning or allowed to heal secondarily. Your surgeon will discuss the specific plan based on your anatomy.

I'm 75 years old with invasive lentigo maligna melanoma. Do I need chemotherapy?

Standard treatment for invasive LMM depends on stage and depth. If your tumor is ≤4mm Breslow depth without SLN involvement (Stage I-IIA), surgery alone is typically adequate with excellent outcomes (80-90% 10-year survival). If deeper or with SLN involvement (Stage III), adjuvant immunotherapy with pembrolizumab or ipilimumab is recommended to improve recurrence-free survival. Traditional chemotherapy is rarely used for melanoma; modern immunotherapy and targeted therapy are preferred. Age alone is not a contraindication to treatment—fitness for intervention is assessed individually.

Can lentigo maligna recur after successful treatment?

Yes, local recurrence rates are 5-15% for surgically excised lentigo maligna, depending on completeness of initial excision. Recurrence may occur months to years after treatment. This underscores the importance of: (1) ensuring clear histopathologic margins at initial excision, (2) considering Mohs surgery on high-risk sites like ears/periocular areas, and (3) long-term surveillance with regular dermatology evaluation every 3-6 months for the first 2-3 years, then annually. Patients also have 3-5% lifetime risk of developing new primary melanomas and should maintain sun protection and surveillance.
