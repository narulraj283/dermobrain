Mycosis Fungoides and Cutaneous Lymphoma

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Mycosis Fungoides and Cutaneous Lymphoma

Mycosis fungoides (MF) is the most common primary cutaneous T-cell lymphoma, representing approximately 50% of all primary cutaneous lymphomas. This slowly progressive malignancy arises from cutaneous T-cells and typically presents with skin manifestations before potential systemic involvement. Early-stage disease has excellent prognosis with skin-directed therapy, while advanced stages require systemic treatment.

Pathophysiology

Mycosis fungoides results from malignant transformation and clonal expansion of CD4+ helper T-cells that preferentially home to the skin. The name derives from historical misconception that the disease was fungal. Genetic and epigenetic alterations drive neoplastic transformation. Certain environmental factors and infections may play roles in pathogenesis.

Clinical Stages

MF progresses through three characteristic stages. Patch stage (T1) features poorly demarcated, flat lesions that may mimic dermatitis. Plaque stage (T2) shows infiltrated lesions with defined borders and potential erosion. Tumoral stage (T3) presents with nodular lesions with risk of ulceration. Erythroderma may develop with extensive skin involvement.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis requires clinicopathologic correlation. Histologic examination demonstrates intraepidermal infiltration by atypical lymphocytes with epidermotropism. Mycosis fungoides cells show characteristic "cerebriform" nuclear appearance on cytology. T-cell receptor (TCR) gene clonality, though not specific, supports diagnosis. Immunophenotyping shows CD4+ CD7- T-cells.

Staging System

Staging incorporates skin (T), lymph node (N), visceral involvement (M), and blood involvement (B). Early MF (stage IA-IIA) has excellent prognosis with median survival exceeding 20 years. Intermediate stages (IIB-IIIA) show median survival of 10-15 years. Advanced stages (IIIB-IV) carry worse prognosis with median survival of 2-5 years.

Early-Stage Disease

Limited patch or plaque stage disease is treated with skin-directed therapies including topical corticosteroids, phototherapy (PUVA or narrowband UVB), topical retinoids, or topical nitrogen mustard. These approaches achieve excellent disease control with minimal systemic toxicity. Many patients achieve long survival with skin-directed therapy alone.

Advanced-Stage Disease

Systemic involvement requires systemic therapy including cytotoxic chemotherapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy, or biological agents. Options include gemcitabine, methotrexate, denileukin diftitox, and romidepsin. Newer agents targeting histone deacetylases and other pathways show promise. Treatment is individualized based on disease extent and patient factors.

Sézary Syndrome

Sézary syndrome represents a leukemic variant of MF with circulating malignant T-cells (≥1000/μL) and erythroderma. This aggressive form carries worse prognosis than conventional MF. Distinction from erythrodermic MF is important for prognostic and therapeutic purposes. Treatment typically involves systemic therapy.

Prognosis and Follow-up

Prognosis correlates strongly with stage at diagnosis. Early-stage disease detected through awareness of warning signs carries excellent prognosis. Regular dermatologic surveillance is essential for monitoring disease progression and detecting transformation. Patients benefit from comprehensive skin examination and assessment for systemic involvement.
