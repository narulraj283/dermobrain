Skin Self-Examination Guide

Regular skin self-examination is a crucial strategy for early detection of skin cancer. Monthly self-examination combined with annual professional skin check significantly improves early detection of both melanoma and non-melanoma skin cancers. This guide provides practical steps for performing comprehensive skin self-examination.

When to Examine Your Skin

Perform skin self-examination once monthly, ideally on the same day each month to establish a routine. Many people choose the first or last day of the month. Choose a time when you are not rushed and can examine your entire body systematically. Early morning or evening in natural light often provides optimal visualization.

Preparation and Environment

Conduct self-examination in a well-lit room, preferably with natural sunlight. Use mirrors to examine hard-to-see areas. A hand mirror aids in viewing back of neck, scalp, and other posterior locations. Have a body map or photograph for comparison to detect changes over time. Ensure privacy and adequate time (15-20 minutes) for thorough examination.

Systematic Examination Steps

Begin with face, ears, and scalp. Use a comb or blow dryer to part hair and examine scalp thoroughly. Examine ears front and back, including the area behind ears. Examine neck, paying special attention to front, sides, and back. Examine shoulders and upper back using mirrors.

Torso and Extremities

Examine anterior chest, abdomen, and groin carefully. Lift arms and examine axillae (underarm areas). Use mirrors to examine back and buttocks. Examine arms, forearms, and hands thoroughly, including palms, sides of hands, and between fingers. Examine legs, examining anterior, posterior, medial, and lateral surfaces.

Feet and Nails

Carefully examine both feet, including soles and between toes. Examine toenails for any pigmentation, particularly longitudinal brown or black bands. Check for any changes in existing moles on feet. Document any concerning findings. Some melanomas develop on non-exposed areas and are easily missed without careful examination.

What to Look For

Use the ABCDE rule to identify concerning lesions. Look for asymmetric lesions, irregular borders, color variation, diameter >6mm, or evolution/change. Note the "ugly duckling sign"—any lesion that looks different from your other moles. Document any new lesions or changes in existing lesions.

Recording Findings

Maintain a skin map documenting locations and descriptions of moles. Take photographs of concerning lesions using consistent lighting and angles. Photograph areas with multiple moles for comparison over time. Document dates of self-examination and any changes observed.

When to Seek Professional Evaluation

Any lesion meeting ABCDE criteria should be evaluated by a dermatologist. Any new lesion in adults warrants evaluation. Lesions showing change over time require professional assessment. Bleeding, itching, or tenderness in a mole is concerning. When in doubt, consult a dermatologist—professional evaluation is always appropriate.

Combining Self-Examination with Professional Screening

Monthly self-examination should complement annual or more frequent professional skin checks, depending on risk factors. High-risk individuals should see a dermatologist every 3-6 months. Fair-skinned individuals or those with personal or family history of skin cancer should have at least annual professional evaluation.

Special Populations

Individuals with numerous moles, atypical moles, or family history of melanoma require more frequent examination. Those with previous skin cancers require more intensive surveillance. Immunosuppressed individuals should have professional evaluation more frequently. Elderly individuals with increasing sun damage warrant increased vigilance.
