Squamous Cell Carcinoma of the Skin

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Squamous Cell Carcinoma of the Skin

Cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) is the second most common skin cancer, arising from malignant transformation of keratinocytes in the epidermis. Unlike basal cell carcinoma, SCC has significant metastatic potential, particularly with certain high-risk features. Early detection and appropriate treatment are essential to prevent morbidity and mortality.

Epidemiology

SCC incidence has increased substantially, with over 700,000 cases diagnosed annually in the United States. The disease predominantly affects older individuals with history of chronic sun exposure. Fair-skinned individuals and those with occupational UV exposure face elevated risk. Immunosuppressed patients, particularly organ transplant recipients, develop SCC at dramatically increased rates.

Risk Factors and Pathogenesis

Cumulative UVB exposure is the primary risk factor for non-melanoma SCC. Actinic keratosis represents the precursor lesion. Immunosuppression profoundly increases risk, with transplant recipients developing SCC at rates 65-250 times higher than general population. Chronic wounds, scars, and areas of prior radiation exposure predispose to SCC development. Infection with human papillomavirus (HPV), particularly in anogenital regions, increases risk.

Clinical Features

SCC typically presents as a firm, erythematous, scaly nodule or plaque on sun-exposed skin. The lesion may demonstrate hyperkeratosis, crusting, or central ulceration. Rapid growth over weeks to months suggests more aggressive behavior. Bleeding, tenderness, or drainage may accompany the lesion. Some SCCs arise from visibly abnormal precursor lesions (actinic keratosis), while others develop de novo.

SCC in Situ (Bowen's Disease)

Bowen's disease represents SCC in situ, confined to the epidermis without dermal invasion. It presents as a well-demarcated, erythematous, scaly patch or plaque, often with a hyperkeratotic surface. Bowen's disease may progress to invasive SCC but generally has excellent prognosis with appropriate treatment.

Invasive Squamous Cell Carcinoma

Invasive SCC demonstrates breaching of the basement membrane with extension into dermis and potentially deeper structures. Clark level classification describes invasion depth relative to skin structures. Depth of invasion, differentiation grade, and other features guide staging and prognostic assessment.

Histologic Grading

SCC is graded as well, moderately, or poorly differentiated based on degree of keratinization and nuclear atypia. Well-differentiated tumors show good keratinization and lower mitotic activity. Poorly differentiated tumors demonstrate minimal keratinization and high mitotic rate. Grade influences prognosis, with poorly differentiated tumors carrying higher metastatic risk.

High-Risk Features

Certain characteristics increase risk of recurrence and metastasis. These include depth >4mm, poor differentiation grade, location on the ear or lip, size >2cm, and rapid growth. Immunosuppression significantly increases aggressive behavior and metastatic potential. Perineural invasion indicates spread along nerve sheaths and is associated with increased recurrence.

Diagnosis and Staging

Diagnosis requires histopathologic examination. Tumor thickness, grade, perineural invasion, and invasion pattern are documented. Sentinel lymph node biopsy may be indicated for high-risk tumors. Imaging is reserved for advanced disease or when metastatic risk is significant.

Treatment Options

Surgical excision with appropriate margins remains primary treatment. Mohs micrographic surgery is preferred for high-risk lesions, recurrent tumors, or anatomically sensitive locations. Radiation therapy may be utilized for inoperable disease or adjuvant treatment of high-risk features. Chemotherapy is reserved for advanced or metastatic disease.

Prognosis

Five-year local recurrence rates range from 3-5% with standard excision and <1% with Mohs surgery. Metastatic risk is generally 1-5% for low-risk tumors but may exceed 10-30% for high-risk disease. Regional lymph node involvement significantly worsens prognosis. Close surveillance is recommended, particularly for high-risk tumors.
