Dysplastic Nevi: Atypical Moles

Dysplastic nevi (atypical moles) are melanocytic nevi with clinical and/or histologic atypia suggesting increased malignant potential. While not malignant themselves, dysplastic nevi represent important melanoma precursors and indicators of increased melanoma risk. Patients with dysplastic nevi require enhanced surveillance and sun protection counseling.

Clinical Features

Dysplastic nevi are typically larger than common nevi (>6mm), have irregular borders, and show variegated coloration with combinations of tan, brown, and red. The surface may be flat or raised. Dysplastic nevi often have indistinct margins fading into surrounding skin. They may develop on sun-exposed and non-exposed areas. The number of dysplastic nevi varies; some individuals have dozens.

Dysplastic Nevi Syndrome

Familial dysplastic nevi syndrome (also termed familial atypical mole and melanoma syndrome or FAMMM) involves presence of numerous dysplastic nevi (often >100) with family history of melanoma. Individuals with FAMMM have markedly elevated lifetime melanoma risk, estimated at 50-80% by age 80. Some families carry CDKN2A mutations predisposing to melanoma.

Histopathologic Features

Dysplastic nevi demonstrate cytologic atypia of melanocytes with nuclear enlargement, irregular chromatin, and prominent nucleoli. Architectural atypia includes irregular nesting and poor circumscription. Junctional activity is present with possible lentiginous growth pattern. Fibroplasia in underlying dermis is characteristic. Mitotic figures may be present.

Malignant Potential

Individual dysplastic nevi have low direct malignant transformation risk. However, they serve as markers of increased melanoma risk. Patients with dysplastic nevi have 10-fold increased melanoma risk. Those with numerous dysplastic nevi and family history of melanoma may have up to 50-fold increased risk.

Risk Stratification

Personal history of melanoma combined with dysplastic nevi indicates high-risk status. Presence of >5 dysplastic nevi increases risk. Fair skin, blue eyes, and poor tanning ability are additive risk factors. Family history of melanoma in first-degree relatives further elevates risk and may suggest genetic predisposition.

Surveillance Strategy

Patients with dysplastic nevi require regular dermatologic examination, ideally every 6-12 months. Total body photography documents baseline and facilitates detection of new or changing lesions. Patients should perform monthly skin self-examination. Any changing lesion warrants evaluation and possible biopsy.

Biopsy Decision-Making

Not every dysplastic nevus requires biopsy. Lesions demonstrating significant change, concerning clinical features (asymmetry, irregular borders, variegated color), or the "ugly duckling sign" warrant evaluation. Biopsies establish diagnosis and help refine risk stratification. Serial photography aids in identifying changing lesions.

Treatment Approach

Dysplastic nevi do not routinely require treatment solely based on histology. Lesions causing cosmetic concern or showing clinical change may be removed. Excision should be complete with adequate margins. Lesions demonstrating melanoma on pathology require wider re-excision and possible sentinel lymph node biopsy.

Prevention and Education

Sun protection is paramount, as UV exposure appears to drive progression. Broad-spectrum sunscreen, protective clothing, and avoidance of peak UV hours are recommended. Regular self-examination and professional screening are essential. Genetic counseling may be beneficial for families with multiple affected members.
