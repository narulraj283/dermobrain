Melanoma: Comprehensive Overview

Melanoma is the most serious type of skin cancer, arising from melanocytes—the pigment-producing cells in the epidermis. Although melanoma accounts for only 1-2% of all skin cancers, it is responsible for the majority of skin cancer deaths. Early detection and treatment significantly improve survival rates, making awareness and regular screening essential.

What is Melanoma?

Melanoma develops when melanocytes undergo malignant transformation, typically in response to ultraviolet (UV) radiation exposure. Unlike basal cell and squamous cell carcinomas, which grow slowly and rarely metastasize, melanoma has a higher propensity for rapid growth and distant spread. The disease can develop in existing moles or appear as new pigmented lesions on previously clear skin.

Risk Factors and Causes

Several factors increase melanoma risk. Intermittent, intense UV exposure—particularly sunburns during childhood—poses the greatest risk. Fair skin, blue eyes, and red or blonde hair indicate reduced melanin protection. A personal or family history of melanoma significantly elevates risk. Atypical moles, defined as irregular nevi larger than 6mm with irregular borders and color variation, are considered precancerous lesions. Immunosuppression from organ transplantation or certain medications also increases susceptibility.

Clinical Presentation

Melanomas typically present as evolving pigmented lesions. Patients often report changes in size, color, or shape. Some melanomas are amelanotic (non-pigmented), making them more challenging to identify. Associated symptoms may include bleeding, itching, or tenderness. The horizontal growth phase (radial growth phase) may precede vertical growth phase, during which invasion into deeper dermis and potential metastasis become possible.

Diagnosis and Staging

Diagnosis requires histopathologic examination via skin biopsy. Dermatologists assess Breslow thickness (depth of invasion in millimeters), ulceration, mitotic rate, and other prognostic factors. Clark level classifies invasion relative to skin structures. Sentinel lymph node biopsy identifies early regional metastasis for tumors thicker than 1mm or with other high-risk features. Imaging studies including CT, PET, and MRI are utilized for staging advanced disease.

Treatment Options

Surgical excision with appropriate margins is the primary treatment. Wide local excision with 0.5-2cm margins depending on thickness removes the tumor and surrounding normal skin. Sentinel lymph node biopsy guides further management. For advanced melanoma, immunotherapy agents including checkpoint inhibitors and targeted therapy for BRAF mutations demonstrate significant efficacy. Radiation therapy may be used for brain metastases or unresectable regional disease.

Prognosis and Survival

Prognosis depends on multiple factors, primarily Breslow thickness. Five-year survival for localized melanoma exceeds 90%, while stage IV disease carries a 5-year survival of 20-30%. Mitotic rate, ulceration, and sentinel lymph node status provide additional prognostic information. Regular surveillance including clinical examination and imaging is essential for detecting recurrence early.
