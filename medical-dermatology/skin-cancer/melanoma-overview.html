Melanoma: What It Looks Like, How It's Caught, and How It's Treated

The Bottom Line

Melanoma is the most serious type of skin cancer, but when caught early, it's highly curable — the 5-year survival rate for stage I melanoma is over 99%. The key is catching it before it grows deeper into the skin.

Use the ABCDE rule to check your moles: Asymmetry, irregular Borders, multiple Colors, Diameter larger than a pencil eraser, and Evolving (changing in size, shape, or color). Any mole that looks different from your others — the "ugly duckling" — deserves attention.

When to see a dermatologist: If you notice a new or changing mole, a spot that doesn't heal, or a mole that looks different from the rest — don't wait. Early detection saves lives. Annual skin checks are recommended for everyone, and more frequently if you have many moles, fair skin, or a family history of melanoma.

What Is Melanoma?

Melanoma is a cancer that develops in melanocytes — the cells responsible for producing melanin, the pigment that gives skin its color. Unlike more common skin cancers (basal cell and squamous cell carcinoma), melanoma has a greater tendency to grow deep into the skin and spread to other parts of the body if not caught early.

The good news: melanoma represents only about 1% of all skin cancers, and survival rates have improved dramatically over the past two decades thanks to earlier detection and revolutionary new treatments. Approximately 100,000 new cases are diagnosed annually in the United States.

The most common type — superficial spreading melanoma — accounts for about 70% of cases. It typically grows outward across the skin surface before growing downward, which is why flat, irregularly shaped pigmented spots that are changing deserve prompt evaluation.

Risk Factors

Understanding your risk helps you know how vigilant to be about skin checks:

UV exposure: Cumulative sun exposure and especially blistering sunburns (particularly before age 20) significantly increase melanoma risk. Indoor tanning beds increase risk by 75% when first used before age 35 — this is why dermatologists strongly advocate against tanning bed use.

Skin type: Fair-skinned individuals who burn easily, those with blond or red hair, light eyes, and freckles are at higher risk. However, melanoma can occur in all skin types and ethnicities — it's sometimes diagnosed later in people with darker skin because it's less expected.

Moles: Having more than 50 common moles or any atypical (dysplastic) moles increases risk. People with atypical mole syndrome — many irregular moles plus a family history of melanoma — have the highest risk.

Family history: About 10% of melanomas occur in people with a family history of the disease. If a first-degree relative (parent, sibling, child) has had melanoma, your risk is 2-3 times higher.

Immune status: People with weakened immune systems (organ transplant recipients, those on immunosuppressive medications) face increased melanoma risk.

How to Check Your Skin: The ABCDE Rule

Dermatologists recommend monthly self-examinations using the ABCDE criteria to evaluate moles and spots:

A — Asymmetry: If you draw a line through the middle of the mole, the two halves don't match. Normal moles are usually symmetrical.

B — Border: The edges are irregular, scalloped, notched, or blurred rather than smooth and well-defined.

C — Color: The mole has multiple colors — combinations of brown, tan, black, red, white, or blue within the same lesion. Normal moles are usually a single shade of brown.

D — Diameter: The spot is larger than 6mm (about the size of a pencil eraser). However, melanomas can be smaller when first detected, so size alone isn't definitive.

E — Evolving: The mole is changing — in size, shape, color, elevation, or symptoms (itching, bleeding, crusting). This is arguably the most important criterion. Any mole that is noticeably different from how it looked a few months ago should be evaluated.

The "ugly duckling" sign: A mole that looks distinctly different from all your other moles — it's the outlier. Most of a person's moles tend to resemble each other. The one that doesn't fit the pattern is the one to watch.

Diagnosis

If your dermatologist identifies a suspicious spot, the next step is a biopsy — a quick procedure where all or part of the lesion is removed and examined under a microscope by a dermatopathologist. This is the only definitive way to diagnose melanoma.

If the biopsy confirms melanoma, the pathology report will include critical information:

Breslow thickness: The depth of the melanoma measured in millimeters. This is the single most important factor in determining prognosis and treatment. A melanoma less than 1mm deep has an excellent prognosis. One deeper than 4mm is more concerning.

Ulceration: Whether the surface of the melanoma has broken down. Ulceration indicates a more aggressive tumor and affects staging.

Mitotic rate: How rapidly the cancer cells are dividing — higher rates suggest more aggressive behavior.

Margins: Whether the melanoma was completely removed. If cancer cells extend to the edge of the biopsy specimen, further surgery is needed.

Staging and What It Means

Melanoma staging helps determine treatment and predicts outcomes:

Stage 0 (melanoma in situ): The melanoma is confined to the top layer of skin (epidermis). Cure rate is essentially 100% with surgical removal.

Stage I: The melanoma is up to 2mm thick without ulceration, or up to 1mm thick with ulceration. Five-year survival rate exceeds 99% for stage IA and is about 97% for stage IB. Treatment is surgical excision with appropriate margins.

Stage II: Thicker melanomas (greater than 1-2mm depending on ulceration) that haven't spread to lymph nodes. Five-year survival ranges from 82-94% depending on thickness and ulceration. Sentinel lymph node biopsy may be recommended to check for microscopic spread.

Stage III: Melanoma has spread to nearby lymph nodes or developed satellite/in-transit lesions. Treatment typically includes surgery plus adjuvant (additional) therapy — usually immunotherapy. Five-year survival ranges from 40-78% depending on the extent of lymph node involvement.

Stage IV: Melanoma has spread to distant organs (lungs, liver, brain, bone). While this was once considered untreatable, modern immunotherapy and targeted therapy have dramatically improved outcomes. Some patients with stage IV melanoma now achieve long-term remissions lasting years.

Treatment

Surgery: The primary treatment for all early-stage melanomas. The melanoma is removed along with a margin of normal-appearing skin. Margin size depends on thickness: 0.5cm margins for melanoma in situ, 1cm for melanomas up to 1mm thick, and 1-2cm for thicker lesions. Most excisions are done in the office under local anesthesia.

Sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB): For melanomas thicker than 0.8mm, your surgeon may recommend testing the first lymph node that drains the tumor site. A radioactive tracer and/or blue dye identifies the sentinel node, which is removed and examined. If it's cancer-free, no further lymph node surgery is needed. If melanoma is found, additional treatment is discussed.

Immunotherapy: These drugs harness your own immune system to fight melanoma. Checkpoint inhibitors — pembrolizumab (Keytruda) and nivolumab (Opdivo) — have transformed melanoma treatment. They work by releasing the "brakes" on immune cells so they can recognize and attack cancer cells. In advanced melanoma, these drugs produce lasting responses in 40-50% of patients. The combination of nivolumab plus ipilimumab (Yervoy) produces even higher response rates (about 58%) but with more side effects. Immunotherapy is now also used after surgery for stage III melanoma to reduce recurrence risk.

Targeted therapy: About 50% of melanomas carry a mutation in the BRAF gene. For these patients, targeted drugs — dabrafenib (Tafinlar) plus trametinib (Mekinist), or encorafenib (Braftovi) plus binimetinib (Mektovi) — block the specific molecular pathway driving cancer growth. Response rates are high (about 70%), though resistance can develop over time, which is why immunotherapy is often preferred as first-line treatment for advanced disease.

Radiation therapy: Not typically a primary treatment for melanoma but may be used after surgery for high-risk lesions, for brain metastases, or for patients who cannot undergo surgery.

Prevention

Melanoma is one of the most preventable cancers. Evidence-based strategies include:

Sun protection: Use broad-spectrum SPF 30+ sunscreen daily on exposed skin, reapplying every 2 hours during outdoor activity. Wear sun-protective clothing, wide-brimmed hats, and UV-blocking sunglasses. Seek shade during peak UV hours (10am-4pm).

Avoid tanning beds: There is no safe way to tan artificially. The World Health Organization classifies tanning beds as a Group 1 carcinogen — the same category as tobacco and asbestos.

Regular skin checks: Monthly self-examinations at home plus annual full-body skin exams by a dermatologist. People at higher risk should be examined more frequently. Take photos of moles to track changes over time — smartphone apps can help with this.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can melanoma appear on skin that never sees the sun?

Yes. While UV exposure is the biggest risk factor, melanoma can develop anywhere — including the soles of the feet, palms, under fingernails or toenails, and inside the mouth or eyes. These "hidden" melanomas are more common in people with darker skin and can be more dangerous because they're often caught later.

I had a mole removed that was "atypical" but not melanoma. Am I at higher risk?

Having atypical (dysplastic) moles does increase your overall melanoma risk, even though the individual atypical mole is not cancer. Your dermatologist may recommend more frequent skin checks — typically every 6-12 months — and careful self-monitoring. It's not a cause for alarm, but it is a reason to be diligent.

How fast does melanoma grow?

It varies significantly. Superficial spreading melanoma (the most common type) often grows slowly over months to years before growing deeper. Nodular melanoma, however, can grow rapidly — over weeks to months — and is more likely to be thick at diagnosis. This is why any new, rapidly changing, or elevated dark spot should be evaluated promptly.

Has melanoma treatment improved in recent years?

Dramatically. Before 2011, advanced melanoma had very limited treatment options and a median survival of about 6-9 months. The introduction of immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors) and targeted therapy has transformed outcomes — many patients with advanced melanoma now survive for years, and some achieve complete, durable remissions. This is one of the great success stories of modern oncology.
