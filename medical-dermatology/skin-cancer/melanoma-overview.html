Melanoma: The Complete Guide to Detection, Staging, and Treatment

Melanoma is the deadliest form of skin cancer, responsible for the vast majority of skin cancer deaths despite accounting for only about 1% of skin cancer diagnoses. In 2025, an estimated 104,960 Americans will be diagnosed with invasive melanoma and approximately 8,430 will die from it, according to the Melanoma Research Alliance. The good news: when caught early, the five-year survival rate exceeds 99%. When caught late, it drops to 35%. That gap — between near-certain survival and a one-in-three chance — is why understanding melanoma matters more than almost any other topic in dermatology.

What Exactly Is Melanoma?

Melanoma develops when melanocytes — the pigment-producing cells that give your skin its color — undergo malignant transformation and begin dividing uncontrollably. Unlike basal cell and squamous cell carcinomas, which tend to grow slowly and rarely spread, melanoma can metastasize to lymph nodes, lungs, liver, brain, and bone relatively early in its course. This aggressive potential is what makes it so dangerous.

There are four major subtypes, each with distinct clinical behavior:

  • Superficial spreading melanoma (70% of cases): The most common type. Grows outward along the skin surface before invading deeper. Usually appears as a flat or slightly raised lesion with irregular borders and varied colors.
  • Nodular melanoma (15–20%): The most aggressive subtype. Grows vertically (downward into the skin) from the start, which is why it often presents as a raised, dome-shaped nodule that may be uniformly dark or even amelanotic (flesh-colored). Often diagnosed at a later stage.
  • Lentigo maligna melanoma (5–10%): Typically develops on chronically sun-damaged skin in older adults, especially the face. Starts as a slow-growing flat patch (lentigo maligna) that may exist for years before becoming invasive.
  • Acral lentiginous melanoma (2–3% overall, but the most common subtype in Black and Asian patients): Occurs on the palms, soles, and under the nails. Not related to sun exposure. This is the type that killed Bob Marley — it started under his toenail.

The ABCDE Rule: How to Spot Melanoma Early

Dermatologists use the ABCDE criteria as the primary screening framework. If a mole or pigmented lesion exhibits any of these features, it warrants urgent evaluation:

  • A — Asymmetry: Draw an imaginary line through the middle. If the two halves don't match, that's a warning sign. Most benign moles are roughly symmetrical.
  • B — Border irregularity: Melanomas tend to have uneven, scalloped, or notched borders, as if the pigment is "leaking" into surrounding skin. Benign moles have smooth, well-defined edges.
  • C — Color variation: Multiple colors within a single lesion — brown, black, tan, red, white, or blue — suggest melanoma. Benign moles are typically one uniform shade of brown.
  • D — Diameter greater than 6mm: Lesions larger than the size of a pencil eraser (6mm or about ¼ inch) are more suspicious, though melanomas can be smaller at initial diagnosis.
  • E — Evolving: Any mole that is changing in size, shape, color, or texture, or that develops new symptoms like itching, bleeding, or crusting. This is arguably the most important criterion — the "ugly duckling" that looks different from your other moles or is changing when others aren't.

Important caveat: Some melanomas don't follow these rules. Nodular melanomas may be symmetrical, uniform in color, and smaller than 6mm. Amelanotic melanomas may be pink or flesh-colored. When in doubt, the "ugly duckling sign" — any mole that looks different from all your other moles — is a powerful additional screening tool.

Who Gets Melanoma? Risk Factors Backed by Data

The lifetime risk of developing melanoma varies dramatically by ethnicity: approximately 1 in 33 for White individuals, 1 in 200 for Hispanic individuals, and 1 in 1,000 for Black individuals (American Cancer Society). However, melanoma in patients with darker skin is often diagnosed at later stages, resulting in significantly worse outcomes — the five-year survival rate is 94% among White patients compared to only 71% among Black patients.

Key risk factors supported by epidemiological data include:

  • UV exposure history: A history of five or more blistering sunburns between ages 15 and 20 increases melanoma risk by 80% (AAD). Both natural UV and tanning bed use are implicated — indoor tanning increases melanoma risk by 75% in people who begin before age 35.
  • Mole count: Having more than 50 common moles or any atypical (dysplastic) nevi significantly increases risk. Individuals with more than 100 moles have approximately 12 times the melanoma risk of someone with fewer than 15.
  • Family and personal history: About 10% of melanoma patients have a first-degree relative with the disease. Germline mutations in CDKN2A (the most common high-penetrance melanoma gene), CDK4, BAP1, and MC1R variants all confer elevated risk.
  • Fair skin, light eyes, red or blond hair: Fitzpatrick skin types I and II (burns easily, rarely tans) carry the highest risk.
  • Immunosuppression: Organ transplant recipients have a 2–5 fold increased risk of melanoma. Chronic immunosuppressive therapy of any kind elevates risk.
  • Age and sex: Incidence rates in adults 50 and older continue to rise by approximately 3% per year in women. Men have historically had higher incidence rates, but the gap is narrowing.

Diagnosis and Staging: What Happens After a Suspicious Mole Is Found

If a lesion is suspicious on clinical or dermoscopic examination, the standard is an excisional biopsy with 1–3mm margins that includes the full depth of the skin. Shave biopsies should be avoided for suspected melanoma because they may transect the lesion, making accurate Breslow depth measurement impossible.

Breslow depth — measured in millimeters from the granular layer of the epidermis to the deepest point of tumor invasion — is the single most important prognostic factor. Tumors less than 1mm thick have an excellent prognosis; those greater than 4mm carry substantially higher risk of metastasis.

Staging follows the AJCC 8th Edition classification:

  • Stage 0 (in situ): Melanoma confined to the epidermis. Treated with surgical excision. Essentially 100% curable.
  • Stage I: Tumor ≤2mm thick, with or without ulceration, no lymph node involvement. Five-year survival: 97–99%.
  • Stage II: Tumor >2mm thick OR any thickness with ulceration, no lymph node spread. Five-year survival: 75–88%. Sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB) is recommended for tumors >0.8mm with ulceration or >1.0mm without ulceration.
  • Stage III: Melanoma has spread to regional lymph nodes or developed satellite/in-transit metastases. Five-year survival varies widely: 24–88% depending on the number of nodes involved and whether they were clinically detected or microscopic. Adjuvant immunotherapy is standard of care.
  • Stage IV: Distant metastases present (lungs, liver, brain, bone, or distant skin). Five-year survival has improved dramatically — from 15% in the mid-2000s to approximately 35% today — largely due to immunotherapy breakthroughs.

At diagnosis, 77% of melanomas are localized (Stage I/II), 9.5% are regional (Stage III), and 4.7% are distant (Stage IV), according to SEER data.

Treatment: The Immunotherapy Revolution

The management of melanoma has been transformed over the past decade by two classes of drugs: immune checkpoint inhibitors and targeted therapies.

Early-Stage Melanoma (Stage 0–II)

Treatment is primarily surgical. Wide local excision with appropriate margins based on Breslow depth:

  • In situ melanoma: 0.5–1cm margins
  • ≤1mm thick: 1cm margins
  • 1.01–2mm: 1–2cm margins
  • >2mm: 2cm margins

For Stage IIB and IIC melanoma, adjuvant pembrolizumab (Keytruda) for one year is now approved and recommended based on the KEYNOTE-716 trial, which showed a 35% reduction in recurrence risk.

Stage III Melanoma

Neoadjuvant immunotherapy — treatment given before surgery — is increasingly preferred for resectable Stage III disease. The NADINA trial and SWOG S1801 trial showed that neoadjuvant pembrolizumab followed by surgery and adjuvant therapy significantly improved event-free survival compared to surgery followed by adjuvant therapy alone.

Adjuvant options after surgery include nivolumab (Opdivo) or pembrolizumab for one year. For patients with BRAF V600E/K mutations (present in approximately 40–50% of melanomas), the combination of dabrafenib (150mg twice daily) plus trametinib (2mg once daily) is an alternative adjuvant option.

Stage IV (Metastatic) Melanoma

First-line systemic therapy per NCCN guidelines:

  • Nivolumab + relatlimab (Opdualag): A newer combination targeting both PD-1 and LAG-3. FDA-approved in 2022.
  • Nivolumab + ipilimumab (Opdivo + Yervoy): Combined PD-1 and CTLA-4 blockade. Most effective immunotherapy combination, but with higher toxicity rates (Grade 3–4 adverse events in approximately 55% of patients).
  • Single-agent pembrolizumab or nivolumab: PD-1 monotherapy. Better tolerated, with response rates of 35–45%.
  • For BRAF-mutant melanoma: BRAF/MEK inhibitor combinations (dabrafenib + trametinib, or encorafenib + binimetinib) remain an option, particularly when rapid tumor response is needed.

The impact of these therapies has been staggering. Age-adjusted melanoma death rates have fallen an average of 2.8% per year since 2014, largely attributable to checkpoint immunotherapy. A disease that was nearly untreatable 15 years ago now has multiple effective systemic options.

Self-Examination: The Most Important Thing You Can Do

Here's a clinical pearl that every dermatologist knows: the majority of melanomas are discovered by patients or their partners, not by physicians during routine exams. Monthly skin self-examinations — using a mirror to check your back, scalp, soles of feet, and between toes — are the single most accessible early detection strategy.

When to see a dermatologist urgently:

  • Any new mole appearing after age 30 (new moles in adults are uncommon and warrant evaluation)
  • Any existing mole that changes in size, color, shape, or texture
  • Any pigmented lesion that itches, bleeds, or crusts
  • A dark streak under a fingernail or toenail not caused by trauma
  • Any sore that won't heal within 3 weeks

For individuals at elevated risk — fair skin, family history, more than 50 moles, history of blistering sunburns — annual full-body skin exams with a dermatologist are recommended by the AAD.

Prevention

The most modifiable risk factor is UV exposure. Evidence-based prevention strategies include:

  • Broad-spectrum SPF 30+ sunscreen applied daily, reapplied every 2 hours during sun exposure
  • Protective clothing (UPF-rated), wide-brimmed hats, and UV-blocking sunglasses
  • Avoiding tanning beds entirely — the WHO classifies them as a Group 1 carcinogen (same category as tobacco and asbestos)
  • Seeking shade between 10 AM and 4 PM when UV index is highest

The Bottom Line

Melanoma is a cancer where individual vigilance genuinely saves lives. The five-year survival gap between early and late detection — 99% versus 35% — is one of the largest in all of oncology. If you remember one thing from this article, let it be this: check your skin monthly, know the ABCDE criteria, and see a dermatologist promptly for anything that's changing. In the age of immunotherapy, even advanced melanoma is more treatable than ever — but catching it early remains the best strategy by far.
