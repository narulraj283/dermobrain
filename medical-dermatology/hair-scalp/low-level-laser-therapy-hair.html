Clinical Overview

Scalp biopsy is a diagnostic procedure in which a small tissue sample is removed from the scalp under local anesthesia and examined histologically to determine the cause of hair loss. This procedure is critical for diagnosis of scarring alopecias (which permanently destroy follicles) versus non-scarring alopecias (where follicles are preserved), and for distinguishing among specific disease entities when clinical diagnosis is uncertain. Scalp biopsy is essential for optimal treatment planning, as management differs substantially between scarring and non-scarring conditions, and between different types of scarring alopecias with different prognoses and treatment responses.

Indications for Scalp Biopsy

Scalp biopsy is indicated when: (1) clinical diagnosis of hair loss etiology is uncertain, (2) presence or absence of scarring must be determined (distinguishing reversible from permanent alopecia), (3) scarring alopecia type must be identified to guide treatment (lichen planopilaris vs. lupus vs. folliculitis decalvans carry different prognoses and treatment approaches), (4) diagnosis will change management significantly, (5) progressive hair loss unresponsive to treatment requires investigation for alternative diagnoses, and (6) atypical features suggest diagnoses other than common alopecias. Biopsy is not indicated for classic androgenetic alopecia or telogen effluvium with obvious clinical features and positive pull test, where diagnosis is established clinically.

Specimen Collection Techniques

Punch biopsy is the most common technique, using a 3-4 mm dermatologic punch to obtain a cylindrical tissue sample through the full thickness of dermis and subcutis. The specimen includes 8-10 complete hair follicles at varying phases of the hair cycle, optimizing histologic evaluation. Punch biopsy causes a small round defect that typically requires a single suture and heals with minimal scarring (most scars become imperceptible over months to years). The wound is managed similarly to any small surgical defect: primary closure with suture removal at 7-10 days.

Shave biopsy using a scalpel blade removes only the epidermis and superficial dermis, providing limited depth of tissue. This technique is inadequate for hair loss diagnosis as full-thickness evaluation including subcutaneous involvement is necessary to assess follicle destruction and fibrosis. Shave biopsy should not be used for suspected scarring alopecia.

Horizontal sectioning involves taking a 4 mm punch biopsy and serially sectioning it horizontally (parallel to the scalp surface) rather than vertically. This provides views of multiple follicles at the same vertical level, improving evaluation of the breadth of inflammation and follicle involvement. Horizontal sectioning is particularly useful for assessment of follicular phase distribution and to evaluate whether follicles are destroyed or preserved. Many dermatopathologists prefer horizontal sectioning for hair loss biopsies.

Specimen Handling and Staining

Specimens should be placed in 10% buffered formalin immediately upon collection. Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining is standard and allows visualization of follicular architecture, cellular infiltrates, and fibrosis. Special stains may be useful: periodic acid-Schiff (PAS) stain identifies fungal organisms if tinea capitis is suspected, and immunofluorescence staining (for IgG, IgM, IgA, C3, fibrinogen) detects immune deposits in lupus erythematosus (where hair follicles typically show positive deposition). Immunohistochemistry (staining for specific immune cell types such as CD4, CD8, CD20) can characterize the inflammatory infiltrate but is not routinely necessary.

Histopathologic Findings in Different Alopecias

Non-scarring alopecias show preserved follicular architecture with intact sebaceous glands and arrector pili muscles. Hair follicles may show inflammatory infiltrate but the follicular structures themselves are not destroyed. Specific findings vary: (1) androgenetic alopecia shows miniaturized follicles (small terminal follicles) with relative preservation of later follicular phases, (2) alopecia areata shows focal lymphocytic infiltration around the hair bulb (CD8+ predominant), characteristic "exclamation mark hairs" at follicle margins, and preserved bulge region, (3) telogen effluvium shows increased proportion of telogen hairs without specific inflammation.

Scarring alopecias show permanent follicular destruction with fibrosis replacing follicles. Sebaceous glands and arrector pili muscles are destroyed. Specific scarring alopecia types show characteristic patterns: (1) lichen planopilaris (lymphocytic scarring alopecia) shows dense perifollicular and interfollicular lymphocytic infiltrate with "lichenoid" interface changes, (2) lupus erythematosus shows interface dermatitis with hyaline body deposition at the dermal-epidermal junction and around follicles, with immunofluorescence showing IgG and C3 deposition, (3) folliculitis decalvans shows severe neutrophilic infiltration with abscess formation around follicles, (4) central centrifugal cicatricial alopecia shows perifollicular lymphocytic infiltrate with variable fibrosis, (5) frontal fibrosing alopecia shows similar pattern to lichen planopilaris with prominent fibrosis.

Timing and Location of Biopsy

Timing is critical for obtaining diagnostic specimens: biopsies should be taken from the advancing margin of hair loss where active disease is present, not from end-stage bald areas where follicles are completely destroyed and diagnostic features are lost. In scarring alopecias, the active inflammatory margin provides the best diagnostic yield. In inflammatory conditions, biopsying the margin maximizes chance of identifying characteristic inflammatory infiltrates.

Location should avoid: (1) previous biopsy sites, (2) areas of extensive scarring (end-stage disease), (3) the postauricular midline where scars are more visible, and (4) areas likely to be in prominent view. Optimal locations include areas at the advancing margin of disease, typically on the crown or temporal regions for most alopecias. The occipital scalp is often used for biopsy as scars in this region are typically not visible.

Complications and Outcomes

Complications from scalp biopsy are rare: (1) infection occurs in <1% of cases, managed with topical or oral antibiotics if necessary, (2) excessive bleeding is uncommon with proper hemostasis using electrocautery or topical hemostatic agents, (3) nerve injury causing persistent paresthesia is extremely rare, (4) permanent scarring occurs in <5% of patients; most biopsy scars become imperceptible over time. Patient counseling should include expectation of mild pain during local anesthesia administration and slight sensation during biopsy, followed by small suture line that heals over 1-2 weeks.

Interpreting Biopsy Results

Interpretation of scalp biopsy requires correlation of histologic findings with clinical presentation, immunofluorescence results, and special stains. A detailed pathology report should specify: (1) presence or absence of fibrosis and follicular destruction (determining scarring vs. non-scarring), (2) type and distribution of inflammatory infiltrate (characterizing disease entity), (3) follicular phase distribution (normal should show 80-85% anagen, 10-15% telogen), (4) presence of special features (exclamation mark hairs, fibrosis pattern, interface dermatitis), and (5) results of special stains and immunofluorescence if performed. Clinicians should discuss results with pathologists when interpretation is uncertain or when clinical presentation seems discordant with pathologic findings.

When Biopsy is Diagnostic and When It Is Not

Biopsy is highly diagnostic in: (1) scarring vs. non-scarring determination (biopsy definitively establishes presence or absence of follicular destruction), (2) lupus erythematosus (immunofluorescence showing immune deposits is highly specific), (3) specific scarring alopecia identification (lichen planopilaris vs. folliculitis decalvans vs. CCCA show characteristic patterns), (4) fungal infections (KOH preparation and fungal stains establish tinea capitis). Biopsy is less helpful for: (1) distinguishing androgenetic alopecia from telogen effluvium (both show non-scarring pattern; clinical history is more diagnostic), (2) determining disease activity in scarring alopecias (both active and inactive disease show fibrosis; biopsy is less helpful than clinical assessment for determining activity), (3) early-stage scarring alopecia before fibrosis is established (may appear non-scarring histologically despite being scarring clinically).

Alternative Diagnostic Modalities

Dermoscopy allows magnified examination of scalp (up to 70x magnification) with polarized light, revealing follicular and vascular patterns without tissue removal. Dermoscopy can identify specific findings (yellow dots in lichen planopilaris, white dots in central centrifugal cicatricial alopecia, exclamation mark hairs in alopecia areata) and suggests diagnosis without tissue invasion. However, dermoscopy cannot definitively distinguish scarring from non-scarring alopecia and cannot characterize histology.

Pull test (gentle pulling on 60 hairs to assess number shed) provides functional assessment of follicle strength and is valuable screening test for telogen effluvium (>6 hairs indicates telogen phase increase) but does not establish diagnosis or characterize histology.

Trichogram (analysis of plucked hairs under microscopy) determines percentage of hairs in different growth phases (anagen, catagen, telogen) but requires plucking multiple hairs and provides limited additional information compared to clinical assessment and dermoscopy.

When to See a Dermatologist for Biopsy

Seek dermatology evaluation if experiencing progressive hair loss with atypical features (scalp symptoms, scarring appearance, resistant to treatment, family history suggesting inherited alopecia), or if clinical diagnosis is uncertain. Dermatologists can determine whether biopsy is indicated, perform the procedure with minimal discomfort, and arrange proper interpretation of results.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will scalp biopsy leave a visible scar? Most scalp biopsy scars become imperceptible over time; approximately 95% of patients develop scars that are not visible on casual inspection. Scars are typically 3-4 mm in diameter and located in inconspicuous areas.

Does scalp biopsy hurt? Local anesthesia injection causes brief stinging followed by numbness. The biopsy procedure itself is painless due to anesthesia. Mild discomfort for 24-48 hours post-procedure is typical.

How long do biopsy results take? Pathology processing typically takes 5-7 business days; results are usually available within 1 week. Complex cases requiring immunofluorescence or special stains may take slightly longer (7-14 days).

Can biopsy be done at the same visit as clinical diagnosis? Yes. Many dermatologists perform biopsy during initial evaluation when diagnosis is uncertain, allowing results to guide treatment planning at follow-up visit.
