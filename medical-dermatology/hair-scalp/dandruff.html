Dandruff: Scalp Flaking and Scaling

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Dandruff is a common scalp condition characterized by excessive scaling and flaking of the scalp affecting up to 50% of the population. While typically a cosmetic concern rather than serious medical condition, dandruff significantly impacts quality of life and patient satisfaction. Effective management requires understanding underlying pathophysiology and appropriate use of topical remedies.

Pathophysiology

Dandruff results from increased scalp keratinocyte turnover and desquamation, often associated with Malassezia species colonization and scalp microbiome dysbiosis. Individual susceptibility varies based on sebaceous gland activity, scalp microbiota composition, genetic factors, and environmental conditions. Lipid metabolism by Malassezia produces inflammatory mediators triggering scaling and pruritus.

Clinical Features

Dandruff manifests with:

  • White or yellow scale flaking from scalp
  • Mild erythema and inflammation
  • Pruritus ranging from minimal to moderate
  • Scale may be visible on shoulders from shedding
  • Often worse in winter or dry seasons
  • Typically does not cause permanent hair loss
  • May be more apparent in individuals with darker hair

Seborrheic Dermatitis Distinction

Dandruff exists on a spectrum with seborrheic dermatitis, representing milder disease with less inflammation. Seborrheic dermatitis demonstrates more prominent erythema, thicker scale, and involvement beyond scalp. Both conditions may present with identical scaling and pruritus.

First-Line Treatments

Over-the-counter medicated shampoos containing zinc pyrithione, selenium sulfide, ketoconazole, or coal tar effectively reduce scale and pruritus in most patients. Shampooing 2-3 times weekly with medicated products provides optimal benefit. Response typically occurs within 2-4 weeks of consistent use.

Medicated Shampoo Selection

Zinc pyrithione shampoos (Head & Shoulders, others) provide first-line accessible option with good efficacy. Selenium sulfide (Selsun) provides alternative mechanism. Ketoconazole (Nizoral) targets Malassezia species and shows excellent efficacy. Coal tar products provide traditional option with dual antimicrobial and keratolytic effects. Rotating between agents may prevent tolerance development.

Topical Scalp Treatments

Topical corticosteroid lotions or foams (low to medium potency) reduce inflammation and pruritus when medicated shampoos alone prove insufficient. Azoles or other antifungals may provide additional benefit. Combination approaches often yield superior outcomes.

Maintenance Therapy

Dandruff typically requires ongoing maintenance therapy to prevent recurrence. Once controlled with medicated shampoos, tapering to 1-2 weekly applications often maintains control. Some patients require continuous treatment. Identifying and avoiding personal triggers (stress, certain hair products) supports long-term management.

Preventive Measures

Gentle scalp hygiene, avoiding harsh shampooing, and regular cleansing support dandruff management. Stress reduction, adequate sleep, and balanced nutrition may decrease flare frequency. Avoiding heavy hair products and ensuring adequate rinsing minimize scale accumulation.

When to Seek Professional Care

Severe or refractory dandruff, associated significant erythema or exudation, or suspected seborrheic dermatitis warrant dermatologic evaluation. Diagnosis confirmation and therapeutic optimization by dermatologist aids resistant cases.
