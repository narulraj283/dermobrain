Hair & Scalp Disorders

Telogen Effluvium: Diffuse Hair Shedding

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Telogen effluvium is a reversible, non-scarring alopecia characterized by synchronized shift of hair follicles from the growth phase (anagen) to the resting phase (telogen), resulting in diffuse hair shedding. It typically affects 10-20% of the hair follicle population and is one of the most common causes of hair loss in dermatology practice.

Pathophysiology

A significant physiologic stress triggers premature termination of anagen phase, shifting follicles into telogen prematurely. After 2-3 months in telogen phase, these hairs are shed, resulting in sudden onset of increased hair loss 2-4 weeks after the triggering event. The condition is self-limited, with spontaneous resolution as the follicle cycle normalizes.

Triggering Factors

Common precipitants include:

  • Fever or acute systemic illness
  • Severe emotional or psychological stress
  • Major surgery or anesthesia
  • Significant weight loss or nutritional deficiency
  • Medications (beta-blockers, anticonvulsants, anticoagulants)
  • Hormonal changes (postpartum, oral contraceptive initiation)
  • Chronic illness including thyroid dysfunction and lupus

Clinical Features

Patients report sudden onset of increased hair shedding, often noticed during shampooing or brushing. Examination reveals diffuse hair thinning without alopecic patches. Hair pull test typically yields 3 or more telogen hairs (compared to normal 0-1). Scalp appears normal without inflammation or scarring.

Diagnostic Approach

Diagnosis is primarily clinical, supported by history of recent stressor and diffuse shedding pattern. Dermoscopy demonstrates telogen hairs with preserved hair follicles. Scalp biopsy is rarely necessary but would reveal increased telogen phase hairs (>25% of follicles in telogen) with normal sebaceous gland structure.

Acute vs. Chronic Telogen Effluvium

Acute telogen effluvium typically resolves within 3-6 months of the triggering event. Chronic telogen effluvium persists beyond 6 months without identifiable trigger, affecting middle-aged women predominantly. Some patients develop persistent shedding despite treatment.

Management

Identifying and addressing the underlying trigger is paramount. Treatment is largely supportive, with reassurance regarding the benign and self-limiting nature of the condition. Nutritional supplementation (iron, zinc, B vitamins) may benefit patients with documented deficiencies. Minoxidil provides supportive benefit in chronic cases.
