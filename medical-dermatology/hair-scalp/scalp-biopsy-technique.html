Clinical Overview

Trichotillomania, commonly known as hair-pulling disorder, is a body-focused repetitive behavior disorder classified as an obsessive-compulsive and related disorder in DSM-5. This condition involves recurrent, irresistible urges to pull out hair from the scalp, eyebrows, eyelashes, or other body areas, resulting in noticeable hair loss and significant distress or functional impairment. Trichotillomania affects approximately 0.5-2% of the population with female predominance (female-to-male ratio 1.5:1). The condition typically begins in adolescence but can manifest at any age. Unlike scarring alopecias which permanently destroy follicles, trichotillomania is non-scarring; follicles remain intact and hair can regrow once the pulling behavior ceases.

Epidemiology and Clinical Features

Trichotillomania affects an estimated 2-5 million Americans. The condition shows female predominance with onset typically in early adolescence (ages 10-13), though adult-onset cases occur. Prevalence may be underestimated due to shame and embarrassment preventing disclosure. Comorbidity with anxiety disorders (60%), depression (35%), and obsessive-compulsive disorder (25%) is common. Some individuals pull hair in response to stress or anxiety (automatic pulling), while others deliberately pull hair as a coping mechanism for negative emotions (focused pulling), and many experience both patterns.

Pathophysiology and Etiology

The etiology of trichotillomania is incompletely understood but likely involves: (1) neurobiological dysregulation of impulse control and reward pathways (dopamine system abnormalities), (2) abnormalities in serotonin neurotransmission (similar to obsessive-compulsive disorder), (3) genetic predisposition (familial clustering documented), (4) psychological factors including anxiety, stress, perfectionism, and negative affect regulation, and (5) sensory gratification from the tactile sensation of pulling. Functional MRI studies show abnormal activation in brain regions implicated in impulse control (anterior cingulate cortex, orbitofrontal cortex). The condition is not caused by poor willpower or conscious self-harm intent; it represents genuine neurobehavioral pathology.

Clinical Presentation

Patients present with visible hair loss typically on the scalp, though eyebrows, eyelashes, and body hair may be affected. Hair loss is non-scarring; the scalp appearance shows areas of thinned or missing hair but scalp skin is normal without erythema, inflammation, or scarring. Patients often have difficulty achieving symmetric hair loss and may show patches of varying hair lengths from repeated re-growing and re-pulling. Behavioral features include: (1) repetitive hair pulling throughout the day often without awareness, (2) conscious pulling episodes triggered by stress or negative emotions, (3) repeated failed attempts to reduce or stop pulling, (4) significant distress or functional impairment from hair loss (embarrassment, social withdrawal, occupational impairment), and (5) hair loss that is not attributable to a dermatologic condition.

Associated features include: (1) examination of pulled hairs (root, feeling texture), (2) plucking of eyelashes and eyebrows, (3) "body-focused repetitive behavior" activities such as skin picking (dermatillomania) or nail biting (onychophagia), (4) shame and concealment of hair loss (wearing hats, wigs, hairstyles to cover loss), and (5) impact on self-esteem and social functioning. Some patients unconsciously pull while concentrating, watching television, or reading (automatic style); others consciously pull during stress (focused style).

Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis

Diagnosis is primarily clinical based on: (1) history of repetitive hair pulling, (2) visible evidence of hair loss, (3) recurrent urges or mounting tension before pulling, (4) gratification or relief following pulling, (5) significant distress or functional impairment, and (6) hair loss not better explained by dermatologic condition or medical disorder. The pull test (gentle pulling on 60 hairs) may show multiple hairs with white bulbs at varying stages of growth consistent with repeated trauma. Dermoscopy may reveal broken hairs at different lengths. Scalp biopsy is rarely needed but shows preserved follicular structures without fibrosis or permanent destruction, distinguishing trichotillomania from scarring alopecias.

Differential diagnosis includes: (1) tinea capitis (fungal infection with positive KOH preparation or culture), (2) alopecia areata (patchy alopecia with exclamation mark hairs and positive autoimmune serology), (3) androgenetic alopecia (gradual pattern-based thinning, not patchy), (4) telogen effluvium (diffuse shedding from specific trigger, not mechanical trauma), (5) trichotillomania-like disorders in autism or intellectual disability, (6) factitious disorder if pulling is intentional self-harm for secondary gain. Careful history regarding pulling behavior distinguishes trichotillomania from these conditions.

Psychosocial Impact

Trichotillomania has substantial psychosocial consequences: (1) body image disturbance and low self-esteem from visible alopecia, (2) social withdrawal and isolation from embarrassment and self-consciousness, (3) reduced quality of life scores comparable to major depressive disorder in some studies, (4) occupational or academic impact from distraction and emotional distress, and (5) interpersonal difficulties and isolation. Many patients experience shame and attempt to conceal hair loss through hats, wigs, or specific hairstyles. Psychological assessment for depression, anxiety, and functional impairment is appropriate.

Treatment Approaches

Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) specifically habit reversal training (HRT) is the most effective psychological intervention with response rates of 60-90% achieving significant reduction in pulling behavior. HRT involves: (1) awareness training—identifying triggers and sensations preceding pulling, (2) competing response training—developing alternative behaviors incompatible with pulling (e.g., clenching fists, putting hands in pockets), (3) motivation enhancement through discussing consequences of pulling, and (4) relapse prevention. Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT) is an alternative CBT approach with emerging evidence. Therapy typically requires 10-20 sessions for meaningful benefit.

Pharmacotherapy options include: (1) selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs)—fluoxetine 40-80 mg daily, sertraline 100-200 mg daily, paroxetine 40-60 mg daily—show response rates of 50-70% in achieving reduction in pulling urges and behavior, though inconsistent across studies. SSRIs are helpful for comorbid anxiety and depression. (2) Clomipramine (tricyclic antidepressant with serotonergic properties) 100-250 mg daily shows response rates of 70-80% in some studies but has side effects limiting use. (3) N-acetylcysteine (NAC), an amino acid supplement, 1200-2400 mg daily has shown promise in small studies with response rates of 50-60% and minimal side effects; however, evidence is preliminary. (4) Habit reversal training combined with SSRI shows superior response (70-80%) compared to either monotherapy.

Supportive measures include: (1) hair care counseling emphasizing gentle handling to allow hair regrowth, (2) scalp protectants (bandages, gloves) to reduce access and tactile feedback from pulling, (3) support groups (Trichotillomania Learning Center provides resources) for psychological support and normalization, (4) family involvement in treatment to provide encouragement and support, and (5) stress management and relaxation techniques to reduce anxiety-driven pulling.

Prognosis

With appropriate cognitive-behavioral therapy, approximately 60-90% of patients achieve significant reduction in pulling behavior (50%+ decrease in hair loss). Combined CBT plus SSRI shows response rates of 70-80%. Untreated trichotillomania may persist for years or decades. Early intervention optimizes outcomes. Hair regrowth typically begins 3-6 months after pulling behavior ceases, with full hair density restoration within 12-24 months if follicles have not been damaged by chronic trauma.

When to Seek Psychiatric or Dermatologic Care

Individuals experiencing recurrent hair pulling with resulting visible hair loss should seek psychiatric evaluation for trichotillomania diagnosis and initiation of CBT or pharmacotherapy. Dermatologists can confirm non-scarring etiology, rule out dermatologic causes of alopecia, and provide supportive counseling. Collaborative psychiatric-dermatologic management optimizes outcomes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is trichotillomania the same as intentional self-harm? Trichotillomania differs from intentional self-harm in that patients typically experience pulling as involuntary or ego-dystonic (inconsistent with their values). While pulling provides gratification or tension relief, it is not deliberate self-injury with intent to cause harm.

Will my hair grow back? Yes. Trichotillomania is non-scarring; follicles are preserved and hair regrows completely once pulling behavior ceases, typically over 6-12 months.

Can medication cure trichotillomania? Medication reduces pulling urges and frequency but does not cure the condition. Cognitive-behavioral therapy combined with medication provides best outcomes; treatment requires ongoing management.

Is trichotillomania related to OCD? Trichotillomania shares features with OCD (repetitive urges, difficulty resisting) and often coexists with OCD. Both respond to similar treatments (CBT, SSRIs) but are classified as distinct disorders.
