Trichotillomania: Hair Pulling Disorder

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Trichotillomania, or hair-pulling disorder, is a body-focused repetitive behavior characterized by recurrent hair pulling resulting in noticeable hair loss and distress. It is classified as an obsessive-compulsive and related disorder and affects approximately 1-2% of the population, with higher prevalence in children and adolescents. The condition often begins in childhood and may persist into adulthood.

Pathophysiology

Trichotillomania results from complex interactions between genetic predisposition, neurobiological factors (particularly serotonin and glutamate dysfunction), psychological stress, and environmental triggers. Some individuals pull hair as a tension-reduction mechanism (automatic pulling), while others pull in response to anxiety or perfectionism concerns (focused pulling).

Clinical Presentation

Typical features include:

  • Recurrent hair pulling from scalp, eyebrows, eyelashes, or body
  • Noticeable hair loss with visible bald patches or thinning
  • Short, broken hairs of variable length at pull site margins
  • Non-scarring alopecia with preserved hair follicles
  • Possible trichobezoar (hairball) formation with gastrointestinal symptoms
  • Associated anxiety, depression, or stress
  • Shame, embarrassment, or distress regarding appearance
  • Attempted hair-pulling cessation with recurrent relapse

Clinical Subtypes

Automatic pulling occurs unconsciously, often during focus on other activities, while focused pulling is intentional response to emotional distress. Mixed presentations are common. Some patients restrict pulling to specific areas while others pull from multiple sites.

Diagnostic Criteria

Diagnosis requires recurrent hair pulling resulting in noticeable hair loss, repeated attempts to decrease or stop pulling, and significant distress or functional impairment. Differentiation from alopecia areata and other causes of hair loss is important.

Psychological Assessment

Comprehensive psychological evaluation assesses severity, triggers, associated psychiatric conditions (obsessive-compulsive disorder, anxiety, depression), and impact on quality of life. Cognitive-behavioral factors including perfectionism and frustration tolerance are relevant.

Treatment Approach

Cognitive-behavioral therapy, particularly habit reversal training and comprehensive behavioral (ComB) model, demonstrates efficacy. Pharmacotherapy with selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) or N-acetylcysteine provides supplementary benefit. Acceptance and commitment therapy offers alternative psychological approach. Family involvement in treatment enhances outcomes, particularly in children.

Prognosis

Early intervention improves outcomes. With appropriate treatment, many patients achieve significant improvement in pulling behavior and associated distress. Some individuals experience chronic relapsing course requiring ongoing management.
