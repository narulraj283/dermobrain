Traction Alopecia: Hair Loss from Physical Tension

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Traction alopecia is a form of acquired non-scarring to scarring alopecia resulting from chronic mechanical tension on hair follicles. It occurs predominantly in individuals who style hair tightly using braids, weaves, cornrows, buns, or extensions. Early recognition and modification of hair styling practices can prevent progression to permanent scarring alopecia.

Pathophysiology

Sustained mechanical traction on hair follicles causes both temporary disruption of the hair growth cycle and chronic inflammation of the follicle and surrounding tissue. Initially, the condition is non-scarring and reversible with cessation of the inciting tension. However, prolonged traction leads to fibrosis, inflammation, and permanent destruction of hair follicles, resulting in irreversible scarring alopecia.

Clinical Presentation

Early traction alopecia presents with:

  • Hair loss in distribution corresponding to tension sites (frontotemporal, marginal areas)
  • Follicular hyperkeratosis or papules at affected margins
  • Possible pruritus or tenderness
  • Preserved hair follicles in early disease
  • History of tight hairstyling practices

Chronic traction alopecia demonstrates scarring with follicular loss and smooth scalp appearance.

Risk Factors

African-American females represent the highest-risk population due to cultural hair styling practices involving tight braiding and extensions. However, traction alopecia occurs across all ethnicities. Duration and frequency of traction, hair texture, and individual follicle sensitivity influence disease severity.

Diagnostic Assessment

Diagnosis is clinical, supported by characteristic alopecia pattern and history of tight hairstyling. Dermoscopy may reveal reduced hair density with increased follicular spacing. Scalp biopsy in chronic traction alopecia demonstrates inflammatory infiltration and follicular fibrosis.

Management and Prevention

Early cessation of tension-inducing hairstyles is critical to prevent irreversible scarring. Hair styling modifications including looser braiding, avoiding extensions, and varying hair styles should be implemented. Topical corticosteroids may reduce inflammation and associated symptoms. Once scarring has occurred, hair transplantation offers the only option for hair restoration.

Prognosis

Early-stage traction alopecia responds well to hairstyle modification, with potential for complete hair regrowth within months to years. Chronic, scarring traction alopecia carries poor prognosis for spontaneous recovery, necessitating surgical intervention for restoration.
