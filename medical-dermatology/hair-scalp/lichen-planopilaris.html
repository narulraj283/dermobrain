Lichen Planopilaris: Inflammatory Scarring Alopecia

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Lichen planopilaris (LPP) is a primary scarring alopecia characterized by lichen planus affecting the hair follicles and scalp. It represents the scarring variant of cutaneous lichen planus and can result in permanent hair loss if inadequately treated. The condition affects both adults and children, though peak incidence occurs in middle age with female predominance.

Pathophysiology

Lichen planopilaris results from a T-cell mediated autoimmune attack on the hair follicle, particularly targeting the outer root sheath. Chronic inflammation leads to fibrosis of the follicle and sebaceous gland destruction, ultimately resulting in permanent alopecia. The etiology remains incompletely understood, though genetic predisposition and possible triggers (infections, drugs, trauma) may be involved.

Clinical Presentation

Patients present with:

  • Slowly progressive hair loss in patches or diffuse distribution
  • Violaceous or erythematous inflammation at active margins
  • Follicular hyperkeratosis and papules
  • Pruritus, burning, or pain at affected areas
  • Possible associated lichen planus on skin or mucous membranes
  • Advanced disease shows scarring alopecia with smooth scalp
  • Peripilar inflammation with perifollicular involvement

Diagnostic Approach

Scalp biopsy is essential for diagnosis, revealing lymphocytic infiltration at the follicular bulge and surrounding tissue with sebaceous gland involvement. Distinctive features include circumscribed interface dermatitis and fibrosis in chronic lesions. Associated skin or mucosal lichen planus may be present.

Disease Classification

Lichen planopilaris is classified as primary scarring alopecia to distinguish it from secondary scarring alopecias. Variants include localized patches or more extensive involvement. Some patients develop coexistent frontal fibrosing alopecia (Graham-Little-Lassueur variant).

Management

Early intervention is paramount to prevent irreversible scarring. Intralesional and topical corticosteroids provide first-line therapy. Systemic corticosteroids may benefit extensive disease. Antimalarial agents (hydroxychloroquine), retinoids, and JAK inhibitors show promise in treatment-resistant cases. Minoxidil provides supportive benefit. Advanced scarring disease requires surgical hair transplantation.

Prognosis and Outcomes

Prognosis depends on disease stage at treatment initiation and treatment response. Early-stage disease with active inflammation responds better to medical therapy. Chronic disease with established scarring carries poor prognosis for medical management and may require surgical intervention.
