Hair & Scalp Disorders

Frontal Fibrosing Alopecia

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Frontal fibrosing alopecia (FFA) is a progressive primary scarring alopecia characterized by fibrosis and destruction of hair follicles along the hairline, with characteristic loss of the marginal row of hair follicles. It primarily affects middle-aged and postmenopausal women, though cases in men and younger individuals have been reported. The incidence appears to be increasing, particularly in Caucasian populations.

Pathophysiology

FFA results from chronic lymphocytic inflammation targeting hair follicles and sebaceous glands in the frontal scalp region. The disease progresses with follicular fibrosis and permanent loss of hair-bearing tissue. The etiology remains poorly understood, though genetic predisposition, hormonal factors, and environmental triggers may contribute. The uniform anterior-to-posterior progression pattern distinguishes FFA from other forms of scarring alopecia.

Clinical Features

Characteristic presentation includes:

  • Gradual recession of the hairline with well-demarcated border
  • Loss of the marginal row of follicles (distinguishing feature)
  • Band-like scarring alopecia of the frontal scalp
  • Preservation of behind-the-hairline follicles
  • Associated eyebrow loss in approximately 40% of cases
  • Associated facial papules and follicular hyperkeratosis
  • Pruritus or paresthesia at affected margins

Associated Features

Some patients develop concurrent lichen planopilaris on the scalp or lichen sclerosus of genital areas. Facial involvement with lichen planus or discoid lupus erythematosus may also occur. Systemic involvement is uncommon.

Diagnostic Approach

Diagnosis is clinical, based on characteristic hairline recession pattern and loss of marginal row follicles. Dermoscopy demonstrates reduced follicle density, follicular keratinization, and absent marginal follicles. Scalp biopsy shows lymphocytic infiltration with follicular fibrosis and sebaceous gland loss, features diagnostic of scarring alopecia.

Treatment Options

Early intervention is critical to slow disease progression. First-line treatments include topical and intralesional corticosteroids to reduce inflammation. Oral antiandrogens (spironolactone), 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride, dutasteride), and antimalarial agents (hydroxychloroquine) may benefit some patients. Newer JAK inhibitors show promise in preliminary reports. Advanced scarring alopecia may require surgical hair transplantation.

Prognosis

FFA is progressive, though rate of progression varies among patients. Early recognition and treatment may slow or stabilize disease. Once significant fibrosis occurs, medical therapy offers limited benefit, and surgical intervention becomes necessary.
