Hair & Scalp Disorders

Iron Deficiency and Hair Loss

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Iron deficiency represents an important modifiable cause of hair loss affecting both ferritin-dependent telogen effluvium and potentially androgenetic alopecia. Iron is essential for hemoglobin synthesis, myoglobin function, and iron-containing enzymes critical to hair follicle metabolism. Recognition and treatment of iron deficiency often improves hair growth, making this a particularly rewarding treatable etiology.

Pathophysiology

Hair follicles are metabolically active tissues requiring substantial ATP production for growth. Iron-containing enzymes including cytochromes and ribonucleotide reductase are essential for cellular proliferation in follicular epithelium. Iron deficiency impairs these pathways, leading to reduced anagen duration and premature telogen conversion.

Hair Loss Presentation

Iron deficiency typically presents as telogen effluvium with diffuse hair shedding and decreased hair density. Some evidence suggests iron deficiency may worsen androgenetic alopecia severity. Hair loss may precede or accompany other iron deficiency manifestations including fatigue, dyspnea, and pallor.

Risk Populations

Women of reproductive age represent the highest-risk group due to menstrual blood loss. Heavy menstrual bleeding dramatically increases iron depletion risk. Postpartum women demonstrate elevated iron loss through pregnancy demands and possible delivery blood loss. Vegetarians and vegans with inadequate iron intake show increased deficiency risk.

Diagnostic Approach

Iron studies including serum iron, ferritin, transferrin saturation, and total iron binding capacity establish diagnosis. Ferritin is most practical screening test, with levels <30 ng/mL indicating depleted iron stores. Hemoglobin and hematocrit assess for concurrent anemia. Complete evaluation addresses underlying cause of iron loss (gastrointestinal bleeding, heavy menstrual periods).

Iron Supplementation and Hair Recovery

Oral iron supplementation (ferrous sulfate 325 mg daily) repletes iron stores and supports hair recovery. Response timeline typically extends 3-6 months following ferritin normalization as follicles complete telogen phase and new growth emerges. Target ferritin levels of 50-100 ng/mL appear optimal for hair recovery.

Supplementation Considerations

Iron supplementation often causes gastrointestinal side effects including nausea, constipation, and abdominal discomfort. Taking iron with food reduces absorption but improves tolerance. Intravenous iron may be considered in patients with gastrointestinal intolerance or malabsorption. Treatment duration of 3-6 months is typically required for complete iron repletion.

Iron Excess Caution

Excessive iron supplementation in iron-replete individuals may cause oxidative stress and potential harm. Iron supplementation should only be initiated in documented iron-deficient individuals. Periodic reassessment prevents excessive iron accumulation.

Combination with Other Therapies

Iron supplementation combines favorably with minoxidil or finasteride for androgenetic alopecia. Nutritional assessment addressing additional micronutrient deficiencies (zinc, vitamin B12, folate) optimizes overall hair health.
