Clinical Overview

Hair loss in children presents unique diagnostic and therapeutic challenges compared to adults, as the differential diagnosis is broader and treatment options are more limited due to safety considerations in pediatric patients. Common causes of alopecia in children include: alopecia areata (most common), tinea capitis (fungal infection), trichotillomania (hair-pulling disorder), telogen effluvium from systemic illness or stress, and rarely, scarring alopecias or genetic hair shaft abnormalities. Accurate diagnosis is critical to guide appropriate treatment and alleviate parental anxiety about permanent hair loss.

Epidemiology of Childhood Alopecia

Alopecia areata is the most common cause of non-scarring alopecia in children, affecting approximately 1-2% of children with peak onset between ages 25-36 years but significant incidence in childhood. Tinea capitis (scalp ringworm) affects 3-14% of children depending on geographic location and race, being more common in African-American children in the United States. Trichotillomania affects 0.5-2% of children with onset typically in ages 5-8 years. Telogen effluvium occurs in children following acute illness, nutritional deficiency, or stress. Scarring alopecias are uncommon in children except for specific entities like folliculitis decalvans (rare) or inherited hair shaft abnormalities. The broader differential diagnosis in children requires systematic evaluation to identify the cause.

Alopecia Areata in Children

Alopecia areata presents identically in children as adults: patchy, non-scarring hair loss often noticed by parents. Children frequently have multiple patches and higher rates of alopecia totalis/universalis progression (15-30%) compared to adults (2-7%). The condition causes substantial psychologic impact in children, affecting self-esteem and social functioning. Many children with AA also have atopic conditions (atopy, asthma, allergic rhinitis, eczema) at higher rates than unaffected controls. Diagnosis is clinical; scalp biopsy is rarely needed in children. Treatment options are more limited in children than adults: (1) intralesional corticosteroid injection (triamcinolone 2.5 mg/mL) is effective and can be performed in office with topical anesthetic; approximately 50-75% achieve significant hair regrowth. (2) Topical corticosteroids (class III-IV potency) applied to patchy areas may provide benefit (response rates 30-40%). (3) Topical minoxidil 5% applied twice daily is safe in children though FDA approval is for adults; approximately 40-50% achieve modest regrowth. (4) JAK inhibitors (baricitinib, ritlecitinib) have been approved for pediatric use (ages 12+ for ritlecitinib), representing a major advance for children with extensive alopecia areata. Response rates are 80-90% according to trials, making JAK inhibitors increasingly first-line for extensive disease in older children.

Tinea Capitis in Children

Tinea capitis (scalp ringworm) is common in children and presents with patchy hair loss, scalp scale, and sometimes pustules or crusting. Diagnosis requires KOH preparation or fungal culture showing dermatophyte growth. Treatment requires systemic antifungal therapy: (1) griseofulvin (20-25 mg/kg/day for 4-6 weeks) is the traditional first-line agent, with cure rates of 85-95%. Side effects are minimal in children. (2) Terbinafine (4-6 mg/kg/day for 4 weeks) is increasingly used as an alternative with similar efficacy (85-95% cure) and potential for shorter treatment duration. (3) Itraconazole (5-10 mg/kg/day for 4-6 weeks) is another effective alternative. Topical antifungals alone are inadequate; systemic therapy is necessary. Adjunctive topical therapy (selenium sulfide shampoo, ketoconazole shampoo) may reduce contagion risk. Household contacts should be evaluated. School attendance restrictions vary by jurisdiction; most schools allow attendance once systemic antifungal treatment is started.

Trichotillomania in Children

Trichotillomania in children typically presents with onset ages 5-8 years, manifesting as hair loss, often with parents noticing patches of shortened hair or hair loss while combing. Some children unconsciously pull during concentration or boredom (automatic style) while others pull in response to stress (focused style). Diagnosis is clinical based on history of pulling behavior and visible non-scarring hair loss. Behavioral approaches including habit reversal training are more effective than pharmacotherapy in children: (1) HRT with behavioral modification, awareness training, and competing response development achieves response rates of 70-90% in motivated children. (2) Family involvement and support is critical for success. (3) SSRIs are used less frequently in children than adults due to black box warning for increased suicidality risk in pediatric depression, though they may be helpful for comorbid anxiety or depression. (4) School counseling and psychological support optimize outcomes. Hair typically regrows within 3-6 months after pulling behavior ceases.

Telogen Effluvium in Children

Telogen effluvium presents with diffuse hair shedding (parents report "hair everywhere") following a triggering event: acute illness (fever, infection), surgery, trauma, nutritional deficiency (iron, protein, zinc), or psychologic stress. Recovery is typically spontaneous within 3-6 months once the trigger is addressed. Management is supportive: ensuring adequate nutrition, treating underlying infections, addressing psychologic stressors, and reassurance that hair loss is temporary and reversible.

Evaluation of Alopecia in Children

History should address: (1) age of onset and duration of hair loss, (2) pattern of hair loss (patchy vs. diffuse), (3) recent illness, fever, or systemic symptoms, (4) recent medication use, (5) nutritional status and dietary habits, (6) behavioral features (pulling behavior, repetitive scalp touching), (7) family history of alopecia or autoimmune disease, and (8) psychologic stressors.

Physical examination should characterize: (1) distribution of hair loss (patchy, diffuse, localized to pulling-prone areas), (2) scalp appearance (inflammation, scale, pustules, erythema, scarring), (3) hair shaft appearance (length variation suggesting pulling), (4) lymphadenopathy, and (5) other stigmata of systemic disease (hepatosplenomegaly, skin findings).

Laboratory assessment is often unnecessary for classic presentations but may include: (1) KOH preparation or fungal culture if tinea capitis suspected, (2) CBC and iron studies if telogen effluvium suspected (screen for anemia and iron deficiency), (3) thyroid function (TSH, free T4) if systemic disease is suspected, (4) vitamin B12 and folate if nutritional deficiency is concern.

Prognosis in Childhood Alopecia

Most childhood alopecias have favorable prognosis: alopecia areata shows spontaneous remission in 40-50% of children with patchy disease, higher remission rates with early-onset; tinea capitis responds to antifungal therapy with cure rates 85-95%; trichotillomania responds well to behavioral therapy with 70-90% improvement in pulling behavior and complete hair regrowth; telogen effluvium resolves spontaneously within 3-6 months. Only scarring alopecias carry poor prognosis for regrowth.

When to See a Pediatric Dermatologist

Any child with unexplained progressive hair loss should be evaluated by a dermatologist. Specific indications include: (1) patchy alopecia (rule out alopecia areata or tinea capitis), (2) scarring alopecia appearance, (3) hair loss with systemic symptoms, (4) alopecia unresponsive to initial treatment, or (5) significant psychologic impact from hair loss. Early diagnosis enables prompt treatment and prevents psychologic morbidity.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will my child's hair grow back? Most childhood alopecias are non-scarring and completely reversible. Hair regrows once the underlying condition is treated or the trigger is removed, typically within 3-6 months for telogen effluvium, tinea capitis, and trichotillomania.

Is alopecia areata contagious? No. Alopecia areata is not contagious. Close contacts will not acquire the condition. However, tinea capitis is contagious; close contacts may acquire fungal infection and require evaluation and treatment.

Should we screen for systemic disease? Children with patchy alopecia should be evaluated for tinea capitis and alopecia areata clinically; extensive laboratory screening is unnecessary unless systemic symptoms are present or clinical evaluation suggests systemic illness.

Can we prevent alopecia in children at high genetic risk? Alopecia areata and androgenetic alopecia have genetic predisposition, but there is no proven prevention. Early recognition and treatment of alopecia areata may improve outcomes.
