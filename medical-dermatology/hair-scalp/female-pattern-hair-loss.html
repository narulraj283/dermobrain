Female Pattern Hair Loss (Androgenetic Alopecia in Women)

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Female pattern hair loss (FPHL) is a non-scarring alopecia affecting women of all ages, though incidence increases significantly after age 50. It accounts for up to 40% of female hair loss complaints and shares similar pathophysiology to male pattern baldness, involving DHT sensitivity in genetically predisposed individuals.

Epidemiology and Risk Factors

FPHL affects approximately 5-10% of women before age 50, increasing to 40-50% by age 80. Risk factors include family history, elevated androgen levels, polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), hormonal fluctuations, and certain medications. Unlike males, women rarely develop complete baldness.

Clinical Features

Female pattern hair loss characteristically presents with:

  • Diffuse hair thinning over the crown and vertex
  • Preserved frontal hairline (unlike male pattern baldness)
  • Widening central hair part
  • Increased shedding when shampooing or brushing
  • Progressive reduction in hair density
  • No scalp inflammation or tenderness

Classification

The Ludwig scale categorizes female pattern hair loss from Type I (minimal widening of center part) to Type III (significant thinning with exposed scalp). The Savin scale provides additional assessment of hair density at crown and frontal regions.

Diagnostic Approach

Clinical examination combined with hair pull test and dermoscopy typically establishes diagnosis. Hormonal evaluation including androgen levels, PCOS screening, and thyroid function testing may be indicated when presenting features suggest endocrinopathy. Scalp biopsy is rarely necessary for uncomplicated cases.

Treatment Modalities

Topical minoxidil is FDA-approved for female pattern hair loss and generally demonstrates better tolerance than oral medications. Oral finasteride, while effective, carries teratogenic risks and is typically reserved for postmenopausal women. Anti-androgens such as spironolactone may benefit women with elevated androgens. Hair transplantation provides long-term restoration.

Management Considerations

Early intervention offers superior outcomes, and combination therapies often provide enhanced benefits. Counseling regarding realistic expectations, treatment duration (3-6 months before visible improvement), and long-term maintenance is essential for patient satisfaction.
