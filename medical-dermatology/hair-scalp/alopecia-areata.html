Alopecia Areata: Autoimmune Hair Loss

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Alopecia areata (AA) is a common non-scarring alopecia characterized by sudden onset of hair loss in well-demarcated patches. It affects approximately 1-2% of the population and results from autoimmune dysfunction targeting hair follicles. Onset can occur at any age, though peak incidence occurs in the second and third decades of life.

Pathophysiology

In alopecia areata, CD8+ T lymphocytes and other immune cells infiltrate hair follicles in the anagen growth phase, leading to premature telogen conversion and hair shedding. Genetic susceptibility and environmental triggers (stress, infections, trauma) likely contribute to disease initiation. Association with other autoimmune conditions including thyroid disease, vitiligo, and systemic lupus erythematosus is well established.

Clinical Presentation

Typical features include:

  • Sudden onset of well-circumscribed alopecic patches
  • Non-scarring alopecia with preserved follicular openings
  • Exclamation mark hairs (fractured hairs with dark tips and pale roots) at patch margins
  • Nail pitting or ridging in 10-20% of cases
  • Possible beard, eyebrow, or body hair involvement
  • Pruritus or paresthesia preceding hair loss

Severity Classification

Disease extent guides prognosis and treatment decisions. Localized patches affecting less than 25% of scalp show better prognosis, while extensive alopecia totalis and alopecia universalis indicate more severe disease with lower remission rates.

Diagnostic Approach

Diagnosis is primarily clinical. Dermoscopy reveals typical features including empty follicles, acute phase inflammation, and structural hair changes. Scalp biopsy, though rarely necessary, demonstrates characteristic lymphocytic infiltration around anagen follicles with preserved sebaceous glands and arrector pili muscles.

Treatment Options

Corticosteroid injections and topical corticosteroids provide first-line therapy for localized disease. Systemic corticosteroids may be considered for extensive involvement. Topical immunotherapy (dinitrochlorobenzene or squaric acid dibutyl ester), minoxidil, and newer JAK inhibitors show promise. Psychological support is essential given the significant emotional impact.

Prognosis

Approximately 50% of patients with localized alopecia areata experience spontaneous remission within 1 year. Negative prognostic factors include early age of onset, extensive alopecia, and associated autoimmune conditions. Long-standing disease with multiple episodes carries lower remission likelihood.
