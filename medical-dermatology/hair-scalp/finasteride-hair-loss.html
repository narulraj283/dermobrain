Finasteride for Hair Loss: 5-Alpha Reductase Inhibition

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Overview

Finasteride is an oral 5-alpha reductase inhibitor FDA-approved for male pattern baldness treatment. By inhibiting conversion of testosterone to dihydrotestosterone (DHT), finasteride addresses the hormonal basis of androgenetic alopecia. It represents the first and most extensively studied oral therapy for hair loss and demonstrates substantial efficacy in slowing and reversing androgenetic alopecia progression.

Mechanism of Action

Finasteride inhibits 5-alpha reductase, the enzyme converting testosterone to DHT. By reducing scalp DHT levels by approximately 70%, follicular miniaturization is halted and potentially reversed. This hormone-specific mechanism makes finasteride particularly effective for DHT-dependent alopecia.

Dosing and Administration

The standard dose for hair loss treatment is 1 mg daily, taken orally without regard to meals. This lower dose (Propecia) was specifically approved for hair loss, distinct from the 5 mg dose (Proscar) used for benign prostatic hyperplasia. Consistent daily administration is necessary, with therapeutic effects requiring 3-6 months to become apparent.

Efficacy in Men

Clinical trials demonstrate that approximately 66% of men using finasteride experience hair regrowth, while 48% achieve dense hair regrowth. In non-responders, finasteride effectively slows or stabilizes hair loss in up to 88% of users. Longer treatment duration (>2 years) yields superior outcomes. Efficacy is greatest in younger men with early-stage baldness.

Use in Women

Finasteride is not FDA-approved for female pattern hair loss and is relatively contraindicated due to pregnancy risks and theoretical concerns regarding decreased 5-alpha reductase in women. However, postmenopausal women may benefit from finasteride for androgenetic alopecia, particularly when elevated androgens are present. Pregnancy category X precludes use in premenopausal women due to potential feminization of male fetuses.

Adverse Effects

Sexual dysfunction occurs in 1-3% of users and may include decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, or ejaculation disorders. These effects are generally reversible upon discontinuation, though delayed resolution occurs in some patients. Gynecomastia (breast enlargement) is rare. Systemic absorption is minimal with standard 1 mg dosing.

Monitoring and Long-Term Outcomes

Long-term studies (5-10 years) demonstrate sustained efficacy and safety with continuous finasteride use. Discontinuation results in hair loss resumption, necessitating indefinite treatment for maintained benefit. Combination with minoxidil enhances efficacy compared to either agent alone.

Drug Interactions and Monitoring

Finasteride has minimal drug interactions. Baseline and periodic PSA (prostate-specific antigen) assessment should be considered, as finasteride may lower PSA levels in men receiving prostate cancer screening. Overall mortality and prostate cancer incidence studies show reassuring safety profiles.
