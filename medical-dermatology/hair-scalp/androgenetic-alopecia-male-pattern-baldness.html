Hair Loss (Androgenetic Alopecia): Why It Happens and How to Treat It

The Bottom Line

Pattern hair loss is the most common cause of thinning hair, affecting about 50% of men by age 50 and 40% of women by age 70. It's driven by genetics and a hormone called DHT that gradually shrinks hair follicles over time.

Two treatments have strong clinical evidence: minoxidil (Rogaine — available over the counter) and finasteride (Propecia — prescription for men). Used consistently, they can slow loss and regrow some hair in the majority of users. Starting early gives the best results because it's easier to maintain existing hair than regrow lost hair.

When to see a dermatologist: If you're noticing your part widening, your hairline receding, or more hair in the drain than usual — especially if it's happening quickly or in patches (which may signal a different type of hair loss).

What Is Androgenetic Alopecia?

Androgenetic alopecia — commonly called male or female pattern hair loss — is a hereditary condition where hair gradually becomes thinner and shorter over years or decades. It's not about hair "falling out" in the dramatic sense. Instead, each growth cycle produces a slightly thinner, shorter strand until the follicle eventually produces only fine, nearly invisible "peach fuzz" (called vellus hair).

In men, this typically shows up as a receding hairline and thinning at the crown — the classic M-shaped pattern. In women, it usually presents as diffuse thinning across the top of the scalp while the hairline stays intact. Dermatologists use the Norwood scale (stages I-VII) for men and the Ludwig scale (stages I-III) for women to track progression.

Why Does It Happen?

Your hair follicles have receptors for a hormone called dihydrotestosterone (DHT). An enzyme called 5-alpha reductase converts regular testosterone into DHT. In people genetically predisposed to pattern hair loss, DHT attaches to receptors in scalp follicles and triggers a process called follicular miniaturization — the follicle gradually shrinks with each hair growth cycle.

Here's what that looks like over time: A healthy scalp hair grows for 2-6 years (the anagen phase), rests briefly, sheds, and regrows. With androgenetic alopecia, DHT shortens this growth phase to just weeks or months. The hair that grows back is thinner, lighter, and shorter each time. Eventually, the follicle produces only tiny vellus hairs or stops producing visible hair altogether.

Genetics are the primary factor. If your parents or grandparents lost their hair, you're significantly more likely to as well — and the pattern often resembles theirs. Multiple genes are involved, inherited from both sides of the family (not just your mother's side, despite the popular myth).

How Dermatologists Diagnose It

Most cases are diagnosed by visual examination alone — the pattern is distinctive. Your dermatologist may also use:

The pull test: Your doctor gently tugs on about 60 hairs in different areas. Losing more than 6 hairs suggests active shedding, which can help distinguish pattern hair loss from other conditions.

Dermoscopy: A special magnifying device examines your scalp at high magnification. In androgenetic alopecia, it reveals miniaturized hairs of varying diameters alongside normal ones — a hallmark finding.

Blood tests: Not always needed for classic pattern hair loss, but your doctor may check thyroid function, iron levels, and hormones (especially in women) to rule out other causes of thinning.

Treatments That Work

Minoxidil (Rogaine): Available over the counter as a 5% foam or 2% liquid, applied to the scalp once or twice daily. Minoxidil works by increasing blood flow to hair follicles and prolonging the growth phase. In clinical trials, about 40% of men using 5% minoxidil saw moderate regrowth after 48 weeks, with another 20-30% maintaining existing hair. Results take 4-6 months to appear, and you need to use it continuously — hair loss resumes if you stop. The main side effect is occasional scalp irritation.

Finasteride (Propecia): A prescription pill (1mg daily) that blocks 5-alpha reductase, reducing DHT levels by about 70%. The landmark Olsen et al. 2002 study in the Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology showed 48% of men taking finasteride experienced moderate to dense regrowth after two years, and 83% maintained their hair compared to placebo. Side effects can include decreased libido or erectile changes in 1-2% of men, which typically resolve if the medication is stopped. Finasteride is not approved for women of childbearing age because it can cause birth defects.

Dutasteride (Avodart): A stronger DHT blocker (0.5mg daily) that inhibits both forms of 5-alpha reductase. Studies show it may be slightly more effective than finasteride for hair regrowth. Currently used off-label for hair loss in the United States but approved for this purpose in several other countries.

Low-level laser therapy (LLLT): FDA-cleared devices like laser combs and helmets deliver red light at 655-1000nm wavelength to the scalp. Clinical trials show modest improvement in hair density — about 20-25% increase in hair count. Best used as an add-on to minoxidil or finasteride rather than as a standalone treatment.

Emerging and Surgical Options

Platelet-rich plasma (PRP): Your own blood is drawn, processed to concentrate growth-factor-rich platelets, and injected into the scalp. Multiple studies show improvements in hair density, though protocols and results vary. Typically requires 3-4 sessions initially, then maintenance treatments every 6-12 months.

Hair transplant surgery: Follicles are moved from the back and sides of the scalp (where they're resistant to DHT) to thinning areas. Modern techniques — follicular unit extraction (FUE) and follicular unit transplantation (FUT) — produce natural-looking results. The transplanted hairs are permanent, but you'll likely still need minoxidil or finasteride to protect the non-transplanted native hairs.

Oral minoxidil: Low-dose oral minoxidil (1.25-5mg daily) has gained popularity among dermatologists as an alternative to the topical form. Early studies suggest comparable or superior effectiveness with better adherence since it's a pill instead of a daily scalp application. Side effects can include slight increases in body hair and rare blood pressure changes at low doses.

What to Expect

Hair loss treatment is a long game. Most treatments require 4-6 months before visible improvement, and the goal is threefold: stop further loss, maintain what you have, and regrow where possible. Earlier treatment produces better results because it's much easier to maintain miniaturizing follicles than to revive dormant ones.

Combining treatments — such as minoxidil plus finasteride — produces better outcomes than either alone. Your dermatologist can tailor a plan based on your stage of loss, age, sex, and personal preferences.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is hair loss from my mom's side only?

No — this is a myth. Androgenetic alopecia involves multiple genes inherited from both parents. While the androgen receptor gene sits on the X chromosome (from your mother), other contributing genes come from both sides. Looking at both parents and grandparents gives a better picture of your risk.

Will wearing hats make my hair fall out?

No. Hats don't cause hair loss. Androgenetic alopecia is driven by hormones and genetics acting on the follicle from inside, not by external pressure or friction from hats. Extremely tight hairstyles (tight braids, ponytails) can cause a different type of hair loss called traction alopecia, but normal hat wearing is perfectly fine.

Do I need to use minoxidil forever?

Yes — if you stop minoxidil, the hair it was maintaining will gradually thin again over 3-6 months. The same is true for finasteride. These treatments manage the condition rather than cure it. The exception is hair transplant surgery, where transplanted follicles are permanent.

Can women use finasteride?

Finasteride is not approved for women and must not be used during pregnancy due to risk of birth defects. However, some dermatologists prescribe it off-label for postmenopausal women with androgenetic alopecia. Spironolactone (an anti-androgen medication) is more commonly used for female pattern hair loss at doses of 100-200mg daily.
