Understanding Chemical Peels: Types and Benefits

Chemical Peel Overview

Chemical peels use exfoliating acids or caustic agents to remove damaged outer skin layers, promoting cellular turnover and collagen remodeling. Peels range from gentle superficial treatments (minimal downtime) to deep peels (significant downtime, more dramatic results). Superficial peels address fine lines, surface texture, and mild hyperpigmentation. Medium peels improve moderate photodamage and acne scars. Deep peels (rarely performed) produce dramatic results suitable for severe photodamage but carry significant side effect risks.

How Chemical Peels Work

Chemical agents disrupt cellular adhesion between keratinocytes and cause controlled exfoliation of epidermis and varying depths of dermis. Exfoliation removes damaged, pigmented cells and promotes new cell turnover. Dermal injury stimulates collagen and elastin remodeling over weeks-to-months. The controlled inflammatory response initiates healing cascade with improved skin texture, firmness, and tone. Results compound with serial treatments; maintenance peels sustain benefits.

Peel Depth Classification

Superficial (Lunchtime) Peels

Remove epidermis only; no dermal involvement. Immediate mild erythema and slight scaling; resolves within 24-48 hours. Minimal downtime; suitable for work/social obligations. Results modest; multiple treatments (monthly) required. Ideal for those unable to tolerate significant downtime. Safe for all skin types.

Medium Peels

Remove epidermis and upper dermis. Erythema and edema 24-48 hours; flaking/peeling 3-7 days post-treatment. Moderate downtime; 1-week social downtime advisable. Results more dramatic than superficial peels; typically 1-3 treatments sufficient. Some dyspigmentation risk in darker skin. Less frequent treatment schedule (3-6 months) sufficient.

Deep Peels

Remove epidermis and deep dermis; rarely performed due to significant side effects (persistent erythema, permanent dyspigmentation, scarring risk). Requires strict post-operative care and significant recovery time (2-3 weeks minimum). Results dramatic but risks substantial, particularly in darker skin types. Reserved for severe photodamage in ideal candidates.

Chemical Agents

Glycolic Acid: Most common AHA; 15-20% superficial peels, 30-70% medium peels. Smallest molecular weight permits deepest penetration. Salicylic Acid: Beta-hydroxy acid; lipophilic, penetrates sebaceous follicles. Excellent for acne-prone and oily skin. Lactic Acid: Milder AHA; larger molecular weight, more superficial. Good for sensitive/dry skin. Trichloroacetic Acid (TCA): Medium-to-deep peeling agent; more intense than AHA/BHA. 10-20% for superficial, 25-35% for medium depth. Jessner's Solution: Combination agent (salicylic acid, lactic acid, resorcinol); medium peel depth. Phenol: Deep peel agent; significant systemic absorption risk; rarely used in modern practice.

Alpha-Hydroxy Acids

Water-soluble acids that exfoliate through disruption of cellular adhesion. Glycolic acid (smallest molecule) penetrates deepest; lactic acid gentler for sensitive skin. Concentrations determine intensity: 5-10% for at-home use; 15-70% for professional treatments. Lower concentrations (at-home) require regular use (2-3x weekly) for cumulative benefits. pH below 3 critical for efficacy; higher pH (more buffered) less irritating but less effective. AHAs increase photosensitivity; strict sun protection required post-treatment.

Beta-Hydroxy Acids

Lipophilic salicylic acid exfoliates within sebaceous follicles and surface epidermis. Excellent for congestion-prone and oily skin due to follicular penetration. Less irritating than AHAs; preferred for sensitive skin. Available at-home in lower concentrations (0.5-2%) and professional treatments (20-30%). Salicylic acid peels particularly beneficial for acne-prone individuals.

Benefits and Outcomes

Superficial peels improve skin texture, fine lines, and mild dyspigmentation. Serial treatments show cumulative collagen stimulation and skin quality improvement. Medium peels produce moderate-to-significant improvement in photodamage, acne scars, and texture. Results visible within 1-2 weeks and continue improving over months as collagen remodels. Deep peels produce dramatic wrinkle reduction and scar improvement but carry significant risk/benefit considerations.

Ideal Candidates

Fair-to-medium skin types tolerate peels best. Darker skin types can undergo peels but require careful agent selection (gentler agents) and monitoring for post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. Individuals with active herpes simplex should receive antiviral prophylaxis. Those with unrealistic expectations or inability to comply with post-treatment care are poor candidates. Pregnancy is relative contraindication for medium-to-deep peels. Recent isotretinoin use (within 1 year) is absolute contraindication.

Expert Tip

Superficial peels are excellent maintenance tool for skin quality; monthly glycolic acid peels sustain radiance and texture improvement. Medium peels show more dramatic results but require coordination with patient schedule due to downtime. Always combine with rigorous post-peel sun protection (SPF 50+) to prevent post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, particularly in darker skin.

Post-Peel Care

  • Avoid sun exposure for minimum 2 weeks post-peel; SPF 50+ daily essential
  • Avoid heat, sweating, and strenuous exercise for 24-48 hours
  • Use gentle cleanser and fragrance-free products until peeling resolves
  • Avoid active ingredients (retinoids, vitamin C, AHAs/BHAs) for 3-7 days post-peel to prevent irritation
  • Apply soothing moisturizer and occlusive barrier repair products during peeling phase
  • Avoid picking or manually removing peeling skin; allow natural shedding
  • For darker skin: consider hydroquinone use post-peel to prevent post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation
  • Avoid extraneous irritants (sauna, chlorine, etc.) for 1-2 weeks
