PCOS and Skin: Understanding Androgenic Effects on Dermatology

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

PCOS Dermatological Manifestations

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) significantly impacts dermatological health through elevated androgen levels. Approximately 70% of PCOS patients experience acne, while hirsutism affects 60-80% of patients depending on ethnic background and diagnostic criteria. Understanding these skin manifestations is crucial for comprehensive PCOS management.

Acne in PCOS Patients

PCOS-related acne typically presents as severe, cystic lesions distributed across the face, chest, and back. The condition often begins or worsens in the teen years and may persist into adulthood without appropriate treatment. Elevated free testosterone and androstenedione promote sebaceous gland hyperplasia and sebum overproduction.

Hirsutism and Hair Growth Disorders

Hirsutism, characterized by terminal hair growth in androgen-dependent areas, affects numerous PCOS patients. Women may experience unwanted facial hair, increased body hair on the arms, legs, chest, and abdomen. Alopecia androgenetica (female pattern hair loss) frequently co-occurs with hirsutism.

Associated Skin Conditions

  • Acanthosis nigricans (dark, velvety patches typically in intertriginous areas)
  • Seborrheic dermatitis and oily scalp
  • Skin tags (acrochordons), particularly in neck and axillae regions
  • Increased propensity for folliculitis

Dermatological Treatment Approaches

Oral contraceptives with low-androgenic progestins (norgestimate, desogestrel) effectively reduce acne severity in PCOS patients. Spironolactone at 50-200 mg daily provides additional androgen blockade. Topical retinoids, benzoyl peroxide, and antimicrobials address acne vulgaris simultaneously. For hirsutism, combination therapy with oral medications and mechanical hair removal provides optimal results.

Systemic Management Considerations

Weight loss, when applicable, reduces androgen production and improves insulin sensitivity. Metformin therapy, beyond its glycemic benefits, may modestly reduce androgen levels. Dermatologists should coordinate with endocrinologists to optimize hormonal management and skincare outcomes.
