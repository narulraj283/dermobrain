Female Pattern Hair Loss: Androgenetic Alopecia in Women

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Female Pattern Hair Loss: Epidemiology and Pathophysiology

Female pattern hair loss (androgenetic alopecia) affects approximately 50% of women by age 50, with significant quality-of-life implications. Unlike male pattern baldness, women typically experience diffuse hair thinning rather than circumscribed alopecia. The condition results from genetic predisposition, hormonal sensitivity to androgens, and age-related changes in hair follicle physiology.

Genetic and Hormonal Factors

Androgenetic alopecia demonstrates polygenic inheritance with complex heritability patterns. The 5-alpha reductase enzyme converts testosterone to dihydrotestosterone (DHT), the primary androgen stimulating hair follicle miniaturization. Women with genetic sensitivity to DHT experience progressive follicle shrinkage. Contrary to misconceptions, elevated androgen levels are not required; normal hormone levels with increased follicular sensitivity suffice.

Classification and Clinical Features

The Ludwig scale categorizes female pattern hair loss severity: Grade I represents minimal thinning at the part line; Grade II demonstrates visible widening at the part line; Grade III shows marked diffuse thinning and scalp visibility. Female pattern hair loss characteristically spares the frontal hairline and temples, differentiating it from male pattern baldness. Hair loss typically begins during the fourth-fifth decades but may manifest earlier with genetic predisposition.

Mechanism of Hair Follicle Miniaturization

DHT binds to follicular androgen receptors, triggering intrafollicular signal transduction that shortens the anagen (growth) phase and lengthens the telogen (resting) phase. Progressive follicle miniaturization reduces hair shaft diameter and pigmentation. Terminal hairs become transformed into vellus-like hairs. This biochemical process, once initiated, typically progresses unless pharmacologically interrupted.

Diagnostic Evaluation

Clinical diagnosis relies on characteristic diffuse thinning pattern with preserved frontal hairline. Dermoscopy reveals reduced terminal/vellus hair ratios and increased follicular variability. Pull test may demonstrate increased telogen hairs. Trichogram analysis quantifies terminal and vellus hair proportions. Laboratory assessment including hormone levels (testosterone, DHEA-S) may identify underlying endocrine abnormalities requiring treatment.

Evidence-Based Treatment Modalities

Minoxidil 2-5% topical solution demonstrates modest efficacy in halting hair loss and promoting regrowth in 40% of women. Finasteride 1 mg daily inhibits 5-alpha reductase, reducing DHT levels and slowing follicle miniaturization. Spironolactone provides additional anti-androgenic effects. Combination therapies utilizing multiple mechanisms optimize outcomes.

Emerging and Adjunctive Treatments

Low-level laser therapy stimulates hair follicle cycling and may promote regrowth through photobiomodulation. Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) injections show promise in early studies, though larger randomized trials are needed. Nutritional supplementation with biotin, iron, and zinc supports hair growth physiology. Stress reduction and lifestyle modifications may limit telogen effluvium concurrent with androgenetic alopecia.
