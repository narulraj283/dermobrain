Melasma: Comprehensive Guide to Hyperpigmentation in Women

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Melasma Epidemiology and Pathophysiology

Melasma is a common acquired hypermelanosis affecting predominantly women, with 90% of cases occurring in female patients. The condition predominantly affects individuals with Fitzpatrick skin types III-VI, particularly those of Hispanic, Asian, Middle Eastern, and African descent. Hormonally mediated, melasma frequently develops during reproductive years in association with oral contraceptives, hormone replacement therapy, and pregnancy.

Etiological Factors

Estrogen and progesterone fluctuations trigger melanocytic proliferation and increased melanin synthesis. Ultraviolet radiation remains a critical cofactor, with cumulative sun exposure accelerating melasma development. Genetic predisposition significantly influences individual susceptibility. Phototoxic medications and cosmetics containing irritating ingredients may exacerbate pigmentation.

Clinical Presentation

Melasma presents as symmetric, brown-to-gray macules and patches on photodistributed areas. The centrofacial pattern (cheeks, nose, forehead, upper lip) represents the most common presentation (55-65% of cases), while the lateral malar pattern affects the cheeks and temples. A perioral pattern occasionally develops. Lesions are typically asymptomatic with sharply demarcated borders.

Diagnostic Evaluation

Clinical diagnosis relies on characteristic morphology and distribution. Wood's lamp examination enhances visualization of epidermal melanin while revealing the depth of pigmentation. Dermoscopy confirms increased melanin without obvious malignancy. Biopsy is rarely necessary unless atypical features suggest lentigo maligna or other conditions.

Evidence-Based Treatment Modalities

Strict photoprotection with broad-spectrum SPF 30+ sunscreen represents the foundation of melasma management. Hydroquinone 2-4% remains the gold standard depigmenting agent, inhibiting tyrosinase activity. Tretinoin enhances penetration and dermal remodeling. Combination therapies, including the Kligman formulation (hydroquinone, tretinoin, and fluocinolone acetonide), demonstrate superior efficacy compared to monotherapy.

Chemical peels utilizing glycolic acid, salicylic acid, or TCA provide adjunctive benefits. Laser and light-based therapies, including Q-switched neodymium:yttrium-aluminum-garnet (Nd:YAG) lasers and fractional non-ablative devices, offer alternatives for treatment-resistant cases. Combination approaches frequently yield optimal results with reduced recurrence.

Maintenance and Recurrence Prevention

Melasma frequently recurs following treatment cessation without rigorous photoprotection. Maintenance therapy with lower-concentration depigmenting agents, topical vitamin C, and niacinamide helps prevent rebound pigmentation. Avoidance of estrogen-containing medications when possible reduces risk.
