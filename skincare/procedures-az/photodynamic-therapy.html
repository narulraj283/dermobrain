Photodynamic Therapy

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Photodynamic Therapy Overview

Photodynamic therapy (PDT) is a two-stage treatment combining a light-sensitizing medication with specific light wavelengths to selectively destroy abnormal cells. PDT effectively treats actinic keratosis, prevents skin cancer development, addresses severe acne, and treats certain skin cancers with excellent cosmetic outcomes.

How Photodynamic Therapy Works

PDT involves applying a photosensitizing agent (typically 5-aminolevulinic acid or methyl aminolevulinate) to the skin, which is selectively taken up by abnormal cells. After an incubation period (varying by formulation, from minutes to hours), the area is exposed to specific light wavelengths (typically red light at 630nm). The photosensitizer absorbs light energy and generates reactive oxygen species that destroy targeted cells. Normal skin is relatively spared.

Indications for PDT

PDT effectively treats:

  • Actinic keratosis (precancerous lesions)
  • Bowen's disease (cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma in situ)
  • Basal cell carcinoma (certain superficial types)
  • Severe acne unresponsive to conventional therapy
  • Photoaging and sun damage prevention
  • Rosacea
  • Sebaceous gland hyperplasia

The Procedure

PDT begins with thorough cleansing and sometimes light curettage of the treatment area to remove surface debris. The photosensitizing agent is applied evenly and left in place for an incubation period (5-20 minutes depending on formulation and indication). After incubation, protective eyewear is applied. The area is exposed to appropriate wavelength light, typically with a handheld device or lamp positioned at a set distance. Light exposure continues for several minutes. During light activation, patients experience warmth and may feel mild stinging or burning sensations.

Results and Recovery

Post-treatment redness is immediate and may be intense. Crusting and peeling typically begin within 24-48 hours and continue for 5-10 days. Treated actinic keratosis lesions progressively slough off. Results develop over 2-4 weeks as healing completes and damaged cells are eliminated. Cumulative benefits continue with repeated treatments. Maximum efficacy typically requires 1-3 treatments spaced 4-6 weeks apart.

Downtime and Aftercare

PDT requires more significant downtime than many procedures. Patients should expect visible crusting and peeling for 5-10 days, necessitating avoidance of social activities. Protective makeup should be avoided during crusting phase. Strict sun avoidance and sunscreen use (SPF 50+) is critical for 2-4 weeks. Most patients can return to work after makeup application once acute reactions resolve. Photosensitivity requires sun protection for 24-48 hours after treatment.

Cost Considerations

PDT costs range from $500-1,500 per treatment depending on area size and photosensitizing agent used. A series of 2-3 treatments costs $1,000-4,500. Insurance coverage varies; therapeutic PDT for precancers may be covered while cosmetic uses may not. Prior authorization may be required.

Risks and Complications

PDT is generally safe. Common temporary effects include redness, crusting, peeling, and stinging during treatment. Rare complications include infection, scarring, paradoxical worsening of acne initially, and contact dermatitis to the photosensitizing agent. Prolonged erythema lasting weeks can occur. Temporary pigmentation changes are possible. Serious complications are uncommon. Sun sensitivity requires careful sun protection for optimal safety. Pregnancy is a relative contraindication for cosmetic treatment.
