Dermabrasion

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Dermabrasion Overview

Dermabrasion is a mechanical skin resurfacing procedure that uses a rotating instrument with an abrasive surface to remove the epidermis and portions of the dermis. This controlled abrasion stimulates collagen remodeling and reveals fresh, healthier skin underneath.

How It Works

During dermabrasion, a diamond fraise or wire brush rotates at high speed to mechanically abrade away damaged skin layers. The depth of abrasion is controlled by the practitioner through hand pressure and instrument technique. Unlike laser resurfacing, dermabrasion removes skin physically rather than thermally, allowing for precise depth control and excellent hemostasis during treatment.

Indications for Treatment

Dermabrasion effectively treats:

  • Acne scars (ice pick, boxcar, and rolling scars)
  • Traumatic scars and lacerations
  • Fine lines and wrinkles
  • Sun-damaged skin
  • Tattoo removal or lightening
  • Rhinophyma (advanced rosacea)
  • Melasma and hyperpigmentation

The Procedure

Dermabrasion is performed under local anesthesia, sedation, or general anesthesia depending on extent. The skin is cleaned and marked. The rotating instrument systematically abrades the treatment area, with the depth carefully controlled to achieve optimal results without excessive tissue damage. The procedure typically takes 1-2 hours for full-face treatment. Immediate hemostasis is excellent, with minimal bleeding. Profound swelling and redness are expected immediately post-procedure.

Recovery Timeline

Recovery is significant. Treated areas are extremely swollen, red, and may ooze for 7-10 days. Patients must keep wounds clean and moisturized per specific protocols. After 10-14 days, peeling begins and continues for 2-3 weeks. Social recovery requires 2-3 weeks before makeup can be applied. Complete skin maturation and final results take 6-12 months as collagen remodeling continues. Strict sun avoidance is critical during healing.

Cost Considerations

Dermabrasion costs range from $1,500 to $4,000 depending on treatment area, depth, and anesthesia requirements. Limited areas cost $500-1,500. Insurance typically does not cover cosmetic dermabrasion, though therapeutic treatments for scars may have partial coverage depending on the policy.

Risks and Complications

Significant complications are possible with dermabrasion. Risks include infection, scarring, prolonged erythema, hypertrophic scarring, contact dermatitis from ointments, and permanent pigmentation changes. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation is common in darker skin types and may be permanent. Herpes simplex reactivation is possible. Proper patient selection and realistic expectations are essential. Not recommended for patients with active acne, certain medication use, or darker skin types due to pigmentation risks.
