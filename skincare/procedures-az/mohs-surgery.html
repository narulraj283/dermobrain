Mohs Surgery

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Mohs Surgery Overview

Mohs micrographic surgery is the gold standard treatment for skin cancers, particularly basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma. This specialized surgical technique combines complete tumor removal with immediate microscopic margin assessment, ensuring complete cancer elimination while maximizing normal tissue preservation.

How Mohs Surgery Works

During Mohs surgery, the visible tumor is first removed, then progressively deeper layers are excised and examined under a microscope. A map is created showing tumor location and extent. The surgeon removes additional tissue only from areas where cancer cells are identified under microscopy. This process repeats until microscopic examination confirms no cancer cells remain. Complete mapping ensures no cancer is missed while removing minimal normal tissue.

Indications for Mohs Surgery

Mohs surgery is indicated for:

  • Basal cell carcinoma, especially recurrent or high-risk cases
  • Squamous cell carcinoma, particularly aggressive variants
  • Melanoma (some cases with specialized technique)
  • Merkel cell carcinoma
  • Tumors in cosmetically sensitive areas (face, ears)
  • Tumors with ill-defined borders
  • Recurrent skin cancers
  • Tumors in high-risk locations for recurrence

The Procedure

Mohs surgery requires local anesthesia. The visible tumor is anesthetized and marked. The surgeon removes it along with a thin border of normal tissue. The specimen is color-coded in quadrants and processed for microscopic examination, typically taking 30-60 minutes. While awaiting results, the patient waits in the office. If cancer cells are identified, the surgeon removes additional tissue from marked areas. This process continues until margins are clear. Total procedure time typically ranges from 1.5 to 3 hours but can extend longer for larger or more complex tumors.

Reconstruction and Healing

After complete tumor removal, the defect is reconstructed using various techniques from simple linear closure to complex flaps or grafts depending on size and location. Small wounds may be closed primarily, while larger defects may require multiple reconstructive stages. Sutures are typically removed in 7-14 days. Healing takes 2-4 weeks depending on defect size and reconstruction complexity. Some patients require additional surgical stages for proper reconstruction.

Recovery and Results

Swelling and redness peak at 2-3 days and gradually improve. Suture sites require careful hygiene. Pain is minimal and managed with over-the-counter analgesia. Most patients return to light activities within 1 week, though strenuous activity should be avoided for 2-3 weeks. Scarring is inevitable but typically minimal due to tissue preservation. Scars mature over 12 months.

Cure Rates and Outcomes

Mohs surgery achieves cure rates of 95-99% for basal cell carcinoma and 90-95% for squamous cell carcinoma, superior to conventional excision. The technique's margin control minimizes recurrence risk. Close histologic examination ensures complete tumor removal. Five-year recurrence rates are significantly lower than other surgical modalities.

Cost Considerations

Mohs surgery costs typically range from $1,500 to $3,000 per case depending on complexity and reconstruction needs. Pathology and reconstruction may add additional costs. Insurance usually covers Mohs surgery as treatment for confirmed skin cancer. Most insurance plans require prior authorization.

Risks and Complications

Mohs surgery has minimal serious complications when performed by trained specialists. Risks include infection (preventable with proper technique), bleeding, nerve damage causing temporary numbness or weakness, and recurrent cancer (rare). Scarring is inevitable but usually cosmetically acceptable due to careful reconstruction. Keloid formation is uncommon. Ensure treatment by a board-certified Mohs surgeon with appropriate credentials.
