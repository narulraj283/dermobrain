Botox Treatment

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Botox Treatment Overview

Botox (botulinum toxin type A) is a purified neurotoxin that temporarily paralyzes muscles, reducing the appearance of wrinkles and fine lines. It is the most commonly performed non-surgical cosmetic procedure in dermatology, FDA-approved for treating dynamic wrinkles caused by facial muscle contractions.

Indications for Treatment

Botox is indicated for:

  • Forehead lines and wrinkles
  • Crow's feet (periorbital lines)
  • Glabellar lines (between eyebrows)
  • Bunny lines on the nose
  • Marionette lines
  • Chin dimpling
  • Therapeutic uses including hyperhidrosis and migraine

The Procedure

The procedure is quick, typically requiring only 10-15 minutes. A topical anesthetic or ice may be applied for comfort. The dermatologist uses a very fine needle to inject precise amounts of Botox into targeted facial muscles. The number of injections varies based on treatment areas, typically ranging from 20-64 units. Patients feel minimal discomfort, often describing it as a slight pinch. Multiple treatment areas can be addressed in a single session, and the procedure requires no downtime.

Results and Timeline

Results are not immediate. Initial effects appear at 3-5 days, with full results visible at 10-14 days. Maximum effects are achieved at 4 weeks. Results typically last 3-4 months, after which muscle activity gradually returns. Maintenance treatments are required to sustain results. First-time patients may experience less dramatic results, with subsequent treatments sometimes providing improved outcomes as muscles adapt.

Cost Considerations

Botox costs range from $200 to $600 per treatment session, depending on geographic location, practitioner expertise, and number of units used. Most practitioners charge per unit, with costs typically $10-25 per unit. Patients requiring ongoing maintenance should budget $600-1,800 annually for treatment.

Risks and Complications

Common side effects include temporary bruising, swelling, and redness at injection sites (usually resolving within 24-48 hours). Rare but serious complications include ptosis (drooping eyelid), facial asymmetry, and headache. Allergic reactions are uncommon. Over-treatment can result in an unnatural appearance. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid Botox. Patients taking blood thinners should disclose this information before treatment.
