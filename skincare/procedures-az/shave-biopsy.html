Shave Biopsy

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Shave Biopsy Overview

A shave biopsy is a quick, minimally invasive diagnostic procedure that uses a razor or scalpel blade to remove a superficial skin lesion. The technique provides tissue for histopathologic examination while removing the lesion in a single procedure. It is ideal for raised, pedunculated, or superficial lesions.

How Shave Biopsy Works

The lesion is anesthetized with local anesthetic. A sharp blade is positioned parallel to the skin surface and used to shave off the lesion and a thin margin of surrounding normal skin. The specimen typically includes epidermis and upper dermis but not deeper dermal or subcutaneous tissue. Hemostasis is achieved with pressure, cautery, or topical hemostatic agents. The specimen is submitted for histopathologic examination.

Indications for Shave Biopsy

Shave biopsy is appropriate for:

  • Raised pigmented lesions suspicious for melanoma (with caveats)
  • Non-melanoma skin cancers
  • Seborrheic keratosis
  • Benign nevi with changing appearance
  • Skin lesions requiring diagnosis
  • Small suspicious lesions
  • Cosmetically sensitive areas where minimal scarring is desired

The Procedure

After local anesthesia with lidocaine, the lesion is marked with a pen. A sharp blade (scalpel or razor) is positioned tangentially to the skin and used to shave the lesion off flush with surrounding skin. The blade is moved with gentle sawing motions for clean removal. The specimen is placed on gauze and submitted in formalin. Hemostasis is achieved using pressure, electrocautery, or hemostatic powder if needed. The procedure typically takes only 5-10 minutes.

Recovery and Healing

Recovery is minimal. The area appears as a shallow erosion or abrasion initially. No stitches are required for typical shave biopsies. Bleeding is minimal and easily controlled. Healing typically takes 1-3 weeks. Mild crusting may occur during healing. Scarring is usually minimal, particularly on the face where cosmetic results are generally excellent. Patients can apply antibiotic ointment and bandage the area if desired. Normal activities can resume immediately.

Diagnostic Considerations

Shave biopsy may not capture the full depth of deeper lesions, potentially missing important diagnostic information. For suspected melanoma, controversy exists regarding whether shave biopsies adequately evaluate the full lesion. Some practitioners avoid shave biopsies for pigmented lesions suspicious for melanoma, preferring excisional biopsy for complete lesion assessment. Consultation with the dermatologist is important to determine the most appropriate biopsy technique.

Cost Considerations

Shave biopsy costs range from $150-400 depending on lesion size and location. Pathology processing adds $100-300. Insurance typically covers shave biopsy for diagnostic purposes. Coverage is more variable for cosmetic removal of benign lesions.

Risks and Complications

Shave biopsy is very safe with minimal complications. Infection is uncommon with proper aftercare (<1%). Bleeding is minimal and easily controlled. Scarring is minimal and typically cosmetically acceptable, particularly for facial lesions. Recurrence can occur if lesion removal is incomplete. The main limitation is potential inadequate depth for certain lesions. Hypertrophic scarring or keloid formation occasionally occurs in predisposed individuals. Overall, shave biopsy is one of the safest dermatologic procedures.
