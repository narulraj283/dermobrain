Laser Skin Resurfacing

Written by DermoBrain Medical Team Medically Reviewed

Laser Skin Resurfacing Overview

Laser skin resurfacing uses focused light energy to remove the outer layers of skin, stimulating collagen remodeling and revealing fresh, healthier skin underneath. Available in ablative and non-ablative versions, laser resurfacing effectively treats wrinkles, scars, discoloration, and other age-related skin changes with precision.

Types of Laser Resurfacing

Ablative lasers (CO2 and erbium:YAG) vaporize skin layers, providing dramatic results but requiring significant recovery time. Non-ablative lasers (Nd:YAG, diode) heat deeper skin layers without removing the epidermis, providing gentler results with minimal downtime. Fractional lasers (creating a grid of treated areas) offer intermediate results and recovery. Each type has specific advantages and is selected based on patient goals and tolerance for downtime.

Indications for Treatment

Laser skin resurfacing addresses:

  • Fine lines and wrinkles, particularly around the eyes and mouth
  • Acne scars (ice pick, boxcar, and rolling)
  • Traumatic scars
  • Sun-damaged and photoaged skin
  • Uneven skin texture and rough skin
  • Hyperpigmentation and age spots
  • Melasma (non-ablative lasers preferred)

Ablative Laser Resurfacing

Ablative procedures require anesthesia and involve complete removal of epidermis and controlled portions of dermis. CO2 lasers (10,600nm) provide the most aggressive ablation, while erbium:YAG (2,940nm) allows more precise depth control with less collateral heat damage. These procedures are maximally effective for scars and wrinkles but require significant recovery. Swelling and redness last 1-2 weeks, with complete healing taking 6-12 weeks. Strict sun avoidance is critical.

Non-Ablative Laser Resurfacing

Non-ablative lasers preserve the epidermis while heating deeper dermal layers, triggering collagen remodeling. Results are more subtle than ablative treatments but develop progressively over months. Multiple treatments (4-6) spaced weeks apart are typically required. Recovery is minimal with only mild redness and swelling. These are ideal for patients with minimal downtime tolerance.

Fractional Laser Resurfacing

Fractional resurfacing treats a fraction of skin surface area, leaving bridges of untreated tissue for faster healing. Both ablative and non-ablative fractional versions exist, offering middle ground between aggressive ablation and gentler non-ablative approaches. Recovery is faster than full-field ablation but more significant than non-ablative treatments.

The Procedure

Ablative treatments are performed under local anesthesia or sedation. The laser systematically treats the entire face or targeted areas, with depth carefully controlled. The procedure typically takes 30-60 minutes for full-face treatment. Fractional and non-ablative treatments require only topical anesthetic and take similar timeframes. Immediate post-procedure appearance is redness and swelling.

Recovery Timeline

Recovery varies dramatically by laser type. Ablative laser recovery includes significant swelling, weeping, and discomfort for 7-10 days. Peeling and crusting continue for 2-3 weeks. Social recovery requires 2-3 weeks. Complete healing and final results take 3-6 months. Fractional laser recovery involves redness and mild swelling for a few days. Non-ablative laser recovery is minimal with only mild redness. Strict sun protection is essential during all recovery periods.

Cost Considerations

Non-ablative laser resurfacing costs $800-2,000 per treatment (4-6 treatments needed). Fractional ablative resurfacing costs $2,000-4,000. Full-field ablative resurfacing costs $3,000-8,000. Treatment of specific lesions costs less than full-face treatment. Insurance typically does not cover cosmetic resurfacing, though therapeutic treatment of certain conditions may have partial coverage.

Risks and Complications

Ablative lasers carry greater risk of complications including infection, scarring, extended erythema, hypertrophic scarring, contact dermatitis, and permanent pigmentation changes. Non-ablative lasers have minimal risks. Common temporary effects include redness, swelling, and temporary pigmentation changes. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation is more common in darker skin types. Herpes simplex reactivation is possible with ablative procedures. Proper patient selection, skin type assessment, and realistic expectations are essential for safety and satisfaction.
